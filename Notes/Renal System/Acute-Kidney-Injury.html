Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

Sudden, rapid loss of kidney function over hours to days — potentially reversible with early treatment

Creatinine = Best Indicator Oliguria <400 mL/day Prerenal → Intrarenal → Postrenal ↑K+ = Cardiac Emergency

1. Three Categories (Causes)

Prerenal

Problem BEFORE the kidney — insufficient blood flow

  • Dehydration / severe bleeding
  • Heart failure (↓ cardiac output)
  • Septic shock, burns
  • NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors (reduce renal perfusion)

Intrarenal

Problem WITHIN the kidney tissue

  • Nephrotoxic drugs: aminoglycosides, contrast dye, NSAIDs
  • Acute tubular necrosis (ATN — most common intrarenal AKI)
  • Glomerulonephritis
  • Ischemia from prolonged prerenal state

Postrenal

Obstruction AFTER the kidney

  • Kidney stones (bilateral or solitary kidney)
  • BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia)
  • Bladder/pelvic tumors
  • Strictures, blocked catheter
NCLEX Tip: Prerenal → give fluids. Postrenal → relieve obstruction. Intrarenal → remove nephrotoxin + supportive care. Treating the cause early can prevent permanent kidney damage.

2. Phases of AKI

Phase 1
Initiation
Onset of injury; may be asymptomatic; labs begin to rise
Phase 2
Oliguric
UO <400 mL/day; ↑ BUN, ↑ Cr, ↑ K+; fluid overload; most dangerous phase
Phase 3
Diuretic
UO↑ (3–5 L/day) but kidneys not filtering well → risk dehydration + hypokalemia
Phase 4
Recovery
BUN/Cr normalize over weeks–months; may recover fully
⚠️ Diuretic Phase Watch: Large urine output but kidneys still can't concentrate or filter effectively → monitor for dehydration + hypokalemia + hyponatremia during this phase.

3. Key Laboratory Values

LabAKI FindingWhy
Creatinine↑ (BEST indicator)Muscle waste product — only cleared by kidneys; most sensitive to kidney function changes
BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen)↑Protein breakdown waste; rises with kidney failure (also elevated in dehydration — less specific)
Potassium (K+)↑ (Hyperkalemia)Kidneys can't excrete K+ → cardiac arrhythmia risk
Urine Output↓ <0.5 mL/kg/hr or <400 mL/dayKidneys not filtering / blood not reaching kidneys
GFR↓Reduced filtration rate
pH↓ (Metabolic acidosis)Kidneys can't excrete H+ ions

4. Signs & Symptoms

  • Oliguria or anuria — classic first sign
  • Edema — fluid overload (peripheral and pulmonary)
  • Fatigue, weakness, confusion (waste buildup)
  • Hyperkalemia → muscle cramps, irregular heartbeat (peaked T-waves on ECG)
  • Hypertension (fluid overload + renin release)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Metallic taste and uremic breath (ammonia smell) — uremia

5. Nursing Management

Monitoring

  • Strict I&O — hourly urine output (foley catheter)
  • Daily weights — best indicator of fluid status (1 kg = ~1 L fluid)
  • Monitor BMP every shift or as ordered (K+, BUN, Cr)
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring for K+-related arrhythmias

Fluid Management

  • Fluid challenge for prerenal (bolus → monitor response)
  • Fluid restriction in oliguric phase (prevent overload)

Diet

  • Low protein (↓ BUN buildup)
  • Low potassium (prevent hyperkalemia → arrhythmias)
  • Low sodium (↓ fluid retention)
  • Low phosphorus; high carbohydrates (energy source to prevent muscle breakdown)

Avoid Nephrotoxins

  • NSAIDs (reduce renal perfusion)
  • IV contrast dye (pre-hydrate if contrast needed)
  • Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin) — monitor trough levels

6. Medications

DrugPurpose
Furosemide (Lasix)Promote diuresis; reduce fluid overload (must have enough blood flow to work)
Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate)Binds K+ in GI tract → removes via stool (slow-acting)
IV Insulin + DextroseDrives K+ into cells → rapid temporary ↓ in serum K+
IV Calcium GluconateStabilizes cardiac membrane in severe hyperkalemia (does NOT lower K+)
Sodium BicarbonateTreats metabolic acidosis; also temporarily shifts K+ into cells
DialysisIf severe AKI with refractory hyperkalemia, fluid overload, or uremia

7. NCLEX Must-Knows

  • ✅ Creatinine = BEST indicator of kidney function
  • ✅ Oliguria = urine output <400 mL/day (or <0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • ✅ Hyperkalemia = cardiac emergency → monitor ECG (peaked T-waves)
  • ✅ Daily weight = best assessment for fluid status
  • ✅ NO contrast dye or NSAIDs in AKI — nephrotoxic
  • ✅ Diuretic phase → high UO → watch for dehydration + hypokalemia
  • ✅ K+ management: Calcium gluconate (stabilize heart first), insulin+glucose (shift K+ in), Kayexalate (remove K+)
🔑 Key Takeaway: AKI = sudden kidney failure over hours to days. Causes: Prerenal (no blood flow), Intrarenal (nephrotoxins/ATN), Postrenal (obstruction). Oliguric phase is most dangerous — ↑K+ → cardiac risk. Creatinine = best indicator. Monitor I&O + daily weights. Avoid nephrotoxins. Dialysis if severe.
