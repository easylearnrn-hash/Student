Nephrotic Syndrome

Massive protein loss through the kidneys — hallmark: severe edema and frothy urine

Proteinuria >3.5g/day Frothy Urine + Periorbital Edema High Protein + Low Sodium Diet Prednisone First-Line

1. Classic Tetrad of Nephrotic Syndrome

FeatureDescriptionWhy It Happens
Massive Proteinuria>3.5 g/day in adults; frothy/foamy urineDamaged glomerular filtration membrane leaks protein
Hypoalbuminemia↓ Serum albumin <3 g/dLProtein lost faster than liver can synthesize it
Generalized EdemaPeriorbital (worse in AM), dependent edema, ascites↓ Oncotic pressure → fluid shifts to tissues
Hyperlipidemia / Lipiduria↑ Cholesterol, triglycerides; "oval fat bodies" in urineLiver overproduces lipoproteins to compensate for lost protein
Children (ages 2–7): Most common cause = Minimal Change Disease (MCD) — responds well to steroids; excellent prognosis

2. Diagnostics

  • Urinalysis: heavy proteinuria, lipiduria, oval fat bodies (Maltese cross under polarized light)
  • 24-hr urine protein: >3.5 g/day confirms nephrotic range
  • Serum albumin: <3 g/dL (hypoalbuminemia)
  • Lipid panel: ↑ total cholesterol, ↑ LDL, ↑ triglycerides
  • BMP: assess creatinine, BUN, electrolytes
  • Renal biopsy: definitive diagnosis (identifies underlying cause)

3. Treatment

TreatmentPurpose
Corticosteroids (prednisone)First-line; reduces glomerular inflammation; induces remission in MCD
Diuretics (furosemide)Reduce edema and fluid overload
IV Albumin + FurosemideRestore oncotic pressure → pull fluid back into vessels → then diurese
ACE inhibitors / ARBsReduce proteinuria; protect kidneys long-term
StatinsTreat associated hyperlipidemia

4. Diet & Fluid Management

  • ✅ High-protein diet — to replace lost protein (unless significant CKD)
  • ✅ Low-sodium diet — reduces edema
  • ✅ Fluid restriction — if severe edema or hyponatremia
  • ✅ Low-fat diet — to address hyperlipidemia

5. Dialysis Overview

When kidneys fail (ESRD), dialysis replaces renal filtration function. Two types:

TypeHemodialysis (HD)Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)
AccessAV fistula, AV graft, or temporary central venous catheterPeritoneal catheter (through abdomen)
WhereDialysis center or hospital; 3x/week × 3–4 hoursHome; daily exchanges (CAPD) or nightly (CCPD)
Key NursingNo BP, IV, or blood draws on fistula arm; check bruit + thrill; hold antihypertensives before HDCloudy effluent = peritonitis; strict aseptic technique
AV Fistula Care: NO blood pressure, NO IV access, NO blood draws on fistula arm — compression can clot off the access
Peritoneal Dialysis — Peritonitis Signs: Cloudy dialysis outflow, abdominal pain, fever, rebound tenderness

6. Electrolyte Complications

ElectrolyteSignECG / Physical Finding
Hyperkalemia (↑ K+)Muscle weakness, cardiac arrhythmiasPeaked T-waves → widened QRS → sine wave → VF/asystole
Hypocalcemia (↓ Ca2+)Muscle cramps, tetany, seizuresChvostek's sign (tap facial nerve → facial twitch); Trousseau's sign (inflate BP cuff → carpal spasm)

7. NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • ✅ Monitor daily weights — 2 lb = 1 L fluid
  • ✅ Strict I&O monitoring
  • ✅ Low-sodium, high-protein, low-fat diet
  • ✅ Check for periorbital edema — especially in the morning
  • ✅ Monitor lab values: albumin, creatinine, BUN, electrolytes, lipids
  • ✅ Infection risk ↑ (immunoglobulins also lost in urine) — implement standard precautions
  • ✅ Skin care — edematous tissue is at risk for breakdown
  • ✅ Administer IV albumin before furosemide (not after)
🔑 Key Takeaway: Massive proteinuria (>3.5g/day) → hypoalbuminemia → edema + frothy urine + hyperlipidemia. Prednisone first-line. High-protein low-Na diet. AV fistula arm = NO BP/IV/blood draws. Cloudy PD effluent = peritonitis. Peaked T-waves = hyperkalemia.
