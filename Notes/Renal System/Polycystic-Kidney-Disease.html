Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)

Genetic disorder causing fluid-filled cysts to replace normal kidney tissue → progressive kidney failure

Cerebral Aneurysm Risk No Cure — Manage BP ADPKD vs ARPKD Avoid NSAIDs

1. Types of PKD

ADPKD (Autosomal Dominant)

  • Most common form — 90% of PKD cases
  • One affected parent passes to 50% of offspring
  • Symptoms develop in adulthood (ages 30–40)
  • Gene mutations: PKD1 (chromosome 16) or PKD2
  • Associated with: cerebral (berry) aneurysms, hepatic cysts, mitral valve prolapse, diverticulosis

ARPKD (Autosomal Recessive)

  • Rare — both parents must carry gene
  • Symptoms present in infancy or childhood
  • Associated with hepatic fibrosis
  • More severe, often fatal in newborns with severe disease
  • May present prenatally as oligohydramnios + enlarged kidneys

2. Clinical Presentation

  • 🔴 Flank pain / back pain — enlarged cystic kidneys pushing on surrounding structures
  • 🔴 Hematuria — cysts rupture into collecting system
  • 🔴 Hypertension — renin secretion from compressed renal vasculature
  • Palpably enlarged kidneys (bilateral)
  • Nocturia — reduced concentrating ability
  • Recurrent UTIs (stagnant urine in cysts)
  • Renal calculi
  • Progressive renal insufficiency → ESRD
  • 🫀 Headaches — from associated cerebral (berry) aneurysms
  • 🫀 Extra-renal cysts — liver, pancreas, or spleen cysts (extra-renal manifestations)
⚠️ CEREBRAL ANEURYSM (ADPKD): Sudden, severe "thunderclap headache" = ruptured cerebral aneurysm until proven otherwise — this is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY

3. Mnemonic — "PKD KIDNEY"

PKD KIDNEY

  • P — Pain in flanks/back
  • K — Kidneys enlarged bilaterally
  • D — Dominant (AD) or Recessive (AR) inheritance
  • K — Killer complication: cerebral aneurysm
  • I — Increased blood pressure (hypertension)
  • D — Decreased GFR → ESRD
  • N — No cure; ACE/ARBs manage BP
  • E — End-stage renal disease — need dialysis or transplant
  • Y — Years 30–40 for ADPKD symptom onset

4. Diagnostics

TestPurpose
Renal UltrasoundFirst-line screening — shows bilateral cysts
CT scan / MRIBetter characterization of cysts; staging
Genetic testingConfirms PKD1/PKD2 mutations; screens family members
BMPMonitor GFR, creatinine, BUN, electrolytes
UrinalysisHematuria, proteinuria, possible infection
MRA / CT angio (brain)Screen for cerebral aneurysm in ADPKD if family history

5. Management

No cure — management is focused on slowing progression and controlling complications
InterventionPurpose
ACE inhibitors / ARBsFirst-line for hypertension; also reduce proteinuria and slow cyst progression
Tolvaptan (Jynarque)V2 receptor antagonist — slows cyst growth; FDA-approved for ADPKD (monitor LFTs)
Adequate hydration (2–3 L/day)Dilutes urine, reduces cyst formation pressure, prevents calculi
AntibioticsTreat cyst infections and UTIs (cysts can harbor bacteria)
Dialysis / Kidney transplantEnd-stage management when GFR <15
  • 🚫 Avoid NSAIDs — nephrotoxic; worsen kidney function
  • ✅ Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — preferred analgesic; use cautiously (avoid large doses)
  • 🚫 Avoid contact sports or activities with abdominal trauma risk — cyst rupture
  • 🚫 Avoid caffeine (may stimulate cyst growth)

6. NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • ✅ Monitor blood pressure — hypertension is the leading modifiable complication
  • ✅ Encourage 2–3 L/day fluid intake (unless ESRD)
  • ✅ Teach to report sudden severe headache IMMEDIATELY — possible aneurysm
  • ✅ Monitor BUN, creatinine, GFR regularly
  • ✅ Avoid nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast dye)
  • ✅ Genetic counseling — 50% inheritance risk with ADPKD
  • ✅ Emotional support — chronic, progressive, genetic disease affects whole family
  • ✅ Low-protein, low-sodium diet as renal function declines
🔑 Key Takeaway: Genetic kidney disease — cysts replace normal tissue. ADPKD = ages 30–40, 1 parent. ARPKD = infancy, 2 parents. Flank pain + hematuria + HTN + enlarged kidneys. Cerebral aneurysm = most dangerous complication — severe headache = emergency. No cure; ACE/ARBs for BP. Avoid NSAIDs.
