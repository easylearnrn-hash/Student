Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs)

Most common cause: E. coli — ascending infection from urethra to bladder (and beyond)

E. coli Most Common Phenazopyridine = Orange Urine Always Treat Pregnant CAUTI Prevention

1. Causative Organisms

  • E. coli — #1 most common (gram-negative bacteria from bowel flora)
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae — common in hospital-acquired UTIs
  • Proteus mirabilis — associated with struvite/urease-producing stones
  • Staphylococcus saprophyticus — common in young sexually active women

2. Lower UTI vs Upper UTI

Lower UTI (Cystitis)

  • Dysuria (painful urination)
  • Urinary frequency & urgency
  • Suprapubic pain / pressure
  • Hematuria (pink/red urine)
  • Low-grade or NO fever
  • Cloudy, foul-smelling urine

Upper UTI (Pyelonephritis)

  • Flank pain / back pain
  • High fever + chills
  • CVA tenderness (costovertebral angle)
  • Nausea & vomiting
  • All lower UTI symptoms PLUS the above

3. Special Populations

PopulationKey Consideration
Elderly patientsMay present as confusion ONLY — no classic UTI symptoms; always check UA in confused elders
Pregnant womenAlways treat — even asymptomatic bacteriuria can lead to pyelonephritis + preterm labor
ChildrenDysuria, bedwetting, abdominal pain; girls more common; teach proper hygiene
MenUTIs are uncommon → investigate for structural cause (BPH, kidney stones)

4. Diagnostics

  • Urinalysis (UA): positive nitrites, leukocyte esterase, WBCs, bacteria, possible RBCs
  • Urine culture & sensitivity: required for pyelonephritis — culture BEFORE antibiotics
  • CBC: elevated WBC with upper UTI/pyelonephritis

5. Treatment

TypeFirst-line AntibioticNotes
Simple cystitis (women)Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid) or TMP-SMX (Bactrim) or Fosfomycin3–7 day course; avoid nitrofurantoin if GFR <45; Fosfomycin = single-dose option
Complicated / male UTICiprofloxacin or cephalosporins7–14 day course
Pyelonephritis (outpatient)Ciprofloxacin 7–14 daysCulture & sensitivity first
Pyelonephritis (inpatient)IV ceftriaxone → transition to oralRequires hospital admission
PregnantCephalexin, Amoxicillin, Nitrofurantoin (avoid 3rd trimester)Culture to confirm clearance
Phenazopyridine (Pyridium): Urinary analgesic — relieves burning/urgency. NOT an antibiotic. Causes orange/red urine — warn patients! Do NOT use >2 days.

6. NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • ✅ Increase fluid intake — 2–3 L/day to flush bacteria
  • ✅ Void frequently — do NOT hold urine
  • ✅ Wipe front-to-back (women) — prevent fecal contamination
  • ✅ Void before AND after intercourse
  • ✅ Complete FULL course of antibiotics
  • ✅ Cranberry juice may help (acidifies urine, prevents E. coli adherence)
  • ✅ Avoid caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods during treatment
  • ✅ Assess confused elderly for UTI — often only presenting symptom

7. CAUTI (Catheter-Associated UTI)

Prevention is Priority #1: Remove indwelling catheters as soon as possible
  • Maintain a closed drainage system — do NOT open
  • Keep drainage bag below bladder level at all times
  • Perineal care with catheter — cleanse daily toward perineum
  • Do NOT irrigate unless ordered
  • Document output every shift

8. Complications

ComplicationDetails
PyelonephritisAscending infection; flank pain + fever + CVA tenderness
UrosepsisLife-threatening systemic infection from UTI → septic shock
Chronic UTI / recurrenceStructural anomalies; prophylactic antibiotics may be needed
Preterm laborUntreated UTI in pregnancy can cause preterm labor / low birth weight
🔑 Key Takeaway: E. coli is #1 cause. Elderly → confusion only. Pregnant → always treat. Phenazopyridine = orange urine (not an antibiotic). Wipe front-to-back. CAUTI prevention = remove catheter ASAP + keep bag below bladder.
