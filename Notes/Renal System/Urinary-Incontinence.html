Urinary Incontinence

Involuntary loss of urine — results from muscular, neurologic, or structural dysfunction

5 Types to Know Kegels for Stress Oxybutynin for Urge Reflex = No Warning

1. Types of Urinary Incontinence

Stress Incontinence

Leakage during increased intra-abdominal pressure: coughing, sneezing, laughing, exercise

Cause: Weak pelvic floor muscles (childbirth, aging, menopause)

Tx: Kegel exercises, pessary, avoid caffeine/alcohol

Urge Incontinence (OAB)

Sudden intense urge followed by involuntary loss — "can't make it in time"

Cause: Detrusor overactivity — stroke, MS, Parkinson's

Tx: Bladder training, oxybutynin (anticholinergic), avoid irritants

Overflow Incontinence

Bladder doesn't fully empty → constant dribbling + feeling of incomplete voiding

Cause: BPH (enlarged prostate), spinal cord injury, weak bladder muscle

Tx: Intermittent catheterization, alpha blockers (for BPH)

Functional Incontinence

Normal bladder function but can't reach toilet in time

Cause: Mobility issues, dementia, vision problems

Tx: Scheduled toileting, bedside commode, safe environment

Reflex Incontinence

Involuntary urination with no warning or urge

Cause: Neurologic dysfunction — spinal cord injury above T12

Tx: Intermittent catheterization; monitor for autonomic dysreflexia

Mixed Incontinence

Combination of two types — usually stress + urge

Tx: Address both causes — pelvic floor therapy + bladder training

3. Nursing Assessment

  • Voiding diary — track frequency, volume, triggers, leaks
  • Bladder scan — post-void residual (PVR) to assess retention
  • Urinalysis — rule out UTI as a cause
  • Assess mobility, cognition, access to toileting facilities
  • Review medications — diuretics, anticholinergics, alpha-blockers all affect bladder

5. NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • ✅ Identify the type of incontinence before choosing interventions
  • ✅ Encourage fluid intake 2–3 L/day (restricting fluids worsens incontinence and infection risk)
  • ✅ Void after intercourse — reduces UTI risk
  • ✅ Avoid bladder irritants: caffeine, alcohol, carbonated drinks, spicy foods
  • ✅ Wear cotton underwear; avoid tight clothing
  • ✅ Functional incontinence → safety: lighting, grab bars, commode
  • ✅ Reflex incontinence + SCI → watch for autonomic dysreflexia (sudden severe HTN, pounding headache)
  • ✅ Preserve dignity — incontinence is distressing; use empathetic communication
Autonomic Dysreflexia (Reflex Incontinence): Full bladder in SCI patient → triggers life-threatening hypertension + pounding headache → sit patient upright + empty bladder IMMEDIATELY + notify provider
🔑 Key Takeaway: Stress = sneezing/laughing → Kegels. Urge = sudden urge → oxybutynin + bladder training. Overflow = dribbling → intermittent cath. Functional = can't get to toilet → commode + schedule. Reflex = neurologic, no warning → cath + watch for autonomic dysreflexia.
