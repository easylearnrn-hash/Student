Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)

NCLEX-RN Overview — the key measurement of how effectively the kidneys filter blood each minute

Lower GFR = Less Kidney Filtration Trend Matters More Than One Value eGFR Most Common in Clinical Practice Low GFR → Medication Toxicity Risk

1. Definition

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) — the amount of blood filtered by the kidneys each minute — is one of the most important measurements of kidney function. It shows how well the kidneys are removing waste products and extra fluid from the blood.

The kidneys contain tiny filtering units called nephrons — microscopic kidney structures that filter blood and form urine. Each nephron has a glomerulus — a cluster of tiny blood vessels where filtration begins. GFR measures how much blood is filtered through these glomeruli.

Key concept: A higher GFR = better filtration. A lower GFR = kidneys filtering less blood than expected.

2. Purpose of GFR

GFR helps the nurse and health care team understand how well the kidneys are working. It is commonly used to:

  • Identify decreased kidney function.
  • Monitor changes in kidney function over time.
  • Help evaluate the severity of kidney impairment.
  • Guide medication safety — some medications are cleared through the kidneys.
  • Help determine whether abnormal lab findings may relate to decreased filtration.
Important: GFR is not used alone. It is interpreted with the client's age, history, hydration status, urine output, serum creatinine, urine findings, and overall clinical condition.

3. Estimated vs. Measured GFR

Estimated GFR (eGFR)

Estimated GFR (eGFR) — a calculated estimate of kidney filtration based on blood test results and client factors — is the most common form reported in clinical practice.

eGFR is usually calculated from serum creatinine, age, and sex. Some calculations also use cystatin C — a small protein made by cells in the body and filtered by the kidneys.

eGFR is an estimate, not a direct measurement. Muscle mass, nutrition, severe illness, pregnancy, and certain medications can affect the result.

Measured GFR (mGFR)

Measured GFR (mGFR) — a direct measurement of kidney filtration using special testing — is more accurate but not used in routine nursing care. It may be ordered when a very precise measurement is needed.

NCLEX focus: Know eGFR — this is the value most often seen in routine laboratory results.

4. Normal and Abnormal GFR Values

GFR is reported in mL/min/1.73 m² — milliliters per minute per 1.73 square meters (adjusted for average body surface area).

eGFR (mL/min/1.73 m²) Interpretation
≥ 90Normal or near-normal kidney filtration
60 – 89Mildly decreased — interpret with other findings
30 – 59Moderately decreased kidney filtration
15 – 29Severely decreased kidney filtration
< 15Very severe loss — may indicate kidney failure
Never diagnose from a single eGFR. One abnormal value should be reported, trended, and interpreted with the client's full clinical picture.

5. Pathophysiology

  1. Blood pressure inside the glomerulus pushes water and small particles from the blood into the nephron.
  2. The glomerulus keeps blood cells and large proteins in the bloodstream.
  3. The filtered fluid eventually becomes urine.
  4. When GFR decreases, waste products, excess fluid, and electrolyte imbalances may develop.
NCLEX key idea: Decreased GFR = decreased kidney filtration.

6. What Affects GFR?

GFR may decrease when kidney blood flow is reduced, when the glomeruli are damaged, or when urine flow is blocked after filtration.

FactorMechanism
DehydrationReduces blood flow to the kidneys
HypotensionReduces kidney perfusion
Heart failureReduces blood flow to the kidneys
Nephrotoxic medicationsMay directly reduce kidney function
Urinary obstructionIncreases back-pressure in the urinary system
AgingGradual natural decline in GFR
Diabetes / HypertensionLong-term risk factors for decreased filtration

7. Clinical Manifestations of Low GFR

A mild GFR decrease may produce no symptoms. As GFR becomes significantly low:

  • Decreased urine output
  • Fatigue
  • Nausea, poor appetite
  • Swelling in legs, ankles, or around the eyes (fluid retention)
  • Shortness of breath if excess fluid affects breathing
  • Confusion or decreased alertness if waste products build up
  • Abnormal electrolyte levels — especially potassium
NCLEX: A low GFR becomes most concerning when paired with decreased urine output, fluid overload, or dangerous electrolyte changes.

8. Assessment Findings

The nurse should assess overall kidney function, not only the GFR number. Key assessment data:

  • Urine output and intake & output (I&O)
  • Daily weight
  • Blood pressure and heart rate
  • Lung sounds
  • Presence of edema
  • Level of consciousness
  • Hydration status
  • Medication history
  • Recent contrast dye exposure
  • Recent illness, vomiting, diarrhea, or poor fluid intake
Priority: A decreasing GFR with decreasing urine output may indicate worsening kidney filtration — requires prompt attention.

9. Diagnostic Tests Related to GFR

Serum Creatinine

Serum creatinine — blood level of creatinine, a waste product from muscle metabolism — is commonly used to calculate eGFR. When creatinine rises, GFR usually decreases.

Note: creatinine may be affected by muscle mass, age, nutrition, and hydration status. A frail older adult may have a creatinine that appears "normal" even when filtration is reduced.

Cystatin C

A protein made by body cells and filtered by the kidneys. Helpful when creatinine alone may not give the most accurate picture.

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

BUN — a blood test measuring urea nitrogen, a waste product from protein breakdown — may rise when kidney function decreases. However, BUN can also increase with dehydration, high-protein intake, GI bleeding, or certain medications. BUN supports the kidney-function picture but does not replace GFR.

Urinalysis

Examines urine for protein, blood, glucose, and infection indicators. May identify abnormal findings that suggest kidney stress or damage.

Urine Albumin-Creatinine Ratio (uACR)

uACR compares albumin protein to creatinine in urine to check for abnormal protein loss. Albumin in the urine can be a sign of kidney filtering problems. Used with eGFR to evaluate kidney health.

10. Nursing Priorities

  • Recognize worsening kidney filtration early.
  • Monitor urine output and fluid balance.
  • Identify signs of fluid overload.
  • Monitor for electrolyte abnormalities.
  • Review medications for kidney safety.
  • Prevent further kidney injury when possible.
  • Notify the provider about significant changes.
The nurse does not treat the GFR number alone. The nurse responds to the whole clinical picture.

11. Nursing Interventions

Monitor Intake and Output (I&O)

Measure all fluids taken in and removed. Report decreased urine output, especially if new or worsening.

Monitor Daily Weight

Weigh at the same time each day with the same scale. A sudden weight gain may indicate fluid buildup.

Assess for Fluid Overload

Assess for edema, crackles, shortness of breath, increased blood pressure, and rapid weight gain.

Review Medication Safety

Many medications are eliminated by the kidneys. When GFR is low, levels may build up and cause toxicity. Check whether prescribed medications require renal dosing.

Avoid Nephrotoxic Substances When Possible

Nephrotoxic substances — medications or chemicals that can harm the kidneys — can worsen kidney function. Examples include NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen), some antibiotics, and contrast dye.

Do not stop prescribed medications independently, but question or clarify orders when kidney safety is a concern.

Trend Laboratory Values

Compare current eGFR with previous values, serum creatinine, BUN, potassium, and urine output. A single value is less useful than the trend.

12. Medication Considerations

When GFR is decreased, some medications may need a lower dose, a longer interval between doses, or close monitoring. Nursing responsibilities:

  • Review kidney function before giving medications that may affect the kidneys.
  • Check whether the medication requires renal dosing.
  • Monitor for signs of medication toxicity.
  • Question orders that may be unsafe for the client's kidney function.
  • Teach the client to avoid over-the-counter NSAIDs unless approved by the provider.
NCLEX caution: Be especially alert when a client with low GFR is prescribed a nephrotoxic medication or one that is mainly cleared by the kidneys.

13. Safety Considerations

A decreased GFR increases the risk for fluid imbalance, electrolyte imbalance, and medication toxicity. Key safety concerns:

  • Hyperkalemia — high potassium — can cause dangerous heart rhythm changes.
  • Fluid overload — can affect breathing and circulation.
  • Drug toxicity — especially from medications cleared by the kidneys.
  • Worsening kidney function if dehydration, hypotension, or nephrotoxic exposure continues.
Airway and breathing always come first. A client with low GFR and shortness of breath, crackles, or oxygen desaturation requires immediate assessment and intervention.

16. When to Notify the Provider

  • Sudden decrease in eGFR
  • New or worsening oliguria (low urine output)
  • Anuria (absence of urine output)
  • Rising creatinine
  • Potassium elevation
  • Signs of fluid overload
  • Shortness of breath or crackles
  • Confusion or decreased level of consciousness
  • Medication orders that may be unsafe with reduced kidney function
  • Recent contrast dye exposure with worsening kidney function
Act promptly when low GFR is paired with symptoms or abnormal electrolytes.

17. Patient Education

Teach the client using simple language: "Your GFR helps show how well your kidneys are cleaning your blood."

  • Keep follow-up appointments for repeat kidney-function testing.
  • Ask the provider or pharmacist before taking over-the-counter medications.
  • Avoid NSAIDs unless approved by the provider.
  • Stay hydrated as instructed, unless fluid restriction has been prescribed.
  • Report decreased urination, swelling, sudden weight gain, shortness of breath, confusion, or severe weakness.
  • Control blood pressure and blood glucose if part of the client's health history.
  • Tell all health care providers about decreased kidney function before procedures or new medications.

18. NCLEX-RN Prioritization Points

  • GFR measures kidney filtration. eGFR is the most common reported form.
  • A lower GFR means lower kidney filtering ability.
  • Trend matters more than one isolated number.
  • Always assess the client, not only the laboratory value.
  • Low GFR increases the risk of medication toxicity.
  • Monitor urine output, daily weight, edema, lung sounds, and electrolytes.
  • Question nephrotoxic medications when kidney function is reduced.
  • Report low GFR with decreased urine output, fluid overload, hyperkalemia, or acute mental status changes.
  • Airway and breathing are the priority if fluid overload causes respiratory symptoms.

19. NCLEX-Style Testing Tips

Low GFR + priority question? Look for immediate danger: shortness of breath, crackles, low O₂ saturation, severe hyperkalemia, or very low urine output.
What does GFR mean? Choose the answer that describes kidney filtration.
Medications? Choose the option that protects the client from nephrotoxicity or medication buildup.
Teaching? Include follow-up testing, medication safety, avoid unapproved NSAIDs, and report decreased urine output or swelling.
Delegation? UAP may collect I&O or daily weight. The RN must interpret abnormal kidney findings and provide teaching.

Key Takeaway

Glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is a major indicator of kidney filtration. For NCLEX-RN, the nurse should understand what GFR means, recognize when a low or falling GFR is concerning, monitor fluid balance and urine output, protect the client from nephrotoxic medications, and notify the provider when decreased kidney filtration is associated with unsafe clinical findings.
