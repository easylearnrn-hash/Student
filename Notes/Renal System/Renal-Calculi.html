Renal Calculi (Kidney Stones)

Hard mineral and salt deposits that form inside the kidneys — can cause severe pain and obstruction

Calcium Oxalate = Most Common Non-Contrast CT = Most Accurate Dx Strain All Urine Hydration = Key Prevention

1. Types of Kidney Stones

TypeCauseNotes
Calcium OxalateMost common (~80%); high oxalate intake, low fluid intakeAvoid spinach, nuts, chocolate, tea
Calcium PhosphateAlkaline urine, hyperparathyroidismOften with calcium oxalate stones
Uric AcidHigh protein diet, gout, acidic urineAvoid organ meats, seafood; alkaline diet
Struvite (Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate)Chronic UTIs (urease-producing bacteria)Can form large "staghorn" calculi
CystineRare; genetic disorder (cystinuria)Requires high fluid intake + alkalinization

2. Risk Factors

  • Dehydration (↓ urine volume → ↑ concentration)
  • High protein, sodium, or oxalate diet
  • Family or personal history of stones
  • Gout (↑ uric acid)
  • Recurrent UTIs (struvite risk)
  • Sedentary lifestyle; obesity

3. Signs & Symptoms

  • Severe, colicky flank or abdominal pain — comes in waves (renal colic) as stone moves
  • Pain radiates from flank → groin → inner thigh
  • Hematuria — blood in urine (micro or gross)
  • Nausea and vomiting (from pain intensity)
  • Urgency, frequency, dysuria (if stone near bladder)
  • Restlessness — patient cannot find a comfortable position (distinguishes from appendicitis)
⚠️ RED FLAG: Fever + no urine output = stone causing obstruction with infection → medical emergency → urologist consult immediately.

4. Diagnosis

TestDetails
Non-contrast CT scanMost accurate — can detect all stone types, location, and size
KUB X-rayQuick, cheap — detects calcium stones; misses uric acid and cystine stones
Renal UltrasoundPreferred in pregnancy (no radiation); detects hydronephrosis
Urinalysis (UA)Blood (hematuria), crystals, elevated pH (guides stone type)
Stone analysisIf stone passed — determine type → guides dietary prevention

5. Treatment

Small Stones (<5 mm) — Likely Pass Spontaneously

  • Hydration — IV fluids + oral; high urine output flushes stone
  • Pain management — NSAIDs (ketorolac) first; opioids if severe
  • Tamsulosin (Flomax) — alpha-blocker; relaxes ureter smooth muscle → facilitates stone passage
  • Strain all urine — catch stone for analysis

Large Stones (>6 mm) — Need Intervention

  • ESWL (Extracorporeal Shock Wave Lithotripsy) — sound waves break stone externally; non-invasive
  • Ureteroscopy — scope through urethra/bladder to fragment or retrieve stone
  • Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy — direct puncture through back into kidney for large stones
  • Ureteral stent — maintains urine flow if ureter obstructed

6. Nursing Care

  • Administer analgesics as ordered — pain management priority
  • Encourage fluid intake 2–3 L/day (or IV fluids)
  • Monitor urine output — report oliguria or anuria
  • Strain all urine — collect any stone passed
  • Monitor for signs of infection: fever, chills, foul-smelling urine
  • Monitor for obstruction: ↓ UO + fever = emergency
  • Teach patient dietary modifications based on stone type

7. Diet by Stone Type

Stone TypeAvoidEncourage
Calcium OxalateSpinach, nuts, chocolate, tea, beets, wheat branHydration, lemon water (citrate inhibits stones), moderate calcium intake
Uric AcidOrgan meats (liver, kidney), red meat, shellfish, seafood, alcoholAlkaline diet (fruits/veggies), fluids, allopurinol if needed
StruviteRecurrent UTIs (treat aggressively)Antibiotics to prevent UTIs; fluids
CystineHigh-protein foodsVery high fluid intake (3–4 L/day), alkalinize urine
ALL typesExcess sodium, dehydration2–3 L/day fluid; low-sodium diet reduces urinary calcium

8. Post-Procedure Care (ESWL / Ureteroscopy)

  • Monitor for hematuria (expected post-ESWL — should resolve in 24–48 hrs)
  • Assess for bruising over flank (expected with ESWL)
  • Strain urine for stone fragments
  • Ensure adequate oral fluid intake
  • Report: fever + chills + cloudy urine = infection
  • Report: worsening pain, decreased urine output (obstruction from fragments)
  • If ureteral stent placed: patient may have frequency/urgency/hematuria — normal; stent removed at follow-up

9. NCLEX Must-Knows

  • ✅ Calcium oxalate = most common stone type
  • ✅ Non-contrast CT = most accurate diagnostic test
  • ✅ Ultrasound preferred in pregnancy
  • ✅ Strain all urine — collect stone for type analysis
  • ✅ Encourage 2–3 L/day fluids — most important prevention measure
  • ✅ Tamsulosin (Flomax) helps pass stones by relaxing ureter
  • ✅ Fever + no UO = obstructive uropathy → call provider stat
  • ✅ Hematuria post-ESWL = expected; persistent fever = not expected
🔑 Key Takeaway: Kidney stones cause severe colicky flank pain radiating to groin + hematuria + restlessness. Calcium oxalate = most common. Non-contrast CT = best test. Small stones → hydration + Flomax + strain urine. Large stones → ESWL or surgery. Diet prevention is type-specific. Fever + no urine = emergency.
