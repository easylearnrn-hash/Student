Wilms Tumor (Nephroblastoma)

Most common kidney cancer in children — ages 2–5 years old

DO NOT Palpate Abdomen Painless Abdominal Mass Ages 2–5 Years Nephrectomy + Chemo

1. Overview

  • Also called Nephroblastoma
  • Most common renal malignancy in children — peak age: 2–5 years
  • Usually unilateral; bilateral in 5–10% of cases
  • Associated with WAGR syndrome (Wilms tumor + Aniridia + Genitourinary anomalies + intellectual Retardation)
  • Genetic: WT1 tumor suppressor gene mutation
  • Prognosis: excellent — overall survival rate >90% with treatment

2. Classic Presentation

  • 🟡 Painless, palpable abdominal mass — smooth, firm, unilateral; often found by parent during bath
  • 🔴 Hematuria (blood in urine) — present in ~20% of cases
  • 🔴 Hypertension — from renin secretion by tumor
  • Abdominal pain / discomfort — if mass large
  • Low-grade fever, anorexia, weight loss, nausea
🚫 NEVER PALPATE THE ABDOMEN — pressure can rupture the tumor capsule, causing cancer to spread to the peritoneum and lymph nodes (metastasis)

3. Mnemonic — WILMS

WILMS

  • W — WAGR syndrome association
  • I — Increased BP (hypertension)
  • L — Lung is primary metastasis site
  • M — Mass (painless abdominal mass)
  • S — Surgery = nephrectomy (primary treatment)

4. Staging

StageDescription
Stage ITumor confined to kidney, completely resectable
Stage IIExtends beyond kidney but completely removed surgically
Stage IIIResidual tumor in abdomen after surgery; lymph nodes involved
Stage IVHematogenous metastasis (lungs, liver)
Stage VBilateral kidney involvement

5. Diagnostics

  • Abdominal ultrasound — first line; non-invasive
  • CT scan of abdomen & chest — staging; check for metastasis
  • Urinalysis — hematuria
  • CBC — anemia; thrombocytopenia possible
  • BMP — renal function (creatinine, BUN)
  • Chest X-ray / CT — check for lung metastasis

6. Treatment

ComponentDetails
Surgery (Nephrectomy)Primary treatment — remove the affected kidney; biopsy taken intraoperatively
ChemotherapyVincristine + Actinomycin D (Stages I–III); add Doxorubicin for Stage III–IV
RadiationStage III, IV, or clear cell sarcoma of kidney

7. Post-Op Nursing Care

  • ✅ Monitor urine output closely — only one kidney remaining; must maintain adequate function
  • ✅ Watch for signs of bleeding or hemorrhage (↓ BP, ↑ HR, hematuria)
  • ✅ Monitor blood pressure — hypertension may persist post-op
  • ✅ Pain management — analgesics, repositioning, comfort measures
  • ✅ Protect the remaining kidney — avoid nephrotoxic medications (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides)
  • ✅ Dietary: avoid high-sodium diet; maintain adequate hydration
  • ✅ No contact sports for life — protect the solitary kidney
  • ✅ Monitor for chemotherapy side effects: nausea, alopecia, immunosuppression

8. Parent Teaching

Tell all caregivers: Do NOT press on or massage the child's abdomen — this is a strict contraindication
  • Wear a "Protect My Kidney" or medical alert band post-surgery
  • Report any fever, hematuria, abdominal pain, or swelling immediately
  • Regular follow-up: CT scans, labs to monitor for recurrence
  • Avoid contact sports and abdominal trauma
🔑 Key Takeaway: Most common kidney cancer in children ages 2–5. Painless abdominal mass + hematuria + hypertension. NEVER palpate abdomen. Treatment: nephrectomy + chemo ± radiation. Post-op: monitor urine output + protect remaining kidney + no contact sports.
