Cystitis (Bladder Infection / UTI)

Inflammation of the bladder — most commonly caused by E. coli

E. coli #1 Cause UA → ↑WBCs + Nitrites Phenazopyridine → Orange Urine Complication: Pyelonephritis

1. Causes

Bacterial (Most Common)

  • E. coli — #1 cause (perineal flora migrating up urethra)
  • Staphylococcus saprophyticus — common in young women
  • Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI) — #1 hospital-acquired infection

Other Causes

  • Bladder irritants (caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods)
  • Interstitial cystitis (chronic, no bacterial cause)
  • Radiation cystitis
  • Chemical exposure (bubble bath, douches)

2. Signs & Symptoms

Classic Bladder Symptoms

  • Dysuria — painful/burning urination
  • Frequency — urge to urinate often
  • Urgency — sudden urge, hard to control
  • Suprapubic pain — pressure above pubic bone
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • Hematuria (blood in urine)

Signs Infection Spread → Kidney

  • Fever + chills (systemic = pyelonephritis)
  • Flank/back pain (costovertebral angle tenderness)
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Patient looks sick, not just uncomfortable
⚠️ No fever with uncomplicated cystitis. Fever = infection spread to kidneys (pyelonephritis) → escalate treatment immediately.

3. Diagnosis

TestFinding in Cystitis
Urinalysis (UA)↑ WBCs (pyuria), positive nitrites, positive leukocyte esterase, hematuria possible
Urine Culture & SensitivityIdentifies organism + guides antibiotic choice (gold standard for treatment)
CystoscopyUsed for chronic/recurrent or interstitial cystitis to visualize bladder wall
NCLEX clue: Positive nitrites in UA → gram-negative bacteria (E. coli). Leukocyte esterase + pyuria → active infection. Always obtain culture before starting antibiotics.

4. Treatment

Antibiotics

DrugNotes
TMP-SMX (Bactrim)First-line for uncomplicated UTI; check local resistance patterns
Nitrofurantoin (Macrobid)First-line; concentrated in urine; do NOT use with kidney impairment
Ciprofloxacin (Cipro)Fluoroquinolone — used for complicated UTIs or antibiotic resistance
FosfomycinSingle-dose option; good for uncomplicated cystitis

Symptom Relief

  • Phenazopyridine (AZO/Pyridium) — urinary analgesic (NOT antibiotic); turns urine orange/red — warn patient!; used only 1–2 days
  • Increased fluid intake: 2–3 L/day to flush bacteria
  • Avoid caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods (bladder irritants)

Prevention

  • Wipe front-to-back after toileting
  • Void after sexual intercourse
  • Adequate daily fluid intake
  • Avoid prolonged use of catheters
  • Cranberry products (may reduce adherence of bacteria)

5. Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI) Nursing Care

  • Remove catheter ASAP — #1 prevention strategy
  • Maintain sterile technique during insertion
  • Keep drainage bag below bladder level at all times (prevent reflux)
  • Keep catheter secured to prevent trauma/movement
  • Do not allow bag to touch the floor
  • Assess urine color, clarity, and odor each shift

6. Complications

Pyelonephritis

  • Infection ascends from bladder to kidneys
  • Signs: fever + flank pain + CVA tenderness
  • N/V, systemically ill
  • Requires IV antibiotics if severe

Urosepsis

  • Systemic infection from untreated/spreading UTI
  • Signs: hypotension, tachycardia, confusion
  • Medical emergency → IV antibiotics + fluid resuscitation

7. NCLEX Must-Knows

  • ✅ Encourage 2–3 L/day fluids to flush bacteria
  • ✅ Remove catheter ASAP — #1 way to prevent CAUTI
  • ✅ Phenazopyridine = pain relief ONLY — warn about orange/red urine
  • ✅ Obtain urine culture before starting antibiotics
  • ✅ Fever + flank pain = pyelonephritis — not simple cystitis
  • ✅ Catheter bag must always remain below bladder
  • ✅ Front-to-back wiping; void after sex = prevention education
