Glomerulonephritis

Inflammatory condition of the glomeruli — the filtering units of the kidney

Cola-Colored Urine Post-Strep: 1–3 Weeks After ↑ ASO Titer Low Na + Low Protein Diet

1. Acute vs Chronic

Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN)

  • Often follows a streptococcal infection (pharyngitis or impetigo)
  • Onset: 1–3 weeks after strep infection
  • Most common in children ages 5–10 years
  • Cause: immune complex deposition in glomeruli → inflammation → ↓ filtration
  • Generally self-limiting in children; may progress in adults

Chronic Glomerulonephritis (CGN)

  • Progressive destruction of glomeruli over years
  • Often no clear precipitating cause
  • Leads to ESRD (end-stage renal disease) and need for dialysis or transplant
  • Associated with: IgA nephropathy, lupus nephritis, diabetic nephropathy

2. Classic Signs & Symptoms

  • 🔴 Hematuria — cola/tea/rust-colored urine (blood and protein in urine)
  • 🔴 Oliguria — decreased urine output (<400 mL/day in adults)
  • 🔴 Periorbital edema — puffiness around eyes, especially in morning
  • 🔴 Hypertension — from sodium/water retention
  • Proteinuria — frothy or foamy urine
  • Flank pain / costovertebral tenderness
  • Malaise, fatigue, headache

3. Mnemonic — "HAD STREP"

HAD STREP

  • H — Hematuria (cola-colored urine)
  • A — ASO titer elevated (anti-streptolysin O)
  • D — Decreased urine output (oliguria)
  • S — Strep throat or skin infection precedes it
  • T — Tender flanks / costovertebral area
  • R — Raised blood pressure (HTN)
  • E — Edema (periorbital — around the eyes)
  • P — Proteinuria (foamy urine)

4. Diagnostics

TestExpected Finding
ASO titer (Anti-streptolysin O)↑ Elevated — confirms recent streptococcal infection
UrinalysisRBCs, WBCs, protein, RBC casts (hallmark), hematuria
BMP / CMP↑ BUN, ↑ Creatinine, ↓ GFR, possible electrolyte imbalances
Complement levels (C3, C4)↓ C3 in post-strep GN (consumed in immune complex reaction)
24-hr urine proteinElevated proteinuria
Renal biopsyGold standard for diagnosis type & severity

5. Treatment

InterventionPurpose
Antibiotics (penicillin for strep)Eradicate underlying strep infection; does NOT reverse GN but prevents spread
Antihypertensives (ACE inhibitors / ARBs)Control blood pressure; reduce proteinuria; slow progression
Diuretics (furosemide)Reduce edema and fluid overload
CorticosteroidsFor autoimmune-related or rapidly progressive GN
DialysisChronic GN → ESRD stage

6. Nursing Management

  • ✅ Low-sodium diet — reduces edema and hypertension
  • ✅ Low-protein diet — reduces workload on damaged glomeruli (if significant proteinuria)
  • ✅ Fluid restriction if oliguric — monitor I&O strictly
  • ✅ Daily weights — 2 lb (1 kg) gain = 1 L fluid retention
  • ✅ Monitor BUN, creatinine, electrolytes, BP
  • ✅ Bed rest during acute phase
  • ✅ Assess for signs of fluid overload: crackles, peripheral edema, hypertension
  • ✅ Monitor urine color changes — expect gradual improvement
🔑 Key Takeaway: Post-strep GN occurs 1–3 weeks after strep infection. Cola-colored urine + periorbital edema + HTN + oliguria + ↑ ASO titer. Low Na + low protein diet. Monitor I&O + daily weights + BP. Chronic GN → ESRD → dialysis or transplant.
