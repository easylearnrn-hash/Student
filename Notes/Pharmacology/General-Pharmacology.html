Pharmacokinetics vs. Pharmacodynamics

Pharmacokinetics — what the body does to the drug

Mnemonic: ADME

  • Absorption — drug enters bloodstream
  • Distribution — spreads to tissues/organs
  • Metabolism — liver breaks down drug (first-pass effect)
  • Excretion — kidneys eliminate drug

Pharmacodynamics — what the drug does to the body

  • Drugs bind to receptors → cause effect
  • Agonist = activates receptor
  • Antagonist = blocks receptor
  • Dose–response relationship
  • Therapeutic index = safety margin

Therapeutic vs. Toxic Levels

  • Therapeutic range — drug works without causing harm
  • Toxic level — too much drug = danger
  • Peak level — highest drug concentration (draw after infusion complete)
  • Trough level — lowest concentration (draw just BEFORE next dose)
🧠
Example:

Digoxin therapeutic = 0.5–2 ng/mL. Above 2 = toxicity (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, confusion, weakness, fatigue, dizziness, blurred vision, yellow/green vision, halos, spots, or blind spots, slow, fast, or irregular pulse).

6 Rights of Medication Administration

  1. Right Patient — 2 identifiers
  2. Right Drug — check name carefully (tall man lettering)
  3. Right Dose — verify calculation
  4. Right Route
  5. Right Time
  6. Right Documentation

Also check: allergies, expiration date, reason for the med

Key Drug Interactions

  • Grapefruit juice — blocks CYP3A4 → drug accumulates (statins, CCBs, some immunosuppressants)
  • Warfarin — interacts with many drugs, herbs (garlic, ginkgo), vitamin K foods
  • NSAIDs — GI bleeding, kidney impairment, counteract antihypertensives
  • Anticholinergics — dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, confusion (elderly)
  • MAOIs + tyramine foods — hypertensive crisis

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Always check renal (BUN/Cr) and liver (LFTs) before high-risk drugs
  • Peak = after infusion; Trough = just before next dose
  • IV = fastest onset and highest risk
  • 6 Rights of medication administration — always apply
  • Know drug class by suffix — saves time on NCLEX
🧠
NCLEX KEY:

If a patient has kidney failure → drugs eliminated by kidneys will accumulate → risk of toxicity. If liver failure → drugs metabolized by liver will accumulate.
