ACE Inhibitors

Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme Inhibitors — ending in -pril

🧠 Mnemonic: "A PRIL puts the pressure down"

Common ACE Inhibitors

  • Captopril
  • Enalapril
  • Lisinopril (most common)
  • Benazepril
  • Ramipril
  • Quinapril

All end in -pril

Mechanism

  • Blocks conversion of Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II
  • ↓ vasoconstriction → ↓ blood pressure
  • ↓ aldosterone release → ↓ fluid/sodium retention
  • ↓ cardiac afterload and preload → used in heart failure

Side Effects — ABC-D Mnemonic

A — Angioedema

  • Sudden swelling of lips, tongue, throat
  • Life-threatening — STOP immediately
  • Treat: epinephrine, airway management

B — Blood pressure ↓ (hypotension)

  • Especially first dose
  • Hold if SBP < 100 mmHg

C — Cough (dry, persistent)

  • Most common reason to switch to ARB
  • Caused by accumulation of bradykinin

D — Don't use in Pregnancy

  • Category D (2nd/3rd trimester)
  • Teratogenic — fetal renal injury

Nursing Monitoring

  • Blood pressure — hold if SBP < 100 mmHg
  • Potassium (K⁺) — hyperkalemia risk (↓ aldosterone)
  • BUN / Creatinine — monitor renal function
  • Watch for dry cough — may need to switch to ARB
  • Angioedema can occur anytime, not just at start

Contraindications

  • Pregnancy (Category D)
  • Bilateral renal artery stenosis
  • Severe hyperkalemia
  • History of angioedema with ACE inhibitor

Indications

  • Hypertension
  • Heart failure (reduces afterload)
  • Post-MI cardioprotection
  • Diabetic nephropathy (kidney protection)
  • CKD with proteinuria

NCLEX Must-Know

  • All ACE inhibitors end in -pril
  • Dry cough → switch to ARB
  • Angioedema = stop immediately, life-threatening
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy (Category D)
  • Hold if SBP < 100 mmHg
  • Monitor K⁺ — hyperkalemia risk
  • Renal protection: ACE inhibitors slow progression of diabetic nephropathy and provide renal protection in CKD
