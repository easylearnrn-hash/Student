Beta Blockers

Antihypertensives that block beta-adrenergic receptors — ends in -olol

🔵 Mnemonic: "LOL the pressure away" — if it ends in -olol, it's a beta blocker

Indications

  • Hypertension
  • Angina pectoris
  • Heart failure (Metoprolol, Carvedilol, Bisoprolol)
  • Post-MI (reduce mortality)
  • Atrial fibrillation / SVT (rate control)
  • Migraine prophylaxis (Propranolol)
  • Hyperthyroidism / thyroid storm (Propranolol)
  • Performance anxiety, essential tremor (Propranolol)

Key Effects

  • ↓ Heart rate (negative chronotrope)
  • ↓ Myocardial contractility (negative inotrope)
  • ↓ Blood pressure
  • ↓ Cardiac output
  • β1-selective agents affect heart primarily; non-selective also affect lungs (β2)

Side Effects & Nursing Priorities

  • Bradycardia, hypotension
  • Fatigue, depression
  • Bronchospasm (non-selective → AVOID in asthma/COPD)
  • Masks hypoglycemia signs (tachycardia hidden) → important for diabetics
  • Cold extremities, dizziness
  • Sexual dysfunction
🧠
NCLEX KEY:

Hold beta blocker if HR <60 bpm or SBP <100 mmHg. NEVER stop abruptly → rebound hypertension or angina.

Contraindications

  • Acute decompensated heart failure
  • Severe bradycardia or AV block (2nd/3rd degree)
  • Asthma / COPD (non-selective agents)
  • Cardiogenic shock

β1 vs. β2 Receptors

ReceptorLocationEffect when blocked
β1Heart↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ BP
β2Lungs, peripheral vasculatureBronchospasm, vasoconstriction
🧠
Mnemonic:

β1 = 1 heart; β2 = 2 lungs. Cardioselective (-β1 only) = safer for COPD/asthma.

NCLEX Must-Know

  • All end in -olol
  • Hold if HR <60 or SBP <100
  • NEVER stop abruptly → taper gradually
  • Masks hypoglycemia tachycardia in diabetics
  • Non-selective (propranolol) → avoid in asthma/COPD
  • Labetalol = safe for hypertensive emergency in pregnancy
