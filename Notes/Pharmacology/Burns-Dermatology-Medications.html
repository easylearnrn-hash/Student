Skin Infection Medications

Bacterial Skin Infections

  • Mupirocin (Bactroban) — topical; impetigo, MRSA decolonization
  • Bacitracin — minor cuts and abrasions
  • Oral Amoxicillin/Dicloxacillin — mild cellulitis
  • IV Vancomycin — MRSA cellulitis

Inflammatory / Allergic Skin

  • Hydrocortisone (topical) — mild eczema/dermatitis
  • Triamcinolone — moderate-severe eczema
  • Diphenhydramine — allergic itch (sedating)
  • Calamine lotion — chicken pox, poison ivy

Fungal Skin Infections

Topical Antifungals

  • Clotrimazole (Lotrimin) — athlete's foot, ringworm, jock itch
  • Miconazole — similar spectrum
  • Terbinafine — onychomycosis (nail fungus); oral also available

Oral Antifungals

  • Fluconazole — widespread/systemic fungal infections
  • SE: hepatotoxicity — monitor LFTs
  • Drug interactions (CYP450)

Parasitic Skin Infections

Pediculosis (Lice)

  • Permethrin 1% (Nix) — apply to hair; leave 10 min; rinse; repeat in 7–10 days
  • Check for and remove nits (comb)
  • Wash all bedding/clothing in hot water

Scabies

  • Permethrin 5% (Elimite) — apply neck to toe; leave 8–14 hours; wash off
  • Treat all close contacts simultaneously
  • Wash all clothing/bedding
  • Itching may persist 2–4 weeks after successful treatment
🧠
NCLEX KEY:

Lice = Permethrin 1%; Scabies = Permethrin 5%. Scabies itching persisting after treatment does NOT mean treatment failed.

TBSA — Rule of Nines (Total Body Surface Area)

Used to estimate burn size for fluid resuscitation and severity classification.

Body AreaAdult % TBSAChild % TBSA (adjusted)

Anterior Trunk18%18%
Posterior Trunk18%18%

Lund and Browder Chart — more accurate than Rule of Nines for children; accounts for age-based proportional differences in head and leg TBSA.

🧠
NCLEX KEY

In children: head = 18% TBSA (vs 9% adult); legs = 14% each (vs 18% adult). Use Lund and Browder chart for pediatric burns for accuracy. Rule of Nines underestimates head in children.

Inhalation Injury — Airway Burns 🚨

Inhalation of smoke, steam, or toxic gases (CO, cyanide) — life-threatening, immediate priority.

Signs of Airway Burn / Inhalation Injury:

  • Soot around mouth or nose
  • Singed nasal hairs or eyebrows
  • Hoarseness (voice changes)
  • Stridor (high-pitched noise on inspiration — airway closing!)
  • Cherry-red skin or confusion → Carbon Monoxide (CO) poisoning
  • Facial burns + enclosed-space fire history
🚨
PRIORITY — INTUBATE EARLY

Intubate before airway swells shut. Edema progresses rapidly — early intubation = life-saving. 100% O₂ via non-rebreather mask for CO poisoning.

Burn Complications

Hypovolemic Shock (Emergent Phase)

  • Massive fluid loss from burn surface within first 24–48 hrs
  • Signs: ↓ BP, ↑ HR, pale/cool skin, ↓ urine output
  • Treat: Parkland formula with LR — 4 mL × kg × %TBSA; ½ in first 8 hr, ½ over next 16 hr

Compartment Syndrome

  • Circumferential burns cause tight eschar → swelling compresses vessels/nerves
  • Signs: 6 Ps — Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pressure
  • Treatment: Escharotomy — surgical incision through eschar to release pressure

Curling's Ulcer

  • Stress ulcer of the stomach/duodenum — occurs in major burn patients due to physiologic stress
  • Prevention: proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) or H₂ blockers (e.g., famotidine) prophylactically
  • Monitor for GI bleeding (hematemesis, melena, ↓ Hgb)

Sepsis

  • Burned skin = loss of protective barrier → bacteria entry
  • Signs: ↑ fever or hypothermia, ↑ HR, ↑ WBC, purulent wound, foul odor
  • Prevention: sterile wound care, silver sulfadiazine, early wound closure

Phases of Burn Care

Emergent Phase (0–48 hrs)

  • Priority: AIRWAY → FLUIDS → SHOCK prevention
  • IV opioids (morphine, fentanyl) for pain — NOT IM or PO (poor absorption with altered perfusion)
  • Cover with clean, dry sheet to reduce infection risk and heat loss

Acute Phase (48 hrs – weeks)

  • Wound healing, debridement, possible skin grafts
  • High-calorie, high-protein diet — burns cause hypermetabolic state (↑↑ caloric needs)
  • Prevent infection: sterile wound care, topical antibiotics

Rehabilitation Phase (months – years)

  • ROM exercises — prevent contractures (stiff, shortened muscles from scar tissue)
  • Compression garments — worn 23 hrs/day for 12–18 months to reduce hypertrophic scarring
  • Psychosocial support — PTSD, body image, grief; refer to support groups
🧠
NCLEX KEY — High-Calorie High-Protein Diet in Burns

Burns cause massive catabolism. High-calorie, high-protein intake is mandatory to promote wound healing, prevent muscle wasting, and support immune function. Tube feeding or TPN may be required.

Special Burn Types

⚡ Electrical Burns

  • External injury may appear small — internal damage is extensive (travels path of least resistance: blood vessels, nerves)
  • Monitor cardiac rhythm → risk for ventricular fibrillation
  • Check urine for myoglobin (dark/tea-colored urine) → rhabdomyolysis → acute kidney injury
  • IV fluids aggressively to flush kidneys; target urine output 75–100 mL/hr (vs normal 30+ mL/hr)
  • Mafenide (Sulfamylon) preferred — penetrates eschar better than silver sulfadiazine

🧪 Chemical Burns

  • Can continue burning until fully removed
  • If dry powder chemical: BRUSH OFF FIRST, then flush with water
  • Flush skin with large amounts of water for 20–30 minutes
  • Remove all contaminated clothing and jewelry
  • Do NOT attempt to neutralize chemicals (neutralization reactions produce heat)

Cellulitis — MRSA Treatment & Lymphangitis Warning

Oral options for MRSA-suspected cellulitis:

  • TMP-SMX (Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole, Bactrim) — first-line oral MRSA option for skin/soft tissue infections
  • Clindamycin — alternative for MRSA; check susceptibility (inducible resistance possible)
  • Doxycycline — alternative oral MRSA option
  • IV Vancomycin — for severe or hospitalized MRSA cellulitis
🚨
NCLEX RED FLAG — Lymphangitis

Red streaks spreading from the wound toward the heart = lymphangitis = EMERGENCY. Infection is tracking along lymphatic channels → systemic spread → sepsis. Notify provider immediately. Do NOT delay treatment.

DVT → PE WARNING SIGNS

Sudden chest pain + shortness of breath + hemoptysis + ↓ O₂ saturation → Pulmonary Embolism emergency. Call provider, apply O₂, prepare for imaging and anticoagulation. Do NOT massage a DVT limb — may dislodge clot.

🧠
VIRCHOW'S TRIAD (DVT Causes)

1. Venous Stasis (immobility, post-op, long flights) · 2. Hypercoagulability (cancer, OCP, clotting disorders) · 3. Endothelial Injury (trauma, surgery, catheter). All three increase DVT risk.

🚨 Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome — Life-Threatening Drug Reaction

Definition: Rare but life-threatening mucocutaneous hypersensitivity reaction, most often drug-induced.

Common triggers (drugs): Sulfa drugs (TMP-SMX), anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine), NSAIDs, allopurinol, antibiotics (penicillin)

Signs:

  • Painful red/purple rash that rapidly spreads
  • Skin sloughs off in sheets (like a burn)
  • Mucosal involvement: mouth, eyes, genitals — blistering and erosion
  • Fever, malaise before skin changes (prodrome)
🚨
NCLEX PRIORITY ACTION

STOP the offending drug IMMEDIATELY. Treat like a burn patient: IV fluids, wound care (sterile), pain control, nutrition, infection prevention. ICU/burn unit admission. Eye involvement → ophthalmology consult urgently.

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Burns: Lactated Ringer's (LR) = first-line fluid (Parkland formula)
  • Silver sulfadiazine: check sulfa allergy; not for face
  • Mafenide (Sulfamylon): painful; can cause metabolic acidosis
  • Pre-medicate before burn dressing changes (pain control)
  • Permethrin 1% = lice; Permethrin 5% = scabies
  • Topical corticosteroids: used for eczema/dermatitis; not for infections
  • Extravasation of vesicant agents (chemo, dopamine) causes burn-like tissue damage — stop infusion immediately; follow antidote protocol
