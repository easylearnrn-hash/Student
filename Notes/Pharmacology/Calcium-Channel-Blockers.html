Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

Block calcium from entering cardiac and smooth muscle cells — ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ BP, vasodilation

🧠 Mnemonic: "Very Nice Drugs" — Verapamil · Nifedipine · Diltiazem

Two Main Types

TypeActs OnKey DrugsPrimary Use
Dihydropyridines (DHPs)Mainly vessels (vasodilation)Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Nicardipine, FelodipineHTN, angina (peripheral)
Non-DihydropyridinesHeart + vessels (rate & BP)Verapamil, DiltiazemAtrial arrhythmias, angina, HTN
🧠
Key Distinction:

Non-DHPs (Verapamil, Diltiazem) slow the AV node → used for rate control in A-fib. DHPs primarily vasodilate → used for BP.

Indications

  • Hypertension
  • Angina (stable and vasospastic/Prinzmetal)
  • Atrial fibrillation / flutter (Verapamil, Diltiazem)
  • SVT (Verapamil)
  • Raynaud's phenomenon

Side Effects & Nursing Priorities

  • Bradycardia (especially non-DHPs — Verapamil, Diltiazem)
  • Hypotension, dizziness, flushing
  • Peripheral edema (especially Amlodipine, Nifedipine)
  • Constipation (especially Verapamil)
  • Headache (from vasodilation)
  • AV block or worsening heart failure (non-DHPs)

Avoid or Use Caution

  • Heart block or bradycardia (non-DHPs)
  • Heart failure with reduced EF (non-DHPs decrease contractility)
  • Grapefruit juice — inhibits CYP3A4 → ↑ CCB levels → toxicity

NCLEX Must-Know

  • DHP (-dipine) = mostly vessels (HTN, angina); Non-DHP (Verapamil/Diltiazem) = heart + vessels (rate control)
  • Monitor BP and HR before administering
  • Verapamil = constipation; Amlodipine/Nifedipine = peripheral edema
  • Avoid grapefruit juice (increases drug levels)
  • Non-DHPs: avoid in HF with reduced EF (worsen function)
