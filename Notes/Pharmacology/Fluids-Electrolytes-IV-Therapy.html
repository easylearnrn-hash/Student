IV Fluid Types

Isotonic

~308 mOsm/L

  • Normal saline (0.9% NS)
  • Lactated Ringer's (LR)
  • D5W (becomes isotonic in body)

Use: dehydration, blood loss, pre-op

SE: fluid overload

Hypotonic

<280 mOsm/L

  • 0.45% NS (half normal saline)
  • 0.33% NS

Use: hypernatremia, DKA maintenance

SE: Fluid moves INTO cells → cerebral edema!

Hypertonic

>308 mOsm/L

  • 3% NS
  • D10W, D50W

Use: cerebral edema, severe hyponatremia

SE: fluid overload, seizures if too fast

🧠
NCLEX KEY:

Hypotonic fluids can cause cerebral edema (fluid rushes INTO brain cells). Hypertonic fluids used for severe hyponatremia — infuse slowly.

IV Complications

Infiltration

  • Non-vesicant fluid into surrounding tissue
  • Signs: cool, pale, swollen site
  • Stop IV; apply warm compress; elevate

Extravasation

  • Vesicant (tissue-damaging) into surrounding tissue
  • Chemotherapy, dopamine
  • STOP infusion; antidote protocol; cold compress for most

Phlebitis

  • Vein inflammation
  • Signs: redness, warmth, hard cord-like vein
  • Remove IV; warm compress; rotate sites

Air Embolism

  • Air enters bloodstream
  • Position: left lateral Trendelenburg
  • High-flow O₂; notify provider

Fluid Overload

  • Signs: dyspnea, crackles, bounding pulse, JVD
  • Slow or stop infusion; diuretics; O₂

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Isotonic: stays in vascular space; Hypotonic: goes into cells; Hypertonic: pulls fluid from cells
  • K⁺ NEVER IV push — always dilute, always pump, always check urine output first
  • Hypokalemia + Hyperkalemia both cause cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Chvostek/Trousseau = hypocalcemia; MgSO₄ overdose antidote = Calcium gluconate
  • Air embolism: left lateral Trendelenburg + O₂
  • Phlebitis + Infiltration: warm compress; Extravasation: stop + antidote
