ARBs — Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers

ARBs

Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers — ending in -sartan

🧠 Mnemonic: "SARTANs spare the cough, but still lower pressure"

Common ARBs

  • Losartan (most common)
  • Valsartan
  • Irbesartan
  • Olmesartan
  • Candesartan
  • Telmisartan

All end in -sartan

Mechanism

  • Block Angiotensin II AT1 receptor (not the ACE enzyme)
  • ↓ vasoconstriction → ↓ blood pressure
  • ↓ aldosterone release → ↓ fluid retention
  • Does NOT increase bradykinin → NO dry cough

Side Effects

  • Dizziness / hypotension
  • Hyperkalemia (↓ aldosterone)
  • Angioedema (less common than ACE)
  • NO dry cough — key difference from ACE inhibitors

Contraindications:

  • Pregnancy (Category D)
  • Severe renal impairment
  • Never combine with ACE inhibitor simultaneously

🧠
NCLEX KEY:

If patient on ACE inhibitor develops dry cough → switch to ARB. Both contraindicated in pregnancy. Both can cause hyperkalemia.

Nursing Monitoring

  • Blood pressure — hold if SBP < 100 mmHg
  • Potassium (K⁺) — hyperkalemia risk
  • Renal function (BUN, creatinine)
  • Signs of angioedema — even rare, watch closely

NCLEX Must-Know

  • All ARBs end in -sartan
  • No cough (NO dry cough unlike ACE inhibitors) — key advantage of ARBs
  • Both ACE inhibitors and ARBs: contraindicated in pregnancy (Category D)
  • Never use ACE + ARB together (additive hypotension/hyperkalemia)
  • Monitor K⁺ (hyperkalemia) and BP with both drug classes
  • ARBs used for diabetic nephropathy — kidney protection in patients with diabetes + HTN
