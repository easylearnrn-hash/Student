Respiratory System

Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

Blood, fat & air emboli; Trendelenburg position; anticoagulation; PE vs COPD distinction

Overview
Pulmonary Embolism (PE) = blockage of the pulmonary arteries → impaired blood flow to lungs → decreased gas exchange → hypoxemia, right heart strain. Can be life-threatening if massive.
3 Types of Emboli

1. Blood Clot (Thromboembolism)

  • Most common type
  • Originates from DVT (deep vein thrombosis)
  • Breaks off → travels to lungs
  • Triggers: post-surgery immobility, prolonged bed rest, hypercoagulable states

2. Fat Embolism

  • Fat globules enter bloodstream
  • Triggers: long bone fractures, compartment syndrome, atherosclerosis
  • Petechial rash (chest, axillae) — classic sign
  • Altered mental status + hypoxia

3. Air Embolism

  • Air enters venous system
  • Nursing error: IV line air, central line insertion
  • Rare but potentially fatal
  • Immediate intervention required
Air Embolism — Emergency Position
⬇️ Trendelenburg Position
Head DOWN — Feet UP
Place patient on their LEFT SIDE (Trendelenburg + left lateral decubitus)
This traps air in the right atrium to prevent it from entering pulmonary circulation
NCLEX Critical: Air embolism during IV therapy → immediately position patient in left lateral Trendelenburg → stop infusion → notify physician → administer O₂.
Risk Factors for PE

Virchow's Triad (think DVT → PE)

  • Stasis — prolonged immobility, long flights, bed rest, post-surgical
  • Hypercoagulability — clotting disorders, pregnancy, OCP, cancer
  • Endothelial injury — IV catheter, surgery, trauma, infections

Additional risks: DVT history, fractures (especially hip/femur), obesity, age >60

Signs & Symptoms

Classic PE Symptoms

  • Sharp pleuritic chest pain
  • Sudden onset dyspnea / SOB
  • "Sense of impending doom"
  • Hemoptysis (bloody sputum)
  • Restlessness, anxiety
  • Crackles / rales on auscultation
  • ↓ O₂ saturation
  • Tachycardia, tachypnea

PE vs. COPD Distinction

  • PE: SHARP chest pain present
  • COPD: Pain is typically absent or dull
  • Both have: SOB, ↓O₂, tachypnea
  • PE: sudden onset; COPD: gradual worsening
  • PE: no barrel chest / pursed-lip breathing
Classic NCLEX Clue: "Sense of impending doom" + sharp chest pain + recent surgery or immobility = Pulmonary Embolism until proven otherwise.

Treatment

Standard PE Treatment

  1. O₂ therapy — maintain SpO₂ >94%
  2. IV fluids — support hemodynamics
  3. Anticoagulation — heparin IV (immediate) → bridge to warfarin or DOAC (long-term)
  4. Bedrest + positioning; monitor vitals
  5. Pain management (pleural chest pain)

Massive PE — Additional Interventions

  • Thrombolytics (tPA) — for hemodynamic instability (hypotension, shock)
  • Surgical embolectomy — last resort
  • IVC filter — for recurrent DVT/PE when anticoagulation is contraindicated

Nursing Priorities

Priority Nursing Actions — Suspected PE

  1. Administer high-flow O₂ immediately
  2. Notify physician (STAT)
  3. Position patient: HOB elevated 30–45° (unless air embolism)
  4. Establish IV access; prepare for anticoagulation
  5. Monitor vital signs continuously
  6. Prepare for emergent CT/V-Q scan if ordered

Prevention of DVT → PE

  • Early ambulation post-surgery
  • Sequential compression devices (SCDs/TEDs)
  • Low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) prophylaxis
  • Adequate hydration
  • Teach patients to avoid prolonged immobility (long flights: stand/walk hourly)
NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • PE hallmark = sharp chest pain + dyspnea + "sense of impending doom"
  • PE vs COPD: PE has sharp pain; COPD does not
  • Air embolism → Trendelenburg + left lateral position
  • FIRST action: administer O₂ → THEN notify physician
  • Heparin first → bridges to warfarin (overlap 5 days minimum)
  • Monitor INR for warfarin (therapeutic: 2.0–3.0)
  • D-dimer elevated: sensitive but not specific; negative D-dimer RULES OUT PE
  • Sudden onset shortness of breath + recent immobility or surgery = suspect PE
