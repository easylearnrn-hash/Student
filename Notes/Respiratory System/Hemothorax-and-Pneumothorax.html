Respiratory System

Hemothorax & Pneumothorax

4 types of pneumothorax, chest tube management, tension pneumothorax emergency, dislodgement protocols

Overview

Pneumothorax

  • Air in the pleural space
  • Air rises → chest tube placed HIGHER
  • Lung collapses under pressure

Hemothorax

  • Blood in the pleural space
  • Blood sinks → chest tube placed LOWER
  • Usually from trauma or surgery
Hemopneumothorax = both air AND blood in the pleural space (may require two chest tubes — one high, one low).
4 Types of Pneumothorax

1. Closed Pneumothorax

  • No external wound; air enters pleural space internally
  • Causes: blunt trauma, rib fractures, mechanical ventilation (barotrauma), bleb rupture
  • Signs: decreased/absent breath sounds, chest pain, dyspnea

2. Open ("Sucking Chest Wound") Pneumothorax

  • Penetrating trauma creates open wound; air moves in/out with breathing
  • You can hear air movement through the wound
  • Emergency treatment: Apply a 3-sided vented dressing (tape 3 sides, leave 1 side open to vent during exhalation)

3. Tension Pneumothorax LIFE-THREATENING

  • Air enters pleural space but cannot escape → one-way valve effect
  • Pressure builds → lung collapses → mediastinum shifts
  • Classic signs: tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side, hypotension, tachycardia, JVD, absent breath sounds
  • Treatment: Immediate needle decompression at 2nd ICS (intercostal space), midclavicular line → then chest tube
Tension Pneumothorax NCLEX: Tracheal deviation = late, ominous sign. Do NOT wait for X-ray. Needle decompression is FIRST priority.

4. Spontaneous Pneumothorax

  • No trauma; ruptured bleb (small air-filled blister on lung surface)
  • Risk profile: tall, thin young males; smokers; COPD patients
  • Onset: sudden sharp chest pain + dyspnea (often at rest)
Chest Tube — Bedside Necessities

Required at Bedside

  • Petroleum gauze — used to seal the wound site if tube dislodges
  • 4×4 gauze pads — for dressing and wound care
  • Transparent dressing — to secure insertion site
  • Chest tube clamp — for brief clamping (e.g., changing collection unit)
  • Three-way sterile gauze dressing — acts as a valve (allows air out, prevents air entry)
  • Sterile water — for partial dislodgement (submerge distal end 2 cm deep)
  • Spare chest tube(s) — same size + one smaller, in case initial tube fails or breaks
Subcutaneous Emphysema

"Rice Krispies" Sign

  • Air trapped under the skin = crepitus (crackly/bubbling feeling on palpation)
  • Commonly around chest wall, neck, face after pneumothorax
  • Action: Notify physician immediately — sign of air leak
Chest Tube — Wet (Water-Seal) System

Part A — Suction Chamber

  • Gentle continuous bubbling = ✅ normal
  • Vigorous bubbling = suction too high
  • No bubbling = suction too low or not connected
  • Adjust to −20 cmH₂O typically

Part B — Water Seal / Tidaling

  • Rises on inhalation, falls on exhalation (spontaneous breathing)
  • Opposite on ventilator
  • No tidaling = lung fully re-expanded ✅ OR tube kinked/clogged ❌

Part C — Air Leak Indicator

  • Intermittent bubbling = normal
  • Continuous bubbling = air leak ⚠️
  • Locate leak by pinching tube every 12 inches from patient toward the collection unit

Part D — Drainage Collection

  • <100 mL/hr = normal
  • >100 mL/hr (red) post-op = expected
  • >130 mL/hr = call physician
  • Dark blood = old blood ✅
  • Bright red = active bleeding ❌ notify STAT
  • Pink/serosanguineous = clearing ✅
Chest Tube — Dry System

Dry (Mechanical) System

  • No water bubbling; uses mechanical pressure-control valve
  • Red "bellow" visible = suction is active
  • Set to −20 to −40 cmH₂O
  • Easier to transport; less maintenance
Chest Tube Rules — NEVER / ALWAYS

NEVER

  • Milk/strip the tube — creates extreme negative pressure → lung injury
  • Clamp the tube (except briefly when changing collection unit)
  • Lift collection unit above chest level
  • Leave an air leak unaddressed

ALWAYS

  • Keep collection unit below chest at all times
  • Tape/secure all connections
  • Mark drainage level with date/time
  • Assess site and breath sounds frequently
Chest Tube Dislodgement

Complete Dislodgement

  1. Cover insertion site with your sterile gloved hand immediately
  2. Apply a sterile occlusive dressing (3-sided)
  3. Notify physician immediately

Partial Dislodgement

  1. Submerge the end of tube in sterile water (2 cm deep)
  2. This creates a water seal preventing air entry
  3. Notify physician immediately
Chest Tube Removal

Removal Protocol

  • Performed by physician
  • Nurse: monitor vitals, administer analgesics beforehand, prepare suture materials
  • Patient instruction: Take a deep breath and BEAR DOWN (Valsalva maneuver) during removal
  • Valsalva ↑ intrathoracic pressure → prevents air re-entry
  • Apply occlusive dressing immediately after removal

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Tracheal deviation AWAY from the affected side = tension pneumothorax → needle decompression STAT
  • Sucking chest wound → 3-sided dressing (NOT fully occlusive)
  • Chest tube for air: place HIGHER; for blood: place LOWER
  • Tidaling stops → lung re-expanded OR tube kinked (assess first)
  • Continuous bubbling in air-leak chamber = air leak
  • >130 mL/hr bright red drainage = call physician immediately
  • Crepitus ("Rice Krispies") = subcutaneous emphysema = notify physician
  • Bearing down (Valsalva) during chest tube removal
  • NEVER milk the tube
  • Collection unit always BELOW chest level
