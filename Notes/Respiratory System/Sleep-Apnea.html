Respiratory System

Sleep Apnea

NCLEX-focused — OSA vs CSA · STOP-BANG · CPAP nursing care · cardiovascular complications

Overview

Sleep apnea is a disorder characterized by repeated pauses in breathing (apneas) during sleep due to airway obstruction or impaired respiratory control. These interruptions lead to:

During Sleep
Hypoxemia (low blood oxygen) during each apneic episode → brief arousal to restore breathing
Long-Term Consequences
Poor sleep quality · daytime fatigue · cardiovascular stress · hypertension · arrhythmias

Types of Sleep Apnea

1 · Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)
Most common
Airway physically collapses or is blocked during sleep despite breathing effort
Throat muscles relax → tongue/soft tissue falls back → blocks airway
2 · Central Sleep Apnea (CSA)
Less common
Brain fails to send proper signals to breathing muscles — no effort made to breathe
Associated with heart failure, opioid use, brainstem disorders
3 · Mixed Sleep Apnea
Combination of obstructive AND central features

Additional Symptoms

  • Morning headaches (from nocturnal hypoxemia/hypercapnia)
  • Gasping or choking during sleep
  • Difficulty concentrating / memory problems
  • Mood swings, irritability, depression
  • Nocturia (frequent nighttime urination)
  • Decreased libido

Diagnosis

TestDescriptionNCLEX Note
Polysomnography (Sleep Study) Gold standard — overnight monitoring of breathing, SpO₂, brain waves (EEG), HR, eye/limb movements in a sleep lab Measures Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) — >5 events/hour = OSA
Home Sleep Apnea Test (HSAT) Portable device worn at home; measures airflow, O₂ sat, HR Less comprehensive than PSG; used for uncomplicated suspected OSA
Pulse Oximetry Overnight oximetry shows intermittent desaturations Supportive — not definitive; may show repeated SpO₂ dips

Treatment

1. Lifestyle Modifications First Step

  • ⚖️ Weight loss — most impactful intervention for obese patients with OSA
  • 🚭 Smoking cessation — reduces airway inflammation
  • 🍷 Limit alcohol and sedatives — especially in the 3–4 hours before bed
  • 🛌 Positional therapy — sleep on side (lateral), not on back (supine worsens OSA)
  • 📅 Regular sleep schedule — avoid sleep deprivation

2. Positive Airway Pressure Devices #1 Treatment for OSA

CPAP — Continuous Positive Airway Pressure
  • Delivers steady, constant air pressure through a mask
  • Acts as a "pneumatic splint" — keeps airway open during sleep
  • First-line treatment for OSA
  • NCLEX Tip: Assess mask fit, skin integrity (pressure injuries over nose/bridge), and patient adherence
  • Most patients need nightly use — compliance is the key challenge
BiPAP — Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure
  • Higher pressure during inhalation (IPAP), lower during exhalation (EPAP)
  • More comfortable for patients who struggle to exhale against CPAP pressure
  • Used in: Central sleep apnea, respiratory failure, obesity hypoventilation syndrome
  • Also used for COPD exacerbations (non-invasive ventilation)
🚫
CPAP Contraindications

CPAP is contraindicated in: unconscious patients, vomiting patients (aspiration risk), patients with CSF leak/recent facial surgery, severe facial trauma.

3. Oral Appliances

Custom-fitted devices that reposition the tongue and jaw forward, preventing airway collapse. Used for mild-moderate OSA or patients who cannot tolerate CPAP.

4. Surgical Options (for anatomical causes)

Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP)
Removes excess throat tissue (uvula, soft palate, tonsils) to enlarge the airway
Other Procedures
Tonsillectomy/adenoidectomy, nasal septum repair, mandibular advancement surgery

Cardiovascular Complications of Untreated OSA

⚠️
Untreated OSA → Serious Systemic Consequences

Hypertension — resistant to medication; nocturnal hypoxemia activates sympathetic nervous system
Atrial fibrillation — most common arrhythmia; OSA is a major modifiable risk factor
Pulmonary hypertension — from chronic nocturnal hypoxemia
Heart failure — left ventricular dysfunction from pressure overload
Stroke — from hypoxia, atrial fibrillation, and hypertension combined
Type 2 Diabetes — OSA worsens insulin resistance

NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • 🩺 Monitor for hypoxia — SpO₂, respiratory rate, level of consciousness
  • 📋 Teach CPAP usage — importance of nightly adherence, mask fitting, cleaning schedule
  • ⚠️ Fall risk — assess for excessive daytime sleepiness and cognitive impairment → safety measures
  • 🚗 Driving safety — educate about risks of driving when fatigued; some states require reporting
  • ⚖️ Promote weight loss and lifestyle modifications
  • 🚫 Avoid CNS depressants — opioids, benzodiazepines, sedatives, alcohol (worsen apnea)
  • 💊 Review medications — many common meds worsen OSA (muscle relaxants, sleep aids)

NCLEX High-Yield Tips

  • ✅ CPAP compliance = #1 intervention for OSA — this is the most-tested point
  • ✅ OSA can lead to hypertension, arrhythmias (especially AFib), stroke, and heart failure
  • ✅ Daytime fatigue + loud snoring are classic NCLEX OSA clues
  • ✅ Assess mask fit and skin breakdown — bridge of nose is prone to pressure injuries
  • ✅ OSA is associated with pulmonary hypertension if untreated
  • ✅ CPAP is contraindicated in unconscious or vomiting patients
  • ✅ Side-lying position helps reduce apneic episodes
  • ✅ STOP-BANG score ≥3 = high risk for OSA → refer for sleep study
  • ✅ BiPAP = CPAP alternative for patients who can't tolerate constant high pressure
