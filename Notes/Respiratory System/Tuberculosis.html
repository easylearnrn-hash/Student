Respiratory System

Tuberculosis (TB)

Airborne precautions · latent vs active · TB RISK + TB SEEDS mnemonics · RIPE/PERI treatment

Overview

🧫
Pathogen

TB = Mycobacterium tuberculosis — acid-fast, aerobic bacillus. Primary infection site: lung apex (high O₂ environment). Can disseminate to brain, spine, kidneys, joints, and lymph nodes. Transmitted by airborne droplet nuclei — NOT large droplets.

Transmission & Infection Control

Airborne Precautions — NOT Droplet!

  • N95 respirator — required for all healthcare workers entering the room
  • Negative-pressure isolation room — door must remain closed at all times
  • Surgical mask on patient when leaving the room
  • Teach cough etiquette; patient should avoid crowded public areas
⚠️
NCLEX Critical

TB = AIRBORNE precautions → N95 mask + negative-pressure room. Frequently confused with droplet precautions (used for flu, COVID, meningitis). Know the difference cold.

B

Diagnostic Tests

TestDetailsKey Points
PPD / Mantoux Intradermal 0.1 mL injection; read at 48–72 hrs Measure induration (hardness) — NOT redness! Use ruler across forearm.
IGRA (QuantiFERON-TB Gold / T-SPOT) Blood test; measures interferon-gamma release NOT affected by BCG vaccine. Single visit. More specific than PPD.
Sputum Culture Gold Standard 3 morning samples on 3 consecutive days Confirms species + drug susceptibility. Takes weeks for results.
AFB Smear Ziehl-Neelsen stain (bacilli = bright red on blue) or auramine-rhodamine (fluorescent). 3 specimens on 3 different days. Rapid, inexpensive. Positive AFB = likely contagious. Culture confirms species + drug susceptibility.
Chest X-ray Upper lobe infiltrates, cavitations PPD+ does NOT mean active TB — follow up with CXR + sputum culture.

PPD Positive Criteria — Induration Thresholds

  • ≥ 5 mm HIV-positive · recent TB contact · chest X-ray suggestive of old TB · organ transplant or immunosuppressed (corticosteroids)
  • ≥ 10 mm Recent immigrants (past 5 yrs) · IV drug users · high-risk settings (prisons, HCWs) · children <4 yrs or recently exposed
  • ≥ 15 mm Healthy individuals with no known risk factors

False Positive

  • Previous BCG vaccine (common in foreign-born patients)

False Negative

  • Immunosuppression
  • Recent TB infection (<8 weeks — window period)
  • Very young or elderly
  • Active viral illness

TB Treatment — Mnemonic: RIPE / PERI

DrugKey Side EffectsNursing Monitor
R — Rifampin 🟠 Orange/red body fluids · hepatotoxicity · ↓ birth control effectiveness LFTs; advise condom use; warn about orange fluids (expected, not harmful)
I — Isoniazid (INH) Peripheral neuropathy · hepatotoxicity Give Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) to prevent neuropathy; monitor LFTs
P — Pyrazinamide ↑ Uric acid → gout · hepatotoxicity Uric acid levels; LFTs
E — Ethambutol Optic neuritis — blurred vision · red-green color blindness Monthly visual acuity checks; report any vision changes immediately
Streptomycin (add-on) Ototoxicity — hearing loss, tinnitus Audiometric testing; monitor renal function
⏱️
Duration & Adherence

6–12 months total. Biggest risk = hepatotoxicity (all four first-line drugs). Biggest patient education point = strict medication adherence — missed doses lead to MDR-TB.

👁️
NCLEX Tip — Ethambutol

Patient on Ethambutol reports blurred vision or trouble distinguishing colors → notify provider immediately — this is optic neuritis and the drug must be stopped.

Priority Nursing Actions

Active TB — Priority Order

  1. Initiate airborne precautions — N95 + negative-pressure room (FIRST action)
  2. Collect sputum culture BEFORE starting antibiotics
  3. Start RIPE regimen as ordered
  4. Monitor respiratory status and O₂ saturation
  5. Monitor medication side effects (vision, LFTs, peripheral neuropathy)
  6. Educate patient on isolation criteria and medication adherence

Latent TB

  • Screen high-risk patients with PPD or IGRA annually
  • Treat with INH × 6–9 months to prevent reactivation
  • Always give Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) with INH
  • Monitor liver function tests (LFTs) regularly throughout treatment

When to Discontinue Isolation?

ALL 3 Criteria Must Be Met

  • ✅ ≥3 weeks of effective anti-TB medications
  • ✅ 3 consecutive negative sputum cultures (on separate days)
  • ✅ Clinical improvement of symptoms

Patient Education

  • Strict medication adherence — missed doses → MDR-TB (drug-resistant TB)
  • Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic substances (excess acetaminophen, herbal supplements)
  • High-protein, high-calorie diet to combat weight loss
  • Screen all exposed household contacts with PPD or IGRA
  • Report worsening cough, hemoptysis, fever, or weight loss immediately
  • Rifampin turns body fluids orange — expected and not harmful; warn the patient in advance
  • Wear a surgical mask in public until declared non-infectious

NCLEX Must-Know

  • First nursing action = airborne precautions + negative-pressure isolation room
  • Most definitive diagnostic test = sputum culture (gold standard)
  • PPD measures induration (hardness) — NOT redness; read at 48–72 hours
  • BCG vaccine → false-positive PPD → use IGRA instead in vaccinated patients
  • Collect sputum BEFORE starting antibiotics
  • Biggest medication risk = hepatotoxicity (all RIPE drugs)
  • Ethambutol → optic neuritis → report visual changes immediately
  • INH → always give Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) to prevent peripheral neuropathy
  • Rifampin → orange body fluids + decreases birth control effectiveness
  • Isolation discontinued only after 3 negative sputum cultures + 3 wks meds + clinical improvement
