Oxygen Delivery Devices — Master Table

Nasal Cannula 24–44% 1–6 L/min Most common, most comfortable. Add humidification at >4 L/min. Works for mild hypoxia.

CPAP vs. BiPAP

CPAP

  • One continuous pressure level
  • For sleep apnea
  • Prevents airway collapse during sleep
  • Patient breathes spontaneously

BiPAP

  • Two pressure levels: IPAP (inhalation) + EPAP (exhalation)
  • For COPD, CHF, neuromuscular disease
  • Easier to exhale against lower pressure
  • Prevents intubation in many cases
NCLEX Must-Know Device Facts

Critical Points by Device

  • Nasal cannula: Max 6 L/min; add humidifier at >4 L/min
  • Simple mask: MINIMUM 6 L/min — below this CO₂ builds up in mask
  • Venturi mask: Most precise → best for COPD (avoid loss of hypoxic drive)
  • NRB mask: Keep reservoir bag inflated; check side valves are intact
  • BVM: Used in CPR and respiratory arrest; maintain tight seal
  • COPD patient: Start with Venturi mask or low-flow NC — avoid high O₂ saturation above 94%
Respiratory Gas Laws

Dalton's Law

Total pressure = sum of partial pressures of all gases in the mixture. Each gas behaves independently.

At sea level (760 mmHg): N₂ = 78% (593 mmHg), O₂ = 21% (160 mmHg), CO₂ = 0.03% (0.3 mmHg)

Boyle's Law

Pressure ↑ → Volume ↓ (inverse relationship at constant temperature). Basis of inspiration/expiration mechanics.

Henry's Law

Amount of gas dissolved in liquid ∝ partial pressure of that gas. Basis of O₂ diffusion into blood.

Altitude Effect: Higher altitude = lower atmospheric pressure → fewer gas molecules → O₂ diffusion into bloodstream is harder → requires acclimatization or supplemental O₂.
Gas Exchange — Partial Pressures

Oxygen (O₂)

  • Alveoli: 104 mmHg
  • Blood entering alveoli: 40 mmHg
  • O₂ diffuses from alveoli → blood (high → low)

Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

  • Blood returning from tissues: 45 mmHg
  • Alveoli: 40 mmHg
  • CO₂ diffuses from blood → alveoli (high → low)
Oxygen Transport in Blood
98.5%
Bound to hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin) — each Hb molecule carries 4 O₂ molecules. Efficient, large capacity.
1.5%
Dissolved in plasma — measured as PaO₂ on ABG. Low capacity but directly reflects partial pressure.

Factors That ↑ O₂ Release from Hemoglobin (Bohr Effect)

  • ↓ pH (acidosis) — tissues exercising, infection
  • ↑ CO₂ — tissues producing waste
  • ↑ Temperature — fever, active muscles
  • ↑ 2,3-DPG (enzyme in RBCs)

These conditions shift the O₂-Hb dissociation curve to the right — O₂ unloads more easily at the tissues.

Carbon Dioxide Transport in Blood
70%
Bicarbonate ion (HCO₃⁻) — CO₂ enters RBCs, converted by carbonic anhydrase: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. HCO₃⁻ moves into plasma. This is the basis of the bicarbonate buffer system.
20%
Bound to hemoglobin (carbaminohemoglobin) — binds to protein portion (not heme), compatible with O₂ transport simultaneously.
10%
Dissolved in plasma — measured as PaCO₂ on ABG (normal 35–45 mmHg).
Surfactant

Surfactant Facts

  • Produced by Type II pneumocytes (alveolar epithelial cells)
  • Function: Reduces surface tension of alveoli → prevents alveolar collapse (atelectasis)
  • Clinical significance: Premature babies lack surfactant → RDS (Respiratory Distress Syndrome)
  • Treatment for RDS: exogenous surfactant administration + CPAP/ventilation

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Simple mask minimum = 6 L/min (flush CO₂)
  • Most precise O₂ delivery = Venturi mask
  • Highest O₂ without intubation = Non-rebreather mask
  • COPD: use Venturi or low-flow NC to avoid suppressing hypoxic drive
  • Target SpO₂ for COPD = 88–92% (not 95–100%)
  • 98.5% O₂ transported via hemoglobin
  • CO₂ primarily transported as bicarbonate (70%)
  • Surfactant = Type II pneumocytes; deficiency → RDS in preterm infants
  • NRB reservoir bag should remain inflated by at least ⅓
