Pneumonia — NCLEX Review

Types, causes, symptoms, diagnosis, treatment, prevention, and nursing care

What is Pneumonia?
Pneumonia: Infection that inflames the alveoli, causing them to fill with fluid or pus. This reduces oxygen exchange → hypoxia. NCLEX core concept: Pneumonia = Fluid-filled alveoli → Impaired gas exchange!
Types of Pneumonia
TypeCauseRisk Factors
Community-Acquired (CAP)Bacteria, viruses, fungi from environmentSmoking, COPD, diabetes, elderly, immunocompromised
Hospital-Acquired (HAP)Hospital bacteria — often antibiotic-resistantHospitalized >48 hours, surgery, weak immune system
Ventilator-Associated (VAP)Bacteria via ventilator tubeIntubated patients, long ICU stays

Atypical / Walking PneumoniaMycoplasma pneumoniaeYoung adults, crowded settings (college, military)
NCLEX Tip: Hospital-acquired pneumonia is harder to treat because it's often antibiotic-resistant!
Causes by Pathogen

Most Common Organisms

  • Bacteria (most common): Streptococcus pneumoniae (CAP #1), Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas (HAP/VAP)
  • Viruses: Influenza, RSV, COVID-19 (common in children and mild cases)
  • Fungi: Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP) — seen in HIV/AIDS patients
NCLEX Tip: Bacterial pneumonia = more severe than viral. Start antibiotics ASAP — do NOT wait for lab results!
Symptoms

Typical (Bacterial Pneumonia)

  • Fever & chills
  • Productive cough — yellow, green, or rust-colored sputum NCLEX Keyword
  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath)
  • Pleuritic chest pain — sharp pain that worsens with breathing
  • Crackles or rhonchi — fluid in alveoli creates these sounds

Atypical / Viral Pneumonia

  • Dry cough (non-productive)
  • Mild fever
  • Fatigue, headache
  • Often ambulatory ("walking pneumonia")

Elderly Patients

  • Confusion — first sign!
  • Low body temperature (instead of fever)
  • Weakness, poor appetite
  • May NOT have classic symptoms
NCLEX Tip: Confusion and weakness in the elderly = may be Pneumonia! Always suspect pneumonia in confused older adults.

NCLEX Nursing Care

Priority Nursing Interventions

  1. Position High Fowler's — upright for easier breathing
  2. Administer O₂ — target SpO₂ ≥ 95%
  3. Encourage coughing & deep breathing — prevents mucus buildup
  4. Increase fluid intake — helps thin and mobilize secretions
  5. Teach incentive spirometer use — prevents atelectasis (lung collapse)
  6. Start antibiotics ASAP — do not wait for full culture results
  7. Monitor respiratory status — SpO₂, breath sounds, RR, signs of worsening
  8. Reassess lung sounds after treatment — check for improvement
Prevention

Vaccinations

  • PCV13 — pneumococcal conjugate (children + high-risk adults)
  • PPSV23 — pneumococcal polysaccharide (adults >65 + high-risk)
  • Annual flu shot — prevents influenza pneumonia
  • COVID-19 booster

General Prevention

  • Hand washing — #1 infection control measure
  • Smoking cessation — damages lung defense mechanisms
  • HOB elevated — prevents aspiration in high-risk patients
  • Oral care for ventilated patients — reduces VAP
NCLEX Tip: PPSV23 is recommended for all adults >65 and high-risk patients!
Complications

Life-Threatening Complications

  • Sepsis — infection spreads to bloodstream (life-threatening)
  • Pleural effusion — fluid builds up around lungs (dull percussion)
  • ARDS — Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (severe lung failure)
  • Lung abscess — pocket of pus in the lung
NCLEX Tip: Severe pneumonia → ARDS — watch for worsening hypoxia despite O₂ therapy!

NCLEX Must-Know

Top 8 Pneumonia Facts

  • Bacterial pneumonia = productive cough, fever, crackles
  • Elderly first sign = confusion (not fever!)
  • Start antibiotics ASAP — don't wait for cultures
  • Chest X-ray = gold standard for diagnosis
  • Rust-colored sputum = classic bacterial pneumonia
  • PPSV23 for all adults >65
  • Aspiration risk: stroke, dementia, dysphagia → keep HOB elevated
  • Reassess breath sounds after treatment to check for improvement
