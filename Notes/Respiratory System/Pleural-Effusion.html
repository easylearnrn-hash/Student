Pleural Effusion

NCLEX-focused — transudate vs exudate · thoracentesis nursing care · complications

What Is Pleural Effusion?

Pleural effusion is the abnormal accumulation of fluid in the pleural space — the thin area between the visceral and parietal pleura surrounding the lungs.

Simple Analogy
"Fluid is squishing the lung from outside" — the lung can't fully expand because the pleural space is filled with fluid.

Normally only 5–15 mL of lubricating fluid exists in the pleural space. Any significant accumulation impairs lung expansion and gas exchange.

Types of Pleural Effusion

1 · Transudative Effusion
  • Fluid: Clear, thin, low protein
  • Cause: Fluid leaks out of blood vessels due to pressure/oncotic changes — not infection
  • Common causes:
    • 💔 Heart failure (most common)
    • 🩺 Liver cirrhosis
    • 🧽 Nephrotic syndrome
2 · Exudative Effusion
  • Fluid: Cloudy, high protein, may contain immune cells, pus, or blood
  • Cause: Inflammation, infection, or malignancy disrupts pleural membrane
  • Common causes:
    • 🦠 Pneumonia (parapneumonic effusion)
    • 🧬 Tuberculosis
    • 🧫 Cancer (lung, breast, lymphoma)
    • PE, pancreatitis, lupus
🧠
NCLEX Memory Trick

Transudate = Think "Trans-clear" — clear fluid, cardiac/liver/kidney cause
Exudate = Think "Ex-cloudy/infected" — cloudy, immune cells, infection/cancer cause

Signs & Symptoms

Respiratory Symptoms
  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath) — worsens with exertion or lying flat
  • Pleuritic chest pain — sharp, worse with deep breath/cough
  • Dry, non-productive cough
  • Orthopnea — difficulty breathing when lying flat (from fluid shifting)
Physical Exam Findings
  • ↓ Breath sounds on affected side
  • ↓ Chest expansion on affected side
  • Dullness to percussion over fluid
  • Tracheal deviation away from effusion (in large effusions)
🔈
NCLEX Physical Findings Clue

Diminished breath sounds + Dull percussion on one side = think pleural effusion
Compare: Pneumothorax = diminished sounds + hyperresonant percussion

Thoracentesis — Nursing Care High-Priority NCLEX

Before the Procedure

  • Informed consent obtained
  • Position: Sitting upright, leaning forward over bedside table (opens intercostal spaces)
  • Baseline vitals and SpO₂ documented
  • Verify imaging — ensure correct side identified
  • Mark site — often with ultrasound guidance

During the Procedure

  • Sterile technique required — NCLEX priority
  • Patient must remain still — instruct not to cough or move suddenly
  • Continuous vital sign monitoring — BP, HR, SpO₂, RR
  • Limit removal to 1,000–1,500 mL at one time to prevent re-expansion pulmonary edema
  • Never remove more than 1,500 mL at one sitting

After the Procedure

  • 🩺 Auscultate lung sounds bilaterally — check for improvement
  • 🩻 Chest X-ray ordered to rule out pneumothorax
  • 📉 Monitor vitals closely — BP, HR, SpO₂
  • 🩹 Assess puncture site — bleeding, hematoma, subcutaneous emphysema
  • 💬 Ask about dyspnea — should improve; worsening = complication
  • 🧪 Label and send fluid to lab for analysis

Complications to Watch For

🚨
Post-Thoracentesis Complications

Pneumothorax — sudden worsening dyspnea, absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation → call provider, prepare for chest tube
Hemothorax — bloody drainage, hypotension
Hypotension — vasovagal response; remove >1,500 mL risk
Re-expansion pulmonary edema — if too much fluid removed too fast → pink frothy sputum, respiratory distress
Infection — fever, purulent drainage at site
