What Is a Tracheostomy?

A tracheostomy is a surgical opening (stoma) created in the trachea (windpipe) to establish an artificial airway. A tracheostomy tube is inserted through this opening to facilitate breathing.

Key Concept
Unlike an endotracheal (ET) tube inserted through the mouth or nose, a tracheostomy goes directly through the neck into the trachea — bypassing the upper airway entirely.

Indications for Tracheostomy

Airway Protection
  • Upper airway obstruction (tumor, trauma, swelling)
  • Severe facial trauma or burns
  • Inability to clear secretions
Prolonged Ventilation
  • Mechanical ventilation >10–14 days
  • Neuromuscular disease (ALS, SCI, Guillain-Barré)
  • Decreased level of consciousness

Types of Tracheostomy Tubes

TypeDescriptionUsed For
Cuffed Inflatable balloon around tube seals the airway below the stoma Ventilated patients; aspiration risk — prevents air leak and protects airway
Uncuffed No balloon; no seal Patients breathing independently; low aspiration risk; often used in pediatrics
Fenestrated Has hole(s) in outer cannula above cuff — allows air to pass through vocal cords Enables speech when cuff is deflated and inner cannula removed/speaking valve added
Non-fenestrated No holes in outer cannula Standard tube; no speech unless speaking valve added externally
Inner cannula Removable inner tube that fits inside the outer cannula Can be removed and cleaned (or replaced) without disturbing the outer cannula
Outer cannula Main tube that sits in stoma Keeps stoma patent; should not be removed without physician order (except emergency)
Obturator Rounded guide used for tube insertion only Inserted during placement; must be removed immediately after insertion — it blocks the airway
🚨
NCLEX: Obturator at Bedside Always

Keep the obturator and a spare trach tube (same size + one size smaller) at the bedside at all times. Required for emergency reinsertion after accidental decannulation.

Tracheostomy Care — NCLEX Essentials

Sterile vs. Clean Technique

Sterile Technique (Hospital)
Required for new tracheostomy care within first 72 hours and for suctioning in hospital settings — stoma is a fresh wound, infection risk is high
Clean Technique (Home)
Acceptable for established tracheostomy care at home — chronic stoma, lower infection risk

Routine Tracheostomy Care Steps

  1. Gather supplies: sterile gloves, trach care kit, suction catheter, NS, gauze, new trach ties
  2. Suction trach before care — clear secretions first (suction BEFORE, not after)
  3. Remove inner cannula — clean with NS, brush, rinse thoroughly; or replace disposable
  4. Clean stoma site — circular motion away from stoma, NS-soaked gauze; dry well
  5. Change trach ties — secure new ties before removing old ones; finger-width space between tie and neck
  6. Apply clean dressing (split gauze) under the flange
  7. Assess stoma — color, drainage, skin integrity around stoma
  8. Ensure humidification — prevents secretion thickening/mucus plugs
💡
Tie Change Rule

Change trach ties one side at a time — leave old ties in place while securing new ones. Never leave the trach unsecured. Should allow one finger-width of slack.

Suctioning — Critical NCLEX Priority

When to Suction — Signs
  • Audible crackles, gurgling, or secretion sounds
  • ↑ Respiratory rate or labored breathing
  • ↓ SpO₂ without other cause
  • Visible secretions in trach tube
  • Patient unable to clear secretions with coughing

Suctioning Procedure

  1. Hyperoxgenate first — give 100% O₂ for 30–60 seconds before suctioning
  2. Sterile technique — sterile gloves and sterile catheter
  3. Insert catheter without suction active — advance gently until resistance felt; withdraw 1 cm
  4. Apply suction while withdrawing — rotate catheter as you pull out
  5. Limit to <10 seconds per pass to prevent hypoxia
  6. Rest 30–60 seconds between passes — reoxygenate
  7. Maximum 3 passes per suctioning episode
  8. Reoxygenate after — monitor SpO₂ recovery
⚠️
Vagal Response During Suctioning

Suctioning can stimulate the vagus nerve → sudden bradycardia, hypotension, or syncope. Monitor HR during suctioning. Stop if HR drops significantly.

🎯
Cuff Pressure

Tracheostomy cuff pressure should be <20 mmHg (or 20–25 cmH₂O). Over-inflation → tracheal mucosal ischemia → stenosis/necrosis.

Emergency Situations

Accidental Decannulation (Tube Dislodgment) Top Priority NCLEX

⏱ Within 72 Hours of Tracheostomy
  • Stoma is not yet mature — track may close quickly
  • DO NOT attempt reinsertion without physician
  • Cover stoma with sterile gauze
  • Use bag-valve mask at nose/mouth to ventilate
  • Call for Rapid Response immediately
⏱ After 72 Hours (Mature Stoma)
  • Stoma is established and stable
  • Attempt reinsertion using obturator with spare tube
  • Never force if resistance felt
  • If unable to reinsert → cover stoma, BVM, call Rapid Response

Signs of Trach Obstruction

Patient unable to breathe or be ventilated · SpO₂ drops rapidly · Patient in distress
Action: Remove inner cannula → attempt to suction → if obstructed, change entire trach tube → call Rapid Response

Speaking With a Tracheostomy

Passy-Muir Valve (PMV)
  • One-way speaking valve placed on trach hub
  • Air flows in through trach → out through mouth/nose → allows speech
  • Cuff must be completely deflated before applying PMV — if cuff is inflated, patient cannot exhale → respiratory distress
  • Requires fenestrated tube or adequate space around tube for air to pass
  • Speech therapy often consulted for PMV trials

Patient & Caregiver Teaching

  • 🧼 Stoma care — daily cleaning technique; signs of infection
  • 💧 Hydration — drink plenty of fluids to keep secretions thin
  • 🚿 Shower protection — use shower shield; avoid direct water into stoma; never submerge in water/swim
  • 💨 Humidifier at home — prevents mucus thickening
  • 🚨 Emergency replacement training — caregiver must know how to reinsert tube and when to call 911
  • 👗 Clothing — cover stoma with loose breathable fabric in public; avoid dust/pollen near stoma
  • 🗣️ Communication — teach alternate communication methods; PMV/speaking valve use

NCLEX High-Yield Tips

  • ✅ Always assess airway first — trach = artificial airway = top priority
  • ✅ Decannulation <72 hrs: cover stoma, BVM at mouth — do NOT attempt reinsertion alone
  • ✅ Decannulation >72 hrs: attempt reinsertion with obturator using spare tube
  • ✅ NEVER force a tube in if resistance is felt
  • ✅ Obturator and spare tubes at bedside — always
  • ✅ Suction BEFORE trach care — not after
  • ✅ Cuff pressure <20 mmHg — prevents tracheal injury
  • ✅ PMV use requires cuff completely deflated
