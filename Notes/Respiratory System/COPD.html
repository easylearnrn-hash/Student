Respiratory System

COPD — Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease

Chronic Bronchitis + Emphysema — pathophysiology, symptoms, treatments, NCLEX priorities

What is COPD?
Definition: Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease is a progressive lung disease causing obstruction of airflow. Two types on NCLEX: Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater") and Emphysema ("Pink Puffer"). Both cause hypoxia and hypercapnia.
COPD Types — NCLEX Comparison

Chronic Bronchitis — "Blue Bloater"

  • Inflamed, mucus-filled bronchi
  • Productive cough ≥3 months × 2 consecutive years
  • Cyanosis (BLUE skin)
  • Peripheral edema (Cor pulmonale)
  • Crackles and wheezes
  • Overweight (bloated appearance)

Emphysema — "Pink Puffer"

  • Destroyed alveolar walls → air trapping
  • Pursed-lip breathing (extends exhalation)
  • Barrel chest (hyperinflated lungs)
  • PINK complexion (compensatory hyperventilation)
  • Cachexia (weight loss, muscle wasting)
  • Dyspnea on exertion
Pathophysiology

What Happens in COPD

  • Gas exchange issues: Low O₂ (hypoxia) + High CO₂ (hypercapnia)
  • Respiratory acidosis: CO₂ retention → blood pH drops
  • Hypoxic drive: In COPD, the body adapts to high CO₂ — O₂ becomes the breathing trigger
  • Polycythemia: ↑ RBC production to compensate for chronic hypoxia → thick blood → clotting risk
  • Cor pulmonale: Pulmonary hypertension → right-sided heart failure
Causes & Risk Factors

Main Causes

  • Smoking — #1 cause
  • Occupational hazards (asbestos, dust, chemicals)
  • Air pollution
  • Genetics (Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency)

Avoid (Triggers)

  • Beta-blockers
  • NSAIDs & aspirin
  • Calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil)
  • Cold weather, stress, GERD
  • Smoking

O₂ Saturation Targets in COPD

Stable COPD

  • Target SpO₂: 88–92%
  • Low-flow O₂ via nasal cannula (1–2 L/min)
  • Goal: avoid suppressing hypoxic drive

Exacerbated COPD

  • SpO₂ may drop to 84–86%
  • Still use controlled, low-flow O₂
  • NEVER give high-flow O₂ → removes hypoxic drive → apnea!
CRITICAL NCLEX: High-flow O₂ in COPD = dangerous! Removes the hypoxic drive → patient stops breathing. Always use low-flow O₂ (1–2 L/min) and target SpO₂ 88–92%.

NCLEX Priority Interventions

Priority Nursing Actions

  • Low-flow O₂ — 1–2 L/min; never high-flow
  • Pursed-lip breathing — prolongs exhalation, prevents air trapping
  • High Fowler's position (or tripod/tripoding) — maximizes lung expansion
  • Smoking cessation — #1 intervention to slow progression
  • Encourage fluids 2–3 L/day — loosens mucus
  • Vaccinations — flu and pneumonia vaccines to prevent exacerbations
  • Monitor polycythemia — thick blood → DVT precautions
  • Avoid triggers — beta-blockers, NSAIDs, aspirin, cold air, GERD
NCLEX Tip: Tripoding position (leaning forward with hands on knees) is a classic COPD sign — patient using accessory muscles + gravity to assist breathing. Always a sign of respiratory distress.
