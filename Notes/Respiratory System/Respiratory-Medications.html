Respiratory System

Respiratory Medications

NCLEX-focused pharmacology — bronchodilators · anti-inflammatories · mucolytics · emergency agents

1. Bronchodilators — Open the Airways

Short-Acting Beta Agonists (SABAs) Rescue Inhaler

Prototype · Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin)
  • Used for: Acute asthma attack, exercise-induced bronchospasm, COPD exacerbation
  • Mechanism: β₂-agonist → relaxes bronchial smooth muscle → bronchodilation within minutes
  • Side effects: Tremors, tachycardia, anxiety, hypokalemia (at high doses)
  • NCLEX tip: SABA = rescue med — used for acute symptoms, NOT maintenance

Long-Acting Beta Agonists (LABAs) Maintenance Only

Salmeterol (Serevent) · Formoterol (Foradil)
  • Used for: Asthma maintenance, COPD long-term management
  • NOT for emergencies — onset too slow
  • Side effects: Mild shakiness, nervousness, tachycardia
  • NCLEX warning: LABAs should not be used as monotherapy in asthma — must be combined with inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)

Anticholinergics (Muscarinic Antagonists)

Short-Acting · Ipratropium (Atrovent)
Used with albuterol in COPD/asthma exacerbations; blocks M3 receptors → reduces secretions + bronchospasm
Long-Acting · Tiotropium (Spiriva)
COPD maintenance; once-daily inhaler; reduces exacerbation frequency
⚠️ Anticholinergic Side Effects — Remember "DUMB" Reversal
Dry mouth, blurred vision, urinary retention, constipation, tachycardia

2. Anti-Inflammatory Medications

Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS) Mainstay of Asthma Prevention

Fluticasone (Flovent) · Budesonide (Pulmicort) · Beclomethasone (QVAR)
  • Used for: Asthma prevention (controller), COPD with frequent exacerbations
  • NOT for acute attacks — anti-inflammatory effect takes days-weeks
  • Side effects: Oral candidiasis (thrush), dysphonia (hoarse voice)
💡
NCLEX Must-Know: Rinse Mouth!

After every inhaled corticosteroid — rinse mouth and gargle to prevent oral thrush. This is a top NCLEX teaching point.

Systemic Corticosteroids Acute Flare-Ups

Prednisone (oral) · Methylprednisolone (IV) · Dexamethasone (IV/IM)
  • Used for: Acute asthma/COPD exacerbations, severe croup, anaphylaxis adjunct
  • Side effects (short-term): Hyperglycemia ↑ blood sugar, mood changes, fluid retention, hypertension
  • Side effects (long-term): Osteoporosis, adrenal suppression, Cushing's syndrome, immunosuppression
  • NCLEX: Monitor blood glucose in diabetic patients; do NOT abruptly stop long-term steroid use

4. Leukotriene Receptor Antagonist

Montelukast (Singulair)
  • Used for: Asthma prevention (especially allergic/exercise-induced), seasonal allergic rhinitis
  • Mechanism: Blocks leukotriene receptors → reduces airway inflammation, mucus, bronchoconstriction
  • Side effects: Neuropsychiatric effects — nightmares, mood changes, suicidal ideation (especially in children)
  • FDA Black Box Warning: Risk of serious neuropsychiatric events
  • NCLEX: Taken orally once daily; not for acute attacks

6. RSV & Croup (Pediatrics)

Racemic Epinephrine (nebulized)
  • Used for: Moderate-to-severe croup (reduces laryngeal edema)
  • Side effects: Tachycardia, hypertension, rebound edema
  • NCLEX: Observe child for 3–4 hours after — rebound swelling can occur; do NOT discharge immediately
Palivizumab (Synagis) — monthly IM injection
  • Used for: RSV prophylaxis in high-risk infants (premature, CHD, CLD)
  • NOT treatment — prevention only (given monthly Oct–March)
  • Side effects: Injection site pain, mild fever

7. Emergency Respiratory Medications

💉
Epinephrine — IM (EpiPen) or IV

Used for: Anaphylaxis (first-line), asthma unresponsive to albuterol, cardiac arrest
Side effects: ↑ HR, hypertension, anxiety, tremors
NCLEX: Anaphylaxis → IM epinephrine to lateral thigh first; then antihistamines/steroids after

🫁
Oxygen Therapy

Used for: Any respiratory distress, hypoxia (SpO₂ <94%)
COPD caution: Some COPD patients are hypoxic drivers — use controlled low-flow O₂ (start 1–2 L/min via nasal cannula); goal SpO₂ 88–92% in COPD
Side effects: Mucosal dryness, O₂ toxicity with prolonged high flow

NCLEX Quick Reference — Inhaler Use Order

When Patient Has Both Bronchodilator AND Steroid Inhaler
  1. First: Bronchodilator (albuterol) — opens airways
  2. Wait 5 minutes
  3. Then: Steroid inhaler — now reaches deeper into open airways
  4. After steroid: Rinse mouth and gargle
