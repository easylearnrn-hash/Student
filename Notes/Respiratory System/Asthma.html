Respiratory System

Asthma — NCLEX Review

Chronic bronchospasm, triggers, peak flow, medications, and nursing interventions

Overview
Asthma: Chronic lung disease causing reversible airway narrowing and inflammation — affects bronchi and bronchioles. No cure, but manageable. Triggers cause smooth muscle constriction + mucus production → air trapping → respiratory acidosis (CO₂ buildup).
Pathophysiology of an Asthma Attack

What Happens During an Attack

  1. Trigger exposure (allergen, cold air, exercise, etc.)
  2. Smooth muscle around bronchi/bronchioles constricts
  3. Mucosal lining becomes inflamed → goblet cells produce excess mucus
  4. Narrowed airway + mucus buildup = severely reduced airflow
  5. Air trapping → CO₂ builds up → respiratory acidosis
  6. Symptoms: cough, wheezing, shortness of breath, chest tightness

Early Signs vs. Active Asthma Attack

Early Warning Signs (1–2 days before)

  • Increased SOB and fatigue
  • Frequent cough (especially at night)
  • Trouble sleeping, irritability
  • Cold-like symptoms (sneezing, scratchy throat)
  • Mild wheezing with activity
  • Decreased peak flow meter readings

Active Asthma Attack

  • Chest tightness (muscle constriction)
  • Wheezing (air through narrowed airways)
  • Uncontrollable coughing
  • Severe difficulty exhaling
  • Increased respiratory rate
  • Cyanosis (blue lips, skin)
  • Chest retractions (ribs/clavicles protruding)
Nursing Interventions — Asthma Attack

Priority Actions (in order)

  1. Position in High Fowler's — upright to maximize lung expansion
  2. Administer oxygen — maintain SpO₂ 95–99%
  3. Give bronchodilators — short-acting (SABA) first
  4. Auscultate lung sounds — before AND after treatment
  5. Monitor vital signs — SpO₂, RR, HR
  6. Keep patient calm — anxiety worsens bronchospasm
  7. Monitor cyanosis and retractions
  8. Assess peak flow meter readings
Peak Flow Meter (PFM) — Action Zones
🟢 GREEN Zone — 80–100% of personal best: Asthma is controlled. Take maintenance medications as prescribed. No acute symptoms.
🟡 YELLOW Zone — 50–79% of personal best: Caution! Asthma is getting worse. Add quick-relief medications. Contact provider if doesn't improve.
🔴 RED Zone — below 50% of personal best: Medical alert! Take quick-relief medication immediately. Call 911/provider if no improvement. May need ER.
NCLEX Tip: Use peak flow meter at the same time each day (morning or bedtime) and compare to personal best. Readings below 80% = worsening asthma.

Asthma & Exercise

Exercise Is NOT Contraindicated!

  • Warm up for 10–15 minutes before exercise
  • Use short-acting bronchodilator before exercise
  • Breathe through nose (warms air before reaching lungs)
  • Wear a scarf in cold weather to warm inhaled air
  • Avoid exercise when sick (respiratory infections worsen asthma)
Patient Education

Asthma Action Plan — What to Teach

  • Identify and avoid personal triggers
  • Recognize early warning signs before an attack
  • Follow the 3-zone action plan (Green/Yellow/Red)
  • Use peak flow meter regularly — same time daily
  • Take maintenance medications even when feeling well
  • Rinse mouth after corticosteroid inhaler
  • Do NOT overuse albuterol — using >2×/week = poorly controlled asthma
