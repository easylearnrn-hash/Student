Aging: Physiological & Psychosocial Changes

Psycho-Social Aspects — NCLEX Overview

Why It Matters

As adults age, normal body changes affect safety, mobility, nutrition, and mental health. The nurse must distinguish normal aging from signs of disease and provide holistic, respectful care.

Normal aging = slower, not diseased. Forgetfulness ≠ dementia. Slower recall is normal; disorientation is not.

Mental Health Red Flags in Older Adults

ConditionKey Feature
DepressionNot just sadness — poor sleep, appetite changes, low energy, hopelessness
DeliriumSudden confusion with fluctuating consciousness → caused by illness, meds, infection, dehydration
DementiaSlow, progressive memory loss — insidious onset; personality + cognition changes
Delirium = sudden and reversible. Dementia = gradual and progressive. Both are NOT normal aging.

Nursing Priorities for Older Adults

  • Fall prevention — non-slip socks, call light within reach, bed in lowest position, bed alarms, remove rugs
  • Skin care — reposition every 2 hours, inspect daily, keep skin moisturized
  • Medication safety — start low, go slow; monitor renal function; avoid polypharmacy
  • Nutrition support — easy-to-chew foods, monitor weight, address appetite
  • Promote social connection — prevent isolation
  • Support independence and dignity — include patient in decisions
  • Screen for depression, abuse, and neglect regularly

NCLEX Must-Knows

  • 🎯 Normal aging = slower, not diseased
  • 🎯 Forgetfulness = normal; disorientation = NOT normal
  • 🎯 Delirium = sudden onset, reversible; Dementia = gradual, progressive
  • 🎯 Presbycusis = hearing loss for high-pitched sounds → speak slowly, face the person; do NOT shout
  • 🎯 Presbyopia = near-vision difficulty (normal age-related)
  • 🎯 ↓ renal clearance = medication toxicity risk → "start low, go slow"
  • 🎯 Erikson stage = Ego Integrity vs. Despair (65+ years)
  • 🎯 Assess for depression — it is common but underdiagnosed in elderly
  • 🎯 Respect autonomy unless the patient is cognitively impaired
