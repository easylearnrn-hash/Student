Abuse & Violence

Psycho-Social Aspects — NCLEX Overview

Why It Matters

Nurses are mandatory reporters. If you suspect abuse — even without proof — you must REPORT it, not investigate it. This applies to children, the elderly, and vulnerable adults (illness, disability, etc.).

Types of Abuse

PhysicalIntentional bodily harmBruises, burns, fractures, "loop marks" from cords
SexualAny non-consensual sexual actSTIs, genital trauma, fear of touch

Child Abuse — Red Flags

  • Injuries inconsistent with the story given
  • Delay in seeking medical care
  • Multiple stages of healing (old + new bruises simultaneously)
  • Fear of caregiver; flinching when touched
  • Sexualized behavior in young children
  • Burns in patterns — cigarette burns, iron marks, immersion lines (stocking/glove)
  • Spiral fractures in non-mobile infants

Elder Abuse — Red Flags

  • Unexplained bruises, fractures, or pressure ulcers
  • Over- or under-medication
  • Poor hygiene in a dependent adult who is under someone's care
  • Caregiver hostility, controlling behavior, or answering for the patient
  • Patient appears fearful or withdrawn around caregiver
  • Sudden changes in finances or legal documents

Intimate Partner Violence (IPV)

Applies to all genders and relationship types.

Signs to Assess For:

  • Frequent injuries with vague or inconsistent explanations
  • Partner answers all questions, speaks for the patient, refuses to leave the room
  • Missed appointments, limited access to money or phone
  • Signs of fear, shame, or isolation
For a competent adult experiencing IPV: provide support, offer resources and safety planning — but you cannot force a report. Autonomy is respected unless the patient is incapacitated or dependent.

Nursing Responsibilities

DO

  • Provide privacy and a nonjudgmental environment
  • Use open-ended questions: "Can you tell me what happened?"
  • Document exact words used by the patient — use quotation marks
  • Report suspected abuse to appropriate authorities (CPS, APS, law enforcement)
  • Ensure patient safety — remove from abuser if possible
  • Photograph injuries with consent and document body map

DO NOT

  • Confront the abuser directly
  • Promise confidentiality — you are legally required to report
  • Ask leading questions: "Did your boyfriend hit you?"
  • Use judgmental language in documentation
  • Leave the patient alone with the suspected abuser

NCLEX Must-Knows

  • 🎯 Suspect abuse? REPORT — do not investigate
  • 🎯 Child or elder abuse = mandatory report to CPS or APS regardless of patient's wishes
  • 🎯 IPV in a competent adult = support + offer resources; cannot force report
  • 🎯 Documentation = use exact quotes, body map, avoid opinion language
  • 🎯 Loop marks, cigarette burns, immersion lines → strong indicators of deliberate abuse
  • 🎯 Multiple stages of healing in a child = highest priority red flag
  • 🎯 All patients deserve safety, autonomy, and dignity
