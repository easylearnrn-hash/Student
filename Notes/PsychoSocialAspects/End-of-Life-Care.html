End-of-Life (EOL) Care

Psycho-Social Aspects — NCLEX Overview

What Is End-of-Life Care?

EOL care supports patients in the final phase of life, focusing on comfort, dignity, and quality of life rather than cure. It includes hospice care, palliative care, emotional and spiritual support.

Palliative vs. Hospice Care

TypeDefinitionKey Point
Palliative CareComfort care given at any time during illnessCan be provided alongside curative treatments
Hospice CareComfort care when life expectancy is <6 monthsCurative treatment stops; focus shifts entirely to comfort
Palliative = anytime. Hospice = terminal (<6 months, no curative treatment).

Physical Changes Near Death (Expected)

SystemChanges Seen
RespiratoryIrregular breathing, Cheyne-Stokes pattern (deep → shallow → apnea), congestion ("death rattle")
CardiacWeak, thready pulse; ↓ BP; cool extremities
GI/GU↓ appetite, ↓ urine output, possible incontinence
NeuroIncreased sleeping, ↓ alertness, confusion, possible hallucinations (seeing deceased loved ones)
SkinMottling (purplish marbling on extremities), cyanosis of fingers and toes
Death rattle = retained secretions — treat with anticholinergics (e.g., atropine drops sublingually) or repositioning. This is normal; educate the family.

Emotional & Psychosocial Signs Near Death

  • Withdrawal from others; reduced communication
  • Saying goodbye, reviewing and reflecting on life
  • Sense of peace or conversely, restlessness
  • Spiritual reflection, seeking forgiveness, or expressing fear
  • Visions or conversations with deceased loved ones (normal, not psychosis)

Nursing Comfort Interventions

  • Pain management — opioids (e.g., morphine): ease pain and reduce air hunger; do NOT withhold for fear of hastening death
  • Oral care — mouth swabs for dryness; moisten lips frequently
  • Repositioning — for comfort, NOT on a strict schedule if repositioning causes pain
  • Moist lips and eyes, warm blankets, dim lighting
  • Do NOT force food or fluids — forced feeding is not comfort care and can increase distress
  • Calm presence — calm voice, gentle touch, therapeutic silence
  • Allow family rituals, prayer, music, and cultural practices

Supporting the Family

  • Provide honest but compassionate information about what is happening
  • Normalize expected changes: "This breathing pattern is common near the end and does not mean your loved one is in pain."
  • Allow family presence, touch, and meaningful rituals
  • Offer spiritual care, chaplain, or grief counselor
  • After death: offer privacy, allow family time with the body, prepare the body respectfully

Legal & Ethical Terms

TermMeaning

NCLEX Must-Knows

  • 🎯 Morphine eases pain AND reduces air hunger (dyspnea) — appropriate in EOL care
  • 🎯 Death rattle = anticholinergics (atropine sublingual) or repositioning — educate family this is normal
  • 🎯 Do NOT force food or fluids at end of life
  • 🎯 Palliative = anytime during illness; Hospice = <6 months prognosis, no curative tx
  • 🎯 Support family presence and cultural/spiritual rituals
  • 🎯 After death: offer privacy, allow time with body, prepare respectfully
  • 🎯 Living will → specifies treatment wishes; DPOA-HC → specifies decision-maker
  • 🎯 Always advocate for comfort, dignity, and patient autonomy
