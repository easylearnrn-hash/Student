Biophysical Profile and Contraction Stress Test — antepartum fetal surveillance, scoring, results, and NCLEX priorities

Overview — Why Both Tests Exist

Both the Biophysical Profile (BPP) and the Contraction Stress Test (CST) are antepartum fetal surveillance tests — tools used to assess how the fetus is doing before labor begins. They are typically ordered when:

  • A nonstress test (NST) is nonreactive or unclear
  • The pregnancy is high risk and closer monitoring is needed
  • There is concern about fetal well-being that requires more information than the NST alone provides

📊 BPP — What It Asks

  • "How is the baby doing right now?"
  • Looks at fetal activity, tone, breathing, and fluid
  • Uses ultrasound ± NST
  • Noninvasive — no stress placed on fetus

⚡ CST — What It Asks

  • "Can the baby handle labor stress?"
  • Looks at FHR response to contractions
  • Uses induced contractions (oxytocin or nipple stimulation)
  • Intentionally stresses the fetus
BPP Biophysical Profile

What Is a Biophysical Profile?

A biophysical profile (BPP) is a noninvasive antepartum test that combines real-time ultrasound with (usually) an NST to evaluate fetal health. It assesses multiple indicators of fetal oxygenation and neurologic function — giving a more complete picture of fetal well-being than the NST alone.

BPP = Ultrasound (4 parameters) + NST (1 parameter) = 5 total
Each parameter scored 0 or 2  |  Maximum score: 10

Why Is It Done?

When Is It Done?

The BPP is generally performed in the third trimester, typically after 32 weeks, when fetal parameters are more reliably assessed. It may be performed as often as weekly or twice weekly in high-risk pregnancies.

How Is It Done?

🛏️ Patient Setup

  • Patient reclines comfortably
  • Semi-Fowler's or left lateral tilt preferred
  • Abdominal ultrasound probe applied

🔬 Ultrasound Component

  • Real-time ultrasound for 30 minutes
  • Provider assesses 4 fetal parameters
  • Each scored 0 or 2

📈 NST Component

  • External FHR monitor placed
  • Reactive NST = 2 points
  • Nonreactive = 0 points

The 5 BPP Parameters — FAME + T

Amniotic fluid volume Largest single pocket of fluid by ultrasound At least 1 adequate pocket ≥2 cm (AFI ≥5 cm) 0 or 2
Movement (gross body) Discrete body or limb movements ≥3 separate body or limb movements in 30 min 0 or 2

Plain English — What Each Parameter Actually Means

💡 How the Scoring Works

The BPP checks 5 things. Each one gets either:

  • 0 points — not reassuring (criteria not met)
  • 2 points — reassuring (criteria met)

The highest possible total is 10 points. There is no middle score — each parameter is either 0 or 2, never 1.

🔤 FAME + T — What Each Letter Really Means

AActivityGross body movement — is the baby moving its body or limbs?
MMovement of breathingFetal breathing movements — is the baby making breathing motions?
EExtensionFetal tone — is the baby stretching and flexing normally?
TTestNST — is the heart rate reacting normally to movement?

Easy Meaning of Each Parameter

1️⃣ Fetal Breathing

The baby should make at least 1 breathing movement lasting 30 seconds or more within 30 minutes.

If yes → 2 points

2️⃣ Amniotic Fluid

There must be enough fluid around the baby — at least one pocket big enough on ultrasound.

If yes → 2 points

3️⃣ Body Movement

The baby should move its body or limbs at least 3 separate times in 30 minutes.

If yes → 2 points

4️⃣ Extension / Tone

The baby should show normal muscle tone — like stretching out and then flexing back, or opening and closing a hand.

If yes → 2 points

5️⃣ NST (Test)

The NST must be reactive — meaning the baby's heart rate goes up normally when the baby moves.

If yes → 2 points

✅ Super Easy Way to Think About It

The BPP is basically asking five questions:

  • Is the baby moving?
  • Is the baby breathing (making breathing motions)?
  • Does the baby have good muscle tone?
  • Is there enough amniotic fluid?
  • Is the heart rate reacting normally to movement?

Each "yes" = 2 points. All 5 yes = 10 points = completely reassuring.

BPP = 5 checks  ×  2 points each  =  10 total
8–10 = Reassuring  |  6 = Questionable, may need more testing  |  4 or less = Concerning

BPP Scoring & Interpretation

ScoreInterpretationClinical Action
8–10 Normal / Reassuring Continue routine monitoring per schedule; no immediate intervention needed
6 Equivocal / Uncertain Repeat BPP within 24 hours; consider delivery if ≥36 weeks or if clinical picture is concerning
4 or less Abnormal / Nonreassuring Immediate evaluation; delivery strongly considered regardless of gestational age

💧 Amniotic Fluid — Special Note

Amniotic fluid volume is sometimes evaluated separately as the Modified BPP (NST + AFI only). Low amniotic fluid (oligohydramnios) is independently concerning and may warrant immediate intervention even if other parameters are normal.

Nursing Points for BPP

  • Explain that the BPP is safe, painless, and noninvasive — the ultrasound poses no risk to the fetus
  • Help position the patient comfortably for both ultrasound and fetal monitoring
  • A low or equivocal score does not automatically mean fetal death or immediate catastrophe — it means more evaluation is needed
  • Document the score clearly and report scores of 6 or below to the provider immediately
  • Reassure the patient while preparing for possible follow-up testing or clinical decisions

⚡ NCLEX Tip — BPP

Think of the BPP as a fuller check of fetal well-being than the NST alone. When the NST is nonreactive or not clearly reassuring, the BPP adds context by evaluating 4 additional ultrasound parameters. A BPP score of 8–10 = reassuring. 4 or below = act now.

CST Contraction Stress Test

What Is a Contraction Stress Test?

A contraction stress test (CST) evaluates how the fetal heart rate responds to uterine contractions. During contractions, fetal blood and oxygen supply are temporarily reduced. The CST checks whether the fetus can handle that reduction — essentially previewing how the baby may tolerate the stress of actual labor.

CST = Induced contractions + FHR monitoring
Normal (good) = Negative CST  |  Abnormal (bad) = Positive CST

⚠️ Key Difference from NST and BPP

Unlike the NST and BPP, the CST intentionally stresses the fetus by inducing uterine contractions. It is the only antepartum test that does this. The fetus is exposed to the same type of stress it would experience in labor.

Why Is It Done?

  • When the NST is nonreactive and the BPP is equivocal or unclear
  • When further evaluation of uteroplacental reserve is needed
  • When the provider wants to assess whether the fetus can tolerate labor contractions
  • When other antepartum surveillance results are not clearly reassuring

When Is It Done?

A CST is typically performed at 34 weeks or later, when delivery would be a reasonable option if results are concerning.

How Is It Done?

🛏️ Patient Position

  • Upper body slightly elevated
  • Semi-recumbent position
  • Left lateral tilt to prevent aortocaval compression

🩺 Monitor Placement

  • Two abdominal belts placed
  • One records fetal heart rate
  • One records uterine contractions

⚡ How Contractions Are Induced

  • IV oxytocin (oxytocin challenge test / OCT)
  • Nipple stimulation (releases endogenous oxytocin)
  • Goal: 3 contractions in 10 minutes, each lasting 40–60 seconds

What Is the Test Looking For?

The CST looks specifically for late decelerations of the fetal heart rate after contractions. A late deceleration is a slowing of the FHR that begins after the peak of a contraction and returns to baseline slowly — a sign of uteroplacental insufficiency.

📈 Late Deceleration — What It Means

A late deceleration occurs when the placenta cannot deliver adequate oxygen during the reduced blood flow of a contraction. The fetal heart rate drops after the contraction peaks and is slow to recover. This is the pattern the CST is designed to detect.

CST Results — The Most Important NCLEX Point

✅ Negative CST Good — Reassuring

  • No late decelerations occur with contractions
  • Fetal heart rate remains stable after each contraction
  • Suggests the placenta has adequate reserve
  • Fetus is likely able to tolerate labor
  • Negative = Normal = Good

⚠️ Positive CST Abnormal — Concerning

  • Late decelerations occur with ≥50% of contractions
  • Fetal heart rate slows and stays slow after contractions
  • Suggests uteroplacental insufficiency
  • Fetus may not tolerate the stress of labor
  • Positive = Abnormal = Bad
ResultLate Decelerations?MeaningAction
Negative None Reassuring — placental reserve adequate Continue monitoring per schedule
Positive ≥50% of contractions Nonreassuring — uteroplacental insufficiency suspected Notify provider; further evaluation or delivery
Equivocal-suspicious Intermittent late decels Not clearly positive or negative Repeat or additional testing

What Happens After a Positive CST?

  • Provider notified immediately
  • More testing may be ordered or results repeated
  • If consistently positive and gestational age is appropriate → delivery may be considered
  • Persistent positive results may lead to consideration of cesarean delivery

Risks & Contraindications

⚠️ Main Risk of CST

Because the CST intentionally induces uterine contractions, the primary risk is that it may trigger preterm labor before the due date. Contractions must be monitored carefully and oxytocin discontinued if hyperstimulation occurs.

ConsiderationReason
Multiple gestationHigher risk of triggering labor; usually avoided
Placenta previaContractions risk catastrophic bleeding
Prior classical cesareanUterine rupture risk with contractions
Preterm labor historyContractions may trigger preterm birth
Premature rupture of membranes (PROM)Contractions may accelerate labor prematurely

Nursing Points for CST

  • Explain that this test is different from the NST — it does place stress on the baby by intentionally causing contractions
  • Obtain informed consent as appropriate per facility policy
  • Monitor fetal heart rate and uterine contractions continuously during the test
  • Watch for uterine hyperstimulation — stop oxytocin and notify provider if contractions are too frequent or prolonged
  • Monitor the patient after the test until all contractions have stopped
  • Document results clearly and report a positive CST promptly to the provider
  • Reassure the patient — explain that a positive result prompts further evaluation, not necessarily emergency delivery

⚡ NCLEX Tip — The Classic CST Trap

Students expect "positive" to mean good and "negative" to mean bad. For the CST, it is the opposite:

  • Negative CST = No late decelerations = Good = Reassuring ✅
  • Positive CST = Late decelerations with contractions = Concerning ⚠️

⚡ NCLEX Must-Know Summary

🧠 High-Yield Points

  • BPP = noninvasive — ultrasound + NST — assesses fetal activity, tone, breathing, fluid, and heart rate
  • BPP is scored 0 or 2 per parameter — max 10 — score 8–10 is reassuring
  • BPP score 6 = equivocal → repeat in 24 hours; score ≤4 = abnormal → act now
  • CST = evaluates FHR response to induced uterine contractions (oxytocin or nipple stimulation)
  • Negative CST = Good — no late decelerations — reassuring
  • Positive CST = Bad — late decelerations with ≥50% of contractions — uteroplacental insufficiency suspected
  • CST is the only antepartum test that stresses the fetus
  • CST risk: may trigger preterm labor — typically avoided in multiple gestation, placenta previa, prior classical cesarean
  • Order of testing progression: NST → BPP → CST (each adds more information or stress)
  • Nursing after nonreassuring results: notify provider immediately, document, reassure patient, prepare for further testing

⚠️ The #1 NCLEX Trap — CST Results

Students confuse "negative" with "bad" because negative sounds like a problem. Remember:
Negative CST = No late decelerations = No problem = GOOD ✅
Positive CST = Late decelerations = Problem found = BAD ⚠️

CST — Think of a Stress Test at the Doctor's Office

When a patient does a cardiac stress test and their heart handles it — the test is negative (no problem). When the heart fails under stress — the test is positive (problem found). CST works the same way.

  • Negative CST = baby's heart handled the contractions fine = no late decels = good
  • Positive CST = baby's heart rate dropped after contractions and stayed down = late decels = bad
