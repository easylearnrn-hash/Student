📈 Nonstress Test (NST)

Antepartum fetal surveillance — Reactive vs. Nonreactive results, nursing responsibilities, and NCLEX priorities

← Back to Notes

What Is a Nonstress Test?

A nonstress test (NST) is an antepartum fetal surveillance test that evaluates fetal well-being by monitoring the fetal heart rate (FHR) in response to fetal movement. It is called "nonstress" because nothing is done to place stress on the fetus — no contractions are induced, no medications are given.

The goal is to observe whether the fetal heart rate rises appropriately with movement, which indicates adequate oxygenation and intact neurologic function. A healthy, well-oxygenated fetus will typically show a temporary increase in heart rate when it moves — the same way a person's heart rate increases with activity.

🩺 Why It Matters

The NST assesses the fetal nervous system's ability to respond to movement. A reactive (normal) result is a sign that the fetus is currently oxygenated and neurologically intact. A nonreactive result means the expected response was not seen — more evaluation is needed.

Why Is It Done?

An NST is ordered to evaluate whether the fetus is receiving adequate oxygen and is doing well inside the uterus. It is one of the most commonly used antepartum surveillance tools.

When Is It Done?

NSTs are typically performed in the third trimester, usually at or after 28 weeks of gestation, because fetal heart rate reactivity to movement becomes more reliable as the fetus matures.

⚠️ Before 32 Weeks

ACOG notes that NST interpretation should be approached cautiously before 32 weeks. A normal, healthy preterm fetus may be less reactive due to neurologic immaturity — this does not necessarily indicate compromise. Results must be interpreted in clinical context.

Frequency depends on clinical indication — some patients may need weekly NSTs, others twice weekly, and some more frequently if the clinical picture is concerning.

How Is It Done?

🛏️ Patient Position

  • Reclining or semi-Fowler's position
  • Left lateral tilt preferred to avoid aortocaval compression
  • Ensure maternal comfort throughout

🩺 Monitor Placement

  • Tocotransducer (TOCO) — detects uterine activity / contractions
  • Ultrasound transducer — picks up fetal heart rate
  • Both placed on maternal abdomen

⏱️ Duration

  • Typically 20 minutes
  • May be extended to 40 minutes if fetus appears asleep or inactive
  • Safe, painless, and noninvasive

📋 Patient Involvement

The patient may be given an event marker button to press each time fetal movement is felt. This allows correlation of maternal-perceived movement with the FHR tracing. Some facilities also use vibroacoustic stimulation (a buzzer applied to the abdomen) to wake a sleeping fetus if the initial tracing is nonreactive.

Reactive vs. Nonreactive — The Core Difference

Reactive = FHR accelerates with movement  →  Reassuring ✅
Nonreactive = FHR does not accelerate enough  →  Needs further evaluation ⚠️

✅ Reactive NST Normal

  • ≥2 accelerations within a 20-minute window
  • Each acceleration: FHR rises ≥15 bpm above baseline
  • Each acceleration lasts ≥15 seconds
  • Before 32 weeks: ≥10 bpm × ≥10 seconds is acceptable
  • Indicates adequate fetal oxygenation and intact neurologic function
  • No immediate further action required (unless other concerns)

⚠️ Nonreactive NST Needs Evaluation

  • Does not meet acceleration criteria in 40 minutes
  • Fetus did not demonstrate the expected FHR response
  • Does not automatically mean fetal distress or danger
  • Common benign causes: fetal sleep cycle, prematurity, maternal medications
  • Always requires further evaluation — not a standalone diagnosis
  • Next steps: extend monitoring, vibroacoustic stimulation, BPP, CST

⚠️ NCLEX Trap — Nonreactive ≠ Fetal Death

A nonreactive NST does not mean the fetus is in immediate danger or that a problem is definite. It means the expected fetal heart rate response was not seen during the testing window. The fetus may simply be in a quiet sleep state. Always interpret nonreactive results as a call for further assessment, not an emergency by itself.

What Happens After a Nonreactive NST?

If the NST is nonreactive, the provider will decide on next steps based on gestational age, clinical context, and other findings. Options include:

Biophysical Profile (BPP) — Overview

The biophysical profile combines an NST with a real-time ultrasound assessment of 5 fetal parameters. Each is scored 0 or 2 — no middle score. The highest possible total is 10 points.

💡 How the Scoring Works

Each of the 5 parameters gets either:

  • 0 points — criteria not met (not reassuring)
  • 2 points — criteria met (reassuring)

All 5 yes = 10 points = completely reassuring.

Easy Meaning of Each Parameter

1️⃣ Fetal Breathing

The baby should make at least 1 breathing movement lasting ≥30 seconds within 30 minutes.

If yes → 2 points

2️⃣ Amniotic Fluid

There must be enough fluid around the baby — at least one adequate pocket visible on ultrasound.

If yes → 2 points

3️⃣ Body Movement

The baby should move its body or limbs at least 3 separate times in 30 minutes.

If yes → 2 points

4️⃣ Extension / Tone

The baby should show normal muscle tone — stretching out and then flexing back, or opening and closing a hand.

If yes → 2 points

5️⃣ NST (Test)

The NST must be reactive — the baby's heart rate goes up normally when the baby moves.

If yes → 2 points

✅ Super Easy Way to Think About It

The BPP is basically asking five questions:

  • Is the baby moving?
  • Is the baby breathing (making breathing motions)?
  • Does the baby have good muscle tone?
  • Is there enough amniotic fluid?
  • Is the heart rate reacting normally to movement?

Contraction Stress Test (CST) — Overview

The contraction stress test evaluates how the fetal heart rate responds to uterine contractions. Unlike the NST, this test intentionally stresses the fetus to assess placental reserve. It asks one question: "Can this baby handle contractions?"

✅ Negative CST Good

  • No late decelerations after contractions
  • Baby's heart rate stayed stable
  • Negative = Normal = Reassuring

⚠️ Positive CST Concerning

  • Late decelerations with ≥50% of contractions
  • Baby's heart rate dropped and stayed down
  • Positive = Abnormal = Concerning

⚠️ NCLEX Trap — CST Results Are Backwards from What You'd Expect

Negative CST = GOOD.   Positive CST = BAD.
Contractions are induced with oxytocin or nipple stimulation. For full detail on CST, see the BPP & CST note.

Nursing Responsibilities

Before the Test

  • Explain that the test is safe, painless, and noninvasive
  • Tell the patient nothing is done to stress the fetus — reassure before monitoring begins
  • Position the patient in a reclining or semi-Fowler's position with left lateral tilt
  • Apply both external monitors correctly — TOCO and FHR transducer
  • Instruct the patient to press the event marker whenever fetal movement is felt
  • Reduce anxiety — explain that a nonreactive result does not automatically mean a serious problem
  • Ask about recent maternal meals (movement is often more active after eating)

During the Test

  • Continuously monitor the fetal heart rate tracing
  • Observe for fetal movement and note its correlation with FHR changes
  • Assess for any uterine activity or contractions on the TOCO tracing
  • Ensure maternal comfort and proper positioning throughout
  • Understand that the test may be extended to 40 minutes if the fetus appears to be in a sleep cycle
  • Report any concerning FHR patterns (decelerations, bradycardia) to the provider immediately

After the Test

  • Document whether the NST was reactive or nonreactive clearly in the chart
  • Report nonreassuring or nonreactive results promptly to the provider
  • Prepare the patient for possible further testing if the result is nonreactive (BPP, CST)
  • Reinforce instructions for fetal kick counts and when to call the provider
  • Provide patient education on follow-up schedule as ordered

✅ Priority Nursing Action After Nonreactive NST

Immediately notify the provider. Document the result. Prepare for possible further assessment — most commonly a biophysical profile (BPP). Reassure the patient while preparing for next steps.

⚡ NCLEX Pearls

🧠 High-Yield NCLEX Points

  • Reactive NST = GOOD — ≥2 accelerations of ≥15 bpm × ≥15 sec in 20 minutes — reassuring, no immediate action needed
  • Nonreactive NST = further evaluation needed — not automatically a crisis — fetus may simply be sleeping
  • NST is part of antepartum surveillance — it observes fetal response without stressing the fetus
  • NST is not the same as a labor contraction monitor — no contractions, no oxytocin
  • After nonreactive NST → most common next step = biophysical profile (BPP)
  • BPP scores ≥8 = reassuring; BPP ≤4 = abnormal, immediate action
  • Before 32 weeks: NST may be less reliable — a healthy preterm fetus may not show same reactivity
  • NST for decreased fetal movement is the most classic NCLEX indication
  • Fetus may need to be aroused with vibroacoustic stimulation if sleeping and nonreactive
  • Maternal position: semi-Fowler's with left lateral tilt to prevent aortocaval compression

⚠️ Classic NCLEX Trap

If the NST is nonreactive, the wrong answer is to immediately prepare for emergency delivery. The correct next step is further evaluation — extend monitoring, vibroacoustic stimulation, or a BPP — not jumping to delivery without more information.
