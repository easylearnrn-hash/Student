Naegele's Rule — Estimated Due Date (EDD)

Used to estimate due date based on Last Menstrual Period (LMP). Assumes 28-day cycle and 280-day pregnancy (40 weeks).

NCLEX Tips

  • LMP must be known; assumes 28-day cycle
  • Always double-check month lengths (September, April, June, November = 30 days)
  • Naegele's rule provides an estimate only, not exact date

Antepartum Care (Pregnancy Before Labor)

1st Trimester · 0–12 weeks
  • Organ development
  • Morning sickness, fatigue
  • Visits: every 4 weeks
2nd Trimester · 13–27 weeks
  • Baby movements = Quickening (~16–20 weeks)
  • Growing belly; less nausea
  • Visits: every 4 weeks (until 28)
3rd Trimester · 28–40+ weeks
  • Rapid fetal growth
  • Braxton Hicks contractions, swelling
  • Visits: q2w (28–36), then weekly

Danger Signs of Pregnancy — Seek Help Immediately!

  • Severe headache + blurred vision + high BP → Preeclampsia
  • Sudden severe swelling in hands/face → Preeclampsia
  • Bright red vaginal bleeding → Placental abruption or previa
  • Decreased fetal movement → Fetal distress
  • Severe abdominal pain → Possible miscarriage or preterm labor

Ectopic Pregnancy

Fertilized egg implants outside the uterus. Most common site: fallopian tube (95%). As embryo grows → tube ruptures → hemorrhage → shock.

Classic TriadOther SignsDiagnosis
  • Unilateral lower abdominal pain
  • Vaginal bleeding
  • Missed period
  • Shoulder tip pain (referred from internal bleeding)
  • Dizziness/fainting (rupture)
  • Hypotension, tachycardia
  • Shock (late — emergency)
  • Positive hCG (rises slowly vs normal)
  • Transvaginal US: no embryo in uterus
  • Serial hCG: doesn't double every 48 hrs
TreatmentWhen UsedKey NCLEX Notes
MethotrexateEarly, before rupture; stable patientAvoid folic acid, alcohol, NSAIDs; wait 3+ months before conceiving
Laparoscopic surgery / SalpingectomyRupture or risk of ruptureEmergency — monitor for hemorrhagic shock
RhoGAMIf mother is Rh-negativePrevent isoimmunization
🚨 DO NOT perform vaginal exam if ectopic is suspected — may cause rupture. Notify provider immediately. Monitor for signs of shock.

Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Severe, persistent nausea and vomiting in pregnancy leading to weight loss (>5% pre-pregnancy weight), dehydration, and electrolyte imbalances. More severe than normal morning sickness.

Can't keep fluids downNeeds IV rehydration

↑ HR, ↓ BP, orthostatic changesHypovolemia → IV fluids needed

Fetal Risks from HEG

  • Low birth weight, IUGR (intrauterine growth restriction)
  • Preterm birth
  • Electrolyte imbalances at birth (rare)
