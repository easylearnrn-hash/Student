FDA Pregnancy Drug Categories (Old System)

The FDA replaced letter categories (A/B/C/D/X) with narrative labeling in 2015, but NCLEX still tests the old system. Know the key ones.

CategoryMeaningExample
AAdequate studies show no fetal riskFolic acid, levothyroxine (low dose)
BAnimal studies show no risk; no adequate human studiesPenicillin, acetaminophen, insulin, metformin
CRisk cannot be ruled out; use only if benefits outweigh risksAspirin (low dose), fluconazole, codeine
DPositive evidence of fetal risk; use only in life-threatening situationsTetracycline, valproate, ACE inhibitors (2nd/3rd tri)
XContraindicated in pregnancy — risk clearly outweighs any benefitIsotretinoin (Accutane), warfarin, thalidomide, misoprostol

Memory: A → X Risk Scale

  • A & B = Generally safe
  • C = Caution — weigh risk vs benefit
  • D = Dangerous — use only if necessary
  • X = Absolutely contraindicated

MedicationUseSafetyKey Points

MedicationUseSafetyKey Points
Penicillin / Amoxicillin Bacterial infections B Commonly used; first-line for GBS prophylaxis in labor

🤢 Nausea & Vomiting

MedicationUseSafetyKey Points
Doxylamine + B6 (Bonjesta) Morning sickness Safe First-line pharmacological treatment for nausea in pregnancy
Ondansetron (Zofran) Nausea/vomiting (hyperemesis) B — use with caution Used when conservative measures fail; some 1st trimester cardiac concerns — use carefully

⚡ Obstetric-Specific Medications

MedicationUseSafetyKey Points
Magnesium Sulfate Seizure prophylaxis (preeclampsia), tocolysis Controlled use Monitor DTRs, urine output (≥25 mL/hr), resp rate (≥12); antidote = calcium gluconate
Betamethasone / Dexamethasone Fetal lung maturity (preterm labor) Used as needed Given IM to mother; administered 24–48h before anticipated preterm delivery (24–34 wks)

⚠️ Always Teach Patients

  • Do NOT take any OTC medication without provider approval
  • Avoid ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin) after 30 weeks — can close ductus arteriosus prematurely
  • Avoid alcohol — no safe amount in pregnancy
  • Avoid herbal supplements (St. John's Wort, black cohosh, blue cohosh) — can stimulate labor
  • Avoid high-dose Vitamin A (retinol) — teratogenic in large amounts

Oxytocin (Pitocin)Labor induction/augmentation; postpartum hemorrhage preventionMonitor uterine hyperstimulation (>5 ctx/10 min); stop infusion if fetal distress
Methylergonovine (Methergine)Postpartum hemorrhage (uterine atony) — persistent PPH after oxytocinCauses vasoconstriction → ↑BP; Contraindicated in hypertension/preeclampsia; given IM or PO postpartum

Vitamin K (Phytonadione)Newborn hemorrhagic disease preventionGiven IM to newborn within 1h of birth (0.5–1 mg)
Erythromycin Eye OintmentNeonatal ophthalmia prophylaxisRequired by law in most states; prevents gonococcal infection; apply within 1–2h of birth

NCLEX Priorities

  • Oxytocin hyperstimulation: Stop infusion, turn patient left lateral, give O₂, notify provider
  • Methylergonovine (Methergine): Second-line for PPH after oxytocin fails; causes vasoconstriction — never give in preeclampsia or HTN
  • Magnesium toxicity: Absent reflexes, respiratory rate <12, urine <25 mL/hr → stop infusion, give calcium gluconate
  • RhoGAM: Given to mother (not baby); must be Rh-negative; within 72h after delivery
  • Insulin in GDM: Does NOT cross placenta; oral hypoglycemics generally avoided (except metformin — some use)

Neonatal Priorities After Birth

🌡️ Thermoregulation

Newborns have a large body surface area relative to mass — they lose heat rapidly and cannot shiver to generate warmth.

InterventionPurpose
Skin-to-skin (kangaroo care)Most effective — maternal body heat; promotes bonding and breastfeeding
Dry and wrap immediately after birthRemoves amniotic fluid that causes evaporative heat loss
Knit cap on headHead = largest surface area; major source of heat loss
Warm blanketsConductive heat preservation
Radiant warmerUsed for at-risk/preterm newborns or during procedures

⚠️ Cold Stress Cascade

Hypothermia → increased oxygen consumption → hypoglycemia (burns glucose for heat) → respiratory distress. Always keep newborn warm — first priority after airway!

🤱 Breastfeeding Education

Teaching PointDetails
Initiate within 1st hour of birthPromotes bonding, initiates milk supply, provides colostrum (immune protection)
Feed every 2–3 hours8–12 times per day (on demand); watch for hunger cues (rooting, sucking fists)
Signs of a good latchWide open mouth, lower lip flanged out, nipple + most of areola in mouth, no pain (initial latch may be uncomfortable for a few seconds), audible swallowing
Signs of adequate intake≥6 wet diapers/day by Day 5; regaining birth weight by 2 weeks; regular yellow seedy stools
ColostrumFirst milk (Days 1–3) — thick, yellowish; high in antibodies (IgA) and nutrients; transitions to mature milk by Day 10
When to call providerFewer than 6 wet diapers by Day 5, significant jaundice, not regaining weight, excessive crying after feeds

🔁 Newborn Reflexes

ReflexHow to ElicitExpected ResponseDisappears By
Moro (Startle)Sudden noise or dropping sensationArms extend out then embrace (hug); may cry~4–6 months
RootingStroke cheek or corner of mouthBaby turns head toward stimulus, opens mouth~3–4 months
SuckingPlace finger or nipple in mouthRhythmic sucking motion~4 months (voluntary)
Palmar GraspPlace finger in palmBaby grips finger tightly~5–6 months
BabinskiStroke sole of foot from heel to toesToes fan out (dorsiflexion) — NORMAL in infants~12 months (up to 2 years)
SteppingHold upright, feet touch flat surfaceAlternating stepping movements~2 months

NCLEX Notes on Reflexes

  • Babinski positive in adults = abnormal (UMN lesion); in newborns = normal
  • Absent Moro reflex at birth → assess for neurological injury or drug effects
  • Rooting + sucking reflexes are essential for feeding — assess before 1st feed
  • All primitive reflexes disappear as cortical control develops
