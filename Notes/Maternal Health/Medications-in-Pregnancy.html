🤰 Medications in Pregnancy

Maternal Health · Safety, common drugs, and nursing considerations

FDA Pregnancy Drug Categories (Old System)

The FDA replaced letter categories (A/B/C/D/X) with narrative labeling in 2015, but NCLEX still tests the old system. Know the key ones.

CategoryMeaningExample
AAdequate studies show no fetal riskFolic acid, levothyroxine (low dose)
BAnimal studies show no risk; no adequate human studiesPenicillin, acetaminophen, insulin, metformin
CRisk cannot be ruled out; use only if benefits outweigh risksAspirin (low dose), fluconazole, codeine
DPositive evidence of fetal risk; use only in life-threatening situationsTetracycline, valproate, ACE inhibitors (2nd/3rd tri)
XContraindicated in pregnancy — risk clearly outweighs any benefitIsotretinoin (Accutane), warfarin, thalidomide, misoprostol

Memory: A → X Risk Scale

  • A & B = Generally safe
  • C = Caution — weigh risk vs benefit
  • D = Dangerous — use only if necessary
  • X = Absolutely contraindicated

Safe / Commonly Used Medications in Pregnancy

MedicationUseSafetyNotes
Folic Acid Neural tube defect prevention Safe (Cat A) 400–800 mcg/day; start BEFORE conception; 4 mg if prior neural tube defect
Iron Supplements Anemia prevention/treatment Safe Take with Vitamin C to enhance absorption; avoid dairy/antacids within 2h
Acetaminophen (Tylenol) Pain, fever Safe (Cat B) Preferred analgesic in pregnancy; do not exceed 3g/day
Insulin Diabetes management Safe (Cat B) Does NOT cross placenta; preferred over oral agents for GDM requiring medication
Penicillin / Amoxicillin Bacterial infections Safe (Cat B) Commonly used; first-line for GBS prophylaxis in labor
Metronidazole (Flagyl) Bacterial vaginosis, trichomoniasis Cat B (after 1st tri) Avoid in 1st trimester; patient should avoid alcohol during treatment
Ondansetron (Zofran) Nausea/vomiting (hyperemesis) Cat B (caution) Used when conservative measures fail; some 1st trimester cardiac concerns (use with care)
Doxylamine + B6 (Bonjesta) Morning sickness Safe First-line pharmacological treatment for nausea in pregnancy
Magnesium Sulfate Seizure prophylaxis (preeclampsia), tocolysis Controlled use Monitor reflexes, urine output, resp rate; antidote = calcium gluconate
Betamethasone / Dexamethasone Fetal lung maturity (preterm labor) Used as needed Administered IM to mother; given 24–48h before anticipated preterm delivery (24–34 wks)

⚠️ Always Teach Patients

  • Do NOT take any OTC medication without provider approval
  • Avoid ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin) after 30 weeks — can close ductus arteriosus prematurely
  • Avoid alcohol — no safe amount in pregnancy
  • Avoid herbal supplements (St. John's Wort, black cohosh, blue cohosh) — can stimulate labor
  • Avoid high-dose Vitamin A (retinol) — teratogenic in large amounts

Key Labor & Delivery Medications

MedicationPurposeKey Nursing Points
Oxytocin (Pitocin)Labor induction/augmentation; postpartum hemorrhage preventionMonitor uterine hyperstimulation (>5 ctx/10 min); stop infusion if fetal distress
Misoprostol (Cytotec)Cervical ripening; labor inductionDo NOT use with prior uterine scar (c-section risk); can cause uterine hyperstimulation
Dinoprostone (Cervidil)Cervical ripeningInsert intravaginally; remove if uterine hyperstimulation; patient must lie flat 2h after
TerbutalineTocolysis (stop preterm labor), relieve uterine hyperstimulationMonitor maternal HR (tachycardia); fetal HR; not for long-term tocolysis (>48–72h)
Epidural AnesthesiaLabor pain reliefMonitor BP (hypotension risk); ensure IV access; assess bladder; monitor FHR
Vitamin K (Phytonadione)Newborn hemorrhagic disease preventionGiven IM to newborn within 1h of birth (0.5–1 mg)
Erythromycin Eye OintmentNeonatal ophthalmia prophylaxisRequired by law in most states; prevents gonococcal infection; apply within 1–2h of birth
RhoGAM (Rho(D) Immune Globulin)Rh sensitization preventionGive to Rh-negative mother at 28 weeks AND within 72h of birth if baby is Rh+; also after miscarriage/amniocentesis

NCLEX Priorities

  • Oxytocin hyperstimulation: Stop infusion, turn patient left lateral, give O₂, notify provider
  • Magnesium toxicity: Absent reflexes, respiratory rate <12, urine <25 mL/hr → stop infusion, give calcium gluconate
  • RhoGAM: Given to mother (not baby); must be Rh-negative; within 72h after delivery
  • Insulin in GDM: Does NOT cross placenta; oral hypoglycemics generally avoided (except metformin — some use)
