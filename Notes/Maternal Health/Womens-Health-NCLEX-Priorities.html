Breast Health & Cancer Screening

TopicKey Points
Breast Self-Exam (BSE) Monthly, 7–10 days after period starts; lying down and in shower; report any lumps, dimpling, nipple discharge, or skin changes
Fibroadenoma Most common benign breast tumor in young women; smooth, mobile, firm, non-tender; NOT cancerous
Fibrocystic Changes Lumpy, tender breasts that worsen before period; benign; treat with NSAIDs, supportive bra, reduce caffeine
Breast Cancer Warning Signs Hard fixed lump, skin dimpling ("orange peel" — peau d'orange), nipple inversion, bloody nipple discharge, lymphadenopathy
NCLEX Priority: A hard, fixed, non-tender lump in the breast = suspect malignancy → refer for mammogram/biopsy. Tender lumps that fluctuate with cycle are more likely benign.

Cervical Cancer & Pap Smear Guidelines

AgeScreening
<21 yearsNo Pap smear (regardless of sexual activity)

30–65 yearsPap + HPV co-test every 5 years, OR Pap alone every 3 years
>65 yearsStop screening if adequate prior negative results and not high risk
Post-hysterectomyStop if no cervix and no history of cervical cancer/precancerous lesions

HPV Vaccine (Gardasil)

  • Recommended ages 9–26 (ideally before sexual activity begins)
  • Can be given up to age 45 with shared clinical decision-making
  • Does NOT replace Pap smear screening
  • Protects against HPV strains 6, 11, 16, 18 (and more in 9-valent)

STI Quick-Reference Priorities

STIPresentationTreatmentNCLEX Key
Chlamydia Often asymptomatic; mucopurulent discharge, dysuria Azithromycin or Doxycycline Most common reportable STI; major cause of PID and infertility; screen all sexually active women <25 annually
Gonorrhea Purulent urethral/vaginal discharge, dysuria; may be asymptomatic Ceftriaxone IM (single dose) Report to public health; causes PID, infertility, neonatal ophthalmia; treat partner
Syphilis Primary: painless chancre. Secondary: diffuse rash (palms/soles). Tertiary: cardiovascular/neuro damage Penicillin G (drug of choice) Can cross placenta → congenital syphilis (Hutchinson's teeth, saddle nose, saber shins); test at 1st prenatal visit
Herpes (HSV-2) Painful vesicles/ulcers on genitals; prodrome of tingling/burning; recurrent Acyclovir (suppresses outbreaks; does NOT cure) Active outbreak at delivery → C-section to prevent neonatal herpes; lifelong viral shedding
HPV Genital warts (condyloma acuminata); may be asymptomatic Topical (podofilox, imiquimod); cryotherapy Strains 16, 18 → cervical cancer; 6, 11 → warts; no cure — manage outbreaks; vaccine prevents
Trichomonas Frothy, yellow-green, foul-smelling vaginal discharge; pruritus; "strawberry cervix" Metronidazole (both partners) Most common curable STI worldwide; partner MUST be treated

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Ascending infection from lower genital tract (usually chlamydia or gonorrhea) → uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, and peritoneum.

FeatureDetails
Classic PresentationLower abdominal/pelvic pain, fever, mucopurulent cervical discharge, cervical motion tenderness (CMT) — "chandelier sign" (patient jumps when cervix is moved)
ComplicationsTubo-ovarian abscess (TOA), infertility (scarring), ectopic pregnancy risk, chronic pelvic pain
TreatmentAntibiotics (ceftriaxone + doxycycline ± metronidazole); hospitalize if severe, pregnant, or TOA suspected
PreventionTreat STIs early; use barrier contraception; screen sexually active women

Contraception NCLEX Priorities

MethodKey Teaching Points
Combined OCPTake at same time daily; no smoking (↑ DVT risk); contraindicated: migraines with aura, smokers >35, history of DVT/PE, breast cancer
Progestin-only "Mini-Pill"Safe for breastfeeding moms and those who can't use estrogen; must take at exact same time daily
IUD — Copper (Paragard)Non-hormonal; lasts 10–12 years; also most effective emergency contraception if inserted within 5 days; heavier periods
IUD — Hormonal (Mirena/Kyleena)Lightest periods or amenorrhea; lasts 3–8 years depending on brand
Plan B (Levonorgestrel)Emergency contraception; most effective within 72h of unprotected sex (up to 120h); NOT an abortifacient — prevents ovulation/fertilization
DiaphragmUse with spermicide; insert <6h before intercourse; leave in ≥6h after; refitted after pregnancy, weight change >10 lbs
Natural Family PlanningAvoid sex during fertile window (Days 10–17); least reliable; requires regular cycles

Top NCLEX Priority Statements

  • OCP + smoking after age 35 = high DVT/stroke risk — do not combine
  • Copper IUD is the most effective emergency contraception overall
  • Plan B prevents pregnancy — it does NOT terminate an existing pregnancy
  • Diaphragm + no spermicide = ineffective — always pair with spermicide
  • Report signs of DVT/PE with OCP use: leg swelling, chest pain, shortness of breath, blurred vision (ACHES mnemonic)

Contraceptive Side Effects — NCLEX Details

MethodSide Effects / Warnings
Depo-Provera (IM q3 months)⚠️ Bone density loss — teach to take calcium + vitamin D; irregular bleeding or amenorrhea; fertility may take up to 1 year to return after stopping
CondomsPrevent both STIs AND pregnancy; ⚠️ use water-based lubricants only — oil-based can break condom latex
Plan B (Emergency Contraception)Must take within 72 hours of unprotected sex (effective up to 120 hrs); ⚠️ may cause nausea, irregular bleeding; NOT for regular use
OCP (Combination)Nausea, breast tenderness, spotting (first 3 months); ↑ DVT/stroke risk with smoking; ⚠️ ACHES signs = stop immediately

🛏️ Post-Op Care — Hysterectomy & Mastectomy

ProcedureNCLEX Priorities
Hysterectomy (removal of uterus)
  • Monitor bleeding (pad count)
  • No heavy lifting for 6 weeks
  • Urinary catheter may be used short-term
  • 🧠 Watch for DVT/PE risk — early ambulation, compression stockings
  • Surgical menopause if ovaries removed (hot flashes, mood changes)
Mastectomy (breast removal)
  • No BP, IV, or lab draws on affected arm (lymphedema risk)
  • Elevate arm to reduce swelling (lymphedema prevention)
  • Teach gentle ROM exercises for arm and shoulder
  • Watch for infection at surgical site
  • 🧠 Emotional support is just as important — assess for grief, body image issues, refer to support groups

Prenatal careEarly and consistent care = healthier outcomes; teach nutrition, danger signs
