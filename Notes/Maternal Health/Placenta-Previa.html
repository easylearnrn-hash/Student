Placenta Previa

Maternal Health · Types, risk factors, nursing management

UterusRigid, "board-like"

🔑 KEY RULE — Memorize This!

Bleeding + Pain → Abruption
Bleeding WITHOUT Pain → Previa

Placenta Previa — Detail

Placenta partially or completely covers the internal cervical os. As the cervix dilates or effaces, placental vessels tear → painless, bright red vaginal bleeding.

Types

TypeDescriptionDelivery
Total (Complete)Completely covers cervical osAlways C-section
PartialPartially covers cervical osC-section (usually)
MarginalPlacental edge at the osMay allow vaginal (monitored closely)
Low-LyingWithin 2 cm of os but not coveringMay deliver vaginally with monitoring

Risk Factors

  • Prior C-section or uterine surgery (scarring → altered implantation)
  • Multiple pregnancies (twins, triplets)
  • Advanced maternal age (>35)
  • Smoking or cocaine use
  • Previous placenta previa
  • Multiparity (many prior pregnancies)
  • Large placenta (multiple gestation)

🚨 Classic Signs & Symptoms

SymptomWhat It Suggests
Painless bright red bleedingCardinal sign of placenta previa — appears after 20 weeks
Soft, non-tender uterusBleeding without contractions
Normal fetal heart tones (initially)Fetal distress may not be present early on
Bleeding may stop, then recurEach episode can worsen — monitor closely

🧪 Diagnosis

  • Confirmed by transabdominal ultrasound (non-invasive imaging) — gold standard
  • NEVER perform a vaginal exam if placenta previa is suspected — can rupture placenta → massive hemorrhage
❗ NCLEX DANGER: Vaginal exam = NO ❌ — Always wait for ultrasound confirmation before any internal exam.

🏥 Nursing Management

Do ✅Don't ❌
Monitor bleeding (pad counts)❌ No vaginal exams
Assess fetal heart rate (FHR)❌ No internal fetal monitoring
Establish IV access, type & crossmatch for blood❌ No intercourse or tampons (pelvic rest)
Prepare for possible C-section❌ Don't delay emergency care if bleeding worsens
Administer corticosteroids (e.g. betamethasone) if <34 weeks❌ No oxytocin without delivery plan
Provide emotional support
Monitor for signs of shock (↓BP, ↑HR, pallor)
⚠️ Highest Priority Alert: If a patient with placenta previa has vaginal bleeding — do NOT perform a vaginal exam. Prepare IV, monitor FHR, and notify the provider immediately.

📘 Key Terminology

TermDefinition
Placenta previaPlacenta covers or is near the cervix
Bright red bleedingIndicates vaginal bleeding from placenta previa (painless)
C-sectionCommon delivery method in complete previa
Pelvic restNo vaginal penetration of any kind (no sex, no exams, no tampons)
Corticosteroids (betamethasone)Meds to mature fetal lungs if early delivery is needed
Transabdominal ultrasoundNon-invasive test to confirm placental location — always first step

✅ Final Must-Know for NCLEX

Top 4 Things to Remember

  • Painless bright red bleeding after 20 weeks = Think placenta previa
  • No vaginal exam if unknown cause of bleeding or suspected previa
  • C-section is almost always required in complete previa
  • Monitor bleeding, FHR, and prepare for delivery
