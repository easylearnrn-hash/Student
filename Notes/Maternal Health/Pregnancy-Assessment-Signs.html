🤰 Pregnancy Assessment Signs

Presumptive, Probable, and Positive signs of pregnancy

← Back to Notes

Three Categories of Pregnancy Signs

CategoryWho Detects ItCertaintyExamples
Presumptive Felt/reported by the woman (subjective) Least certain — can be caused by other conditions Amenorrhea, nausea, breast tenderness, quickening, fatigue, urinary frequency
Direct confirmation Definitive — no other cause possible Fetal heartbeat by provider, ultrasound visualization, provider palpating fetal movement
NCLEX Rule: Only positive signs confirm pregnancy. A pregnancy test is a probable sign — it can be false positive. Only fetal heartbeat/ultrasound/provider-felt movement = definitive.

Presumptive Signs (Reported by Woman)

Amenorrhea
Presumptive

Absence of menstrual period; often the first sign noticed. Can also be caused by stress, illness, hormonal disorders.

Nausea & Vomiting (Morning Sickness)
Presumptive

Begins ~4–6 weeks; often resolves by end of 1st trimester. Caused by rising β-hCG. Can occur any time of day despite name.

Breast Changes
Presumptive

Tenderness, tingling, fullness, darkening of areola, Montgomery's tubercles enlarge; begins very early.

Quickening
Presumptive

First fetal movement felt by the mother — "fluttering" or bubbling sensation. Primigravida: ~18–20 weeks. Multigravida: ~16 weeks. Perceived only by the mother.

Urinary Frequency
Presumptive

Pressure of enlarging uterus on bladder in 1st trimester; returns in 3rd trimester when baby drops.

Fatigue
Presumptive

Extreme tiredness in 1st trimester due to rising progesterone and metabolic demands.

Linea Nigra / Chloasma / Striae
Presumptive

Linea nigra = dark line from navel to pubis. Chloasma = "mask of pregnancy" (darkened facial patches). Striae = stretch marks. All from hormonal pigmentation changes.

Probable Signs (Observed by Examiner)

Goodell's Sign
Probable

Softening of the cervix (normally feels like tip of nose; in pregnancy, feels like lips). Occurs around 6–8 weeks.

Hegar's Sign
Probable

Softening of the lower uterine segment (isthmus — just above the cervix). Detected on bimanual exam. Occurs 6–12 weeks.

Chadwick's Sign
Probable

Bluish-purple discoloration of cervix, vagina, and vulva due to increased vascular congestion. One of earliest signs — occurs 6–8 weeks.

Ballottement
Probable

Examiner taps cervix upward → fetus floats up then drops back (rebound). Assessed via vaginal exam. Occurs 16–20 weeks.

Palmer's Sign
Probable

Irregular, painless uterine contractions felt by examiner during bimanual exam (Braxton Hicks begin early). Noted in early pregnancy.

Positive Pregnancy Test (β-hCG)
Probable

Urine or serum hCG positive. Classified as probable (not positive) because false positives can occur with certain tumors (gestational trophoblastic disease, choriocarcinoma).

Uterine Enlargement
Probable

Palpable uterus above symphysis pubis by ~12 weeks. Fundal height measurement (McDonald's rule) corresponds to gestational age. Can also occur with fibroids.

Positive Signs (Definitive Confirmation)

Fetal Heartbeat — Heard by Provider
Positive ✅

Doppler ultrasound: detected as early as 10–12 weeks. Fetoscope (non-electronic): ~18–20 weeks. Normal FHR: 110–160 bpm.

Fetal Movement — Felt by Examiner
Positive ✅

The provider can objectively feel or palpate fetal movement on abdominal exam. Distinct from quickening (which is the mother's subjective perception).

Visualization of Fetus on Ultrasound
Positive ✅

Direct visualization of gestational sac, embryo, or fetus by ultrasound. Transvaginal ultrasound can detect gestational sac as early as 4.5–5 weeks.

The Rule

  • Presumptive = Reported by woman — cannot be confirmed by exam
  • Probable = Detected by provider — but could have another cause
  • Positive = Direct fetal evidence — cannot be any other cause
  • Pregnancy test = probable (tumors produce hCG too!)
  • Fetal heartbeat = positive (only a live fetus has a heartbeat)
