Maternal Health

🤢 Hyperemesis Gravidarum

Severe nausea and vomiting of pregnancy

← Back to Notes

Definition & Overview

Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HG) is intractable nausea and vomiting in pregnancy beyond normal morning sickness, leading to dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, nutritional deficiency, and weight loss of ≥5% of pre-pregnancy body weight.

Normal Morning Sickness (NVP)

  • Nausea ± vomiting in 1st trimester
  • Usually resolves by 12–14 weeks
  • Patient able to keep some food/fluid down
  • No significant weight loss
  • No ketonuria or electrolyte disturbance
  • Managed with diet/lifestyle changes
  • Ketonuria, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance
  • Requires IV fluids / hospitalization

    • Key Fact

    HG is associated with high levels of β-hCG — occurs more commonly with multiple gestation (twins) and hydatidiform mole (molar pregnancy). Increased β-hCG = increased stimulation of vomiting centers.

    Pathophysiology

    Exact cause is multifactorial:

    • Rising β-hCG: Stimulates vomiting center; peaks 8–10 weeks (parallels HG peak)
    • Estrogen: Also associated with nausea; increases gastric transit time
    • Gastric motility changes: Progesterone relaxes GI smooth muscle → delayed gastric emptying
    • Thyroid stimulation: β-hCG has mild TSH-like action → transient hyperthyroidism can occur (gestational thyrotoxicosis)
    • Psychological/autonomic factors: Stress, anxiety can worsen severity

    Assessment / Diagnostic Criteria

    Diagnosis is clinical. Labs confirm severity and rule out other causes (UTI, appendicitis, cholecystitis).

    Assessment FindingSignificance
    Weight loss ≥5% of pre-pregnancy weightKey diagnostic criterion for HG
    Ketonuria (urine dipstick)Body catabolizing fat → ketones → dehydration
    Dehydration signsDry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, concentrated urine
    Hyponatremia, hypokalemia, hypochloremiaElectrolytes lost through vomiting
    Elevated hematocrit (hemoconcentration)Reflects dehydration
    Elevated urine specific gravity (>1.025)Concentrated urine from dehydration
    Metabolic alkalosisLoss of HCl from vomiting
    Elevated LFTs, mild jaundiceSevere/prolonged cases

    ⚠️ Wernicke's Encephalopathy Risk

    Prolonged HG → Thiamine (Vitamin B1) deficiency → Wernicke's encephalopathy. Classic triad: confusion, ophthalmoplegia (eye movement problems), ataxia. Give IV thiamine BEFORE IV glucose in malnourished pregnant patients.

    Treatment: Step-Up Approach

    1. Dietary modifications first — Small, frequent meals; dry crackers before getting up; avoid triggers; bland foods; ginger (safe); cold foods (less odor)
    2. Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine) ± Doxylamine — First-line pharmacologic. Doxylamine is safe in pregnancy. Often given together (Diclegis/Bonjesta).
    3. Antiemetics — Promethazine (Phenergan), Metoclopramide (Reglan), Ondansetron (Zofran). Note: Zofran use in 1st trimester is debated (possible cleft palate risk at high doses).
    4. IV Fluid Replacement — Normal saline or Lactated Ringer's for rehydration. Add potassium for hypokalemia. Correct electrolyte imbalances.
    5. IV Thiamine (Vitamin B1) — Always give before IV dextrose in patients with prolonged vomiting and malnutrition.
    6. Hospitalization — For severe dehydration, inability to tolerate oral antiemetics, significant weight loss, electrolyte imbalance.
    7. Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) / NG tube feeding — Last resort for refractory HG when all else fails. Nasogastric feedings may be tried before TPN.

    Nursing Care Priorities

    PriorityActionRationale
    1 — Fluid/electrolyte balanceI&O, daily weights, monitor electrolytes, skin turgor, mucous membranesCorrect dehydration and prevent imbalances
    2 — NutritionAdvance diet slowly; start with clear liquids → ice chips → crackers → small bland mealsPrevent further weight loss while reducing vomiting triggers
    3 — AntiemeticsAdminister as prescribed, evaluate effectivenessBreak the nausea-vomiting cycle
    4 — Fetal monitoringMonitor FHR, growth scans as orderedEnsure adequate fetal perfusion and nutrition
    5 — Psychological supportAcknowledge distress; provide information; involve familyHG can cause anxiety, isolation, fear of harming baby
    6 — EnvironmentQuiet, odor-free room; limit visitors; dim lighting; small meals coveredReduce stimuli that trigger nausea

    NCLEX Priorities for HG

    • Check urine for ketones → indicator of inadequate nutrition
    • Daily weight → best objective measure of fluid balance
    • Give IV thiamine BEFORE IV dextrose (Wernicke's prevention)
    • Monitor for hypokalemia (muscle weakness, cardiac dysrhythmias)
    • Reassure patient — HG does NOT mean the baby is harmed; outcomes usually good
    • HG may be associated with higher β-hCG → rule out molar pregnancy
