Placenta Previa & Placental Abruption

Maternal Health · OB bleeding emergencies, nursing priorities, DIC risk

Head-to-Head Comparison

🌺 Placenta Previa

Placenta implants over or near the cervix

FeaturePrevia
PainNO PAIN — Painless
BleedingBright red, visible
UterusSoft, relaxed
FHRUsually normal initially
Vaginal Exam❌ CONTRAINDICATED
DeliveryScheduled C-section (complete previa)
🌪 Placental Abruption

Placenta separates EARLY from uterine wall

FeatureAbruption
PainYES — Sudden, sharp
BleedingDark red; may be concealed
UterusRigid, tender, board-like
FHRDistress or absent
Vaginal Exam❌ CONTRAINDICATED
DeliveryOften emergency C-section

KEY Rule

Bleeding + Pain = Abruption | Bleeding WITHOUT Pain = Previa

Placenta Previa — Detail

TypeWhat It Means
Complete/Total previaPlacenta completely covers the cervix
Partial previaPlacenta partially covers the cervix
Marginal previaPlacenta is close to, but not covering, the cervix
Low-lyingPlacenta in lower uterus, not at cervix

Risk Factors

  • Previous placenta previa or prior cesarean birth (scar tissue alters implantation)
  • Multiple gestation (twins, triplets)
  • Multiparity, smoking, advanced maternal age

Nursing Management

Do ✅Do NOT ❌
  • Monitor bleeding (pad counts)
  • Assess fetal heart rate (FHR)
  • IV access (in case of emergency blood loss)
  • Corticosteroids (betamethasone) for fetal lung maturity if preterm
  • Emotional support; pelvic rest education
  • Prepare for possible C-section
  • No vaginal exams! (can rupture placenta)
  • No internal fetal monitoring
  • No intercourse or tampons (pelvic rest)
  • Do not delay emergency care if bleeding worsens
🚨 NCLEX: Painless, bright red bleeding after 20 weeks = Placenta Previa until proven otherwise. Diagnosis = transabdominal ultrasound (non-invasive).

Placental Abruption — Detail

TypeDescription
PartialOnly part of placenta detaches
CompleteEntire placenta separates — very dangerous
ConcealedBleeding trapped inside uterus — no visible bleeding (higher risk, harder to detect)
RevealedBleeding exits cervix and is visible

Risk Factors (NCLEX Favorites)

  • Trauma to abdomen (car crash, fall)
  • Hypertension or preeclampsia
  • Cocaine or smoking use
  • Previous abruption
  • Sudden loss of amniotic fluid (rupture of membranes)
  • Multiparity, advanced maternal age

THINK FAST Mnemonic

F
Firm uterus
A
Absent or abnormal FHR
S
Sudden pain with bleeding
T
Treatment = emergency delivery

Nursing Priorities

Do ✅Do NOT ❌
  • Monitor FHR closely
  • Left lateral position to improve blood flow
  • O₂ via mask (8–10 L/min)
  • IV fluids + type and cross for blood
  • Prepare for emergency C-section
  • Monitor for DIC (clotting disorder)
  • Provide emotional support
  • No vaginal exams
  • No fundal massage if uterus is firm/tender
  • Do not delay treatment — urgent!

DIC Risk — Watch For!

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation can be triggered by severe abruption. Signs: bruising, bleeding gums, petechiae, low platelets. Treat: blood products, address underlying cause.
