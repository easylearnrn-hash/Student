What Is the APGAR Score?

The APGAR score is a rapid assessment tool used to evaluate a newborn's condition at 1 minute and 5 minutes after birth. Each of 5 criteria is scored 0–2, for a maximum score of 10.

Mnemonic: A-P-G-A-R

A
Appearance — Skin color (cyanosis)
P
Pulse — Heart rate
G
Grimace — Reflex irritability (response to stimulus)
A
Activity — Muscle tone
R
Respirations — Breathing effort

Key Points

  • Score is assessed at 1 minute (reflects birth transition) and 5 minutes (reflects response to interventions)
  • If 5-min score <7, reassess every 5 min up to 20 minutes
  • APGAR does not predict long-term outcome — it guides immediate resuscitation
  • Acrocyanosis (blue hands/feet) in a term newborn at 1 min is normal — scores a 1 for Appearance
  • A score of 10 is rare — most healthy newborns score 7–9 due to acrocyanosis

⚠️ NCLEX Priority: A 1-minute APGAR of 6 — nurse's first action is to stimulate the newborn (dry, warm, rub back) and provide free-flow O₂, then reassess at 5 minutes.

Nursing Interventions by Score

ScoreIntervention
7–10Routine care: dry, warm, position, promote skin-to-skin
4–6Stimulate (rub back, flick soles), provide free-flow O₂, reposition airway, reassess
0–3Immediate resuscitation: PPV (positive pressure ventilation), chest compressions if HR <60 despite PPV, epinephrine if HR <60 after compressions

NCLEX Memory Tips

  • Appearance = Acrocyanosis is ok at 1 min (score 1, not 0)
  • Pulse: ≥100 = safe; <100 = concern; absent = emergency
  • Grimace = Gag-like response to suction catheter stimulus
  • Activity: Active flexion = healthy tone; flaccid = neurological concern
  • Respirations: Strong cry = 2; weak/irregular = 1; none = 0 → resuscitate
  • APGAR is a snapshot, not a predictor of long-term neurological outcome
