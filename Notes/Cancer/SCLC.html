Oncology · Respiratory

Small Cell Lung Cancer
(SCLC)

NCLEX-focused study note — pathophysiology · paraneoplastic syndromes · staging · treatment

Overview

Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC) is a highly aggressive form of lung cancer that originates in the bronchi (main airways). It is strongly associated with smoking (95% of cases) and is characterized by rapid growth and early metastasis — most patients already have metastatic disease at diagnosis.

Common Metastatic Sites

Most Common Metastatic Sites
  • Brain — neurological symptoms, personality changes, headache
  • Liver — jaundice, RUQ pain, elevated LFTs
  • Bones — bone pain, pathologic fractures
  • Adrenal glands — adrenal insufficiency, hormonal effects
Risk Factors
  • Smoking — #1 cause (95%)
  • Radon gas exposure
  • Asbestos exposure
  • Family history of lung cancer
  • Secondhand smoke

Clinical Symptoms

  • Persistent cough — new or changed in a smoker
  • Hemoptysis — coughing up blood (bright red sputum)
  • Dyspnea — shortness of breath
  • Chest pain — from direct invasion or pleural involvement
  • Weight loss and fatigue — systemic effects
  • Wheezing or stridor — from airway obstruction
  • Symptoms of metastasis: headache (brain), bone pain, jaundice (liver)

Paraneoplastic Syndromes Extremely High-Yield NCLEX

SCLC produces ectopic hormones — hormones made by the tumor itself rather than the normal gland. These cause paraneoplastic syndromes — systemic effects distant from the tumor.

1 · SIADH

Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH

  • Tumor secretes excess ADH
  • ↑ Water retention → dilutional hyponatremia
  • Symptoms: confusion, headache, seizures, lethargy
  • Monitor Na levels — treat with fluid restriction
2 · Cushing's Syndrome (Ectopic ACTH)

Ectopic ACTH Production

  • Tumor secretes excess ACTH → adrenal cortisol overproduction
  • Symptoms: moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity, hyperglycemia, hypertension, muscle weakness, hypokalemia
  • Monitor glucose and potassium
3 · Lambert-Eaton Syndrome (LEMS)

Autoimmune Neuromuscular Disorder

  • Antibodies against voltage-gated calcium channels at neuromuscular junction
  • Proximal muscle weakness — gets better with repeated movement (opposite of Myasthenia Gravis)
  • Dry mouth, autonomic dysfunction
  • Treat SCLC → LEMS may improve
💡
NCLEX Differentiator — LEMS vs Myasthenia Gravis

LEMS: Weakness IMPROVES with repeated activity (calcium accumulates at NMJ)
Myasthenia Gravis (MG): Weakness WORSENS with repeated activity (acetylcholine receptors depleted)

SCLC Staging — Limited vs Extensive

Limited Stage SCLC
  • Cancer confined to one lung + nearby regional lymph nodes
  • Can be encompassed in a single radiation field
  • Less common at diagnosis (~30%)
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy (cisplatin + etoposide) + concurrent chest radiation + PCI
  • Better prognosis than extensive
Extensive Stage SCLC
  • Cancer has spread beyond one lung — opposite lung, distant organs (brain, liver, bones, adrenals)
  • Most common at diagnosis (~70%)
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy (cisplatin/carboplatin + etoposide) ± immunotherapy (atezolizumab or durvalumab) + PCI
  • Poorer prognosis

Diagnostics

Treatment

Chemotherapy — First-Line

Standard Regimen
Cisplatin + Etoposide — most common combination
(Carboplatin used instead of cisplatin in patients with renal disease — equally effective, less nephrotoxic)
Immunotherapy (Extensive Stage)
Atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1) or Durvalumab — added to chemotherapy for extensive-stage SCLC; improves overall survival

Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation (PCI) High-Yield

🧠
PCI — Prevents Brain Metastases

PCI = Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation — low-dose radiation to the entire brain, given to patients who respond to initial chemotherapy.
Purpose: prevent brain metastases (SCLC spreads to brain very early).
Side effects: fatigue, memory impairment, cognitive changes.

Surgery

Surgery is rarely used for SCLC — because most patients present with extensive metastatic disease. Exception: very rare very early limited-stage solitary nodule.

Nursing Priorities

🌬️
Breathing — Position and O₂

High-Fowler's position (60–90°) to maximize lung expansion. Monitor SpO₂. Supplemental O₂ as needed. Teach pursed-lip breathing.

🧂
SIADH — Monitor Sodium

Serum sodium closely — confusion or seizures may indicate severe hyponatremia. Implement fluid restriction. Report Na <125 mEq/L immediately. Do NOT give free water.

🍬
Ectopic Cushing's — Monitor Glucose & Potassium

Check blood glucose frequently (risk for hyperglycemia). Monitor K⁺ (risk for hypokalemia from cortisol). Assess for moon face, HTN, muscle weakness.

💊
Chemotherapy Nursing

Administer antiemetic (ondansetron) before chemotherapy. Monitor CBC — neutropenia nadir 7–14 days post-chemo. Neutropenic precautions if ANC <500. Oral care for mucositis.
