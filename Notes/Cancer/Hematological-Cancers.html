Oncology · Hematology

Hematological Cancers
Leukemia · Lymphoma · Multiple Myeloma

NCLEX-focused study note — types, risk factors, symptoms, diagnostics, treatment, and nursing priorities

Leukemia

Cancer of WBCs originating in the bone marrow. Abnormal WBCs crowd out normal blood cells → infections, bleeding, anemia.

Risk Factors

  • Radiation exposure (including prior radiation therapy)
  • Prior chemotherapy (especially alkylating agents)
  • Benzene exposure (chemical carcinogen)
  • Down syndrome (trisomy 21) — increased ALL/AML risk
  • Viral: HTLV-1 (T-cell leukemia), EBV

Types of Leukemia

TypeFull NameWho Gets ItSpeedKey Feature
ALL Acute Lymphocytic Leukemia Most common in children Rapid B-cell or T-cell; excellent prognosis in children
AML Acute Myelogenous Leukemia Adults (most common acute leukemia in adults) Rapid — emergency Auer rods on smear; aggressive — immediate treatment needed
CLL Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia Elderly (age >60) Slow Often discovered incidentally; most common adult leukemia overall
CML Chronic Myelogenous Leukemia Middle-aged adults Slow → blast crisis Philadelphia chromosome (BCR-ABL fusion); treated with imatinib

Symptoms (All Leukemias)

↓ WBCs / Infection
Fever, frequent infections, poor wound healing
↓ RBCs / Anemia
Fatigue, pallor, dyspnea on exertion, weakness
↓ Platelets / Bleeding
Easy bruising, petechiae, prolonged bleeding, gum bleeds

Also: bone pain (marrow expansion), night sweats, weight loss, lymphadenopathy

Lymphoma

Cancer of the lymphatic system — affects lymphocytes. Presents as painless lymph node enlargement.

Risk Factors

  • Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV)
  • HIV/immunosuppression
  • Age >60 (NHL) or bimodal 15–35 + >60 (Hodgkin's)
  • Prior organ transplant (immunosuppressive drugs)

Hodgkin's vs Non-Hodgkin's

Hodgkin's Lymphoma (HL)
  • Hallmark: Reed-Sternberg cells ("owl-eye" giant cells)
  • Bimodal age peak: young adults (15–35) AND elderly (>60)
  • Orderly spread — usually starts in cervical LN, spreads contiguously
  • Better prognosis overall; highly curable with ABVD chemo
Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma (NHL)
  • No Reed-Sternberg cells
  • More common than HL; diverse subtypes
  • Non-contiguous spread — skip pattern
  • Generally worse prognosis; treated with CHOP + rituximab

B Symptoms (Both HL & NHL) — High-Yield NCLEX

  • 🌡️ Fever — unexplained
  • 💦 Night sweats — drenching, recurrent
  • ⚖️ Weight loss — >10% body weight in 6 months
  • 🔴 Pruritus — without visible rash (paraneoplastic phenomenon)

B symptoms = systemic disease involvement → worse prognosis (Stage IIIB/IVB vs A)

Multiple Myeloma

Cancer of plasma cells (antibody-producing cells) in the bone marrow. Leads to accumulation of abnormal immunoglobulins (M-proteins) → organ damage.

Risk Factors

  • Radiation, pesticide exposure, chronic infections
  • African American — highest risk group
  • Age >65 (rare before 40)
  • Male sex

CRAB Criteria — Classic Features Must-Know NCLEX Mnemonic

C
Calcium elevated — hypercalcemia → nausea, constipation, confusion, kidney stones
R
Renal failure — ↑BUN, ↑creatinine; Bence-Jones proteins damage tubules
A
Anemia — bone marrow crowded with plasma cells → ↓RBC production
B
Bone lesions — osteolytic (punched-out) lesions; pathologic fractures; osteoporosis

Treatment

Leukemia Treatment

  1. Induction: Intensive chemotherapy to achieve remission (eliminate visible disease)
  2. Consolidation: Prevent relapse in remission
  3. Maintenance: Long-term low-intensity chemo (especially ALL)
  4. Stem cell transplant: For high-risk or relapsed disease; allogeneic preferred
  5. Targeted therapy: Imatinib (Gleevec) — BCR-ABL inhibitor for CML; highly effective

Lymphoma Treatment

  • Hodgkin's: ABVD regimen (doxorubicin, bleomycin, vinblastine, dacarbazine) + radiation
  • Non-Hodgkin's: CHOP regimen + rituximab (anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody)

Multiple Myeloma Treatment

  • Bortezomib (Velcade) — proteasome inhibitor; disrupts plasma cell survival
  • Lenalidomide — immunomodulatory
  • High-dose chemo + autologous stem cell transplant — for eligible patients
  • Bisphosphonates (e.g., zoledronic acid) — prevent pathologic fractures
  • Supportive: EPO for anemia, dialysis for renal failure, calcium management

Stem Cell Transplant Types

Autologous
Patient's own stem cells collected and reinfused. Lower rejection risk but no graft-vs-tumor effect.
Allogeneic
Donor stem cells. Higher cure potential but risk of GVHD (Graft-vs-Host Disease): rash, liver damage, GI involvement.

Nursing Priorities

🚨
Neutropenia — Priority #1

ANC <500 = severe neutropenia. Strict hand hygiene, private room, no fresh flowers/raw produce, no rectal temps, report any fever ≥38°C (100.4°F) immediately as potential sepsis.

🩸
Thrombocytopenia — Bleeding Precautions

Platelets <50,000 = significant risk. Platelets <20,000 = emergency — may need transfusion. Soft toothbrush, electric razor, no IM injections, avoid Valsalva, monitor for intracranial bleeding (headache, confusion).

💡
Key Nursing Interventions

EPO injections for anemia (if ordered). Antiemetics before chemo. Oral care for mucositis. Monitor renal function (myeloma). Bone pain management (myeloma — fall risk, pathologic fracture prevention). Emotional support for chronic illness.
