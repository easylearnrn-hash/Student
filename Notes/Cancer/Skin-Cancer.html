Common Risk Factors — All Skin Cancers

Environmental / Behavioral
  • Excessive sun exposure (UV radiation) — #1 risk
  • Use of tanning beds (UV-A + UV-B)
  • History of childhood sunburns — increases melanoma risk
  • Geographic location — closer to equator, higher altitude
  • Exposure to toxic chemicals (arsenic, coal tar)
  • Ionizing radiation exposure
Personal / Genetic
  • Fair skin, light eyes (blue/green), red or blonde hair
  • Personal or family history of skin cancer
  • Many moles / atypical moles (dysplastic nevi)
  • Immunosuppression (transplant, HIV)
  • Xeroderma pigmentosum (DNA repair defect)
  • Chronic skin irritation or scarring (Marjolin's ulcer)

Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)

Most common type of skin cancer. Arises from basal cells in the stratum basale. Grows slowly and rarely metastasizes — but can cause local tissue destruction if untreated.

Classic Appearance
  • Pearly, waxy, or glossy appearance
  • Translucent with visible telangiectasias (dilated blood vessels)
  • Slightly depressed center with raised, rolled borders
  • May bleed easily or ulcerate centrally
  • Most common on sun-exposed areas: face, ears, nose, neck, scalp
Treatment
  • Surgical excision — primary treatment; remove with clear margins
  • Mohs micrographic surgery — highest cure rate; removes layer by layer
  • Cryosurgery (liquid nitrogen) — freeze and destroy lesion
  • Electrodesiccation + curettage — scrape and burn; for superficial lesions
  • Topical chemotherapy (5-fluorouracil, imiquimod) — for superficial BCC
  • Rarely fatal; regular follow-up important due to recurrence risk
📝
NCLEX Key: BCC = Most Common Skin Cancer / Slow-Growing / Rarely Metastasizes

Most common: BCC. Least aggressive. "Pearly, rolled borders, depressed center." Watch for on face/nose. Surgical excision first line.

Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)

Second most common skin cancer. Arises from squamous cells in the stratum spinosum. Grows faster than BCC and has potential for metastasis (especially if neglected or on mucosal surfaces/ears/lips).

Classic Appearance
  • Crusty, scaly, hard raised lesion
  • Reddish-pink color
  • May ulcerate and bleed
  • Appears on sun-exposed areas: face, lips, ears, neck, hands, forearms
  • May arise from actinic keratosis (precancerous lesion)
  • Also arises on mucous membranes — oral cavity, genitals
Treatment
  • Surgical excision — standard first-line
  • Mohs surgery — for high-risk areas (face, ears, lips)
  • Radiation therapy — when surgery not possible
  • Chemotherapy or targeted therapy — for metastatic SCC
  • Lymph node dissection if regional spread

Actinic Keratosis — PRE-CANCEROUS

⚠️
Actinic Keratosis = Precancerous → Can Progress to SCC

Actinic keratosis is NOT yet cancer — it is a precancerous lesion. Left untreated, it can develop into squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). Must be treated and monitored.

  • Appears as rough, scaly, reddish-brown patches on sun-damaged skin
  • Found on sun-exposed areas: face, scalp, ears, forearms, backs of hands
  • Most common in older adults with fair skin
  • May itch, burn, or feel tender
  • Treatment: Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen), topical 5-FU (fluorouracil), imiquimod cream, photodynamic therapy, curettage

Melanoma — Most Aggressive Skin Cancer

Melanoma arises from mutated melanocytes in the stratum basale. Although it accounts for only ~1–5% of all skin cancers, it causes the most skin cancer deaths due to its tendency to metastasize early.

Common Metastatic Sites of Melanoma

  • Lungs — most common distant site
  • Bones
  • Brain
  • Liver
  • Regional lymph nodes (early spread)

ABCDE Rule — Melanoma Assessment Highest-Yield NCLEX Mnemonic

ABCDE — Warning Signs of Melanoma
A
Asymmetry — one half does NOT match the other; irregular shape that is not symmetrical
B
Borders — irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges (normal moles have smooth borders)
C
Color — multiple colors or variation within one lesion: dark brown, black, red, white, blue (normal moles = uniform tan/brown)
D
Diameter — larger than 6mm (size of a pencil eraser); any growth should be evaluated
E
Evolution — any change in size, shape, color, bleeding, crusting, or new symptom (itching, pain) over time

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis
  • Skin biopsy — gold standard (excisional biopsy preferred for suspected melanoma)
  • Breslow thickness — depth of invasion; key prognostic factor
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy — assess regional spread
  • CT/PET/MRI — staging for metastatic disease
Treatment
  • Surgical excision with wide margins — primary treatment
  • Immunotherapy — pembrolizumab (Keytruda), nivolumab, ipilimumab
  • Targeted therapy — BRAF/MEK inhibitors (vemurafenib) for BRAF-mutant melanoma
  • Radiation — for brain metastases
  • Systemic chemotherapy — for metastatic disease (less preferred)

Nursing Priorities & Patient Education

☀️
Sun Protection — Primary Prevention

• Avoid sun exposure between 10am – 4pm (peak UV radiation hours)
• Apply broad-spectrum SPF ≥ 15 (SPF 30+ preferred) 30 minutes before going outside; reapply every 2 hours when outdoors
• Wear protective clothing: wide-brimmed hat, UV-blocking sunglasses, long sleeves
• No tanning beds — UV-A in tanning beds penetrates deeper, causes DNA damage
• Childhood sun protection is critical — even one severe childhood sunburn increases melanoma risk

🔍
Skin Self-Exam — Monthly

Teach patients to perform monthly skin self-examination: use well-lit room and full-length mirror; examine all skin surfaces including scalp, between toes, under nails. Report any new or changing lesion using ABCDE rule.

🩹
Post-Procedure Wound Care (After Excision/Cryotherapy)

• Apply petroleum jelly (Vaseline) to keep wound moist and promote healing
• Cover with non-stick bandages (Telfa) — change daily or per instructions
• Monitor for signs of infection: increased redness, warmth, swelling, purulent drainage, fever
• Keep wound out of direct sun while healing
• Avoid picking or scratching healing site
