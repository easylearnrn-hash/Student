NCLEX-focused study note — symptoms, risk factors, diagnostics, treatments, and post-op nursing care

Esophageal Cancer

Symptoms
  • Progressive dysphagia — solids first, then liquids
  • Significant weight loss
  • Hoarseness (laryngeal nerve involvement)
  • Regurgitation, chest pain
  • Odynophagia (painful swallowing)
Risk Factors
  • GERD / Barrett's esophagus — #1 risk (chronic acid → metaplasia)
  • Smoking and alcohol
  • Obesity
  • Hot liquids, achalasia

Diagnostics & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Upper endoscopy + biopsy; barium swallow (shows stricture/irregularity)
  • Treatment: Esophagectomy (surgical removal); cisplatin-based chemotherapy + radiation
  • Nursing post-op: Monitor for anastomotic leak (fever, chest pain, dysphagia worsening); HOB elevated 30–45°; liquid/soft diet progression

Gastric (Stomach) Cancer

Symptoms
  • Early satiety — stomach fills quickly
  • Epigastric pain / discomfort
  • Unintended weight loss
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Occult (hidden) GI bleeding → iron-deficiency anemia
Risk Factors
  • H. pylori infection — #1 modifiable risk
  • Smoked, pickled, and cured foods
  • Smoking
  • Family history
  • Blood type A

Diagnostics & Treatment

  • Diagnosis: Upper endoscopy + biopsy; H. pylori testing; CT scan for staging
  • Treatment: Gastrectomy (partial or total); trastuzumab (Herceptin) if HER2-positive
  • Post-gastrectomy complication — Dumping Syndrome:
Dumping Syndrome — Post-Gastrectomy

Cause: Rapid emptying of hyperosmolar food into small intestine → fluid shifts + GI symptoms

  • Early dumping (15–30 min after eating): dizziness, sweating, nausea, diarrhea, tachycardia
  • Late dumping (1–3 hrs after): reactive hypoglycemia — tremors, weakness
  • Management: Small frequent meals (6/day), lie down after eating, avoid high-carb/sugary foods, increase protein & fat

Colorectal Cancer Screening High-Yield

Symptoms
  • Change in bowel habits — constipation/diarrhea alternating
  • Hematochezia (bright red) or melena (dark/tarry) — rectal bleeding
  • Pencil-thin stools — from narrowed lumen
  • Iron-deficiency anemia (occult bleeding)
  • Abdominal pain, cramping, gas
  • Unexplained weight loss
Risk Factors
  • Low-fiber, high-fat diet
  • IBD (Crohn's, Ulcerative Colitis)
  • Obesity and physical inactivity
  • Family history / genetic syndromes (FAP, Lynch)
  • Age >45
  • Red/processed meat consumption

Diagnostics & Treatment

TestDetails
ColonoscopyGold standard screening — every 10 years starting age 45; every 3–5 years if adenomas found
FOBT (Fecal Occult Blood Test)Annual screening — detects hidden blood; positive → follow-up colonoscopy
CEA (Carcinoembryonic Antigen)Tumor marker — not for screening; used to monitor treatment response and detect recurrence
  • Treatment: Colectomy (surgical resection); 5-FU chemotherapy; immunotherapy nivolumab (MSI-H/dMMR tumors)
  • Nursing: Post-colostomy care — stoma assessment (moist, pink/red, no necrosis), odor control, patient teaching

Liver Cancer (Hepatocellular Carcinoma — HCC)

Symptoms
  • Jaundice — yellowing of skin/sclera
  • RUQ pain — liver capsule distension
  • Ascites — fluid in abdomen
  • Fatigue, weight loss
  • Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
Risk Factors
  • Chronic Hepatitis B or C — most important
  • Liver cirrhosis (from any cause)
  • Alcohol use disorder
  • Aflatoxin (mold on grains/peanuts)
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)

Diagnostics & Treatment

  • Tumor marker: AFP (Alpha-fetoprotein) — elevated in HCC; also used in testicular cancer
  • Imaging: Abdominal ultrasound (screening), CT/MRI (confirmation)
  • Treatment: Liver resection (if localized), liver transplant, sorafenib (targeted/VEGF inhibitor), transarterial chemoembolization (TACE)
  • Prevention: HepB vaccine, treat HepC with antivirals, avoid alcohol

Pancreatic Cancer Worst Prognosis

Symptoms
  • Abdominal/back pain — dull, persistent
  • Painless jaundice — bile duct obstruction
  • Dark urine, clay-colored stools (obstructive jaundice)
  • New-onset diabetes mellitus — pancreatic islet cell destruction
  • Profound weight loss, anorexia
  • Pruritus (from bile salt deposits)
Risk Factors
  • Smoking — #1 modifiable risk
  • BRCA1/2 mutations
  • Chronic pancreatitis
  • Obesity and type 2 diabetes
  • Age >65, male sex

Diagnostics & Treatment

  • Tumor marker: CA 19-9 — not diagnostic alone; used for monitoring
  • Imaging: CT abdomen (gold standard), ERCP, endoscopic ultrasound
  • Treatment: Whipple procedure (pancreaticoduodenectomy) — removes head of pancreas + duodenum + part of common bile duct; gemcitabine chemotherapy
🚨
Post-Whipple Nursing — Watch For Bile Leakage

Signs: severe abdominal pain, fever, jaundice worsening after initial improvement. Also monitor blood glucose (disrupted endocrine function), drain output, and nutritional status (malabsorption from lost exocrine function).
