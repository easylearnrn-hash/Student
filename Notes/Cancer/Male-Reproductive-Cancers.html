Male Reproductive Cancers

Prostate · Testicular · Penile — NCLEX-focused study note

Prostate Cancer

Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men (excluding skin cancer). It is typically slow-growing, but can be aggressive. The prostate gland (walnut-sized) encircles the urethra — enlargement causes urinary symptoms.

Risk Factors
  • Age >50 — most important risk factor
  • African American (AA) men — higher incidence and mortality
  • Family history / BRCA1, BRCA2 mutations
  • High-fat diet
  • Occupational exposure (cadmium)
Classic Symptoms
  • Urinary hesitancy — difficulty starting urination
  • Dribbling — incomplete bladder emptying
  • Nocturia — waking at night to urinate
  • Hematuria — blood in urine
  • Low back/bone pain — from bone metastases (spine, hips, pelvis)
  • Erectile dysfunction
  • Early stages are often asymptomatic

Screening & Diagnosis

Screening
  • PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen) — blood test; elevated in prostate cancer, BPH, prostatitis
  • DRE (Digital Rectal Exam) — feel for hard, nodular, irregular prostate
  • Screening decision at age 50 (average risk), age 40–45 (high risk: AA men, BRCA+, family history)
Diagnostic Confirmation
  • Transrectal ultrasound (TRUS) — guided needle biopsy
  • Prostate biopsy — confirms diagnosis; Gleason score grades aggressiveness
  • Bone scan — if bone metastases suspected
  • CT/MRI — staging and extent of disease

Treatment

TreatmentDetail
Active surveillanceWatchful waiting for low-risk, slow-growing cancer in elderly; monitor PSA and DRE
Radical prostatectomySurgical removal of entire prostate; may include pelvic LN dissection; robotic-assisted (da Vinci) common
External beam radiationTargeted radiation; used for localized or locally advanced disease
BrachytherapyRadioactive seeds implanted directly into prostate gland
Androgen Deprivation Therapy (ADT)Leuprolide (Lupron) — GnRH agonist; reduces testosterone; used for metastatic disease
ChemotherapyDocetaxel — for castrate-resistant metastatic prostate cancer

Post-Prostatectomy Nursing Care

🔵
Post-Op Prostate Surgery — Key NCLEX Points

• Urinary catheter in place — monitor urine output, color, patency; report clots or blockage
• Urinary retention risk after catheter removal — teach the patient to void and report inability
• Erectile dysfunction (ED) risk — nerve-sparing surgery helps but ED is common; sildenafil (Viagra) may be prescribed
• Kegel exercises — strengthen pelvic floor; start as soon as catheter removed; improves urinary continence
• Report: bright red blood in urine (small amount of blood-tinged urine normal post-op), fever, inability to void

Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancer is the most common cancer in young men aged 15–35, but is highly curable when caught early. It often presents with a painless testicular lump.

Risk Factors
  • Cryptorchidism (undescended testicle) — #1 risk factor
  • Personal or family history of testicular cancer
  • Age 15–35 (peak incidence)
  • White/Caucasian race (more common than other races)
  • Klinefelter syndrome
  • Prior testicular cancer (contralateral risk)
Classic Symptoms
  • Painless, firm testicular lump or swelling — #1 presenting symptom
  • Feeling of heaviness in the scrotum
  • Dull ache in lower abdomen or groin
  • Gynecomastia (enlarged breasts) — some tumors produce hCG/estrogen
  • Pain is uncommon — absence of pain does NOT rule out cancer
🔍
NCLEX Alert — Painless Testicular Lump = Testicular Cancer Until Proven Otherwise

A painless testicular mass in a young male = testicular cancer until proven otherwise. Do NOT wait for pain. Immediate evaluation with scrotal ultrasound is required.

Tumor Markers — Testicular Cancer

MarkerSignificance
AFP (Alpha-Fetoprotein)Elevated in non-seminomatous germ cell tumors (NSGCT); normal in pure seminoma
β-hCG (Beta-Human Chorionic Gonadotropin)Elevated in both seminoma and NSGCT; may cause gynecomastia
LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase)Non-specific; elevated in advanced disease; used for staging and prognosis

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis
  • Scrotal ultrasound — initial test; differentiates solid vs cystic mass
  • Serum tumor markers (AFP, β-hCG, LDH)
  • CT abdomen/pelvis/chest for staging
  • Definitive diagnosis via radical inguinal orchiectomy — complete removal of affected testicle (trans-inguinal approach only — scrotal biopsy is contraindicated, risks lymphatic spread)
Treatment
  • Radical inguinal orchiectomy — first step regardless of type/stage
  • Seminoma: radiation (early stage) or cisplatin-based chemo (advanced)
  • NSGCT: BEP chemotherapy (Bleomycin + Etoposide + Cisplatin) — standard regimen
  • Retroperitoneal lymph node dissection (RPLND) — if LN involvement
  • Highly curable — 90%+ survival even in metastatic disease
🏦
Sperm Banking — Before Surgery or Chemotherapy

Offer and recommend sperm banking before orchiectomy or chemotherapy for fertility preservation. Chemotherapy and loss of one testicle may reduce fertility. This is an important pre-treatment counseling point — discuss with all reproductive-age patients.

🩺
Testicular Self-Exam (TSE)

Teach monthly testicular self-exam (TSE): best performed after a warm bath/shower (scrotum is relaxed). Gently roll each testicle between thumb and fingers. Report any new lump, hardness, or change in size/shape.

Penile Cancer

Risk Factors
  • HPV infection — especially HPV 16 and 18
  • Poor penile hygiene — smegma accumulation under foreskin
  • Phimosis (inability to retract foreskin)
  • Smoking
  • Uncircumcised males (higher risk)
  • Multiple sexual partners
Symptoms & Presentation
  • Red, ulcerated, or non-healing lesion on penis
  • Foul-smelling discharge from under foreskin
  • Pain, itching, or burning on penis
  • Swollen, palpable inguinal lymph nodes (LN involvement)
  • Often diagnosed late due to embarrassment/delay

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis
  • Physical examination and penile biopsy — confirms histology
  • Inguinal LN palpation and biopsy if enlarged
  • CT/MRI for staging
Treatment
  • Penectomy (partial or total removal of penis) — for invasive cancer
  • Local excision — for very small, superficial lesions
  • Laser therapy or topical chemotherapy — for early in-situ disease
  • Radiation and lymph node dissection for advanced disease
🛡️
Prevention — HPV Vaccine & Hygiene

• HPV vaccine (Gardasil) — protects against HPV 16/18 which cause penile cancer
• Daily penile hygiene — retract foreskin fully, clean with soap and water, replace foreskin after cleaning (prevents smegma buildup)
• Circumcision reduces risk but is not required if hygiene maintained
