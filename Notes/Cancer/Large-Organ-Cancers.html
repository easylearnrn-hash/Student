Liver · Lung · Kidney (RCC) · Pancreatic

NCLEX-focused study note — risk factors, classic presentations, diagnostics, and treatments

Liver Cancer (Hepatocellular Carcinoma — HCC)

Classic Symptoms
  • Jaundice — yellow skin and sclera from bile duct obstruction
  • RUQ pain — liver capsule distension
  • Ascites — abdominal fluid accumulation
  • Fatigue, unintentional weight loss
  • Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
  • Spider angiomas, caput medusae (if cirrhotic)
Risk Factors
  • Chronic Hepatitis B or C — most important
  • Liver cirrhosis (any etiology)
  • Chronic alcohol use
  • Aflatoxin exposure (mold on grains and peanuts)
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/NASH)

Diagnostics & Treatment

ItemDetails
AFP (alpha-fetoprotein)Key tumor marker; elevated in HCC; not diagnostic alone
Abdominal ultrasoundSurveillance every 6 months in cirrhosis / chronic HepB patients
CT/MRI abdomenCharacterizes lesion — arterial enhancement + washout pattern = HCC
TreatmentLiver resection (localized), liver transplant, sorafenib (VEGF inhibitor), TACE (transarterial chemoembolization)
PreventionHepB vaccination, treat HepC, avoid alcohol, maintain healthy weight

Lung Cancer

Smoking is the #1 cause — responsible for approximately 85% of all lung cancers.

Classic Symptoms
  • Chronic, persistent cough (new or changed)
  • Hemoptysis — coughing up blood
  • Shortness of breath (SOB), dyspnea
  • Chest pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Recurrent pneumonia in same lobe
  • Hoarseness (recurrent laryngeal nerve compression)
Risk Factors
  • Smoking — #1 cause (85% of cases)
  • Secondhand smoke exposure
  • Asbestos exposure (especially + smoking = synergistic risk)
  • Radon gas (residential exposure)
  • Air pollution
  • Family history

Diagnostics & Treatment

ItemDetails
Chest X-rayInitial screening — may show mass, atelectasis, pleural effusion
CT scan of chestBetter characterization of lesion; staging
Bronchoscopy + biopsyTissue diagnosis — gold standard confirmation
PET/CT scanDetect distant metastasis; staging
Sputum cytologyNon-invasive; less sensitive
TreatmentSurgery (lobectomy for NSCLC); pembrolizumab (PD-1 inhibitor); SCLC: cisplatin + etoposide + PCI
Low-dose CT screeningAnnually for high-risk smokers: age 50–80, ≥20 pack-year history, current smoker or quit within 15 years

Kidney Cancer (Renal Cell Carcinoma — RCC)

Classic Symptoms — Classic Triad
  • Hematuria — blood in urine (most classic — painless)
  • Flank pain — dull, aching pain in back/side
  • Palpable abdominal/flank mass
  • HTN (renin overproduction)
  • Weight loss, fatigue, fever
  • Polycythemia (EPO overproduction)
Risk Factors
  • Smoking — #1 modifiable risk
  • Hypertension
  • Obesity
  • Chronic kidney disease / dialysis
  • Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) syndrome — genetic
  • Male sex, age 50–70
💡
NCLEX Classic Clue

Hematuria + flank pain + palpable mass = Renal Cell Carcinoma until proven otherwise. Hematuria is PAINLESS in early RCC — do not dismiss it.

Diagnostics & Treatment

ItemDetails
Abdominal CT / MRIBest imaging — characterizes renal mass; no biopsy usually needed if imaging is classic
UrinalysisHematuria (microscopic or gross)
Renal function testsBUN, creatinine — baseline before nephrectomy
NephrectomySurgical removal of kidney — definitive for localized disease; partial nephrectomy if small tumor
SunitinibVEGF receptor inhibitor — targeted therapy for metastatic RCC; first-line
ImmunotherapyNivolumab (anti-PD-1) or combinations for advanced disease

Pancreatic Cancer

Classic Symptoms
  • Abdominal pain radiating to the back
  • Painless jaundice — bile duct obstruction by tumor head
  • Dark urine, clay-colored (acholic) stools
  • New-onset diabetes mellitus — islet cell destruction
  • Profound weight loss, anorexia
  • Pruritus (bile salt deposits in skin)
Risk Factors
  • Smoking — #1 modifiable risk
  • BRCA1/2 and PALB2 mutations
  • Chronic pancreatitis
  • Obesity and type 2 diabetes
  • Age >65
  • Heavy alcohol use

Diagnostics & Treatment

ItemDetails
CA 19-9Tumor marker — elevated in pancreatic cancer; used for monitoring, not screening
CT abdomen with contrastGold standard imaging — shows mass, vascular involvement, staging
ERCPEndoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography — biliary stenting for palliation
Whipple procedurePancreaticoduodenectomy — removes head of pancreas + duodenum + gallbladder + part of common bile duct; curative intent if resectable
GemcitabineFirst-line chemotherapy; FOLFIRINOX for fit patients
Prognosis5-year survival <12% overall; most diagnosed at advanced stage
🚨
Post-Whipple Procedure — Priority Nursing Assessments

1. Bile/anastomotic leak — fever, severe abdominal pain, worsening jaundice
2. Blood glucose — pancreatic endocrine disruption → DM may develop
3. Drain output — monitor color, consistency, volume
4. Nutrition — malabsorption from loss of exocrine pancreatic function → enzyme replacement
