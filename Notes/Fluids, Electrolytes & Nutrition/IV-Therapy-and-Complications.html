IV Therapy & Complications

Site assessment, complication recognition + response, fluid monitoring, lab interpretation, and aseptic technique

1. IV Complications — Recognize, Act, Document

🧊
Infiltration

What: IV fluid leaks into surrounding tissue (not the vein)

S/S: Cool, pale, swollen skin at site; discomfort/pain; site feels tight

Action: Stop IV → elevate limb → apply warm compress → restart IV in new site (different limb if possible)

🔥
Phlebitis

What: Inflammation of the vein wall — from mechanical, chemical, or bacterial causes

S/S: Redness, warmth, pain along vein track, palpable cord, vein feels hard

Action: Stop IV → warm compress → document on IV site assessment → monitor for infection → rotate sites

Rotate IV sites every 72–96 hours per policy

🧨
Extravasation

What: Vesicant (tissue-damaging) medication leaks into tissue — e.g., chemotherapy, vasopressors

S/S: Burning, stinging at site, swelling, blistering, tissue necrosis

Action: Stop IV — do NOT flush → aspirate medication if possible → administer specific antidote if ordered (e.g., hyaluronidase for some agents) → notify provider → document extensively

🫧
Air Embolism ⚠️

What: Air enters the venous system → travels to lungs or heart → blocks circulation

Causes: Disconnected IV tubing, failing to prime the line, central line placement/removal

S/S: Sudden SOB, chest pain, cyanosis, hypotension, "mill-wheel" murmur, anxiety, confusion

Action:

  1. Left lateral Trendelenburg position (left side + head down) — traps air in right atrium, away from pulmonary circulation
  2. Administer high-flow oxygen
  3. Call provider IMMEDIATELY
  4. Monitor vitals continuously
💧
Fluid Overload

What: Too much IV fluid administered too fast — fluid accumulates in lungs and tissues

S/S: Crackles in lungs, SOB, ↑ BP, weight gain, peripheral edema, bounding pulse

Action: Slow or stop IV rate → notify provider → administer diuretics (furosemide) as ordered → elevate HOB → monitor I&O

2. IV Site Best Practices

  • Site rotation: Every 72–96 hours (or per facility policy) to prevent phlebitis
  • Securement: Tape or transparent dressing to prevent dislodgment
  • Sterile technique: Scrub port with alcohol ≥15 seconds before each use (prevents CLABSI)
  • CLABSI prevention: Central line-associated bloodstream infections require hand hygiene, full barrier precautions during insertion, chlorhexidine skin prep, daily need assessment for line removal
  • Assess IV site for redness, swelling, warmth, pain, and drainage each shift

3. Fluid Monitoring

Daily Weight

  • Most accurate method for assessing fluid balance
  • 1 kg weight change ≈ 1 liter of fluid gained or lost
  • Same time, same scale, same clothing every day
  • Rapid weight gain = fluid retention → think heart failure, kidney failure

Intake and Output (I&O)

  • Normal urine output = ≥ 30 mL/hr (minimum for kidney perfusion)
  • Track: oral intake, IV fluids, tube feeds, urine, emesis, drains, wound output
  • Sudden ↓ UO → kidney issue, hypovolemia, or obstruction

5. Aseptic Technique for IV Procedures

  • Hand hygiene — wash hands or use alcohol-based rub before and after all patient contact
  • Sterile gloves and supplies — sterile to sterile only; never contaminate sterile field
  • Skin preparation — clean with chlorhexidine or alcohol before IV insertion
  • Maintain sterile field — never turn back on it or reach across it; replace contaminated items immediately
  • PPE — gloves, mask, sterile gown depending on procedure (central line = full barrier precautions)
  • Minimize talking/movement — reduces spread of microorganisms

6. NCLEX Must-Knows

  • ✅ Stop IV = first action for infiltration, phlebitis, and extravasation
  • ✅ Air embolism → left lateral Trendelenburg + O₂ + call provider
  • ✅ Extravasation: do NOT flush — aspirate first if possible
  • ✅ Normal UO = ≥ 30 mL/hr — report less to provider
  • ✅ Daily weight = best fluid balance assessment tool (1 kg = 1 L)
  • ✅ Rotate IV sites every 72–96 hours to prevent phlebitis
  • ✅ Scrub IV ports with alcohol (scrub the hub) before every access
  • ✅ K⁺ with diuretics = monitor ECG; peaked T-waves = cardiac emergency
🔑 Key Takeaway: For all IV site complications — STOP the IV first. Air embolism = left lateral Trendelenburg + O₂. Daily weight (1 kg = 1 L) is the best fluid monitor. UO must stay ≥ 30 mL/hr. Sterile/aseptic technique prevents CLABSI and infection. Always monitor K⁺ with diuretics or kidney issues.
