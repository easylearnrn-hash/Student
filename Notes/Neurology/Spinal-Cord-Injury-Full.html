Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)
Spinal Shock · Neurogenic Shock · Autonomic Dysreflexia

NCLEX-focused study note — injury levels · 3 major complications · nursing care · BATHROOM mnemonic

Overview

A spinal cord injury (SCI) is damage to the spinal cord resulting in partial or complete loss of motor function, sensation, or autonomic control below the level of injury. It is a neurological emergency requiring rapid assessment, stabilization, and ongoing care.

Complete SCI

Total loss of motor AND sensory function below the injury level. No signals pass through.

Incomplete SCI

Partial function remains below injury. Includes specific syndromes (central cord, anterior cord, Brown-Séquard, etc.).

3 Major Complications — Must Know All Three

1. Spinal Shock
  • Occurs immediately after injury
  • Flaccid paralysis below injury
  • Areflexia — no reflexes
  • No sensation, no bowel/bladder
  • Temporary — days to weeks
  • Resolves when reflexes return
2. Neurogenic Shock
  • Injuries above T6
  • Loss of sympathetic tone
  • ↓ BP + ↓ HR
  • Warm, dry (vasodilated) skin
  • Tx: IV fluids, vasopressors, atropine
3. Autonomic Dysreflexia
  • After spinal shock resolves
  • Injuries T6 and above
  • ↑↑ BP + ↓ HR
  • Pounding headache
  • Flushed above injury, pale below
  • Sweating, goosebumps
  • Life-threatening if untreated
🚨
Autonomic Dysreflexia — Emergency Response

① Sit patient upright immediately (lowers BP)
② Loosen clothing, remove constrictive garments
③ Check and remove the trigger — empty bladder (most common cause), relieve bowel impaction
④ Administer antihypertensives (hydralazine, nifedipine) if BP remains high
⑤ Notify provider immediately

Nursing Care

Acute Phase
  • Immobilize spine — cervical collar, backboard
  • Maintain airway (esp. C1–C4 → may need vent)
  • Monitor for shock signs (spinal vs neurogenic)
  • Foley catheter — monitor output, prevent retention
  • Prevent pressure ulcers — turn q2h, inspect skin
  • DVT prophylaxis — compression stockings, anticoagulants
Chronic / Long-Term Phase
  • Bowel and bladder programs (timed, structured)
  • ROM exercises — prevent contractures
  • Assistive devices for mobility
  • Psychosocial support and rehab planning
  • Patient and family education

BATHROOM Mnemonic — AD Triggers

  • B
    Bladder distension
    Most common trigger — check for obstructed/kinked catheter or full bladder
  • A
    Anal impaction
    Bowel blockage activates reflex arc → BP spike
  • T
    Tight clothing
    Constrictive waistbands, straps, pressure on skin
  • H
    High blood pressure
    Sustained hypertension = the dangerous result of AD
  • R
    Reflexes overactive
    Exaggerated autonomic response below injury
  • O
    Obstructed catheter
    Check for kinks, clogs, or displaced Foley
  • O
    Overheating or skin irritation
    Pressure ulcers, ingrown toenails, burns
  • M
    Monitor and manage immediately!
    Sit up + find and remove the cause + antihypertensives
