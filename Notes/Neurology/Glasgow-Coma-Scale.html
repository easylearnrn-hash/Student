Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
Level of Consciousness · Head Injury · Clinical Scoring Tool

NCLEX-focused study note — eye opening · verbal response · motor response · interpretation · nursing priorities

Overview

The Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) is a standardized neurological tool used to assess and quantify a patient's level of consciousness (LOC) following head injury or altered mental status. It is used in emergency departments, ICUs, and trauma settings worldwide.

Why It's Used
  • Standardized, reproducible LOC assessment
  • Trending over time (more important than a single score)
  • Guides treatment decisions (imaging, surgery, intubation)
  • Communication tool among healthcare team
Formula

E + V + M

Eye Opening + Verbal Response + Motor Response

Range: 3 – 15  |  15 = fully conscious  |  3 = deep coma

⭐
NCLEX Critical Fact — Trending Over Time

A single GCS score is less meaningful than a trend. A patient going from 14 → 12 → 10 is deteriorating — notify the provider immediately. Never document GCS without context of change from previous scores.

Component 1 — Eye Opening (E)

👁 Eye Opening (E)  ·  Maximum Score: 4
4
SpontaneousEyes open without any stimulation
3
To SpeechEyes open in response to voice or verbal command
2
To PainEyes open only when painful stimulus is applied (sternal rub, nail bed pressure)
1
No Eye OpeningNo response to any stimulus
NT

NT = Not Testable (document if eyes cannot be opened due to swelling/trauma — note reason).

Component 2 — Verbal Response (V)

🗣 Verbal Response (V)  ·  Maximum Score: 5
5
OrientedKnows person, place, time, and event (A&O × 4)
4
ConfusedConversational but disorientated — wrong time/place; sentences intact
3
Inappropriate WordsRecognizable words but no meaningful conversation; random words, swearing
2
Incomprehensible SoundsGroaning, moaning, no recognizable words
1
No Verbal ResponseCompletely silent, no sounds
NT

NT = Not Testable (e.g., intubated patients, language barrier — document appropriately with "T" for tube, or "1T").

Component 3 — Motor Response (M)

💪 Motor Response (M)  ·  Maximum Score: 6
6
Obeys CommandsFollows two-step verbal instructions ("raise your hand, then show two fingers")
5
Localizes PainPurposefully moves hand toward painful stimulus to push it away
4
Withdraws from PainPulls away from painful stimulus — non-purposeful, flexion/extension not specific
3
Abnormal Flexion (Decorticate)Wrist/elbows flex, legs extend in response to pain — indicates damage above brainstem
2
Extension (Decerebrate)Arms & legs extend and rotate — indicates brainstem damage — worse prognosis
1
No Motor ResponseNo movement to any stimulus
NT

Motor response is the most prognostically significant component of GCS — assess best response from any limb.

🚨
GCS ≤ 8 = Airway Emergency

A GCS of 8 or below indicates the patient cannot protect their own airway. This is the threshold for considering intubation. Report immediately to the provider. Prepare intubation equipment and ensure suction is at bedside.

Decorticate vs Decerebrate Posturing NCLEX Classic

Decorticate Posturing — GCS Motor 3

🧠 → Cortex

  • Arms flex toward core (wrists curl inward)
  • Legs extend
  • Indicates damage above the brainstem (cortex/internal capsule/cerebral hemispheres)
  • Better prognosis than decerebrate

Memory tip: "Decorticate = arms toward the CORe" — Flexion toward the body

Decerebrate Posturing — GCS Motor 2

⬇️ Brainstem

  • Arms extend and rotate outward
  • Legs also extend
  • Indicates damage at the brainstem level (more severe)
  • Worse prognosis — indicates deeper brain injury

Memory tip: "DeCEREBrate = CEREBrum disconnected" — Everything extends outward

💡
NCLEX Priority Rule for Posturing

If a patient progresses from decorticate → decerebrate posturing, this signals neurological deterioration. Report to provider immediately — this is a medical emergency. Never delay when posturing changes.

Nursing Priorities

  • 🌬️ Airway first (GCS ≤ 8) — position, suction, prepare for intubation; "can't protect airway" = immediate action
  • 📉 Trend the GCS — document every assessment; report any decrease of ≥ 2 points to provider urgently
  • 🩺 Neuro checks — q1h or per protocol; include pupils (size, equality, reactivity) with every GCS assessment
  • 🧠 ICP monitoring awareness — HOB 30°, no hip flexion, no neck rotation, avoid Valsalva (clustering care)
  • ⚠️ Seizure precautions — padded rails, suction at bedside, no oral stimulation during seizure
  • 📋 Documentation — record individual scores (E_V_M format), not just total; note NT reasons; time-stamp every assessment
  • 🔍 Identify cause — GCS is a measurement tool, not a diagnosis; work to identify underlying etiology (trauma, bleed, metabolic, toxin)
✅
Documentation Best Practice

Always document GCS as individual scores, e.g., E3V4M5 = 12. Reporting just "GCS 12" loses critical information about which component changed.
