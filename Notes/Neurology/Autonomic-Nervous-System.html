What Is the ANS?

The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) controls involuntary body functions — processes that happen automatically without conscious thought: heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, respiratory rate, pupil size, bladder, and gland secretion.

Two Divisions — Always in Balance
🔥 Sympathetic (SNS) — "Fight or Flight" — activates under stress; increases energy output

🌿 Parasympathetic (PNS) — "Rest and Digest" — activates during rest; conserves and restores energy
💡
The Golden Rule for NCLEX

These two systems are antagonists — they have opposite effects on virtually every organ. Mastering this opposition is the key to understanding all autonomic pharmacology.

ANS Pharmacology — NCLEX Drug Categories

Drug ClassMechanismKey ExampleNCLEX Side Effects / Priorities
Beta Blockers (end in -lol) Block β receptors → ↓ SNS Metoprolol, atenolol, propranolol Watch: bradycardia, hypotension, bronchospasm — do NOT give in asthma
Beta Agonists Stimulate β₂ receptors → ↑ SNS → bronchodilation Albuterol, salmeterol Tachycardia, tremor — used in asthma/COPD
Alpha Agonists Stimulate α receptors → vasoconstriction → ↑ BP Phenylephrine, norepinephrine Used in shock/sepsis; monitor BP continuously
Anticholinergics Block ACh → ↓ PNS effects Atropine, scopolamine, diphenhydramine SLUDGE reversal: dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, tachycardia, blurred vision, confusion (elderly)
Cholinergic Agonists Mimic ACh → ↑ PNS effects Bethanechol, pilocarpine, neostigmine SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI cramps, Emesis — bradycardia
Adrenergic Agonists Stimulate adrenergic (α + β) receptors Epinephrine, dopamine Used in anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, septic shock
💡
SLUDGE — Cholinergic Toxidrome (Excess PNS)

Salivation · Lacrimation · Urination · Defecation · GI cramps · Emesis
Seen in: cholinergic drug overdose, organophosphate (pesticide) poisoning
Antidote: Atropine (anticholinergic)

High-Yield Clinical Scenarios

Sympathetic Surge — "Fight or Flight"
  • Acute stress, trauma, hemorrhage, pain
  • Anaphylaxis → massive epinephrine release
  • Cocaine/amphetamine overdose → extreme SNS activation
  • Signs: tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, dry skin, agitation
Parasympathetic Excess — "SLUDGE"
  • Organophosphate (nerve agent/pesticide) poisoning
  • Cholinergic medication overdose
  • Vagal syncope (vasovagal response)
  • Signs: bradycardia, hypotension, bronchospasm, salivation, urination, vomiting
🚨
Anaphylaxis — Maximum SNS Activation

First-line: Epinephrine IM (epinephrine 1:1000, 0.3–0.5 mg) — stimulates α + β receptors → vasoconstriction + bronchodilation
Then: antihistamines, corticosteroids, fluids, O₂ — but epinephrine is always first

Nursing Priorities

  • 🔥 Adrenergic/SNS drug patients: monitor HR, BP continuously; watch for hypertensive crisis, tachyarrhythmias
  • 🛑 Beta blockers: hold if HR < 60 or systolic BP < 90; never stop abruptly (rebound tachycardia)
  • 🌿 Anticholinergics in elderly: watch for confusion, urinary retention, constipation, falls
  • 💊 Cholinergics: monitor for SLUDGE symptoms; keep atropine nearby
  • 🩺 Atropine: used for symptomatic bradycardia — dose 0.5 mg IV; may repeat up to 3 mg total
  • ⚡ Epinephrine: used for anaphylaxis — always check concentration (1:1000 IM vs 1:10,000 IV)
