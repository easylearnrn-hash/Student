What Is a Stroke?

A stroke (CVA) occurs when blood flow to part of the brain is blocked or reduced, causing brain cells to die. Time is brain — every minute counts. It is a medical emergency.

Ischemic Stroke — 87%
  • Blood clot blocks flow to brain
  • Causes: atherosclerosis, Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)
  • Treat with tPA within 3–4.5 hrs
Hemorrhagic Stroke — 13%
  • Blood vessel bursts — bleeding into brain
  • Causes: hypertension (HTN), aneurysm, trauma
  • Do NOT give tPA — worsens bleed
TIA — "Mini-Stroke"
  • Temporary blockage — minutes to hours
  • No permanent damage
  • Warning sign: high risk of future stroke

Risk Factors

Modifiable (Can Be Changed)
  • Hypertension — #1 risk factor
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • High cholesterol
  • Obesity
  • Atrial Fibrillation (AFib) — clot source
  • Alcohol & drug use (cocaine)
Non-Modifiable (Cannot Be Changed)
  • Age > 55
  • Family history
  • Race (higher risk: African Americans, Hispanics)
  • Previous TIA — major warning sign

Diagnosis

  • CT Scan — ordered FIRST; differentiates ischemic from hemorrhagic; required before tPA
  • MRI — more detail on brain damage extent
  • Carotid ultrasound — blocked neck arteries
  • Echocardiogram — clots in heart (AFib patients)
  • Blood tests — coagulation, glucose, cholesterol

Treatment

Ischemic Stroke

tPA — Tissue Plasminogen Activator
  • Clot-busting drug — must be given within 3–4.5 hours of symptom onset
  • CT scan must rule out bleed FIRST
  • Contraindications: hemorrhagic stroke, recent surgery, active bleeding, BP > 185/110
  • Post-tPA: monitor closely for bleeding, BP, neuro changes
Additional Ischemic Treatment
  • Aspirin or Clopidogrel — antiplatelet therapy (prevent future clots)
  • Mechanical thrombectomy — catheter-based clot removal for large vessel occlusion
  • Anticoagulation if AFib is cause

Hemorrhagic Stroke

Priority: Control Bleeding
  • Lower blood pressure (prevent further bleeding)
  • Stop ALL blood thinners — warfarin, aspirin, heparin worsen hemorrhage
  • Surgery: evacuate hematoma, clip/coil aneurysm
  • Reverse anticoagulation if present

Left vs Right Brain Stroke

Left Hemisphere Stroke
  • Right-sided weakness/paralysis (hemiplegia)
  • Aphasia — difficulty speaking/understanding language
  • Reading, writing, calculation problems
  • Slow, cautious behavior
Right Hemisphere Stroke
  • Left-sided weakness/paralysis
  • Left-sided neglect — unaware of left side
  • Poor judgment, impulsive behavior
  • Vision problems (homonymous hemianopia)

Nursing Priorities

🚨
Dysphagia Assessment BEFORE Food or Fluids

Stroke causes dysphagia (swallowing difficulty) → aspiration pneumonia risk. Always perform a swallowing screen before giving PO fluids or food. Keep HOB elevated, give thickened liquids if ordered, and involve speech therapy.

  • 🕐 Time is brain — document exact time of symptom onset
  • 🧠 Serial neuro checks using GCS — report deterioration immediately
  • 💉 tPA monitoring — q15 min BP/neuro checks; no IM injections, no Foley; watch for bleeding
  • 🏥 Safety — fall precautions, aspiration precautions, side rail padding
  • 🗣️ Communication support — use simple words, picture boards for aphasia
  • 🧸 Rehabilitation — PT/OT/SLP referral early; reposition q2h; ROM exercises
