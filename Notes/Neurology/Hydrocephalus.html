📌 What Is Hydrocephalus?

Core Definition

Hydrocephalus — an abnormal buildup of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) inside the brain's ventricles (fluid-filled spaces inside the brain).

  • CSF accumulates → ventricles enlarge → press on surrounding brain tissue
  • Can raise intracranial pressure (ICP) — especially when buildup is rapid
  • Can damage brain tissue and impair neurologic function
Word Breakdown
  • Hydro- = water or fluid
  • -cephalus = head
  • Literally: "water on the brain" — though the fluid is specifically CSF, not ordinary water
⭐
Most Important NCLEX Concept

Hydrocephalus is not just extra fluid — it is a pressure and circulation problem. The dangers are increased ICP, brain compression, neurologic deterioration, and developmental delay in children.

💧 What CSF Normally Does

Normal CSF Functions
  • Cushions the brain and spinal cord
  • Helps deliver nutrients
  • Removes waste products
Normal CSF Circulation
  • Produced mainly in the choroid plexus (tissue inside the ventricles)
  • Circulates through ventricles and around the brain and spinal cord
  • Reabsorbed into the bloodstream
How Hydrocephalus Develops
  • Too much CSF is produced
  • CSF flow is blocked (most important mechanism)
  • CSF absorption is impaired (most important mechanism)

In practice, blockage or poor absorption are the most important mechanisms for NCLEX.

🔄 Pathophysiology

Step-by-Step Flow
  • 1
    CSF production, movement, or reabsorption becomes imbalancedBlockage or impaired absorption is most common
  • 2
    CSF accumulates in the ventriclesFluid cannot move or be absorbed normally
  • 3
    Ventricles enlargeBrain tissue gets compressed from expanding fluid spaces
  • 4
    ICP may riseEspecially when buildup is rapid — rigid skull cannot accommodate the extra volume
  • 5
    Different outcomes by ageInfants: skull can expand → enlarged head. Older children/adults: skull rigid → pressure symptoms dominate
🔑 Simple NCLEX Way to Think About It
Too much CSF in the ventricles → stretches ventricles → presses on brain

🔀 Main Types of Hydrocephalus

TypeMechanismKey Notes
Communicating CSF can still flow through ventricles but absorption is impaired after it exits Problem is with reabsorption, not internal blockage
Noncommunicating (Obstructive) Blockage within the ventricular system prevents normal CSF flow Fluid builds up behind the blockage, enlarging upstream ventricles
Congenital Present at birth — may result from genetic causes or fetal brain development problems Important in infants and children
Acquired Develops after birth — from head injury, bleeding, infection, tumors, stroke, etc. Can occur at any age
Normal-Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) Ventricles enlarge and symptoms develop — but measured CSF pressure may be in usual range Mostly in older adults — classic triad: gait + cognition + bladder (see below)

⚡ Causes

Infants & Children
  • Congenital malformations
  • Genetic causes
  • Brain infections
  • Intraventricular hemorrhage (bleeding in the brain)
Older Children & Adults
  • Tumors blocking CSF flow
  • Brain hemorrhage
  • Traumatic brain injury
  • Stroke
  • Meningitis or infection that scars CSF pathways

👶 Signs & Symptoms — Infants

Infants show different signs than older patients because skull sutures are not yet fully fused — the head can still expand.

Classic Infant Findings
Unusually large head Rapid head growth Bulging fontanel Sun-setting eyes Vomiting Lethargy / sleepiness Irritability Poor feeding Seizures (severe cases)
Bulging Fontanel

The fontanel (soft spot on an infant's skull) bulges when ICP is elevated. This is a direct, visible sign of increased intracranial pressure in an infant.

Sun-Setting Eyes

Sun-setting eyes — downward deviation of the eyes so the whites are visible above the iris. A classic sign of hydrocephalus in infants.

⭐
Classic NCLEX Infant Point

In infants, increasing head circumference is a major clue to worsening hydrocephalus. Serial head circumference measurement is a critical pediatric nursing action.

🧑 Signs & Symptoms — Older Children & Adults

In older patients, the skull is rigid and cannot expand, so pressure symptoms dominate instead of head enlargement.

ICP-Related Symptoms
  • Headache
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Blurry vision or visual changes
  • Drowsiness or decreased alertness
Neurologic Symptoms
  • Balance problems
  • Gait changes
  • Bladder control problems
  • Thinking and memory problems

🧓 Normal-Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH) — Classic Triad

NPH occurs mainly in older adults. Measured CSF pressure may be in the usual range, but ventricles are enlarged and symptoms are present.

💧
WET
Urinary incontinence — loss of bladder control
🚶
WOBBLY
Gait disturbance — often the earliest sign. Slow, shuffling, feet "stuck" to the floor
🧠
WACKY
Cognitive impairment — memory, thinking, attention, judgment problems. May resemble dementia but can improve with treatment
🔑 NPH Memory Aid
Wet · Wobbly · Wacky
Urinary incontinence · Gait problems · Cognitive changes
Gait disturbance is often the earliest and most prominent symptom.

⬆️ Increased ICP in Hydrocephalus

Signs of Increased ICP
  • Headache
  • Vomiting
  • Visual changes
  • Decreased LOC
  • Restlessness
  • Seizures
  • Pupillary changes
  • Abnormal posturing (severe)
☠️ Cushing's Triad — Late Emergency Sign

A late, dangerous sign of severely elevated ICP — present in hydrocephalus and any severe neuro emergency.

  • Bradycardia — slow heart rate
  • Widened pulse pressure — large gap between systolic and diastolic
  • Irregular respirations — abnormal breathing pattern
🚨
Critical Neuro Emergency

Cushing's triad = impending brain herniation. Report immediately. This is not unique to hydrocephalus — it is a red flag in any severe ICP elevation.

🔍 Assessment Findings

All Patients
  • Level of consciousness
  • Headache severity and pattern
  • Vomiting
  • Pupil size and reaction
  • Seizure activity
  • Gait and balance
  • Bladder function
  • Cognition and memory
Infants — Additional Key Assessments
  • Head circumference — serial measurement
  • Fontanel tension and bulging
  • Feeding tolerance
  • Sun-setting eye sign
  • Irritability vs lethargy

Rapidly increasing head circumference = major pediatric nursing red flag.

🧪 Diagnostic Tests

TestPurposeNotes
Cranial Ultrasound Images brain through the open fontanel using sound waves Used in infants with open fontanels — quick, no radiation
CT Scan Shows enlarged ventricles, bleeding, masses, or obstruction Useful in urgent situations — fast and widely available
MRI Detailed imaging using magnets and radio waves — best defines ventricular enlargement and identifies cause Test of choice for evaluating adult hydrocephalus

💊 Treatment

Main Goal

Divert or drain excess CSF to reduce ventricular pressure and protect brain tissue.

VP Shunt (Most Common)

Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt — a shunt that drains CSF from the brain's ventricles into the peritoneal cavity (abdomen).

  • Uses tubing and a valve to control drainage
  • Most common long-term treatment for hydrocephalus
Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy (ETV)

Creates an opening in the floor of the third ventricle so CSF can bypass an obstruction — an alternative to shunting in selected cases.

💡
NCLEX Point

Hydrocephalus is often treated with a VP shunt. Always know shunt complications — they are very high-yield.

⚠️ Shunt Complications

Shunt Malfunction / Obstruction

The shunt becomes blocked — CSF can no longer drain → ICP rises → patient develops symptoms of hydrocephalus again.

  • Headache, vomiting, decreased LOC
  • Irritability, lethargy (in infants)
  • Bulging fontanel (in infants)
Shunt Infection

The shunt or surrounding tissue becomes infected — a major safety concern.

  • Fever
  • Redness or swelling along the shunt tract
  • Worsening neurologic status
  • Can lead to meningitis
Other Shunt Complications
Overdrainage Underdrainage Bleeding Seizures Abdominal complications (VP shunt)
🚨
Very High-Yield NCLEX Point

A patient with a shunt who develops return of ICP symptoms (headache, vomiting, decreased LOC, irritability) has shunt malfunction or infection until proven otherwise. Report immediately.

🩺 Nursing Management

All Patients

Nursing Priorities
  • Frequent neurologic checks — LOC, pupils, speech, motor
  • Monitor for signs of increased ICP
  • Monitor for shunt malfunction — return of headache, vomiting, decreased LOC
  • Monitor for shunt infection — fever, redness along shunt tract, worsening neuro status
  • Seizure precautions when indicated
  • Support nutrition and hydration
  • Family teaching

Infants & Children — Additional Priorities

Pediatric-Specific Nursing Care
  • Measure and document head circumference serially
  • Assess fontanel tension at each assessment
  • Monitor feeding tolerance and vomiting
  • Assess developmental progress
  • Monitor for irritability and lethargy
  • Seizure precautions

Children may need long-term developmental follow-up and supportive therapies because hydrocephalus can interfere with brain growth.

📚 Patient & Family Teaching

Report to Provider Immediately
  • Headache — new or worsening
  • Vomiting
  • Irritability or sleepiness
  • Fever
  • Seizures
  • Visual changes
  • Swelling or redness along the shunt path
  • Decline in school performance or cognition
  • Return of any prior hydrocephalus symptoms
Infant-Specific — Parents Should Watch For
  • Bulging fontanel
  • Poor feeding
  • Increasing head size
  • Sun-setting eyes

🔴 Complications

Neurologic / Developmental
  • Developmental delay
  • Intellectual impairment
  • Physical disability
  • Seizures
  • Nerve damage
  • Death in severe untreated cases
Treatment-Related
  • Shunt obstruction or malfunction
  • Shunt infection
  • Meningitis or encephalitis from infection
  • Overdrainage or underdrainage
  • Serious complications from shunt surgery

📋 Nursing Diagnoses

Common Nursing Diagnoses
  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion — decreased ability to maintain adequate blood flow to the brain
  • Risk for injury — from seizures, falls, or neurologic decline
  • Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements — not taking in enough nutrients due to vomiting or feeding difficulties
  • Delayed growth and development — slower than expected physical or developmental progress
  • Acute pain — headache
  • Risk for infection — especially with shunts or postoperative care

🎯 Common NCLEX Clues

If NCLEX Describes Any of These → Think Hydrocephalus
Infant with enlarged head + bulging fontanel Sun-setting eyes in an infant VP shunt patient with headache and lethargy Older adult: gait change + urinary incontinence + cognitive decline Worsening headache and vomiting → increased ICP

🏆 High-Yield NCLEX Pearls

Pearl #1

Hydrocephalus = excess CSF in the ventricles causing pressure and brain compression.

Pearl #2

In infants: rapidly increasing head circumference + bulging fontanel + sun-setting eyes.

Pearl #3

In older children and adults: headache, vomiting, visual changes, decreased LOC — pressure symptoms dominate.

Pearl #4

NPH triad = Wet + Wobbly + Wacky (urinary incontinence + gait disturbance + cognitive decline).

Pearl #5

A VP shunt is the most common long-term treatment — drain CSF to the abdomen.

Pearl #6

Shunt patient with return of ICP symptoms = shunt malfunction or infection until proven otherwise. Report immediately.
