Neurology · Autoimmune

Scleroderma
(Systemic Sclerosis)

NCLEX-focused study note — vasculopathy · fibrosis · immune activation

What It Is

Scleroderma (hard skin) is an autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks the body, characterized by three core processes:

1 · Vascular
Vasculopathy — blood-vessel dysfunction with narrowing and ischemia
2 · Structural
Fibrosis — excess collagen causing thickening and scarring
3 · Immune
Immune activation — autoantibodies and chronic inflammation

Systemic sclerosis (SSc) (systemic = whole body; sclerosis = hardening) is the systemic form — skin and internal organs.

Types You Must Know

1) Localized Scleroderma

Morphea
Localized plaques of thickened skin. Usually skin-only, not life-threatening.
Linear Scleroderma
Line-like thickened band. Can limit growth/movement in the affected limb. More common in children.

2) Systemic Sclerosis (Skin + Organs)

Limited Cutaneous (lcSSc)
  • Skin: distal (fingers/hands/forearms; sometimes face)
  • Classic association: CREST syndrome
  • Higher risk of PAH (pulmonary arterial hypertension) later
Diffuse Cutaneous (dcSSc)
  • Skin: proximal (upper arms/thighs/trunk)
  • Higher risk of early aggressive ILD (interstitial lung disease)
  • Higher risk of Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC)

Pathophysiology

  1. Endothelial injury (damage to vessel lining) → vasoconstriction + intimal proliferation (thickening inside vessels)
  2. ↓ Blood flow → ischemia → Raynaud phenomenon (episodic digital color change from vasospasm)
  3. Fibroblast activation (collagen-producing cells become overactive) → collagen deposition
  4. Result: skin thickening, organ fibrosis, digital ischemia/ulcers
💡
NCLEX Translation

Think "vasospasm + scarring" → cold painful fingers + tight skin + organ damage.

Classic Clinical Picture

Skin & Extremities

  • Early: edema (swelling) of hands → "puffy fingers"
  • Progressive: skin tightening → decreased ROM
  • Mask-like facies, microstomia (small mouth), beak-like nose
  • Pruritus, hyper/hypopigmentation
  • Raynaud: triggered by cold or stress

Musculoskeletal

  • Arthralgia, myalgia
  • Tendon friction rub — grating sensation over tendons; suggests diffuse disease

GI Very Common on NCLEX

  • Dysphagia from esophageal dysmotility
  • Reflux → heartburn
  • Gastroparesis → early satiety, nausea
  • SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth) → malabsorption
  • Constipation/diarrhea

Lungs Top Cause of Mortality

ILD
Dry cough, exertional dyspnea, "Velcro" crackles (fine inspiratory crackles)
PAH
Progressive dyspnea, fatigue, syncope, right heart strain

Kidneys — Scleroderma Renal Crisis High-Yield Emergency

🚨
SRC: Abrupt Onset

Severe hypertension · AKI · Proteinuria/hematuria · Sometimes MAHA and thrombocytopenia

Heart

  • Arrhythmias from fibrosis
  • Pericarditis
  • Heart failure if myocardial involvement

Treatment Overview

No single cure — treatment targets individual organs and symptoms.

Raynaud / Digital Ischemia

  • CCBs (calcium channel blockers) — e.g., nifedipine (vasodilator)
  • PDE-5 inhibitors — e.g., sildenafil (vasodilator)
  • Prostacyclin analogs — strong vasodilators/platelet inhibitors (severe cases)
  • Smoking cessation — critical (stops nicotine vasoconstriction)

Skin / Inflammatory DMARDs

  • Methotrexate — for skin/joint inflammation
  • Mycophenolate mofetil — often for ILD/skin
  • Cyclophosphamide — severe ILD

PAH

  • Endothelin receptor antagonists, PDE-5 inhibitors, prostacyclin pathway agents

GERD / Esophageal

  • PPIs, H2 blockers, prokinetics
  • Small frequent meals + upright after meals

Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC) Must-Know

🚨
First-Line: ACE Inhibitor (often captopril)

Use ACE inhibitor even if creatinine rises initially — it improves outcomes.
Avoid high-dose steroids in systemic sclerosis — can increase SRC risk.

Nursing Priorities (NCLEX-Style)

Top Priorities by System

  1. Airway/breathing: assess dyspnea, cough, crackles; monitor SpO₂; watch ILD/PAH
  2. Circulation/perfusion: Raynaud severity, digital ulcers, cap refill, skin temperature
  3. Renal/BP: monitor BP trends (sudden rise!); urine output; labs
  4. GI safety: dysphagia/GERD, aspiration risk, weight loss, malabsorption
  5. Skin integrity + mobility: prevent ulcers, maintain ROM

Core Nursing Interventions

  • 🧤 Keep warm — gloves, layered clothing, warm environment (prevent vasospasm)
  • 🚭 Avoid vasoconstrictors — no nicotine, limit caffeine, avoid decongestants
  • 🧴 Hand/skin care — moisturize, protect from cracking
  • 🏃 ROM exercises daily + splints as ordered
  • 🩹 Wound care — protect digits, report infection signs (redness, drainage, fever)
  • 🍽️ GERD prevention — elevate HOB, small meals, no late meals, avoid triggers
  • 🗣️ Dysphagia precautions — swallow eval, small bites, upright positioning
  • 💬 Psychosocial support — body image, chronic pain, fatigue

Watch For — Critical Complications

🚨
1) Scleroderma Renal Crisis (SRC)

Red flags: sudden severe HTN · new headache/vision changes/confusion · oliguria · rising creatinine
Action: Notify provider immediately, anticipate ACE inhibitor, monitor labs/urine output.

🫁
2) Pulmonary Complications

Worsening dyspnea + ↓SpO₂ + new crackles → ILD progression
Exertional syncope + loud P2 + edema → PAH / right heart failure

⚠️
3) Aspiration Risk

Esophageal dysmotility/GERD → chronic cough, recurrent pneumonias

🩹
4) Digital Ischemia / Infection

Ulcers → cellulitis or osteomyelitis (bone infection)

Common NCLEX Question Patterns

  1. Priority: Sudden severe hypertension + headache → SRC → priority is BP/renal management
  2. Teaching: Raynaud — avoid cold, wear gloves, stop smoking, manage stress
  3. Complication recognition: Progressive dyspnea + crackles → ILD; dyspnea + syncope → PAH
  4. GI safety: Severe reflux/dysphagia — upright after meals, small meals, meds (PPI)
  5. Skin/mobility: Maintain ROM and protect skin to prevent contractures/ulcers
