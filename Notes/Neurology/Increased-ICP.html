Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)
Neurological Emergency · Cushing's Triad · ICP Management

What Is Increased ICP?

Increased intracranial pressure occurs when pressure inside the rigid skull rises, compressing brain tissue and impairing blood flow. It is a neurological emergency — untreated, it leads to herniation and death.

Normal Values — Must Memorize
  • Normal ICP: 5–15 mmHg
  • Elevated ICP: > 20 mmHg (sustained)
  • CPP (Cerebral Perfusion Pressure) = MAP − ICP → Goal: CPP ≥ 60 mmHg
  • ↑ ICP → ↓ CPP → ↓ blood flow → brain ischemia

Causes

Structural
  • Head trauma (bleeds, contusions, swelling)
  • Brain tumor
  • Stroke (especially hemorrhagic)
  • Hydrocephalus (fluid buildup in ventricles)
Physiological
  • Meningitis, encephalitis (infections)
  • Hypoxia (low O₂ → cerebral vasodilation)
  • Hypercapnia (↑ CO₂ → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ ICP)

Signs & Symptoms

Early Signs (Subtle)
  • Headache — worse with coughing, straining, bending over
  • Change in LOC — confusion, restlessness (earliest sign)
  • Vomiting — often projectile, without nausea
  • Blurred or double vision (diplopia)
  • Pupil changes — sluggish or unequal
Late Signs — Imminent Herniation
  • Cushing's Triad (see below)
  • Decorticate or decerebrate posturing
  • Seizures
  • Fixed, dilated pupils — "blown pupil"
  • Coma
⭐
NCLEX #1 Early Sign

Change in Level of Consciousness (LOC) is the earliest and most sensitive indicator of increased ICP. Any new restlessness, confusion, or decreased responsiveness should trigger immediate neurological assessment.

Cushing's Triad — Late Emergency Sign

📈
↑ Systolic BP
Widened pulse pressure — the brain is trying to force blood in against the pressure
💓
Bradycardia
Heart rate slows — reflex response to hypertension
😮‍💨
Irregular Respirations
Cheyne-Stokes breathing or apnea — brainstem compression
🚨
Cushing's Triad = Impending Brain Herniation

This is a pre-terminal sign. Notify the provider immediately. Prepare for emergency interventions — hyperventilation, osmotic diuretics, possible surgical decompression.

Nursing Care — ICP Management

Positioning (Critical)
  • HOB 30° (promotes venous drainage from brain)
  • Keep head midline and neutral — no flexion, no rotation, no hyperextension
  • NEVER flat or Trendelenburg — both increase ICP
  • No tight cervical collars or neck compression
CO₂ & Oxygenation
  • Maintain PaCO₂: 35–38 mmHg
  • ↑ CO₂ → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ ICP
  • Avoid hypoxia — maintain O₂ sat > 94%
  • Suction cautiously and briefly — suctioning spikes ICP
General Interventions
  • Maintain quiet, low-stimulation environment — cluster care, dim lights
  • Prevent fever — ↑ metabolic demand → ↑ ICP; use antipyretics
  • Strict I&O — monitor for SIADH or Diabetes Insipidus (both occur post-neuro injury)
  • Seizure precautions — padded rails, suction, anticonvulsants
  • Avoid Valsalva maneuver — no straining, coughing, bearing down; stool softeners
  • Neuro checks every 1–2 hours or per protocol
