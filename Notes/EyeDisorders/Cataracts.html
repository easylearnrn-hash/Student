Cataracts

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition & Pathophysiology

Cataract = clouding (opacity) of the lens of the eye → progressive, painless vision loss.

Mechanism: Proteins in the lens clump together → cloudy area forms → blocks/distorts light from reaching the retina → blurred/dim vision that worsens over time.

No pain — cataracts develop slowly and silently.

2. Types of Cataracts

3. Risk Factors

  • Aging (most common)
  • Prolonged corticosteroid use
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Smoking, alcohol use
  • Excessive UV/sunlight exposure
  • Eye injury or prior eye surgery
  • Family history

4. Signs & Symptoms

  • ✅ Painless, gradual vision loss
  • ✅ Blurry or cloudy vision
  • ✅ Glare or halos around lights (especially at night)
  • ✅ Poor night vision; difficulty driving at night
  • ✅ Faded colors
  • ✅ Double vision in one eye
  • ✅ Frequent prescription changes for glasses

5. Diagnosis

  • Visual acuity test (Snellen chart)
  • Slit-lamp examination (magnifies eye structures)
  • Ophthalmoscope (detects lens cloudiness)
  • Tonometry (rules out glaucoma)

6. Treatment — Surgery Only

No medication cures cataracts — surgical removal is the ONLY effective treatment.

Phacoemulsification (Most Common)

  • Small ultrasonic probe breaks up (emulsifies) the cloudy lens
  • Lens fragments suctioned out
  • Clear artificial intraocular lens (IOL) implanted
  • Outpatient procedure under local anesthesia

Pre-op Nursing Care

  • NPO 6–12 hours before surgery
  • Administer mydriatic eye drops (e.g., atropine) to dilate pupil
  • Antibiotic drops may be prescribed pre-op
  • Ensure informed consent signed

7. Post-op Nursing Care

  • ✅ Wear eye shield, especially at night
  • ✅ Avoid bending, lifting >5–10 lbs, straining, coughing (↑ IOP)
  • ✅ Position: semi-Fowler's (HOB elevated)
  • ✅ Administer antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops as prescribed
  • ✅ Do NOT rub or press the eye
  • ✅ Avoid getting water in the eye while bathing
  • ✅ Use sunglasses to protect from bright light
  • ✅ Avoid driving until cleared by provider
  • ✅ Full recovery: 4–6 weeks
Report immediately post-op:
  • Sudden severe eye pain
  • Sudden vision loss
  • Increased redness, swelling, or discharge
  • Nausea/vomiting (may indicate ↑ IOP)

8. Complications

ComplicationDescription
Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO)Clouding behind the IOL → laser treatment (YAG capsulotomy)
Endophthalmitis (infection)Inflammation/infection inside the eye — urgent
Retinal DetachmentFlashes, floaters, curtain over vision → EMERGENCY
Increased IOPMay lead to glaucoma post-surgery

9. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Cataracts = gradual, painless vision loss
  • ✅ Surgery (phacoemulsification + IOL) = only cure
  • ✅ Mydriatics (atropine) used before surgery to dilate pupil
  • ✅ Post-op: no bending, lifting, rubbing — wear eye shield
  • ✅ Sudden post-op pain + vision loss = emergency complication
  • ✅ Vision improves gradually over several days/weeks
NCLEX: "No medications cure cataracts" — if a stem describes a patient with blurry, painless, gradual vision changes → think cataracts → surgery.
