Glaucoma

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition & Pathophysiology

Glaucoma = progressive optic neuropathy caused by increased intraocular pressure (IOP) → optic nerve damage → vision loss.

Normal IOP: 10–21 mmHg

Pathway: Aqueous humor produced by ciliary body → drains through trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm. When drainage is impaired → IOP rises → optic nerve compressed → gradual peripheral vision loss (tunnel vision).

POAG Risk Factors

  • Age >40, African American descent
  • Diabetes mellitus, hypertension
  • Family history
  • Long-term corticosteroid use

AACG Risk Factors

  • Asian descent, hyperopia (farsightedness)
  • Sudden pupil dilation (dark room, stress, anticholinergic meds)
AACG Signs: Sudden severe eye pain, headache, nausea/vomiting, blurred vision, halos around lights, fixed mid-dilated pupil → EMERGENCY — reduce IOP immediately!

3. Diagnosis

  • Tonometry (gold standard) — measures IOP
  • Ophthalmoscopy — optic disc cupping (excavation)
  • Visual field testing (perimetry) — detects peripheral vision loss
  • Gonioscopy — evaluates drainage angle (open vs. closed)

4. Medications

💧 Prostaglandin Analogs — Latanoprost, Bimatoprost, Travoprost
Action: Increase aqueous humor outflow (first-line for POAG)
SE: Darkened iris pigmentation, eyelash growth, eye redness
💧 Beta-Blockers — Timolol, Betaxolol
Action: Decrease aqueous humor production
SE: Bradycardia, hypotension, bronchospasm
Contraindicated in: asthma, COPD, bradycardia, heart block
💧 Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors — Acetazolamide, Dorzolamide
Action: Decrease aqueous humor production; used in acute angle-closure
SE: Sulfa allergy cross-reaction, metabolic acidosis, diuresis
💧 Alpha Agonists — Brimonidine, Apraclonidine
Action: Decrease production & increase drainage of aqueous humor
SE: Dry mouth, fatigue, ocular allergy
💧 Miotics — Pilocarpine (cholinergic)
Action: Constricts pupil, opens drainage angle; used in AACG
SE: Blurred vision, brow ache, dim vision in low light
NEVER give anticholinergics (atropine) or mydriatics in glaucoma — they dilate the pupil and close the drainage angle, raising IOP.

5. Surgical Interventions

ProcedureUse
Laser TrabeculoplastyPOAG — opens trabecular meshwork to improve drainage
Laser Peripheral IridotomyAACG — creates hole in iris to relieve acute pressure
TrabeculectomySevere cases — creates new drainage pathway

Post-op Care

  • Avoid straining, heavy lifting, coughing
  • Do NOT rub or press on the eye
  • Use prescribed drops as directed
  • Wear protective eyewear
  • Report sudden eye pain, vision changes, or redness immediately

6. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ POAG = gradual, painless peripheral vision loss — most common
  • ✅ AACG = sudden, severe eye pain + nausea + fixed dilated pupil = EMERGENCY
  • ✅ Lifelong medication compliance — glaucoma has no cure
  • ✅ Avoid anticholinergics/antihistamines (dilate pupils → raise IOP)
  • ✅ Regular eye exams for those >40 or at risk
  • ✅ Miotics (pilocarpine) = constrict pupil = open angle
  • ✅ Mydriatics (atropine) = dilate pupil = closed angle = DANGEROUS
Tunnel vision on NCLEX → think Open-Angle Glaucoma (peripheral vision lost first).
