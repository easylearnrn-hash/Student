Conjunctivitis vs. Keratitis

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Comparison

1. Overview

Conjunctivitis

Inflammation of the conjunctiva — the clear mucous membrane lining the inner eyelids and covering the white sclera.

  • Usually mild, self-limiting
  • Vision typically NOT affected
  • Minimal to no pain
  • Often infectious (bacterial/viral)

Keratitis

Inflammation or infection of the cornea — the transparent dome-shaped layer at the very front of the eye.

  • Potentially sight-threatening
  • Vision can be seriously impaired
  • PAINFUL — significant discomfort
  • Photophobia prominent

3. Keratitis — Causes

TypeCauseKey Notes
BacterialPseudomonas aeruginosa, Staph aureusMost common with contact lens overwear; rapid corneal destruction with Pseudomonas
Viral (HSV)Herpes Simplex VirusDendritic (branching) corneal ulcer on stain; most common infectious keratitis in developed world; recurrent
FungalFusarium, Aspergillus, CandidaAfter corneal trauma (plant/dirt); indolent course
AcanthamoebaProtozoan in waterContact lens wearers who use tap water; severe ring infiltrate; very painful
Non-infectiousUV exposure (welder's flash, snow blindness), corneal scratch, dry eyePhotokeratitis = UV damage; symptoms 6–12 hrs after exposure

4. Keratitis — Diagnosis

  • Slit-lamp exam — gold standard; visualizes corneal layers, ulcer depth
  • Fluorescein staining — dye applied to cornea; under cobalt blue light, ulcers/abrasions glow green; dendritic pattern = HSV
  • Corneal scraping + cultures — identifies bacterial, fungal, or Acanthamoeba organisms
  • Visual acuity testing — determines degree of impairment

5. Keratitis — Treatment

TypeTreatment
BacterialFortified topical antibiotics — ciprofloxacin, gentamicin drops (hourly initially)
HSV ViralAntiviral drops — trifluridine; oral acyclovir or valacyclovir; NO corticosteroids
FungalNatamycin (topical antifungal); systemic voriconazole for severe cases
AcanthamoebaPHMB (polyhexamethylene biguanide) + propamidine isethionate drops
UV/Non-infectiousCycloplegic drops, lubricants, patching; heals in 24–48 hrs
Never apply corticosteroid eye drops to an undiagnosed red, painful eye — can dangerously worsen HSV keratitis and fungal keratitis.

6. Keratitis — Nursing Care

  • Refer immediately — do NOT delay with home remedies
  • Stop contact lens use immediately; discard contaminated lenses and case
  • Wear sunglasses for photophobia relief
  • Administer prescribed drops on tight schedule (may be q1h initially)
  • Never rinse contact lenses with tap water (Acanthamoeba risk)
  • Monitor for corneal ulcer progression or perforation

7. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Conjunctivitis = no pain, vision intact, benign; Keratitis = pain + blurred + photophobia = EMERGENCY
  • ✅ Fluorescein staining — green glow = corneal ulcer/abrasion; dendritic = HSV
  • ✅ Contact lens + tap water = Acanthamoeba keratitis risk
  • ✅ HSV keratitis = NO steroids (will worsen significantly)
  • ✅ Pseudomonas + contact lens = bacterial keratitis (rapid corneal destruction)
  • ✅ Treat keratitis aggressively — permanent scarring = permanent vision loss
Quick rule: Eye red but no pain + vision OK → Conjunctivitis. Eye red + PAINFUL + vision blurred + photophobia → Keratitis → urgent.
