Retinal Detachment

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition

Retinal detachment = medical emergency where the retina (light-sensitive layer) separates from the choroid (vascular layer supplying oxygen).

Separation → ischemia → permanent vision loss if untreated.

No self-healing — retinal detachment is a medical emergency requiring immediate surgery.

2. Types

TypeMechanismCommon Cause
Rhegmatogenous (most common)Retinal tear/hole → vitreous fluid seeps under retina → pushes it awayAging, myopia, eye trauma
TractionalScar tissue on retina pulls it away from choroidDiabetic retinopathy
Exudative (Serous)Fluid accumulates under retina without tearInflammation, tumors, hypertension

3. Risk Factors

  • Aging (vitreous shrinks → pulls on retina)
  • Severe myopia (nearsightedness) — elongated eyeball stretches retina
  • Previous eye surgery (especially cataract surgery)
  • Eye trauma (blunt or penetrating)
  • Diabetic retinopathy
  • Family history
  • Inflammatory eye disorders, tumors

4. Signs & Symptoms — Painless & Sudden

  • 🔦 Flashes of light (photopsia)
  • ⚫ Sudden increase in floaters (spots, cobwebs, dark spots)
  • 🪟 Shadow or curtain over part of visual field
  • 👁 Blurred or decreased vision
NCLEX cue: "Curtain or shadow over vision" + "sudden floaters/flashes" = retinal detachment = notify provider STAT.

5. Diagnosis

  • Fundoscopic exam (ophthalmoscopy) — direct view of retina, detects tears/detachment
  • OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography) — cross-sectional retinal imaging
  • Ocular ultrasound — used if blood/debris blocks retinal view

6. Pre-op Nursing Care

  • Keep patient supine to prevent further detachment
  • Cover both eyes to reduce eye movement (if advised)
  • Minimize head movements
  • Notify ophthalmologist immediately

7. Surgical Procedures

ProcedureMechanismKey Point
Laser PhotocoagulationLaser seals small tears by creating scar tissueFor small tears before full detachment
CryopexyFreezes and seals retinal tearSimilar to laser but uses cold
Pneumatic RetinopexyGas bubble injected into vitreous to press retina backPatient must maintain face-down position for days
Scleral BucklingSilicone band sewn onto sclera to push eye wall against retinaCommon for rhegmatogenous
VitrectomyRemoves vitreous gel (especially with hemorrhage or scar tissue)Used in tractional detachment or complex cases

8. Post-op Nursing Care

  • ✅ Position as instructed — often face-down after gas bubble surgery (to keep bubble against retina)
  • ✅ Avoid activities that increase IOP: bending, heavy lifting, straining, forceful sneezing/coughing
  • ✅ Wear eye shield especially while sleeping
  • ✅ Use prescribed antibiotic and anti-inflammatory drops
  • ✅ Do NOT rub or press on the eye
  • ✅ No flying if gas bubble used (air pressure can expand bubble → damage)
  • ✅ Regular ophthalmology follow-up
Report immediately: sudden vision changes, increasing floaters, flashes, or pain after surgery.

9. Complications

  • Permanent vision loss if untreated
  • Re-detachment of retina
  • Endophthalmitis (eye infection)
  • Cataracts or glaucoma as treatment side effects

10. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Retinal detachment = painless but causes sudden vision loss
  • ✅ Classic signs: flashes, floaters, curtain/shadow over vision
  • ✅ Priority: notify provider immediately → emergency surgery
  • ✅ Post-op: face-down position (gas bubble); no flying
  • ✅ Highest risk: myopia, diabetic retinopathy, prior eye surgery, trauma
