Eye Disorders

Retinal Detachment

Separation of the neurosensory retina from RPE — sight-threatening emergency

Overview
🚨 EMERGENCY: Retinal detachment is a sight-threatening emergency. Macula-off detachment = hours to days before permanent central vision loss. Any new curtain, shadow, or shower of floaters = STAT ophthalmology referral.
Types

Rhegmatogenous (Most Common)

  • "Rhegma" = break/tear
  • Retinal tear → vitreous fluid enters subretinal space → detachment
  • Risk: myopia (nearsightedness), trauma, prior cataract surgery
  • Surgical emergency

Tractional

  • Fibrovascular membranes pull retina from RPE
  • Causes: proliferative diabetic retinopathy, sickle cell, ROP
  • Progressive, not as acute
  • Vitrectomy required

Exudative (Serous)

  • Fluid accumulates without tear; from inflammation or tumors
  • Causes: choroidal tumors, posterior scleritis, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada
  • No tear present; shifting subretinal fluid on exam
  • Treat underlying cause
Risk Factors

Who Is at Risk

  • High myopia (>-6 D) — most significant risk factor
  • Prior retinal detachment (10–15% recurrence)
  • Lattice degeneration of peripheral retina
  • Cataract surgery (especially complicated/historical)
  • Trauma to the eye
  • Age (vitreous liquefaction and PVD)
  • Family history
  • Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (tractional type)
Prodrome: Posterior Vitreous Detachment (PVD)
Teaching Point: PVD is the precursor to retinal detachment. It presents with sudden new floaters ± flashes. Most PVDs are benign — but all require urgent exam to rule out retinal tear.
Clinical Manifestations

Classic Symptoms (NCLEX Must-Know)

  • Floaters — "cobwebs", "hair", "spots" suddenly appearing
  • Photopsia (flashes) — flashing lights, especially in peripheral vision
  • Shadow / curtain / veil — gray shadow advancing across visual field
  • Painless — always painless (key distinguishing feature)
  • Vision may be normal initially if macula attached
  • Central vision loss = macula-off detachment (worse prognosis)

Surgical Treatment
1
Pneumatic Retinopexy — Gas bubble injected into vitreous; head positioning keeps bubble over tear for 1–2 weeks. Laser/cryo seals tear. For superior tears.
2
Scleral Buckle — Silicone band sewn around globe to indent sclera → closes tear. Gold standard for rhegmatogenous RD, young patients.
3
Vitrectomy — Remove vitreous; laser barricade; gas or silicone oil tamponade. For complex detachments, PDR-related tractional.
Post-operative Nursing Care

Priority Post-Op Interventions

  • Positioning is CRITICAL: head positioning per surgeon instruction
  • Gas bubble → face-down or specific position to keep bubble over tear (23 hrs/day)
  • NO FLYING while gas bubble present — altitude change expands gas → IOP spike (risk of blindness)
  • No nitrous oxide anesthesia while gas bubble in eye
  • Activity restrictions: no strenuous activity, no bending, no lifting
  • Silicone oil: must be surgically removed later; long-term positioning less strict
  • Monitor for IOP elevation, endophthalmitis, re-detachment
🚨 NO FLYING: Patients with intraocular gas bubble must NOT fly. Rapid altitude decrease in cabin pressure causes gas expansion → acute IOP rise → optic nerve damage → blindness.
⚠️ NCLEX: Post-retinal detachment surgery — patient is found crying, face down, refusing to move. The nurse's priority is to explain that this positioning is essential for the gas bubble to seal the retinal tear. Compliance = vision.
