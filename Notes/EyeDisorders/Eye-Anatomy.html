Eye Anatomy & Physiology

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Overview

The eye captures light and produces a clear image on the retina. The retina processes visual information and sends it to the brain via the optic nerve.

Three main layers: Outer (protective) → Middle (vascular) → Inner (sensory/retina)

2. Outer Coat — Protective Layer

Structure% of EyeFunctionKey Feature
Sclera ("white of eye")93%Protection; muscle attachmentOpaque — no light passes through
Cornea (front)7%Light entry; focusingTransparent — light must pass through
Limbus (junction)—Transition zoneContains stem cells that regenerate corneal epithelium
Cornea has no blood supply — it gets oxygen/nutrients from aqueous humor and tears.

3. Middle Coat — Vascular & Nutritive Layer

StructureFunctionNCLEX Key
ChoroidBlood supply to retina; absorbs excess lightVascular layer — damaged in many eye diseases
Ciliary BodyProduces aqueous humor; controls lens shape (accommodation)Dysfunction → glaucoma; target of many glaucoma drugs
IrisControls pupil size → regulates light entering the eyeBright light → pupils constrict; dim light → pupils dilate
PupilOpening in iris for light passageFixed, mid-dilated pupil = acute angle-closure glaucoma

4. Inner Coat — Retina (Sensory Layer)

Layers

  • Pigment Layer — contains melanin; absorbs excess light; contributes to eye color
  • Neural Layer — contains photoreceptor cells (rods and cones)

Photoreceptors

TypeFunctionLocation
RodsLight intensity (black & white vision, peripheral, night vision)Peripheral retina
ConesColor vision (red, green, blue)Macula/fovea — central retina
Macula = area of sharpest central vision (damaged in AMD). Optic disc = blind spot (no photoreceptors; where optic nerve exits).

5. Eye Fluids (Humors)

FluidLocationFunctionNCLEX Key
Aqueous HumorAnterior chamber (front)Nourishes cornea and lens; maintains IOPDrainage failure → ↑ IOP → Glaucoma
Vitreous HumorPosterior chamber (back)Maintains eye shape; supports retinaGel-like; floaters appear when it degenerates
Aqueous humor drainage: Produced by ciliary body → flows through pupil → drains via trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm. Obstruction = Glaucoma.

6. Lens

  • Clear, flexible, biconvex structure behind the iris
  • Focuses light onto the retina
  • Ciliary muscles + zonular fibers (suspensory ligaments) change lens shape for near/far focus (accommodation)
  • Clouding of lens = Cataract

7. Optic Nerve

  • Cranial Nerve II
  • Carries visual signals from retina to brain
  • Exits at optic disc (blind spot — no photoreceptors)
  • Progressive damage = Glaucoma → irreversible vision loss
