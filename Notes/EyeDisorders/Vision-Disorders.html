Vision Disorders Overview

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Refractive Errors

ConditionDescriptionVision ProblemCorrection
Myopia (Nearsightedness) Eyeball too long; light focuses in front of retina Blurry far, clear near Concave (minus) lenses; LASIK
Hyperopia (Farsightedness) Eyeball too short; light focuses behind retina Blurry near, clearer far Convex (plus) lenses; LASIK
Astigmatism Irregular corneal/lens curvature; multiple focal points Blurry at all distances; distortion Cylindrical lenses; toric contacts; LASIK
Presbyopia Age-related stiffening of lens (loses accommodation) Difficulty reading small print — starts ~age 40 Reading glasses; bifocals; progressive lenses
Presbyopia is a NORMAL aging change, not a disease — everyone develops it after age 40.

2. Strabismus (Crossed Eyes)

Strabismus = misalignment of the eyes; they do not point in the same direction simultaneously.

  • Types: Esotropia (eye turns in), Exotropia (turns out), Hypertropia (turns up)
  • Common in children; may be congenital or acquired
  • Causes: muscle imbalance, cranial nerve palsy (CN III, IV, VI), refractive error
  • Risk: If untreated → amblyopia (lazy eye)
  • Diagnosis: cover-uncover test, Hirschberg corneal light reflex test

Treatment

  • Corrective glasses/prisms
  • Patching the stronger (dominant) eye to force use of weaker eye
  • Eye exercises (orthoptics)
  • Surgery to realign eye muscles
  • Botulinum toxin injection into eye muscles
Treat before age 7 — the brain's visual pathways are still developing; late treatment = permanent deficit.

3. Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)

Amblyopia = reduced vision in one eye due to the brain suppressing its input (usually because the other eye is dominant).

  • Most common cause of visual impairment in children
  • Causes: strabismus, significant refractive error difference, cataracts, ptosis
  • Eye looks structurally normal but brain "ignores" it
  • Treatment: patch the GOOD eye ×hours/day to force weaker eye to work; atropine drops to blur good eye; glasses
  • Must treat in childhood (before age 7–9) for best results

4. Glaucoma (Summary)

  • ↑ intraocular pressure → optic nerve damage → peripheral vision loss
  • POAG (Primary Open-Angle): most common; silent, slow; visual field changes on testing
  • AACG (Acute Angle-Closure): emergency — severe eye pain, halos, headache, nausea
  • Treatment: eye drops, laser, or surgery to reduce IOP
  • NEVER give anticholinergics (atropine) to glaucoma patients — dilates pupil → increases IOP → crisis

5. Cataracts (Summary)

  • Clouding of the natural lens → blurry/foggy vision, glare, halos, faded colors
  • Most common cause: aging (senile cataracts); also diabetes, steroids, UV exposure
  • Only treatment: surgical removal (phacoemulsification) + intraocular lens (IOL) implant
  • Post-op: avoid bending, straining, heavy lifting; eye shield at night; report sudden pain

6. Retinal Detachment (Summary)

  • Retina separates from underlying RPE → ischemia → permanent vision loss if untreated
  • Symptoms: painless curtain/shadow over vision, floaters, flashes of light
  • Emergency — requires surgical repair (laser, cryotherapy, scleral buckle, vitrectomy)

7. Macular Degeneration (Summary)

  • Damage to macula → central vision loss (peripheral intact)
  • Dry (90%) = gradual; Wet (10%) = sudden (neovascularization)
  • Wet: metamorphopsia (wavy lines) = danger sign; treat with anti-VEGF injections
  • Dry: AREDS2 supplements, Amsler grid monitoring

8. Diabetic Retinopathy (Summary)

  • Hyperglycemia → retinal vessel damage → ischemia → neovascularization
  • NPDR (early) → PDR (advanced, dangerous)
  • All diabetics: annual dilated eye exam
  • Control HbA1c <7% and BP <130/80

9. Nursing Care — Visually Impaired Patients

Safety & Fall Prevention

  • Orient patient to environment; keep items in consistent locations
  • Keep call light, phone, and personal items within reach
  • Adequate lighting; remove trip hazards (rugs, cords)
  • Use bed alarms; assist with ambulation
  • Side rails up; non-slip footwear

Communication & Feeding

  • Clock method for meals: describe food locations as clock positions ("potatoes at 3 o'clock, chicken at 6 o'clock")
  • Announce yourself when entering the room
  • Describe the environment verbally; don't grab — guide the patient's hand
  • Allow patient to hold nurse's arm when walking (sighted guide technique)

Medication Administration

  • Use large-print labels on medication containers
  • Pill organizers for home medication management
  • Teach nasolacrimal occlusion for eye drops
  • Verify patient can self-administer eye drops correctly

10. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Presbyopia after age 40 = normal aging, not pathology
  • ✅ Strabismus + amblyopia = treat before age 7 for best outcomes
  • ✅ Patch the GOOD eye in amblyopia treatment
  • ✅ Glaucoma = NO atropine or anticholinergics ever
  • ✅ Poor vision = fall risk — always assess and implement safety measures
  • ✅ Clock method for meal trays in visually impaired patients
  • ✅ AACG = sudden eye pain + halos + HA + N/V = emergency
  • ✅ Cataracts post-op: avoid IOP-raising activities (bending, straining, coughing, lifting)
Priority nursing diagnoses for vision impairment: Risk for injury (falls), Disturbed sensory perception, Self-care deficit.
