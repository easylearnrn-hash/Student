Refractive Errors & Vision Disorders

Myopia, hyperopia, astigmatism, amblyopia, strabismus — diagnosis and correction

Refractive Errors Overview
Key Concept: Refractive errors occur when light is not focused precisely on the fovea. They are the most common cause of visual impairment worldwide. Most are correctable with glasses, contact lenses, or refractive surgery.

Myopia (Nearsightedness)

Features

  • Most common refractive error
  • Increasing prevalence worldwide (50% by 2050 projected)
  • High myopia (>-6D) → risk for retinal detachment, glaucoma, macular degeneration
  • Onset: childhood; often stabilizes in 20s
  • Control: orthokeratology, atropine eye drops in children

Surgical Options

  • LASIK — flap + laser ablation; most common; rapid recovery
  • PRK — no flap; better for thin corneas; slower recovery
  • SMILE — newer flapless procedure
  • ICL (Implantable Collamer Lens) — for high myopia not suitable for LASIK
Amblyopia ("Lazy Eye")

Amblyopia — Critical for NCLEX Pediatrics

  • Reduced visual acuity in one eye due to abnormal visual development during sensitive period (birth–8 years)
  • Causes: strabismus (misalignment), anisometropia (unequal refractive errors), visual deprivation (cataract, ptosis)
  • Brain suppresses input from weaker eye → cortical changes
  • Treatment: Correct underlying cause + patching of the STRONGER eye to force use of weaker eye
  • Time-sensitive — treatment after age 8–10 is much less effective
  • Goal: achieve equal best-corrected visual acuity in both eyes

Diplopia (Double Vision)

Monocular Diplopia

  • Persists with one eye closed
  • Cause: within the eye (astigmatism, cataract, corneal irregularity, dislocated lens)
  • Not a neurological emergency

Binocular Diplopia

  • Resolves with one eye closed
  • Cause: extraocular muscle or cranial nerve dysfunction
  • CN III palsy (posterior communicating artery aneurysm!) — emergency
  • CN VI palsy — increased ICP, Wernicke's
  • Thyroid eye disease, myasthenia gravis
🚨 Emergency: New painful CN III palsy with pupil involvement (dilated pupil) = posterior communicating artery aneurysm until proven otherwise. Immediate CT/CTA + neurosurgery.
Color Vision Deficiency

Color Blindness

  • X-linked recessive — affects ~8% males, 0.5% females
  • Red-green most common (OD1/OD2 gene mutations)
  • Ishihara plates — screening test
  • No treatment; adapt environment (labels, symbols, not color-coded alone)
  • Nursing: medication labels/IV tubes should not rely solely on color coding
