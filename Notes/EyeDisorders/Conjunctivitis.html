Eye Disorders

Conjunctivitis

Inflammation of the conjunctiva — bacterial, viral, or allergic

Overview
Key Concept: "Pink eye" = conjunctival inflammation. Distinguish type by discharge, onset, and symptoms — treatment differs significantly. Bacterial requires antibiotics; viral is self-limiting.

Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Bacterial (Including Gonococcal/Chlamydial)

  • Common organisms: S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae
  • Neonatal (ophthalmia neonatorum): GC or Chlamydia — prophylaxis with erythromycin ointment at birth
  • Gonococcal: hyperacute, copious purulent discharge — systemic antibiotics + topical
  • Treatment: Erythromycin, trimethoprim-polymyxin, fluoroquinolone drops
  • Duration: 5–7 days
Viral Conjunctivitis

Viral (Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis)

  • Adenovirus most common; also HSV, EBV
  • Highly contagious — contact precautions, handwashing essential
  • Follicles on palpebral conjunctiva (vs. papillae in allergic)
  • Resolves in 2–3 weeks spontaneously
  • HSV: dendritic ulcers on cornea → antiviral (acyclovir/trifluridine)
  • Supportive: cool compresses, artificial tears
Allergic Conjunctivitis

Allergic

  • IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation → histamine release
  • Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC) — contact lens wearers
  • Treatment: Olopatadine (Pataday) — combined antihistamine + mast cell stabilizer
  • Oral antihistamines, avoid allergen, cool compresses
  • Avoid eye rubbing — worsens mast cell release
Nursing Interventions

Key Actions

  • Contact precautions for viral/bacterial — wash hands before/after eye care
  • Do NOT share towels, pillowcases, washcloths, makeup
  • Administer drops from inner to outer canthus; do not touch dropper to eye
  • Warm compresses for bacterial; cool for allergic/viral
  • Avoid contact lenses until resolved + 24h after antibiotics
  • Educate: discard old makeup, replace contact lens case
⚠️ NCLEX: Corneal involvement (pain, photophobia, decreased vision) = KERATITIS, not simple conjunctivitis → urgent ophthalmology referral.
Neonatal Considerations
🚨 Neonatal Alert: Ophthalmia neonatorum (gonococcal) can cause corneal perforation and blindness within days. Prophylactic erythromycin 0.5% ointment at birth is standard of care in the US.
