Conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye")

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition

Conjunctivitis = inflammation or infection of the conjunctiva (the clear membrane lining the inner eyelid and covering the white of the eye).

  • One of the most common eye conditions
  • Presents with redness, discharge, and irritation
  • Usually does NOT affect vision (key distinguishing feature from keratitis)

2. Types — Comparison Table

TypeCauseDischargeKey FeaturesTreatment
Bacterial Staph aureus, Strep, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae, Chlamydia Thick, yellow-green, purulent; lid crusting on waking Starts unilateral → spreads to both eyes; highly contagious Antibiotic eye drops (erythromycin, ciprofloxacin, tobramycin)
Viral Adenovirus (most common), HSV, Enterovirus Watery, clear Often with URI (cold symptoms); preauricular lymphadenopathy; self-limiting 1–2 weeks; highly contagious Supportive — cool compresses, artificial tears, rest. HSV → antivirals (acyclovir)
Allergic Pollen, dust mites, pet dander, latex, cosmetics Stringy, white or clear mucus Bilateral; severe itching (hallmark); seasonal or perennial; no contagion Antihistamine drops (olopatadine/Pataday), mast cell stabilizers, avoid allergen
Chemical Chlorine, acid/alkali splashes, harsh chemicals Watery; no true discharge Immediate burning, tearing; pain on exposure; no infectious component Immediate irrigation with sterile saline or water ×15–20 min; do NOT delay

4. Neonatal Conjunctivitis (Ophthalmia Neonatorum)

High-priority NCLEX topic!
  • Caused by: N. gonorrhoeae or Chlamydia trachomatis (acquired during delivery through infected birth canal)
  • Gonococcal: presents in first 24–48 hours; severe, can cause corneal perforation and blindness
  • Chlamydial: presents 5–14 days after birth
  • Prevention: Mandatory prophylactic erythromycin ophthalmic ointment 0.5% applied within 1 hour of birth for ALL newborns
  • Treatment: systemic antibiotics (IV penicillin for gonorrhea; oral erythromycin for Chlamydia)

5. Infection Control

  • Bacterial and viral conjunctivitis are highly contagious
  • Wash hands frequently (before and after touching eyes)
  • Do NOT share towels, pillowcases, washcloths, or eye drops
  • Avoid touching or rubbing eyes
  • No contact lenses until infection fully resolved + 24 hours after antibiotics
  • Stay home from school or work until discharge has cleared (usually 24 hrs on antibiotics)
  • Use Contact/Standard precautions for hospitalized patients

6. Nursing Considerations

  • Assess type of discharge (helps identify cause)
  • Apply warm or cool compresses for comfort (do NOT share cloths)
  • Instill eye drops/ointment from inner canthus outward
  • Teach patient to complete full antibiotic course even if symptoms improve
  • Do NOT use corticosteroid drops unless prescribed — can worsen viral (HSV) infections
  • Refer for culture if N. gonorrhoeae suspected

7. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Bacterial = yellow-green thick discharge; treat with antibiotics
  • ✅ Viral = watery discharge; self-limiting; supportive care only (except HSV)
  • ✅ Allergic = severe itching + bilateral; antihistamines
  • ✅ Chemical = immediate saline irrigation first — don't wait for orders
  • ✅ Neonatal = erythromycin ointment prophylaxis for ALL newborns
  • ✅ Highly contagious — teach hand hygiene, no contact lenses, no sharing items
  • ✅ Vision is usually NOT affected (distinguishes from keratitis)
  • ✅ Do NOT use steroids in HSV conjunctivitis — can cause corneal ulceration
Memory aid: "Pink eye = red + discharge." Type of discharge tells you the cause.
