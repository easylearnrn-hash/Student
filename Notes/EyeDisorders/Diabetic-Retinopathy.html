Diabetic Retinopathy

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition & Pathophysiology

Chronic hyperglycemia damages the microvasculature of the retina through this cascade:

  1. High blood glucose → weakens capillary walls
  2. Microaneurysms form (tiny bulges in vessels)
  3. Vessels leak fluid, blood, lipids into retina (hard exudates)
  4. Retinal ischemia → body creates new abnormal vessels (neovascularization)
  5. New vessels bleed into vitreous or cause retinal detachment
Bottom line: Uncontrolled blood sugar = damaged retinal vessels = progressive vision loss and potential blindness.

2. Stages / Types

StageDescriptionFeatures
NPDR
(Non-Proliferative)		 Early stage — no new vessel growth yet Microaneurysms, dot/blot hemorrhages, hard exudates (lipid leakage), macular edema, cotton wool spots (nerve fiber infarcts). Often asymptomatic early on.
PDR
(Proliferative)		 Advanced stage — neovascularization present New fragile blood vessels grow on retinal surface and vitreous face. Risk of: vitreous hemorrhage, tractional retinal detachment, severe vision loss. This is the dangerous stage.

3. Risk Factors

  • Long duration of diabetes mellitus (both Type 1 & Type 2)
  • Poor glycemic control (HbA1c >7%)
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperlipidemia
  • Nephropathy (kidney disease)
  • Smoking
  • Pregnancy (can accelerate progression)
Duration of diabetes + poor blood sugar control = two biggest risk factors.

4. Signs & Symptoms

NPDR (Early)

  • Often asymptomatic until macular edema develops
  • Blurred or fluctuating vision
  • Reduced color perception
  • Occasional floaters

PDR (Advanced)

  • Sudden vision loss
  • Cobweb or spider-web floaters (vitreous hemorrhage)
  • Flashes of light (photopsia)
  • Dark curtain over visual field (retinal detachment)
EMERGENCY: Floaters + flashes of light + "curtain" over vision in a diabetic = possible retinal detachment or vitreous hemorrhage → report immediately.

5. Diagnosis

  • Dilated fundoscopic exam — gold standard; visualizes microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates, neovascularization. Annual exam required for ALL diabetics.
  • Fluorescein Angiography — dye injected IV; maps leaky vessels and ischemic areas
  • OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography) — detects macular edema; cross-sectional imaging
  • Visual Acuity Testing — assesses degree of vision loss

6. Treatment

Medical Management (First Line)

  • Glycemic control — HbA1c <7% target (most important prevention measure)
  • BP control — <130/80 mmHg
  • Lipid management — statins for hyperlipidemia
  • Smoking cessation

Procedural Treatments

  • Focal Laser Photocoagulation — treats macular edema by sealing leaking vessels near macula
  • Pan-Retinal Photocoagulation (PRP) — treats PDR; destroys peripheral ischemic retina to reduce neovascularization stimulus (1,200–1,600 laser burns)
  • Anti-VEGF Injections (Intravitreal) — Bevacizumab (Avastin), Ranibizumab (Lucentis), Aflibercept (Eylea); reduces neovascularization and macular edema
  • Vitrectomy — surgical removal of vitreous gel when vitreous hemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment occurs

7. Nursing Considerations

  • Educate all diabetic patients: annual dilated eye exam is mandatory even without symptoms
  • Stress importance of HbA1c <7% and consistent blood sugar monitoring
  • Teach to report immediately: sudden vision loss, new floaters, flashes, curtain effect
  • Post-laser: temporary blurred vision is expected; avoid driving until cleared
  • Post-intravitreal injection: watch for signs of endophthalmitis (severe pain, redness, worsening vision)
  • Post-vitrectomy: follow positioning instructions; teach activity restrictions (no heavy lifting)

8. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ All diabetics need annual dilated eye exams — even if asymptomatic
  • ✅ NPDR = non-proliferative (early, often no symptoms); PDR = proliferative (neovascularization = dangerous)
  • ✅ Floaters + flashes + curtain = emergency (retinal detachment or hemorrhage)
  • ✅ HbA1c <7% = primary prevention strategy
  • ✅ Anti-VEGF + PRP laser = main treatments for PDR
  • ✅ Neovascularization = hallmark of PDR
  • ✅ Cotton-wool spots = nerve fiber infarcts (ischemia)
Memory aid: Diabetic Retinopathy = "Sugar destroys small vessels → retina suffers → new leaky vessels form → vision lost."
