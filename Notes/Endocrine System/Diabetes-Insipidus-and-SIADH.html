Diabetes Insipidus & SIADH

ADH disorders — DI (too little ADH) vs SIADH (too much ADH), types, treatment, water deprivation test

ADH — Antidiuretic Hormone Overview

What Is ADH?

  • Produced by the hypothalamus
  • Stored and released by the posterior pituitary gland (via nerve fibers)
  • Function: tells kidneys to keep water (reduces urine output); also constricts blood vessels
  • Also called vasopressin
Memory: "ADH = Anti-Diuretic Hormone = keeps water IN." When ADH is high → less urine (oliguria). When ADH is low → lots of urine (polyuria).
🧪 Urine Output Terms — NCLEX Ranges
  • Normal urine output (expected adult urine output): about 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr or at least 30 mL/hr
    normal kidney perfusion and hydration
  • Polyuria (excessive urine output): > 3,000 mL/day
    too much urine; common with diabetes insipidus, uncontrolled diabetes mellitus, or diuretics
  • Oliguria (decreased urine output): < 400–500 mL/day or < 30 mL/hr
    too little urine; may indicate dehydration, shock, kidney injury, or obstruction
  • Anuria (absent or almost absent urine output): < 100 mL/day
    very little to no urine; medical emergency
NCLEX memory:
Normal = at least 30 mL/hr  |  Poly = too much  |  Oligo = too little  |  An = almost none
💧 Diabetes Insipidus (DI) — Low ADH
💡 Tip: DI = Hypovolemic / Hypernatremia

Causes

  • Head trauma
  • Damage to the hypothalamus or posterior pituitary
  • Pituitary surgery / hypophysectomy (surgery involving the pituitary gland)
  • Brain tumor
  • Brain infection or inflammation (such as meningitis or encephalitis)
⚠️ Important NCLEX note:
Demeclocycline / Declomycin is used for SIADH, but it can cause nephrogenic DI (kidneys do not respond to ADH) — this is different from central DI, where ADH is low.

Symptoms

Low ADH = too much water loss

  • ADH is low (central DI: body does not make/release enough ADH)
  • Polyuria (excessive urine output, often >3 L/day)
  • Polydipsia (extreme thirst)
  • Dehydration (body loses too much free water through urine)
  • Hypernatremia (high sodium because water is lost, so blood becomes concentrated)
  • Low urine specific gravity (very dilute urine, often <1.010)
  • Pale, dilute urine (urine looks very light because it has too much water)
  • Weight loss (from fluid loss)
  • Low BP / orthostatic hypotension (from hypovolemia)
  • Dizziness (from low blood volume)
  • Decreased LOC may occur (from severe dehydration or hypernatremia)
NCLEX memory:
DI = Dry Inside → too much urine, too much thirst, high sodium, low urine specific gravity.

DI Types — Neurogenic vs Nephrogenic

  • Neurogenic (Central): Brain/pituitary malfunction → can't make ADH
  • Nephrogenic: Kidney malfunction → kidneys don't respond to ADH
Water Deprivation Test (How to Tell Which Type)

Vasopressin Test Protocol

  • Initial assessment — measure weight, urine output, blood tests (baseline)
  • Water restriction — patient fasts from water for 8 hours
  • Administer Vasopressin → observe urine output:
    — If urine stops → Neurogenic DI (brain problem, responds to ADH)
    — If urine continues → Nephrogenic DI (kidneys don't respond)
  • Monitoring — track urine output, BP, weight throughout
Diabetes Insipidus Treatment

Neurogenic DI / Central DI

low ADH production or release from hypothalamus/posterior pituitary

  1. Desmopressin / DDAVP (synthetic ADH replacement)
  2. Adequate water intake (prevents dehydration)
  3. IV fluids if severe dehydration (given carefully based on sodium level)
  4. Monitor: urine output, daily weight, BP, serum sodium, and urine specific gravity

Nephrogenic DI

  • Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ/Microzide) — paradoxical: lowers urine output in nephrogenic DI
  • Na 0.45% IV (half-normal saline) — for rehydration
  • Vasopressin will NOT work (kidneys don't respond)
💧 SIADH — Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH Secretion (High ADH)
💡 Tip: SIADH = Hypervolemic / Hyponatremia
Memory (Spanish): "Sí, Sí ADH" = YES, ADH is HIGH. ADH keeps water inside — kidneys don't release enough water → hypervolemia + hyponatremia.

Causes

  • Small cell lung cancer
  • Damage to hypothalamus or pituitary
  • Infections (pneumonia, meningitis)
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome
  • Too much DI medication (excess Desmopressin)

Symptoms

  • ADH is HIGH
  • Oliguria (low urine output)
  • Extreme weight gain (mostly water)
  • HYPOnatremia (low sodium)
  • Urine specific gravity ↑ (dark, concentrated)
  • Disorientation, altered mental status (from hyponatremia)
  • ↑ HR and ↑ BP (from fluid overload/hypervolemia)

SIADH Treatment

  • Water restriction to 1 liter per day (limits fluid intake)
  • Declomycin (Demeclocycline — antibiotic that blocks ADH effect)
  • Lasix (Furosemide), Tolvaptan, or Naproxen — promote water excretion
  • 3% Hypertonic saline — for severe symptomatic hyponatremia (correct slowly to prevent osmotic demyelination)

SIADH Monitoring

  • Daily weights (key indicator of fluid retention)
  • Auscultate lungs for crackles (fluid overload sign)
  • ECG — monitor potassium (hyponatremia often coexists with K changes)
  • Strict intake and output (I&O)

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • DI = low ADH = polyuria + hypernatremia + dehydration
  • SIADH = high ADH = oliguria + hyponatremia + fluid overload
  • DI urine: pale, low specific gravity; SIADH urine: dark, high specific gravity
  • Vasopressin test: stops urine = Neurogenic; continues = Nephrogenic
  • Neurogenic DI → Desmopressin (nasal spray, one cavity)
  • Nephrogenic DI → HCTZ (paradoxically lowers urine output)
  • SIADH → water restrict 1L/day + 3% saline for severe hyponatremia
  • Monitor SIADH: daily weights + lung sounds for crackles
  • Most common cause of SIADH = small cell lung cancer
