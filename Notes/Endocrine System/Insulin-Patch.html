Insulin Patch & Pump

Transdermal delivery, basal vs bolus, infusion set changes, DKA risk, pump removal, site rotation

What is an Insulin Patch?
An insulin patch is a transdermal (through-the-skin) device that delivers continuous basal insulin in small, steady amounts throughout the day. It is an alternative insulin delivery method for patients who prefer fewer injections. Memory: "Patch = Painless Pump".
How It Works

Basal Delivery

  • Delivers a slow, steady low-level dose of insulin throughout the day
  • Controls fasting blood glucose
  • Replaces long-acting insulin injection

Bolus Delivery (some patches)

  • On-demand larger dose given with meals
  • Activated via remote controller, tap, or app
  • Controls postprandial (after-meal) glucose spikes

Examples of Insulin Patches/Pumps

  • V-Go — disposable, pre-filled, 24-hour patch with basal + bolus
  • CeQur Simplicity — wearable, bolus via button
  • Omnipod — tubeless patch pump, controlled by PDM remote
Indications

Who Uses Insulin Patches?

  • Type 1 or Type 2 DM patients who need basal or basal-bolus insulin
  • Patients who prefer needle-free delivery
  • Patients struggling with adherence to multiple daily injections (MDI)
  • Those wanting discreet, convenient insulin management

Infusion Set Changes

Change Every 48–72 Hours

  • Infusion sets must be changed every 2–3 days (48–72 hours)
  • Leaving sets in >4 days increases risk of:
    • Infection at site
    • Lipodystrophy (abnormal fat distribution)
    • Decreased insulin absorption
  • Rotate infusion sites each change to prevent lipodystrophy
  • Remove old patch before applying new one — never double-patch (overdose risk)
DKA Risk with Pumps/Patches

Removing the Pump Stops ALL Insulin Delivery

  • No pump → no insulin → rapid hyperglycemia
  • If prolonged (no backup insulin) → Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • Pumps increase DKA risk if:
    • Infusion set is blocked or kinked
    • No backup long-acting insulin is used
  • Hypoglycemia occurs from too much insulin (not pump removal)
NCLEX: Patient removes insulin pump for surgery → at risk for DKA (not hypoglycemia). Ensure backup insulin plan is in place.
Insulin Used in Pumps vs Patches

Insulin Pumps

  • Use rapid-acting insulin only (e.g., Aspart/NovoLog)
  • Clear insulin — do not roll
  • Delivers basal + bolus automatically

Insulin Patches (e.g., V-Go)

  • Use regular or rapid-acting insulin (pre-filled by manufacturer)
  • Not the same as traditional pumps
  • No manual dialing of doses in most models
Memory: Clear insulin in pumps = do NOT roll. Cloudy (NPH) = roll. Rapid-acting in pumps is always clear.
Nursing Considerations

Patient Safety & Teaching

  • Rotate patch sites — avoid irritated or damaged skin
  • Do not place patch over broken, scarred, or edematous skin
  • Monitor for hypoglycemia (sweating, tremor, confusion, tachycardia) and hyperglycemia (dry mouth, thirst, polyuria)
  • Always remove the old patch before applying a new one
  • If patch is not sticking → insulin absorption reduced → assess and replace
  • Maintain hygiene at application site to reduce infection risk

Blood Glucose Monitoring Still Required

  • Insulin delivery does NOT replace glucose monitoring
  • Frequent BG checks still required
  • Consider pairing with CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitor) for real-time data
