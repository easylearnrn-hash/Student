Insulin Patch / Patch Pump

Wearable insulin delivery, basal vs. bolus dosing, DKA risk, nursing considerations, patient teaching

What Is an Insulin Patch?
An insulin patch is a small wearable device placed on the skin that delivers insulin into the subcutaneous tissue — the fatty tissue just under the skin. It is used to help control blood glucose in patients with diabetes who need insulin.

Important: It is not always completely "needle-free." Some patches use a tiny cannula, microneedle, or automatic insertion system under the skin. CeQur delivers rapid-acting insulin through a tiny flexible cannula. Omnipod is a tubeless patch pump that delivers insulin subcutaneously through a small cannula under the device.
How It Works

Insulin Delivery Modes

  • The patch may deliver basal insulin — a slow, steady insulin dose throughout the day
  • Some devices also allow bolus insulin — an extra dose given before meals or to correct high blood glucose
  • Some devices are controlled by a remote controller, app, or manual click mechanism
  • The goal is to reduce the need for multiple daily injections and improve adherence
When Is It Used?

Indications

  • Type 1 diabetes mellitus — patients who require continuous insulin replacement
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus — patients who require basal insulin, mealtime insulin, or both
  • Difficulty following multiple daily injection schedules
  • Desire for a more discreet or convenient insulin delivery method
  • Need for more flexible insulin delivery

Nursing Considerations

Site Care & Safety

  • Rotate patch sites to prevent skin irritation and lipodystrophy
  • Do not place patch over broken, irritated, infected, scarred, or swollen skin
  • Assess the patch site for redness, pain, swelling, leakage, or infection
  • Make sure the patch is sticking properly — if loose or falling off, insulin delivery may be reduced
  • Remove the old patch before applying a new one to prevent accidental overdose
  • Do not cut, tear, or modify the patch
  • Verify the insulin type, dose, device settings, and patient understanding
  • Teach proper storage and disposal — used devices may contain needles or cannulas and should be discarded safely
Blood Glucose Monitoring

Monitoring Requirements

  • Patients still need frequent glucose monitoring
  • Insulin delivery devices do not replace blood glucose checks
  • If the patient uses a CGM, they still need to follow provider/facility instructions for finger-stick checks, especially if symptoms do not match CGM readings
  • Monitor for both hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia
Hypoglycemia vs. Hyperglycemia

Signs of Hypoglycemia

Low blood glucose

  • Sweating
  • Tremors
  • Hunger
  • Confusion
  • Irritability
  • Tachycardia
  • Weakness / dizziness
  • Seizures if severe

NCLEX action: If conscious and able to swallow, give 15 g fast-acting carbohydrate, recheck glucose in 15 minutes, and repeat if still low.

Signs of Hyperglycemia

High blood glucose

  • Dry mouth / thirst
  • Frequent urination
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Nausea
  • Fruity breath or Kussmaul respirations if DKA develops
DKA Risk With Pumps / Patch Pumps
Patients using insulin pumps or patch pumps usually receive rapid-acting insulin only. They may not have long-acting insulin in the body as backup. If the pump stops working, becomes blocked, falls off, or runs out of insulin, the patient can develop hyperglycemia and DKA faster.

Causes of DKA With Pumps

  • Pump removed for too long
  • Patch falls off
  • Empty insulin reservoir
  • Blocked cannula or infusion site
  • Incorrect settings
  • Dead battery or device failure
  • Infection or illness
  • No backup insulin plan

Key Teaching

  • Pump removed = insulin delivery stops → hyperglycemia → DKA risk
  • Hypoglycemia happens from too much insulin, not from removing the pump
  • Monitor glucose frequently
  • Inspect site and device daily
  • Always have a backup insulin plan
Infusion Set / Patch Site Changes

Change Schedule

  • Many pump infusion sets are changed every 2–3 days, unless the device has different manufacturer instructions
  • Some patch devices are changed every 24 hours, 3 days, or 4 days depending on the product
  • Leaving infusion sites on too long increases risk of poor absorption, infection, and site problems
  • Rotate sites to prevent lipodystrophy and inconsistent insulin absorption
Insulin Types Used in Pumps

Rapid-Acting (Most Common)

  • Most pumps and patch pumps use rapid-acting insulin
  • Insulin aspart (NovoLog)
  • Insulin lispro (Humalog)
  • Insulin glulisine (Apidra)
  • Rapid-acting insulin is clear — do not roll

Cloudy Insulins

  • NPH (intermediate-acting) — cloudy; gently roll before use
  • Premixed insulin — may be cloudy; gently roll before use
NCLEX Tip:
Clear insulin = do not roll.
Cloudy insulin = gently roll to mix.
Insulin Storage

Storage Rules

  • Store unopened insulin as directed — usually refrigerated
  • Do not freeze insulin
  • Do not leave insulin in a hot car
  • Keep insulin away from heat and direct sunlight — temperature extremes reduce insulin effectiveness
Patient Teaching

What to Teach the Patient

  • Rotate sites with every patch change
  • Wash hands before applying the patch
  • Apply to clean, dry skin — avoid lotions, oils, or creams under the adhesive
  • Check that the patch is attached correctly
  • Carry backup insulin and supplies at all times
  • Know how to treat hypoglycemia (15 g carb rule)
  • Check ketones if glucose is very high or if sick, per provider instructions
  • Report repeated high blood glucose, patch failure, signs of infection, or symptoms of DKA
  • Never stop insulin delivery without a backup plan
