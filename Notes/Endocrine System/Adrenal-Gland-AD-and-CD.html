Adrenal Gland — Addison's & Cushing's

Adrenal cortex hormones, Addison's disease (↓ cortisol) vs Cushing's disease (↑ cortisol)

Adrenal Gland Anatomy

Adrenal Gland Structure

  • Cortex (outer): Produces steroid hormones — regulated by ACTH from anterior pituitary
  • Medulla (inner): Produces catecholamines (Epinephrine, Norepinephrine) — NOT regulated by ACTH
HormoneTypeFunction
CortisolGlucocorticoid (Cortex)↑ blood glucose, suppresses immune system, stimulates fight-or-flight
AldosteroneMineralocorticoid (Cortex)↑ Na reabsorption → ↑ water → ↑ blood pressure; regulates K and H⁺
AndrogensSex hormones (Cortex)Testosterone precursors
Epinephrine/NorepinephrineCatecholamines (Medulla)Fight-or-flight response
Memory: "Aldosterone — wherever sodium goes, water follows." Na⁺ reabsorption → water retention → ↑ blood pressure + blood volume.

Aldosterone (a mineralocorticoid) acts like a messenger that tells the kidneys to hold on to sodium. This is important because where sodium goes, water follows. When the body keeps more sodium in the bloodstream, more water stays in the blood too. This increases blood volume, which then increases blood pressure and helps maintain hydration and circulation.

⬆ Cushing's Disease (Too Much Cortisol)
💡 Tip: Everything goes up — Potassium goes down.  |  Volume status: Hypervolemic / Hypernatremia
Cause: Caused by a pituitary adenoma that produces too much ACTH. The excess ACTH overstimulates the adrenal cortex, causing high cortisol levels. NCLEX wants cortisol to be HIGH in Cushing's disease.

Symptoms — Too Much Cortisol

  • Truncal obesity (weight gain mainly in the abdomen/trunk)
  • Thin arms/legs (muscle wasting makes extremities look thin)
  • Purple striae (wide purple stretch marks from thin, fragile skin)
  • Moon face (round, full-looking face)
  • Buffalo hump (fat pad on the upper back/base of neck)
  • Hirsutism (excessive hair growth, especially in females)
  • Acne (skin breakouts from hormonal changes)
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure)
  • Hyperglycemia (high blood sugar)
  • Hypokalemia may occur (low potassium)
  • Metabolic alkalosis may occur (blood becomes too basic/alkaline)
  • Osteoporosis (weak bones from cortisol breaking down bone)
  • Muscle weakness (protein breakdown and muscle wasting)
  • Thin skin, easy bruising, poor wound healing (cortisol weakens skin and slows repair)
  • Increased infection risk (cortisol suppresses the immune system)
  • Mood changes (irritability, depression, anxiety)

Treatment

  • Transsphenoidal pituitary surgery (removal of the ACTH-producing pituitary tumor)
  • After surgery: monitor cortisol levels and signs of adrenal insufficiency
  • Temporary glucocorticoid replacement may be needed (hydrocortisone or prednisone)
  • Never stop steroid replacement abruptly (can cause adrenal crisis)
  • If surgery fails or disease returns: repeat pituitary surgery, radiation, cortisol-lowering medications, or bilateral adrenalectomy
  • Bilateral adrenalectomy (removal of both adrenal glands) is usually a last-resort/life-saving option → requires lifelong hormone replacement

Cushing's Syndrome vs Cushing's Disease

  • Cushing's Syndrome: too much cortisol from any cause — long-term steroid use, adrenal tumor, pituitary tumor, or ectopic ACTH tumor such as small-cell lung cancer
  • Cushing's Disease: one specific type of Cushing's Syndrome caused by a pituitary adenoma producing too much ACTH → adrenal cortex releases too much cortisol
  • Key NCLEX difference: Disease = pituitary ACTH problem  |  Syndrome = high cortisol from any cause
⬇ Addison's Disease (Too Little Cortisol)
💡 Tip: Everything goes down — Potassium goes up.  |  Volume status: Hypovolemic / Hyponatremia
Cause: Usually autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex; may also occur after bilateral adrenalectomy, adrenal infection such as tuberculosis, adrenal hemorrhage, or metastatic cancer → low cortisol and low aldosterone.

Symptoms — Low Cortisol + Low Aldosterone

  • Weight loss (loss of body weight due to poor appetite and low cortisol)
  • Fatigue / weakness (low cortisol causes low energy and muscle weakness)
  • Bronze skin / hyperpigmentation (darkening of skin from high ACTH stimulation)
  • Dehydration / dry skin (low aldosterone causes sodium and water loss)
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure from low fluid volume)
  • Orthostatic hypotension (BP drops when standing, causing dizziness or fainting)
  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar because cortisol normally helps raise glucose)
  • Hyponatremia (low sodium because aldosterone is low)
  • Hyperkalemia (high potassium because aldosterone is low)
  • Metabolic acidosis (blood becomes too acidic, often related to hyperkalemia)
  • Hypovolemia (low blood volume from sodium and water loss)
  • Salt craving (body wants sodium because sodium is low)
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea (common GI symptoms)
  • Mood changes (depression, irritability, low motivation)
  • Stress intolerance (body cannot make enough cortisol during illness, surgery, or trauma)

NCLEX memory: Addison = low cortisol + low aldosterone → everything goes down, but potassium goes up.

Treatment — Replace What the Adrenal Glands Cannot Make

  • Glucocorticoid replacement (hydrocortisone or prednisone replaces cortisol)
  • Mineralocorticoid replacement (fludrocortisone replaces aldosterone and helps keep sodium/water)
  • Adrenal crisis: IV hydrocortisone + IV normal saline + dextrose (emergency treatment)
  • Take daily steroids as prescribed; never stop abruptly (stopping suddenly can cause adrenal crisis)
  • Increase steroid dose during stress (illness, surgery, trauma, fever, severe emotional stress)
  • Adequate sodium and fluids (especially with sweating, vomiting, diarrhea, or hot weather)
  • Medical alert bracelet + emergency steroid injection kit (needed because crisis can be life-threatening)

NCLEX memory: Addison = low cortisol/aldosterone → replace steroids, give salt/fluids, increase dose during stress, never stop abruptly.

🚨 Addison's Crisis (Adrenal Crisis)

Extreme Emergency

  • Triggered by: infection, surgery, trauma, severe stress, dehydration, or missed steroid doses
  • Severe hypotension (BP can drop dangerously low, such as 70/50 mmHg)
  • Symptoms: severe weakness, confusion, vomiting, abdominal pain, dehydration, shock
  • Labs: hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia may occur
  • Treatment: immediate IV hydrocortisone, IV normal saline, and dextrose
  • Patient teaching: never miss steroid doses; increase dose during illness/stress; carry medical alert ID and emergency steroid injection

NCLEX memory: Adrenal crisis = shock + low cortisol → give IV hydrocortisone + fluids fast.

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Cushing's = high cortisol; Addison's = low cortisol
  • Bronze skin = Addison's disease hallmark
  • Moon face + buffalo hump + purple striae = Cushing's hallmarks
  • Adrenal crisis → IV hydrocortisone + IV normal saline + dextrose immediately
  • Never stop steroid replacement abruptly (can cause adrenal crisis)
  • Increase steroid dose during stress/surgery (Addison's/adrenal insufficiency patients)
  • Cushing's: sodium/water retention → hypervolemia, hypertension, possible hypernatremia; potassium may go down
  • Addison's: sodium/water loss → hypovolemia, hyponatremia; potassium goes up
  • Aldosterone manages Na⁺/K⁺/water — key for BP and fluid balance

NCLEX memory:
Cushing = too much cortisol → volume/BP/sugar go up; potassium may go down.
Addison = low cortisol + low aldosterone → volume/BP/sugar/sodium go down; potassium goes up.
