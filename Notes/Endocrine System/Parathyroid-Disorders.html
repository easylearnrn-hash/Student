Parathyroid Disorders

PTH function, hyperparathyroidism "Bones Stones Groans Moans", hypoparathyroidism tetany & signs

What Are the Parathyroid Glands?

Anatomy & Function

  • Four small glands located behind the thyroid gland in the neck
  • Release Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) which raises blood calcium
  • Calcium is essential for: bones, nerves, muscles, and heart function

How PTH Raises Calcium

  • ✅ Pulls calcium from bones into blood
  • ✅ Helps intestines absorb more calcium from food
  • ✅ Tells kidneys to keep calcium instead of excreting it in urine
  • ✅ Activates Vitamin D → enhances calcium absorption
Ca and Phosphorus work OPPOSITELY: High PTH → High Ca + Low Phosphorus. Low PTH → Low Ca + High Phosphorus.

⬆ Hyperparathyroidism (Too Much PTH → Too Much Calcium)
Mnemonic: "Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans" — the classic way to remember hypercalcemia symptoms.

Causes

  • Primary: Parathyroid adenoma (benign tumor → excess PTH); parathyroid hyperplasia (all 4 glands enlarged)
  • Secondary: Chronic kidney disease (low Ca → compensatory PTH overproduction); Vitamin D deficiency

Symptoms (High Calcium)

  • 🦴 Bones — osteoporosis (fragile, brittle bones)
  • 💎 Stones — kidney stones (calcium deposits)
  • 🤢 Groans — nausea, vomiting, constipation
  • 😞 Moans — depression, confusion, extreme fatigue
  • 💗 Heart — hypertension, arrhythmias

Diagnosis

  • Ca > 10.5 mg/dL (high)
  • PTH ↑ (high)
  • Phosphorus ↓ (low)
  • Bone density scan → osteoporosis

Treatment

  • Parathyroidectomy — surgery to remove overactive glands
  • IV Fluids + Diuretics (Furosemide) — flush excess calcium
  • Calcimimetics — Cinacalcet (Sensipar) — lowers PTH production
  • Bisphosphonates (Alendronate) — strengthen bones

NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • Encourage weight-bearing exercise (walking, lifting) — strengthens bones
  • Increase water intake — prevents kidney stones
  • Limit high-calcium foods (dairy, leafy greens)
  • Monitor for arrhythmias (high Ca affects heart)
⬇ Hypoparathyroidism (Too Little PTH → Low Calcium)

Causes

  • Thyroid surgery — accidental removal or damage to parathyroid glands (most common)
  • Autoimmune disease — body attacks parathyroid glands
  • Low magnesium — Mg is needed for PTH to work; hypomagnesemia → functional hypoparathyroidism

Symptoms (Low Calcium = Overactive Nerves)

  • ⚡ Tetany — painful muscle cramps and spasms
  • 🖐 Tingling in hands, feet, lips (pins-and-needles)
  • Chvostek's Sign — face twitches when you tap the cheek
  • Trousseau's Sign — hand curls inward when BP cuff is inflated
  • 💗 Heart — low BP, arrhythmias, prolonged QT on EKG
Classic Signs of Hypocalcemia
😨
Chvostek's Sign: Tap the cheek (facial nerve area) → face twitches. Sign of hypocalcemia. Think: "C for Cheek".
🤚
Trousseau's Sign: Inflate a BP cuff on the arm → hand/wrist carpopedal spasm (curls inward). More sensitive than Chvostek's. Think: "T for Tourniquet".
Both Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs = CLASSIC NCLEX signs of low calcium (hypocalcemia). Always check these in post-thyroidectomy patients.

Treatment for Hypoparathyroidism

  • Calcium supplements (oral) — raise calcium levels
  • Vitamin D — helps intestines absorb calcium
  • IV Calcium Gluconate — hospital emergency treatment for severe hypocalcemia
  • Magnesium supplements — must correct low Mg first (Mg is needed for PTH to function)

NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • Monitor for tetany (twitching, muscle spasms, seizures)
  • Keep IV Calcium Gluconate at bedside — emergency treatment
  • Encourage high-calcium, low-phosphorus diet (dairy, green vegetables)
  • Avoid high-phosphorus foods (soda, processed meats — they lower calcium)

  • Hypoparathyroidism = Low Ca = "Twitching & Muscle Spasms"

  • Keep IV Calcium Gluconate at bedside in hypo patients
  • Encourage fluids + weight-bearing exercise in hyper patients
