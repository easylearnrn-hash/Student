Insulin Storage & Blood Glucose Checking

Refrigeration rules, 28-day expiration after opening, vial handling, fingerstick glucose steps

Insulin Storage Guidelines

1. Refrigeration — Unopened Vials

  • Store all unopened insulin vials in the refrigerator at 2–8°C / 36–46°F.
  • When stored correctly, unopened insulin is usually good until the expiration date printed on the package.
  • Organize the fridge: keep opened vials on one side and unopened vials on the other side.
  • Keep insulin away from the freezer compartment, back wall, or cooling vent — never freeze insulin.
  • Never store insulin in a car because heat and cold extremes can damage insulin and make it less effective.

2. Before Opening — Check & Label

  • Always check the insulin type, concentration, and expiration date before use.
  • Check the appearance of the insulin. Do not use it if it looks abnormal.
  • If expired or abnormal → discard immediately and document.
  • Once the vial is opened or punctured → label the vial with the date opened and discard date.

3. After Opening — 28-Day Rule

  • After opening, most insulin vials are discarded after 28 days, unless the manufacturer or facility policy says otherwise.
  • Store opened vials in the refrigerator or at room temperature.
  • If stored at room temperature, keep it below 30°C / 86°F.
  • Keep insulin away from direct sunlight and heat sources because heat and sunlight can make insulin less effective.
  • Room-temperature insulin may be more comfortable to inject than cold insulin.
Key Rule: Opened insulin is usually discarded after 28 days, whether it is refrigerated or kept at room temperature. Always label the vial with the date opened and discard date. If the opening date is unknown, do not use it — discard per facility policy.
Preparing Insulin for Administration

Warming & Handling

  • If insulin is refrigerated: let it reach room temperature or gently roll the vial in the palms before injection to reduce discomfort.
  • Never use heat to warm insulin. Do not microwave it, place it in hot water, or leave it in direct sunlight.
  • Never shake insulin because shaking can create bubbles/foam and may affect accurate dosing.
  • Cloudy insulin, such as NPH: gently roll the vial between the palms and gently turn/invert it until the insulin looks evenly cloudy.
  • Clear insulin, such as Regular, rapid-acting, and most long-acting insulin: do not mix or roll for mixing. It is ready to use after inspection.
  • Always inspect insulin before drawing it up. Do not use it if it has clumps, particles, crystals, discoloration, or an abnormal appearance.

If Accidentally Shaken

  • Allow the vial to rest undisturbed until bubbles or foam disappear.
  • Inspect the insulin carefully before drawing.
  • For clear insulin: it must remain clear, colorless, and free of particles.
  • For NPH/cloudy insulin: it should look evenly cloudy/milky after gentle mixing, with no clumps or particles.
  • If the appearance is abnormal, or if bubbles/foam do not settle → discard and use a new vial.

Insulin Patch Pumps

Patch Pump Storage / Change Note

  • Insulin patch pumps contain rapid-acting U-100 insulin only.
  • The pod / patch pump site is usually changed every 2–3 days / up to 72 hours, depending on the device and manufacturer instructions.
  • Leaving the pod/site on too long (>4 days) can increase the risk of infection, skin irritation, lipodystrophy, poor insulin absorption, and hyperglycemia.
  • Because pumps use only rapid-acting insulin, interrupted delivery can cause blood glucose to rise quickly and may lead to ketones/DKA.
  • Rotate sites with each change to protect the skin and improve insulin absorption.
  • Change the pod/site sooner if there is redness, swelling, pain, leakage, loose adhesive, unexplained high blood glucose, or pump/pod alarm.
Steps to Check Blood Glucose — Fingerstick

14-Step Protocol

  1. Perform hand hygiene and apply gloves.
  2. Identify the patient and verify the order, if in a clinical setting.
  3. Check the glucose meter, test strip expiration date, and supplies.
  4. Insert the test strip into the glucose meter.
  5. Clean the fingertip with an alcohol swab and allow it to dry completely.
  6. Prick the side of the fingertip, not the center, using a lancet.
  7. Gently squeeze from the base of the finger if needed, but do not milk the finger aggressively.
  8. Wipe away the first drop of blood if required by facility policy.
  9. Place the second drop of blood on the test strip.
  10. Apply clean gauze or cotton to stop bleeding; apply a bandage if needed.
  11. Read the result from the glucose meter.
  12. Dispose of the lancet in a sharps container and discard contaminated supplies.
  13. Remove gloves, perform hand hygiene, and document the result.
  14. Report or treat abnormal blood glucose results according to protocol.
Why wipe away the first drop? It may be diluted with tissue fluid (interstitial fluid), giving a falsely low reading. Always use the second drop for accuracy.
NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Opened insulin expires in 28 days — label with date opened
  • Unopened insulin → refrigerator until use
  • NEVER freeze, NEVER shake insulin
  • Cloudy Regular insulin → discard (should always be clear)
  • NPH cloudy → normal; roll gently
  • Warm refrigerated insulin in palms before injecting
  • Fingerstick: wipe away first drop, use second drop
  • Rotate injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy
  • Patch pump: change infusion set every 48–72 hours
