Endocrine System

Endocrine System Overview

Glands, hypothalamus, pituitary hormones — anterior vs posterior

Key Concept
TROPIN suffix: If you see a hormone ending in -tropin, it means it travels to another tissue to tell it to release another hormone.
Hypothalamus & Pituitary Gland

How the Hypothalamus Communicates

The hypothalamus talks to both aspects of the pituitary gland — Anterior and Posterior — but in different ways.

  • Anterior pituitary: connected via a blood supply. The hypothalamus releases hormones that travel through that blood pathway to tell the anterior pituitary to release its hormones.
  • Posterior pituitary: connected via nerve fibers. The hypothalamus sends a nervous signal directly — no releasing hormones needed. It stores and releases hormones made in the hypothalamus.
  • The pituitary gland is also known as the Hypophysis.

Anterior Pituitary Hormones

Anterior Pituitary — Blood Supply Connection 🩸

  • TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone) → stimulates thyroid to release T₃/T₄
  • ACTH (Adrenocorticotropic Hormone) → stimulates adrenal cortex to release cortisol
  • GH (Growth Hormone) → stimulates growth of bones and tissues
  • Prolactin → stimulates breast milk production
  • LH (Luteinizing Hormone) → triggers ovulation; stimulates testosterone in males
  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) → stimulates egg/sperm development

Most of these hormones end in -tropin or -stimulating — they go tell another gland to do something.

Posterior Pituitary Hormones

Posterior Pituitary — Nerve Fiber Connection ⚡

  • ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone / Vasopressin) → tells kidneys to retain water → ↑ blood volume and ↑ BP
  • Oxytocin → stimulates uterine contractions during labor; promotes milk ejection (let-down reflex)

These hormones are made in the hypothalamus and only stored and released by the posterior pituitary.

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • -tropin suffix = hormone that tells another gland to release a hormone
  • Anterior pituitary → connected via blood supply → releases TSH, ACTH, GH, Prolactin, LH, FSH
  • Posterior pituitary → connected via nerve fibers → releases ADH and Oxytocin
  • ADH retains water → low ADH = diabetes insipidus (too much urine); high ADH = SIADH (fluid retention)
  • Oxytocin → labor contractions + milk ejection
  • ACTH → stimulates cortisol; high ACTH + low cortisol = Addison's; high ACTH + high cortisol = Cushing's disease
  • TSH → stimulates thyroid; low TSH + high T₃/T₄ = hyperthyroidism; high TSH + low T₃/T₄ = hypothyroidism
