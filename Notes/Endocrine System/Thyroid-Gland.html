Endocrine System

Thyroid Gland — Structure & Physiology

Anatomy, hormone synthesis steps, T3/T4 pathway, calcitonin, NCLEX essentials

Location & Anatomy

Where Is It?

  • Neck — wrapped around the trachea (windpipe)
  • Butterfly shape: two lobes connected by isthmus
  • Between the Adam's apple (laryngeal prominence) and the suprasternal notch

Microscopic Structure

  • Follicles: tiny sacs that store thyroid hormones
  • Follicular cells: produce thyroglobulin protein
  • Colloid: gel-like fluid inside follicles where hormones are synthesized and stored
How Thyroid Hormones Are Synthesized
  • Iodine intake: dietary iodine (salt, eggs, seafood) → converted to iodide (I⁻)
  • Transport: iodide enters follicular cells via sodium-iodide symporter → moves into colloid → oxidized using H₂O₂
  • Binding: enzyme thyroid peroxidase attaches iodide to thyroglobulin:
    1 iodine → MIT (Monoiodotyrosine) | 2 iodine → DIT (Diiodotyrosine)
  • Hormone formation: T₄ = DIT+DIT (4 iodine) | T₃ = MIT+DIT (3 iodine)
  • Conversion: enzyme deiodinase (requires selenium) converts T₄ → T₃ (active form)
Key: T₄ is inactive. T₃ is active. Most T₄ (80%) is converted to T₃ in peripheral tissues by deiodinase (selenium-dependent).
Transport & Activity

How T₃/T₄ Travel in Blood

  • Most hormone is protein-bound (inactive while bound):
  • TBG (Thyroxine-Binding Globulin) — carries 80%
  • Albumin — carries 20%
  • Only free T₃ (~1%) is biologically active → enters cells → acts on DNA
Calcitonin — The Other Thyroid Hormone

Calcitonin

  • Produced by parafollicular C-cells of the thyroid
  • Lowers blood calcium when levels are too high
  • Mechanism: promotes calcium reabsorption into bone tissue
  • Opposes PTH (parathyroid hormone)
  • Used therapeutically: hypercalcemia, osteoporosis
Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Thyroid Axis
GlandHormone ReleasedEffect
HypothalamusTRH (Thyrotropin-Releasing Hormone)Tells anterior pituitary to release TSH
Anterior PituitaryTSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)Tells thyroid to produce T₃ and T₄
Thyroid GlandT₃ and T₄Regulate metabolism, HR, development
Negative feedback: High T₃/T₄ → suppresses TRH and TSH (shuts off signal). Low T₃/T₄ → increases TRH → TSH rises → thyroid makes more. This keeps levels steady.
T₃ Pathway Into Cells & DNA

Molecular Mechanism

  • Bound T₃ (to TBG/albumin) → only free T₃ enters cells
  • Free T₃ crosses cell membrane via transporters
  • Enters nucleus → binds thyroid hormone receptors (TRs) on DNA
  • T₃-TR complex binds Thyroid Response Elements (TREs)
  • Turns genes on or off → proteins are made → metabolic effects occur
Thyroid Disorders Overview

Hyperthyroidism

  • Causes: Graves' disease, pituitary tumors (excess TSH)
  • Fast metabolism → weight loss
  • Heat intolerance, sweating
  • Atrial fibrillation risk
  • Exophthalmos (Graves')
  • Goiter (enlarged thyroid)

Hypothyroidism

  • Causes: Hashimoto's Thyroiditis (autoimmune), iodine deficiency, low TSH
  • Weight gain, cold intolerance
  • Fatigue, slow heart, depression
  • Puffy face (periorbital edema)
  • Buffalo hump (fat deposits)
  • Goiter (if iodine deficiency)

T₃ LevelKey Symptoms

NCLEX Essentials

Must-Know Summary

  • ✅ T₄ = inactive; T₃ = active
  • ✅ Synthesis requires iodine and selenium
  • ✅ Controlled by TRH → TSH → T₃/T₄ (HPT axis)
  • ✅ Negative feedback keeps levels steady
  • ✅ Graves' = hyper; Hashimoto's = hypo
  • ✅ Calcitonin → lowers blood calcium (stores it in bones)
  • ✅ Only free T₃ is active; protein-bound T₃ is inactive
