Insulin Types & Administration

Rapid, short, intermediate, long-acting; peak times, food tray timing, IV use, injection technique

Overview
Insulin Peak Time = when insulin reaches its highest effectiveness in lowering blood sugar. The patient must eat food during peak hours to prevent hypoglycemia. Food tray timing is critical and depends on insulin type.

Key Points Per Insulin Type
Rapid-Acting (Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine)
  • Fastest onset — provide food tray within 15 minutes to prevent hypoglycemia
  • Used in insulin pumps (Aspart is most common pump insulin)
  • Clear appearance; if cloudy → discard
Short-Acting Regular Insulin (R) Identified by Red Circle 🔴
  • ONLY insulin type that can be given IV
  • Clear fluid — if cloudy, discard immediately and document
  • Provide food tray 2 hours after administration
  • In DKA: goal is glucose to drop 50–75 mg/dL per hour
Intermediate-Acting NPH (N)
  • Identified by letter "N"
  • Cloudy appearance — normal; roll gently before use (do NOT shake)
  • SubQ only — cannot be given IV
  • Provide food tray 6 hours after administration (long delay due to slow onset)
Long-Acting (Glargine/Lantus, Detemir/Levemir)
  • No peak time — provides steady 24-hour baseline insulin
  • Given typically before bedtime
  • No specific food tray required
  • Teach patient: eat a snack before bed to prevent morning hypoglycemia
  • Our body activates sugars in the morning (dawn phenomenon) — long-acting covers this
  • Cannot be mixed with any other insulin
Critical Administration Rules

NEVER

  • Give NPH insulin IV — SubQ only
  • Give Long-acting insulin IV
  • Mix long-acting (Lantus) with any other insulin
  • Use cloudy Regular insulin (discard)
  • Inject in the same spot repeatedly (causes lipodystrophy)

ALWAYS

  • Only Regular (R) insulin can be given IV
  • Provide food tray at appropriate peak time
  • Rotate injection sites daily
  • Check insulin appearance before use
  • Warm refrigerated insulin in palms before injecting
NCLEX Critical: Regular insulin (R) = ONLY type given IV. All others SubQ only. If a question asks which insulin to add to an IV bag → Regular insulin.
Injection Sites & Technique

Subcutaneous (SQ) Injection

  • Abdomen: 1.5–2 inches (5 cm) from umbilicus — fastest, most consistent absorption
  • Thighs, buttocks, upper arms (under biceps)
  • Rotate daily — prevents lipodystrophy (lipohypertrophy or lipoatrophy)
  • If refrigerated: warm vial between palms first — prevents discomfort
  • Do NOT shake — roll gently (cloudy NPH) or use as-is (clear)
NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Rapid-acting → food within 15 min
  • Regular (R) → food at 2 hours
  • NPH (N) → food at 6 hours
  • Long-acting → no peak, no specific food tray
  • Only Regular insulin can be given IV
  • Regular insulin: clear — cloudy = discard
  • NPH: cloudy is normal — roll, don't shake
  • Long-acting: cannot mix with anything
  • Rotate injection sites → prevent lipodystrophy
  • Dawn phenomenon: morning hyperglycemia → long-acting insulin before bed helps
