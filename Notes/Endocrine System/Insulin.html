Insulin Types & Administration

Rapid, short, intermediate, long-acting; peak times, food tray timing, IV use, injection technique

Overview

Insulin Overview

  • Insulin Peak Time is the time when insulin is working at its strongest effect to lower blood glucose. During the peak time, the patient is at the highest risk for hypoglycemia. The patient should eat meals or snacks as scheduled during this period. Peak time depends on the type of insulin being used.
  • Insulin should be injected subcutaneously (SQ), which means into the fatty tissue under the skin. The abdomen is a common injection site. Inject insulin about 1.5–2 inches away from the belly button (umbilicus). Other approved injection sites include the thighs, buttocks, and back of the upper arms.
  • Rotate injection sites regularly. Do not inject into the exact same spot every day. Repeated injections in the same area can cause lipodystrophy, which is abnormal fat tissue under the skin. This can affect how insulin is absorbed and may cause unstable blood glucose levels.
  • In the early morning, the body naturally releases hormones that increase blood glucose to help the body wake up. This can cause morning hyperglycemia, also known as the dawn phenomenon.
Key Points Per Insulin Type
Rapid-Acting Insulin  Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine
  • Fastest onset: begins working very quickly, usually within 5–15 minutes; give only when the patient's meal is available or within 15 minutes before eating to prevent hypoglycemia
  • Peak time: peaks quickly, around 45–75 minutes; this is when the patient is at the highest risk for hypoglycemia
  • Duration: lasts about 3–5 hours
  • Use: commonly used as mealtime insulin and may also be used in insulin pumps
  • Appearance: should be clear; if cloudy, clumped, discolored, or has particles — discard it
Short-Acting Regular Insulin (R) Identified by Red Circle 🔴
  • Standard insulin used IV, especially for DKA treatment
  • Clear insulin: regular insulin should be clear and colorless; if cloudy, discolored, or has particles — do not use it and follow facility policy
  • Meal timing: give subcutaneous regular insulin about 30 minutes before meals to prevent hypoglycemia
  • Peak time: peaks around 2–4 hours after administration; this is when the patient is at the highest risk for hypoglycemia
  • DKA goal: blood glucose should decrease gradually, about 50–75 mg/dL per hour; if it drops too fast → risk for complications; if not enough → insulin infusion may need adjustment
Intermediate-Acting NPH (N)
  • Identified by the letter "N."
  • Cloudy appearance is normal. Gently roll the vial or pen before use to mix the insulin evenly. Do not shake, because shaking can create bubbles and affect accurate dosing. NPH should look uniformly cloudy before administration.
  • Subcutaneous use only. NPH insulin cannot be given IV and should not be used in an insulin pump.
  • Peak time: NPH usually peaks around 4–12 hours after administration. This is when the patient is at the highest risk for hypoglycemia.
  • Food safety: Make sure the patient eats meals and snacks as scheduled. For morning NPH, watch for hypoglycemia later in the day. For evening NPH, watch for possible overnight hypoglycemia.
Long-Acting (Glargine/Lantus, Detemir/Levemir)
  • Provides basal insulin coverage for about 24 hours.
  • Has no pronounced peak, so there is no specific "peak-time food tray" required.
  • Given once daily at the same time every day; some long-acting insulins may be given once or twice daily depending on the order.
  • Continue regular meals as scheduled. A bedtime snack may be needed only if ordered or if the patient is at risk for overnight hypoglycemia.
  • Helps control fasting blood glucose and morning hyperglycemia related to the dawn phenomenon.
  • Do not mix long-acting insulin with other insulin in the same syringe. Lantus and Levemir both state not to dilute or mix with other insulin or solutions.
  • Long-acting insulin is for subcutaneous use, not IV or insulin pump use.

Critical Administration Rules

NEVER

  • Never give NPH insulin IV — NPH is subcutaneous only.
  • Never give long-acting insulin IV — long-acting insulin is for subcutaneous use only.
  • Never mix long-acting insulin, such as Lantus, with any other insulin in the same syringe.
  • Never use cloudy Regular insulin — Regular insulin should be clear. If it is cloudy, discolored, or has particles, discard it.
  • Never inject insulin into the exact same spot repeatedly — rotate injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy/lipohypertrophy, which can affect insulin absorption.

ALWAYS

  • Always use Regular insulin as the standard IV insulin, especially for DKA treatment. NCLEX often teaches Regular insulin as the IV insulin, but some rapid-acting insulins may also be used IV under medical supervision.
  • Always give meal-time insulin with food available. Rapid-acting insulin: meal tray should be present or given within about 15 minutes. Regular insulin SQ: usually given about 30 minutes before meals.
  • Always monitor for hypoglycemia during peak time. Peak time is when insulin has its strongest glucose-lowering effect.
  • Always rotate injection sites within the same body region from one injection to the next to prevent lipodystrophy and poor insulin absorption.
  • Always check insulin appearance before use. Regular and rapid-acting insulin should be clear. NPH is normally cloudy.
  • Always bring refrigerated insulin to room temperature before injecting. Do not heat it. You may gently roll the insulin between your palms if needed, especially for cloudy insulin like NPH, but do not shake.
NCLEX Critical: Regular insulin (R) = ONLY type given IV. All others SubQ only. If a question asks which insulin to add to an IV bag → Regular insulin.
Injection Sites & Technique

Subcutaneous (SQ) Injection

  • Abdomen: Inject at least 1.5–2 inches / about 5 cm away from the umbilicus. The abdomen usually provides the fastest and most consistent absorption.
  • Other approved sites: Use the thighs, upper outer buttocks/hips, or back of the upper arms.
  • Rotate injection sites with each injection within the same body region to prevent lipodystrophy/lipohypertrophy, which can interfere with insulin absorption.
  • If insulin is refrigerated: Allow it to warm to room temperature before injecting, or gently warm the vial/pen between the palms. Do not heat insulin.
  • Do not shake insulin. For cloudy insulin, such as NPH, gently roll the vial or pen to mix it. Clear insulin does not need mixing. NPH is normally a cloudy suspension for subcutaneous use.

Top NCLEX Points

  • Rapid-acting insulin → give when meal tray is present or within 15 minutes before eating
  • Regular insulin (R) → give SQ about 30 minutes before meals; monitor for hypoglycemia during peak time, about 2–4 hours
  • NPH insulin (N) → cloudy is normal; gently roll, do not shake; monitor for hypoglycemia during peak time, about 4–12 hours
  • Long-acting insulin → no pronounced peak; no specific peak-time food tray required
  • Regular insulin is the standard IV insulin, especially for DKA treatment
  • Regular insulin should be clear — if cloudy, discolored, or particles are present, discard
  • NPH is cloudy normally — roll gently, do not shake
  • Long-acting insulin cannot be mixed with any other insulin
  • Rotate injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy and poor insulin absorption
  • Dawn phenomenon = morning hyperglycemia caused by natural hormone release; basal/long-acting insulin may help control fasting morning glucose when prescribed
