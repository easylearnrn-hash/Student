DKA & HHNS

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (Type 1) vs Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Nonketotic Syndrome (Type 2) — causes, symptoms, treatment

Overview

DKA — Type 1 DM Only

  • Rapid onset — develops within 4–6 hours
  • Blood glucose: ~500–600 mg/dL
  • Caused by absolute insulin deficiency → body breaks down fat → ketones

HHNS — Type 2 DM

  • Slow onset — gradual development
  • Blood glucose: >600 mg/dL
  • More dangerous than DKA due to extreme hyperosmolarity
  • Enough insulin to prevent ketones — but not enough to control glucose
Causes (Same Triggers for Both)

Common Precipitating Factors

  • Viral infections (stomach virus, flu, COVID)
  • Physical trauma or surgery
  • Sepsis
  • Emotional or neurological stress
  • Management of stress triggers: increase insulin dosage

Treatment — Same First & Second Line for Both
1st Line
Establish IV access (peripheral) — start large-bore IV immediately
1st Line
Administer Normal Saline 0.9% — rapidly corrects dehydration and hypotension
2nd Line
Continuous IV Regular Insulin — only Regular insulin can be given IV; lowers blood glucose
Monitor
Check glucose every hour — goal: drop 50–75 mg/dL per hour
Target
Maintain glucose 250–300 mg/dL — do NOT drop too fast; prevents hypoglycemia
Concurrent
IV Dextrose 5% (D5W) via separate IV line or port — given simultaneously once glucose approaches target to prevent hypoglycemia
Potassium Monitoring (Both Conditions)

Critical Potassium Rules

  • Monitor potassium levels closely throughout insulin infusion
  • Normal K: 3.5–5.1 mEq/L — if low, administer potassium replacement
  • Place patient on ECG monitor — watch for peaked T waves (sign of hyperkalemia) or flat/inverted T waves (hypokalemia)

DKA — Starts with HyperKalemia

  • K⁺ shifts OUT of cells during acidosis → initially HIGH
  • As insulin is given → K⁺ shifts back INTO cells → risk of hypokalemia
  • Monitor closely; replace K⁺ as needed

HHNS — Potassium Stable

  • No acidosis → K⁺ stays in cells → levels remain normal
  • Still monitor as insulin infusion can shift K⁺
Risks of Continuous Insulin Infusion

Two Key Risks (Both DKA & HHNS)

  • 🔻 Hypoglycemia — blood glucose drops too low; prevent by running D5W concurrently
  • 🔻 Hypokalemia — insulin drives K⁺ into cells; monitor ECG and K⁺ levels; replace as needed
Subcutaneous Insulin (Transitioning from IV)

Once Patient Stabilizes

  • Transition from IV insulin to SubQ insulin
  • Inject 1.5–2 inches from the umbilicus (abdomen preferred) or under biceps
  • Rotate injection sites daily — prevents lipodystrophy (abnormal fat at injection site)
NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • DKA = Type 1 only; HHNS = Type 2
  • HHNS is more dangerous than DKA (extreme hyperosmolarity)
  • DKA mnemonic: D·KKK·AAA (Dehydration, Ketones, Kussmaul, HyperKalemia, Acidosis, Acetone, Anorexia)
  • HHNS: NO ketones, NO acidosis, stable K⁺
  • First treatment for BOTH: Normal Saline 0.9% IV
  • Only Regular insulin can be given IV
  • Drop glucose 50–75 mg/dL/hr; maintain at 250–300 mg/dL
  • Run D5W concurrently to prevent hypoglycemia
  • Monitor for hypokalemia during insulin infusion (peaked T waves = hyperkalemia; flat T = hypokalemia)
  • Rotate SubQ injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy
