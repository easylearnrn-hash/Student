Endocrine System

Metformin

First-line Type 2 DM medication, kidney monitoring, contrast dye precaution, HbA1c target

Overview
Metformin is the first-line oral medication for Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus when diet and exercise alone are insufficient. It works by reducing hepatic glucose production (liver releases less glucose) and improving insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues. It does NOT cause hypoglycemia on its own.
Indication & Dosing

When to Start Metformin

  • Type 2 DM when exercise and diet alone are not sufficient to control BG
  • First-line medication before considering insulin
  • Frequency: Twice daily (BID), taken with or after meals
  • Taking with food ↓ GI side effects
Safety Precautions

Kidney Toxicity — Monitor Renal Function

  • Metformin is excreted by the kidneys — renal impairment → drug accumulates → lactic acidosis (life-threatening)
  • Check kidney function panel every 6 months
  • Hold Metformin if SCr >1.5 (males) or >1.4 (females), or GFR <30

Surgery & Contrast Dye Procedures

  • STOP Metformin 24 hours BEFORE any surgery or procedure involving contrast dye
  • Contrast dye can temporarily impair kidneys → Metformin builds up → lactic acidosis
  • If taken by mistake: reschedule the surgery/procedure
  • Restart Metformin 48 hours after procedure (once kidney function confirmed normal)
NCLEX Critical: Patient scheduled for CT with contrast → hold Metformin 24 hours before AND 48 hours after the procedure.
Side Effects

GI Side Effects (Most Common)

  • Dyspepsia — indigestion, heartburn
  • Distended abdomen — bloating, gas
  • Diarrhea or constipation
  • Nausea (especially if taken on empty stomach)

Reduce by taking with food. Usually improves after first few weeks.

Serious: Lactic Acidosis RARE

  • Buildup of lactic acid in blood
  • Signs: muscle pain, weakness, stomach pain, difficulty breathing, cold/cyanotic skin
  • Risk ↑ with renal impairment, liver disease, heavy alcohol use, dehydration
  • Stop Metformin immediately if suspected
Monitoring Effectiveness

HbA1c Monitoring at 3 Months

  • After starting Metformin: check HbA1c at 3 months
  • Target: HbA1c ≤ 7%
  • If HbA1c >7% after Metformin optimization → consider adding or switching to insulin
Type 2 DM Treatment Ladder

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Diet and exercise — lifestyle modification first
  2. Metformin — first-line oral medication; add if lifestyle insufficient
  3. Additional oral agents or GLP-1 — if Metformin alone insufficient
  4. Insulin — when HbA1c remains >7% despite oral medications

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Metformin = first-line for Type 2 DM, not Type 1
  • Take with food (twice daily) to reduce GI side effects
  • Monitor kidney function every 6 months
  • Hold 24 hours before contrast dye procedures or surgery
  • Restart 48 hours after contrast (once renal function confirmed)
  • If taken before contrast by mistake → reschedule procedure
  • Does NOT cause hypoglycemia by itself
  • Most common side effects = GI (dyspepsia, bloating, diarrhea)
  • Serious rare complication = lactic acidosis (more likely with renal failure)
  • HbA1c goal = ≤7%; check at 3 months after starting
