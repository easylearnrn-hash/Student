Endocrine System

Metformin

First-line Type 2 DM medication, kidney monitoring, contrast dye precaution, HbA1c target

Overview
Metformin is commonly the first-line oral medication for Type 2 DM, but in real practice, some patients with heart failure, chronic kidney disease, or high cardiovascular risk may need medications like SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists early. For NCLEX, saying first-line oral medication is correct.

Metformin has low risk of hypoglycemia by itself because it does not increase insulin secretion. Hypoglycemia can happen if it is taken with insulin or sulfonylureas — oral Type 2 diabetes medications that stimulate the pancreas to release more insulin (examples: Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride).

Metformin lowers blood glucose by decreasing glucose release from the liver and improving the body's response to insulin. It does not stimulate insulin secretion from the pancreas, which is why it usually does not cause hypoglycemia when taken alone.
Indication & Dosing

When to Start Metformin

  • Type 2 DM when diet and exercise alone are not enough to control blood glucose.
  • Usually considered the first-line oral medication before insulin, unless contraindicated or another medication is preferred for specific conditions.
  • Immediate-release metformin: commonly taken twice daily with meals.
  • Extended-release metformin: commonly taken once daily with the evening meal.
  • Taking metformin with or after food decreases GI side effects such as nausea, diarrhea, abdominal discomfort, and upset stomach.
Safety Precautions

Kidney Toxicity — Monitor Renal Function

  • Metformin is excreted by the kidneys — renal impairment → drug accumulates → lactic acidosis (life-threatening)
  • Check kidney function panel every 6 months
  • Hold Metformin if SCr >1.5 (males) or >1.4 (females), or GFR <30

Surgery & Contrast Dye Procedures

  • STOP Metformin before any surgery or procedure involving iodinated contrast dye.
  • This is done because surgery, fasting, dehydration, low blood pressure, or contrast dye can temporarily reduce kidney function.
  • If the kidneys slow down, metformin can accumulate → lactic acidosis.
  • If the patient took metformin by mistake before the procedure, notify the provider immediately.
  • Do not restart metformin until 48 hours after the procedure and only after kidney function is confirmed stable.
  • The patient should also be eating, drinking, and medically stable before restarting.
NCLEX Critical: Patient scheduled for CT with contrast → hold Metformin 24 hours before AND 48 hours after the procedure.
Side Effects

GI Side Effects (Most Common)

  • Dyspepsia — indigestion, heartburn
  • Distended abdomen — bloating, gas
  • Diarrhea or constipation
  • Nausea (especially if taken on empty stomach)

Reduce by taking with food. Usually improves after first few weeks.

Serious: Lactic Acidosis RARE

  • Buildup of lactic acid in blood
  • Signs: muscle pain, weakness, stomach pain, difficulty breathing, cold/cyanotic skin
  • Risk ↑ with renal impairment, liver disease, heavy alcohol use, dehydration
  • Stop Metformin immediately if suspected
Monitoring Effectiveness

HbA1c Monitoring at 3 Months

  • After starting Metformin: check HbA1c at 3 months
  • Target: HbA1c ≤ 7%
  • If HbA1c >7% after Metformin optimization → consider adding or switching to insulin
Type 2 DM Treatment Ladder

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Diet and exercise — lifestyle modification first
  2. Metformin — first-line oral medication; add if lifestyle insufficient
  3. Additional oral agents or GLP-1 — if Metformin alone insufficient
  4. Insulin — when HbA1c remains >7% despite oral medications

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Metformin = first-line for Type 2 DM, not Type 1
  • Take with food (twice daily) to reduce GI side effects
  • Monitor kidney function every 6 months
  • Hold 24 hours before contrast dye procedures or surgery
  • Restart 48 hours after contrast (once renal function confirmed)
  • If taken before contrast by mistake → reschedule procedure
  • Does NOT cause hypoglycemia by itself
  • Most common side effects = GI (dyspepsia, bloating, diarrhea)
  • Serious rare complication = lactic acidosis (more likely with renal failure)
  • HbA1c goal = ≤7%; check at 3 months after starting
