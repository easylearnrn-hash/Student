Always Prioritize — MSK NCLEX Strategy

NCLEX-RN Priority Assessment & Intervention Framework

1. Neurovascular Assessment — The 5 P's

Assess circulation, sensation, and movement. Must check routinely after any orthopedic intervention (casting, traction, surgery, splinting).

PainUnrelieved/increasing despite meds = compartment syndrome
PulseCompare bilaterally; weak or absent = ischemia
PallorPale/cool skin = poor perfusion
ParesthesiaTingling/numbness = nerve involvement
ParalysisNo movement = nerve or muscle damage
Compartment Syndrome = EMERGENCY: Pain out of proportion + tight/tense compartment + paresthesia → remove/bivalve cast → notify provider → fasciotomy may be needed.

2. Traction Principles

Skin Traction

  • Tapes/straps attached to skin (non-invasive)
  • Examples: Buck's traction (pre-op hip/femur fracture), Bryant's traction (infants, congenital hip dislocation), Pavlik harness (infants, developmental dysplasia of the hip)
  • Check skin under straps for irritation, redness, breakdown
  • Maintain alignment; keep weights off floor
  • Prevent complications: pressure ulcers, nerve damage, DVT

Skeletal Traction

  • Pins/wires inserted directly into bone — longer duration
  • Example: 90-90 traction (femoral fractures in children)
  • Pin site care: sterile technique; clean with chlorhexidine or saline per protocol
  • Monitor pin sites: redness, drainage, odor, fever = infection
Traction rules:
  • NEVER remove or adjust weights without a provider order
  • Weights must hang freely — never rest on bed frame or floor
  • Maintain proper body alignment at all times

4. Positioning & Immobilization

Casts

  • Do not get wet; use palm to handle wet plaster
  • Check 5 P's frequently (especially first 24–48 hrs)
  • Elevate above heart to reduce swelling
  • Teach family signs of compartment syndrome

Braces & Splints

  • Ensure proper skin care under brace — prevent skin breakdown
  • Encourage compliance with wear times
  • Provide emotional support for body image concerns (especially pediatric scoliosis brace)

Hip Arthroplasty (Hip Replacement) — Post-Op Precautions

  • Avoid hip flexion >90°
  • No crossing legs (adduction)
  • No internal rotation
  • Use elevated toilet seat, abduction pillow in bed
  • Sleep on back; avoid bending to put on shoes/socks
  • Signs of dislocation: sudden severe hip pain, leg shortening, internal rotation

5. NCLEX Strategy Highlights

  • 🚨 Neurovascular assessment FIRST after any orthopedic intervention — always the priority
  • 🚨 Compartment syndrome = pain out of proportion = remove cast + notify provider + prepare for fasciotomy
  • 🚨 Fat embolism = petechiae + confusion + SOB after long-bone fracture = emergency (treat like PE)
  • 🚨 Dislocation + absent pulse = vascular emergency — report immediately
  • 🚨 Hip dislocation signs after arthroplasty = sudden pain + shortened leg + abnormal rotation
  • ✅ Traction: NEVER remove weights without order; always hanging freely
  • ✅ Pin care: sterile; clean with chlorhexidine or saline
  • ✅ Teach proper use of crutches, walkers, canes
  • ✅ NSAIDs delay bone healing — educate patients
  • ✅ Steroids: taper slowly; never abrupt stop
  • ✅ Weight-bearing exercise promotes bone density
  • ✅ Fall prevention = priority for all orthopedic and osteoporotic patients
