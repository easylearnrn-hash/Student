Sprains, Strains & Dislocations

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definitions

ConditionTissue AffectedMechanismCommon Sites
Sprain Ligament (bone → bone) Stretching or tearing of ligament around a joint Ankle, wrist, knee
Strain Muscle or tendon (muscle → bone) Stretching or tearing of muscle/tendon Lower back, hamstring, shoulder
Dislocation Bone/joint Displacement of bone out of its normal joint position Shoulder, hip, knee, finger
Memory: Sprain = ligament (S = joint Stability). Strain = muscle/tendon (S = muscle Strength). Dislocation = bone out of socket.

2. RICE — First-Line Treatment

RRest — Stop activity; avoid movement to prevent further injury
IIce — Apply 20 min on / 1–2 hrs off for first 24–48 hours; reduces swelling and pain via vasoconstriction
CCompression — Elastic bandage (ACE wrap) to reduce swelling; not too tight → monitor circulation
EElevation — Keep injured part above heart level → minimize swelling
Ice only for first 24–48 hours → then switch to heat to promote circulation and healing.

3. Signs & Symptoms

Sprain & Strain

  • Pain and tenderness at site
  • Swelling and bruising (ecchymosis)
  • Decreased range of motion
  • Muscle spasms (more common with strains)

Dislocation

  • Severe pain — often immediate and intense
  • Visible deformity — joint looks "out of place"
  • Inability to move the joint
  • Swelling and numbness (if nerves are compressed)
Dislocation + absent distal pulse = vascular emergency — notify provider immediately.

4. NCLEX Priority Interventions

Sprains & Strains

  1. Apply RICE
  2. Administer NSAIDs (ibuprofen) for pain and inflammation
  3. Assess neurovascular status distal to injury: capillary refill (<2 sec), color, temperature, sensation, movement
  4. Teach gradual return to activity
  5. Provide assistive devices if needed (crutches, cane)

Dislocation

  1. Do NOT attempt to reduce (put back) the joint as a nurse
  2. Immobilize joint in position found
  3. Apply ice; keep patient NPO (sedation may be needed for reduction)
  4. Prepare for:
    • Closed reduction — manual realignment under sedation/anesthesia
    • Open reduction — surgical if closed fails or vascular/nerve injury present
  5. Monitor for neurovascular compromise and compartment syndrome

5. Post-Reduction Care

  • Assess neurovascular status every 1–2 hours after reduction
  • Immobilize with splint, sling, or brace as ordered
  • Apply ice to reduce post-procedure swelling
  • Elevate affected extremity
  • Teach cast/splint care if applied
  • Physical therapy referral for strengthening and ROM restoration

6. Complications

  • Joint instability — chronic laxity from ligament damage (common with repeated sprains)
  • Avascular necrosis (AVN) — blood supply to bone disrupted at dislocation site → bone death (especially hip)
  • Nerve damage — from compression or stretching at time of dislocation
  • Compartment syndrome — from significant swelling/hemorrhage
  • Osteoarthritis — long-term after repeated joint injuries

8. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Sprain = ligament; Strain = muscle/tendon; Dislocation = bone out of joint
  • ✅ RICE = Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation
  • ✅ Ice first 24–48 hrs → then heat
  • ✅ Nurse does NOT reduce dislocations
  • ✅ Dislocation + no pulse = EMERGENCY
  • ✅ Always assess neurovascular status after any musculoskeletal injury
  • ✅ Hip dislocation risk of AVN — needs prompt reduction
Mnemonic — Sprain vs. Strain: "Sprain your Ankle (ligament). Strain your hamString (muscle)."
