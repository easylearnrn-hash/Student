Fractures

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition

A fracture is a break in the continuity of a bone, caused by trauma, overuse, or bone-weakening conditions (e.g., osteoporosis, cancer).

2. Types of Fractures

TypeDescription
Closed (Simple)Bone breaks but skin remains intact
Open (Compound)Bone pierces through skin — high infection risk
ComminutedBone shatters into multiple fragments

TransverseHorizontal break across the bone

SpiralTwisting force — suspect abuse in children
ImpactedOne fragment driven into another

PathologicBreak caused by bone disease (cancer, osteoporosis)

3. Signs & Symptoms

  • Pain and tenderness at site
  • Swelling and bruising (ecchymosis)
  • Deformity or shortening of limb
  • Crepitus — grating/crunching sound of bone ends rubbing
  • Loss of function
  • Muscle spasms
  • Bleeding (especially open fractures)

4. NCLEX Priority — The 6 P's of Neurovascular Assessment

Assess every patient after fracture, casting, or traction:

PainUnrelieved by meds/elevation → compartment syndrome
PallorPale skin → ↓ circulation
ParesthesiaTingling/numbness → nerve damage
PulselessnessAbsent distal pulse → late sign, arterial compromise
ParalysisNo movement → nerve/muscle damage
PoikilothermiaCool limb → poor perfusion
Any of the 6 P's present = suspect Compartment Syndrome → report immediately.

5. Treatment

Reduction

  • Closed reduction — manual manipulation (no incision)
  • Open reduction (ORIF) — surgical fixation with hardware (plates, rods, screws)

Immobilization

  • Cast — plaster (24–72 hrs to dry) or fiberglass mold
  • Splints/braces — removable support
  • Traction — weights and pulleys to align bones or relieve spasms

6. Cast Care

  • Keep cast dry (plaster takes 24–72 hrs to fully dry)
  • Use palm of hand (not fingers) to handle a wet cast — prevents dents that create pressure points
  • Do NOT insert anything inside the cast
  • Elevate limb above heart level for 24–48 hours to reduce swelling
  • Report: pain, numbness, tingling, cool skin, foul odor, drainage, or discoloration

7. Traction Care

TypeDescriptionExample
Skin tractionStraps attached to skin (non-invasive)Buck's traction (pre-op hip/femur)
Skeletal tractionPins inserted into bone (long-term)90-90 traction (femoral fracture)
  • Never remove or adjust weights without a provider order
  • Maintain weights hanging freely (not resting on floor or bed)
  • Check pin sites for infection (redness, drainage, odor)
  • Provide skin care; prevent pressure ulcers

8. Complications — NCLEX Must-Know

Compartment Syndrome

  • ↑ Pressure within closed muscle compartment → impairs perfusion → tissue necrosis
  • Hallmark: Pain out of proportion, not relieved by elevation or analgesics; tight/swollen limb
  • Emergency intervention: Remove/bivalve cast → fasciotomy

Fat Embolism Syndrome

  • Fat droplets from bone marrow (especially long bones — femur) enter bloodstream
  • Triad: Respiratory distress + petechiae (chest/axillae) + confusion
  • Treatment: Oxygen, ventilatory support, report STAT — treat like PE

Other Complications

  • Osteomyelitis — bone infection (common with open fractures) → long-term IV antibiotics
  • DVT/PE — from immobility → SCDs, early ambulation, anticoagulants
  • Delayed union / Malunion / Nonunion — healing problems
Fat embolism: Petechiae on chest/axillae + sudden confusion + SOB in a long-bone fracture patient = emergency.

9. Medications

  • Analgesics — acetaminophen, opioids for pain control
  • NSAIDs — ibuprofen (anti-inflammatory); avoid NSAIDs long-term — they delay bone healing
  • Muscle relaxants — cyclobenzaprine for muscle spasms
  • Antibiotics — for open fractures to prevent infection
  • Anticoagulants — DVT prophylaxis after fracture/surgery
  • Calcium + Vitamin D — support bone healing
NSAIDs and smoking delay fracture healing — teach patients to avoid both.

10. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ 6 P's after every fracture/cast/traction — abnormals = call provider
  • ✅ Open fracture = high infection risk
  • ✅ Spiral fracture in child = suspect abuse
  • ✅ Compartment syndrome = remove cast + fasciotomy = EMERGENCY
  • ✅ Fat embolism = petechiae + confusion + respiratory distress
  • ✅ Traction weights = NEVER touch or remove without order
  • ✅ Cast: palm to handle, elevate 24–48 hrs, keep dry
  • ✅ NSAIDs delay bone healing
Priority: Neurovascular checks (6 P's) are the nurse's #1 assessment after any orthopedic procedure.
