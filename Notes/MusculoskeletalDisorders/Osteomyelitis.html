Osteomyelitis

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. Definition

Osteomyelitis = infection of the bone.

  • Acute — sudden onset, responds well to antibiotics
  • Chronic — long-standing or recurring; harder to treat; necrotic bone present

Most common pathogen: Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA)

2. Pathophysiology

  1. Pathogen enters bone via hematogenous spread (bloodstream), direct inoculation (open fracture, surgery), or spread from adjacent soft tissue infection
  2. Infection causes inflammation → swelling → ↑ pressure inside bone
  3. Increased pressure cuts off blood supply → bone necrosis (dead bone = sequestrum)
  4. Antibiotics cannot penetrate necrotic bone → chronic infection risk
Sequestrum = dead bone fragment. Involucrum = new bone formed around the sequestrum. Chronic osteomyelitis until sequestrum is removed.

3. Risk Factors

  • Open fractures (bone pierces skin)
  • Orthopedic surgery / joint replacements
  • Diabetic foot ulcers (most common in adults)
  • IV drug use (hematogenous spread)
  • Pressure ulcers with underlying bone exposure
  • Peripheral vascular disease (↓ blood flow = ↓ healing)
  • Immunocompromised (cancer, HIV, steroids)
  • Sickle cell disease (Salmonella is common pathogen)

4. Signs & Symptoms

  • Constant, localized, throbbing bone pain — hallmark symptom
  • Fever (systemic sign of infection)
  • Swelling, redness, warmth over affected area
  • Limited range of motion of nearby joints
  • Purulent drainage if open wound present
  • Elevated WBC, ↑ ESR, ↑ CRP (inflammatory markers)
NCLEX cue: Fever + localized constant bone pain in patient with recent surgery, trauma, or diabetes = suspect osteomyelitis.

5. Diagnosis

TestPurpose
MRIBest imaging — shows early bone/soft tissue changes (gold standard for imaging)
Bone scanDetects early osteomyelitis before X-ray changes visible
X-rayNormal early; bone changes visible after 10–21 days
Blood culturesIdentify causative organism
Bone biopsyGold standard (definitive diagnosis) — identifies pathogen
WBC, ESR, CRPElevated in active infection

6. Treatment

IV Antibiotics

  • Long-term IV antibiotics: 4–6 weeks minimum (often via PICC line)
  • Common agents: Vancomycin (MRSA), Ceftriaxone, Clindamycin, Nafcillin
  • Monitor for adverse effects: nephrotoxicity (vancomycin), ototoxicity

Surgical Intervention

  • Debridement — surgical removal of necrotic (dead) bone and infected tissue
  • Sequestrectomy — removal of sequestrum (dead bone fragment) in chronic cases
  • Amputation — last resort in severe, unresponsive, or vascular-compromised cases

Wound Care

  • Sterile technique for all dressing changes (prevents reinfection)
  • Daily dressing changes; monitor for purulent (pus-like) drainage, odor, tunneling

7. PICC Line Nursing Care

  • Teach patient/family PICC line care: flush daily as instructed, keep clean/dry
  • Report redness, swelling, pain, drainage at insertion site
  • Report fever (may indicate line sepsis)
  • No MRI with older PICC lines — check compatibility
  • Confirm placement before use (X-ray confirmation)

8. Nursing Interventions

  • Administer and monitor IV antibiotics (timing, levels, adverse effects)
  • Assess neurovascular status distal to infection — color, capillary refill, temperature, sensation, movement, pulses
  • Perform sterile wound care and document wound changes
  • Monitor for sepsis: fever, hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status
  • Encourage nutrition: protein, vitamin C, zinc — essential for wound healing and immune function
  • Immobilize affected limb (splint or bed rest) to minimize pain and prevent pathologic fracture
  • Provide adequate pain management

9. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Most common cause = Staphylococcus aureus
  • ✅ Sickle cell disease + osteomyelitis = think Salmonella
  • ✅ Gold standard diagnosis = bone biopsy
  • ✅ Treatment = 4–6 weeks IV antibiotics via PICC line
  • ✅ Chronic osteomyelitis = necrotic bone (sequestrum) must be surgically removed
  • ✅ Sterile technique for all wound care
  • ✅ Monitor vancomycin levels — nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity risk
  • ✅ Nutrition critical — protein, vitamin C, zinc for healing
NCLEX tip: Fever + bone pain + elevated WBC/ESR/CRP + recent open fracture, surgery, or diabetic ulcer = osteomyelitis.
