Pediatrics — Integumentary

Pediatric Integumentary Dysfunction

Diaper dermatitis, eczema, impetigo, head lice, burns — assessment and nursing care

Diaper Dermatitis (Diaper Rash)

Irritant contact dermatitis from prolonged skin exposure to urine and feces.

Irritant Diaper Rash
  • Red, inflamed skin in diaper area
  • Spares the skin folds (folds protected from contact)
  • Treatment: keep clean and dry; zinc oxide barrier cream; change diapers frequently; pat dry (don't rub); avoid alcohol wipes
Candidal (Yeast) Diaper Rash
  • Bright red, beefy rash
  • Satellite lesions (small red spots scattered outside main rash area)
  • Involves the skin folds
  • Treatment: topical antifungal (nystatin cream)
💡
NCLEX Tip

Satellite lesions = Candida = antifungal. Rash spares folds = irritant = barrier cream. Don't confuse the two!

Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)

Chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin condition. Strong association with asthma and allergic rhinitis ("atopic triad"). Caused by impaired skin barrier + immune dysregulation.

Infant Distribution

Cheeks, scalp, extensor surfaces of arms and legs (reaching, crawling areas)

Older Child Distribution

Flexural creases (inside elbows, behind knees), wrists, ankles

Treatment:

  • Moisturize generously after every bath (emollients, fragrance-free)
  • Avoid triggers: heat, sweating, harsh soaps, wool, fragrances, food allergens
  • Topical corticosteroids for flares (lowest effective potency)
  • Antihistamines (diphenhydramine) for itch, especially at night
  • Wet wrap therapy for severe cases
  • Keep nails short to minimize excoriation

Impetigo

Highly contagious superficial bacterial skin infection. Caused by Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pyogenes. Common in children, especially in warm/humid weather.

Classic Signs
  • Begins as red sores that rupture
  • Honey-colored (golden) crusted lesions
  • Most common: face (around mouth/nose), hands
  • Bullous impetigo: fluid-filled blisters (staph)

Treatment: Topical mupirocin (Bactroban) for limited lesions; oral antibiotics for widespread. Meticulous hand hygiene; separate towels/linens. Child should stay home until 24 hours after antibiotics started.

Pediculosis Capitis (Head Lice)

Pediculus humanus capitis infestation. Spread by direct head-to-head contact. Not a sign of poor hygiene.

Signs: Intense scalp itching, visible lice or nits (eggs cemented to hair shafts, especially behind ears and at nape of neck).

Treatment:

  • Permethrin 1% (Nix) lotion/cream — apply to dry hair, leave 10 min, rinse
  • Fine-tooth nit comb to remove nits daily
  • Wash bedding, clothing, towels, stuffed animals in hot water and dry on high heat
  • Vacuum furniture and car seats
  • Repeat treatment in 7–9 days to kill newly hatched lice
  • Notify school/daycare

Burns

Pediatric burns are more serious than adult burns due to thinner skin, larger surface area relative to body size, and immature thermoregulation.

DepthAppearancePainHealing
Superficial (1st degree)Red, dry, no blisters (like sunburn)Painful3–5 days
Partial-thickness (2nd degree)Blisters, moist, red/pinkVery painful2–3 weeks
Full-thickness (3rd degree)White, waxy, leathery, or charred — dry, no blistersPainless (nerve destruction)Requires grafting

Nursing Priorities for Burns

  1. Airway — facial/inhalation burns = top priority (edema can obstruct rapidly)
  2. IV fluids — Parkland formula (4 mL/kg/% TBSA in first 24 hrs; half in first 8 hrs)
  3. Pain management
  4. Prevent infection — sterile dressings; topical silver sulfadiazine
  5. Monitor for compartment syndrome (circumferential burns → escharotomy)
  6. Prevent hypothermia (large surface area + fluid loss)
🚨
Full-Thickness Burns = No Pain

Full-thickness (3rd degree) burns destroy nerve endings = painless. This is a red flag — the child may not complain. Monitor and treat aggressively.

Pediatric Burn — TBSA Estimation

Rule of Nines — Child Adjustments

Children have different body proportions than adults — standard Rule of Nines must be adjusted. The Lund and Browder Chart is the most accurate method for pediatric burns because it accounts for age-based changes in head and leg size.

Body AreaAdult % TBSAChild % TBSA
Head & Neck9%18% — larger relative to body in children
Each Leg18%14% — smaller relative to body in children
Anterior Trunk18%18%
Posterior Trunk18%18%
Each Arm9%9%
🧠
NCLEX KEY

Use the Lund and Browder Chart for pediatric burns — more accurate than Rule of Nines. Head = 18% TBSA in children (vs 9% in adults). Legs = 14% each in children (vs 18% in adults). Using adult Rule of Nines on a child will underestimate TBSA.

Scald Burns & Non-Accidental Trauma (Abuse) 🚨

Suspicious Burn Patterns — Child Abuse Indicators

Scald burns are the most common type of burn in young children. Always assess for signs of non-accidental trauma (NAT/abuse):

  • Stocking/glove distribution — uniform scald line on hands/feet (child held in hot water; defensive splashing would create irregular burn)
  • Bilateral symmetric burns — both hands or both feet equally burned (not typical of accidental contact)
  • Burns on buttocks or perineum — "dunking" pattern
  • Delay in seeking care — caregiver waited hours/days to bring child in
  • Inconsistent history — story doesn't match burn pattern or child's developmental level
  • Multiple burns at different stages of healing
🚨
MANDATORY REPORTER

Nurses are mandatory reporters. Suspected child abuse → report to child protective services (CPS) and notify charge nurse/provider immediately. Document objectively: exact burn location, shape, size, and caregiver's explanation.

Scabies (Pediatric)

Scabies — Sarcoptes scabiei Infestation

Parasitic skin infestation. Very contagious — spreads by direct skin-to-skin contact. Common in schools, daycares, and households.

  • Key symptom: Intense itching — worse at night (characteristic)
  • Appearance: Small red bumps + burrow tracks (thin, thread-like lines under skin)
  • Location: Between fingers, wrists, waist, axillae; in infants — palms, soles, face, scalp

Treatment:

  • Permethrin 5% cream — apply from neck to toes (full body); leave on 8–14 hours, then wash off; repeat in 7 days if needed
  • Treat all household members and close contacts simultaneously
  • Wash all clothing, bedding, towels in hot water; dry on high heat
  • Itching may persist 2–4 weeks after successful treatment — does NOT mean treatment failed
🧠
NCLEX KEY

Scabies = intense itching worse at night + burrow tracks. Permethrin 5% = treatment. Treat entire household. Persistent itching after treatment ≠ failure (inflammatory response continues).

Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) in Children

SJS — Life-Threatening Drug Reaction

Rare but life-threatening mucocutaneous hypersensitivity reaction, most often triggered by medications.

Common triggers: Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine), sulfa drugs (TMP-SMX), NSAIDs, allopurinol, antibiotics

  • Painful red/purple rash that spreads rapidly
  • Skin sloughs off in sheets (like a severe burn)
  • Mucosal involvement: mouth ulcers, eye inflammation, genital lesions
  • High fever, malaise precede the rash (prodrome)
  • Affects <10% of BSA in SJS; >30% = Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN) — more severe
🚨
PRIORITY — STOP THE OFFENDING DRUG IMMEDIATELY

Identify and discontinue the causative drug. Treat like a burn patient: IV fluids, infection prevention, sterile wound care, pain control, nutritional support. Ophthalmology consult if eyes involved. ICU or burn unit admission.

Periorbital Cellulitis (Pediatric)

Periorbital (Preseptal) vs. Orbital Cellulitis

Periorbital cellulitis is most common in children under 10. Always distinguish from orbital cellulitis (emergent).

FeaturePeriorbital (Preseptal)Orbital Cellulitis
LocationIn front of orbital septum — eyelidBehind orbital septum — within orbit
Pain with eye movement❌ Absent✅ Present — KEY sign
Vision changes❌ None✅ Possible
Proptosis (bulging eye)❌ No✅ Yes
SeveritySerious but manageableEmergency — risk of vision loss, meningitis
TreatmentOral amoxicillin-clavulanate (mild); IV antibiotics if <1 yr or worseningIV antibiotics (ceftriaxone ± vancomycin) + ophthalmology consult + possible surgical drainage

Common causes in children: Eyelid trauma, insect bite, sinusitis (especially ethmoid sinus), stye/chalazion

🚨
NCLEX RED FLAG

Pain with eye movement + proptosis + restricted extraocular movements = orbital cellulitis = emergency. Educate caregivers: return immediately if child's eye appears to bulge, vision changes, or eye movement becomes painful.

Tinea Infections (Ringworm)

Tinea is a fungal infection (dermatophyte) — not actually a worm. Named by body location:

TypeLocationSignsTreatment
Tinea capitisScalpCircular patches of hair loss, scaling, possible kerion (boggy swelling); most common in school-age childrenOral griseofulvin (topical antifungals don't penetrate hair follicles); selenium sulfide shampoo as adjunct
Tinea corporisBody (trunk/extremities)Ring-shaped, red, scaly plaque with central clearing and raised borderTopical antifungal: clotrimazole or miconazole × 2–4 weeks
Tinea pedisFeet (athlete's foot)Scaling, cracking, maceration between toes; itching and burningTopical antifungal (clotrimazole/terbinafine); keep feet dry; moisture-wicking socks
Tinea crurisGroin (jock itch)Red, itchy rash with raised border; spares scrotumTopical antifungal; keep area dry; loose clothing
💡
NCLEX Tip — Tinea Capitis

Tinea capitis = scalp ringworm = must use oral griseofulvin (not topical). Educate: do not share combs, hats, or hair accessories. Exclude from school until treatment started.

Psoriasis (Pediatric)

Chronic, immune-mediated inflammatory skin disease; can begin in childhood. Family history often present.

  • Signs: well-defined silvery-white plaques on red base; typically on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees), scalp, lower back
  • Auspitz sign: pinpoint bleeding when scale is removed
  • Koebner phenomenon: new plaques form at sites of skin trauma
  • Triggers: stress, streptococcal infection (guttate psoriasis — droplet-shaped lesions after strep throat), skin injury, certain medications

Treatment (pediatric):

  • First-line: topical corticosteroids (reduce inflammation), topical calcipotriene (vitamin D analog)
  • Coal tar preparations (shampoo or cream)
  • Emollients/moisturizers to reduce scaling
  • Severe cases: phototherapy (UVB), systemic agents (methotrexate)
⚠️
NCLEX Note

Psoriasis = silvery plaques on extensor surfaces. Guttate psoriasis in children is often triggered by streptococcal pharyngitis — treat the strep. Condition is chronic; focus on symptom management and psychosocial support.

Acne Vulgaris (Adolescent)

Most common skin disorder in adolescents; caused by follicular plugging, sebum overproduction, and Cutibacterium acnes overgrowth.

  • Comedones: open (blackheads) and closed (whiteheads) — non-inflammatory
  • Inflammatory: papules, pustules, nodules, cysts — risk of scarring

Treatment (stepwise):

SeverityTreatment
Mild (comedones)Topical retinoids (tretinoin — first-line, unplugs follicles) + topical benzoyl peroxide (antibacterial)
Moderate (papules/pustules)Add topical antibiotics (clindamycin) ± oral doxycycline
Severe (nodular/cystic)Oral isotretinoin (Accutane) — requires iPLEDGE program: monthly pregnancy tests, no blood donation, no vitamin A supplements
🚨
Isotretinoin Safety (NCLEX)

Isotretinoin = highly teratogenic (Category X). Female patients must use two forms of contraception and have monthly negative pregnancy tests. Educate: avoid sun exposure, waxing, and do not donate blood during treatment.
