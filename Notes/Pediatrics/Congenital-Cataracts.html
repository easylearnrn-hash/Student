Pediatrics — Eye Disorders

Congenital Cataracts

Clouding of the lens present at birth — urgent intervention required

Definition

Congenital cataracts are clouding or opacity of the lens of the eye present at birth or developing during infancy, which can interfere with the development of normal vision. If not treated early, congenital cataracts can lead to amblyopia (lazy eye) and permanent vision loss.

Pathophysiology

  • The lens is normally clear and helps focus light onto the retina
  • In congenital cataracts, the lens is partially or completely opaque → light cannot reach the retina properly
  • The visual cortex doesn't receive proper stimulation → vision development is impaired → leads to amblyopia

Causes / Risk Factors

CategoryExamples
Genetic/HereditaryFamily history of congenital cataracts
TORCH Infections during pregnancyToxoplasmosis, Rubella, Cytomegalovirus (CMV), Herpes simplex
Metabolic disordersGalactosemia, hypoglycemia
Chromosomal abnormalitiesDown syndrome, Trisomy 13 or 18

Signs & Symptoms

  • White or gray pupil reflex (leukocoria)
  • Absent or abnormal red reflex (seen during eye exam)
  • Nystagmus (involuntary eye movement)
  • Strabismus (misaligned eyes)
  • Poor visual tracking — does not follow objects
  • Delayed visual development
  • Excessive squinting or blinking
  • In bilateral cases — infant may appear less responsive to visual stimuli (faces, toys)

Diagnosis

  • Red Reflex Test (at birth and well-baby checks) — normally both eyes reflect red; in cataracts → reflex is absent or white
  • Slit-lamp examination — detailed view of the lens
  • Ophthalmologic referral — pediatric ophthalmologist confirms diagnosis
  • Genetic or metabolic testing — if cause unclear

Treatment

🚨
Urgent Intervention Required!

Especially in the first 6–8 weeks of life to prevent permanent visual loss.

InterventionPurpose
Surgical lens removal (lensectomy)Performed if cataract is large or blocking vision
Intraocular lens (IOL) implantSometimes delayed; infants may wear contact lenses or glasses post-op
Post-op visual rehabilitationCorrective lenses or glasses to focus light on the retina
Patching therapyPatch the stronger eye to stimulate the weaker eye and prevent amblyopia
Regular ophthalmology follow-upMonitor visual development and prevent complications

Postoperative Nursing Care

  • Administer prescribed eye drops (antibiotics, corticosteroids)
  • Teach parents how to apply eye drops safely
  • Monitor for signs of infection: redness, swelling, discharge, fever
  • Use eye shield or protective eyewear to prevent injury
  • Instruct caregivers to report any decrease in vision or eye alignment changes

Complications If Untreated or Delayed

🚨
Permanent Vision Loss

Amblyopia (irreversible) • Strabismus • Blindness • Delayed development • Glaucoma (can occur after surgery)

Parent Teaching — NCLEX Focused

  • Screening at birth and well-child visits is critical
  • Emphasize the importance of early surgery (ideally within weeks for dense cataracts)
  • Adherence to eye drops, follow-ups, and patching is key to successful treatment
  • Help parents understand the risk of amblyopia and importance of vision rehabilitation
  • Teach safe handling of eye protection and contact lenses/glasses post-surgery

NCLEX Practice Q&A

Q: A newborn assessment shows absent red reflex in the left eye. Best action?
A: Notify the healthcare provider immediately (may indicate congenital cataract or retinoblastoma)

Q: Which infection during pregnancy is associated with congenital cataracts?
A: Rubella (TORCH infection)
