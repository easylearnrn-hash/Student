Pediatrics — GI

Pediatric Gastrointestinal Disorders

Cleft lip/palate, TEF, pyloric stenosis, intussusception, Hirschsprung, GERD, appendicitis, celiac

Cleft Lip & Palate

Incomplete fusion of lip or palate during fetal development. Cleft lip = groove in upper lip; Cleft palate = opening in roof of mouth. Can occur separately or together.

Feeding Before Surgery
  • Use specialized cleft bottles/nipples (Haberman feeder, Mead Johnson)
  • Upright or semi-upright during feeding
  • Burp frequently (swallows more air)
Post-Op Care
  • Elbow restraints — prevent hands touching repair site
  • Clean suture site gently after feedings
  • Soft diet for specified period
  • No pacifiers, straws, or fingers near repair

Esophageal Atresia (EA) / Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF)

EA: Esophagus ends in a blind pouch (doesn't connect to stomach). TEF: Abnormal connection between trachea and esophagus. These often occur together.

Signs at birth: Frothy saliva and bubbles, choking/coughing with first feeding, cyanosis, inability to swallow.

Action: NPO immediately; NG tube to low suction (to prevent aspiration); position upright; IV fluids; surgical repair is required.

Pyloric Stenosis

Hypertrophy of the pyloric muscle → narrowed pyloric channel → stomach cannot empty. Most common at 2–8 weeks of age. More common in first-born males.

Classic Signs
  • Projectile vomiting after feeding (non-bilious — NOT green)
  • Olive-shaped mass palpable in right upper abdomen
  • Constant hunger (hungry right after vomiting)
  • Weight loss, dehydration, metabolic alkalosis

Diagnosis: Ultrasound. Treatment: Stabilize fluids and electrolytes first, then pyloromyotomy (surgical cut of pyloric muscle).

Intussusception

Telescoping of one bowel segment into another → obstruction. Most common age 6 months – 3 years.

Classic Signs (TRIAD)
  • Sudden paroxysmal abdominal pain (child draws knees to chest, then resolves)
  • Red, jelly-like stools (blood + mucus = "currant jelly" stool)
  • Vomiting

Diagnosis: Ultrasound ("target sign"). Treatment: Air enema (first-line, often curative). Surgery if enema fails or bowel is compromised.

Hirschsprung Disease

Absence of ganglion (nerve) cells in the colon → no peristalsis in affected segment → functional obstruction.

  • No meconium within 24 hours of birth (hallmark sign in newborns)
  • Abdominal distension
  • Ribbon-like stools (narrow)
  • Vomiting, feeding intolerance

Treatment: Surgery (pull-through procedure) to remove aganglionic segment. May need temporary colostomy.

GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease)

Stomach contents reflux back into esophagus due to weak/incompetent LES. Normal spitting up vs. GERD = distinction based on frequency and complications.

  • Signs: frequent spitting up, arching back (Sandifer posture), poor weight gain, irritability, hoarse voice
  • Treatment: small frequent feedings, thicken formula (rice cereal), upright 30 min after feeding, avoid overfeeding, PPIs if severe

Appendicitis

Inflammation of the appendix. Common in school-age and adolescents. Can rupture → peritonitis.

  • Pain begins around navel → migrates to right lower quadrant (McBurney's point)
  • Fever, nausea, anorexia, rebound tenderness, guarding
🚨
Sudden Pain Relief = Rupture Risk

If abdominal pain suddenly stops, suspect perforation. Do NOT apply heat to abdomen, do NOT give enemas. Keep NPO. Report immediately. Appendectomy needed.

Celiac Disease

Autoimmune reaction to gluten (protein in wheat, barley, rye) → damage to small intestine villi → malabsorption.

Signs: Chronic diarrhea, steatorrhea (fatty/greasy stools), abdominal bloating and pain, failure to thrive, weight loss, iron-deficiency anemia, fatigue.

Treatment: Lifelong strict gluten-free diet — No wheat (bread, pasta, cereal), barley, rye. Oats = sometimes contaminated. Rice, corn, potatoes = safe.

📝
NCLEX Summary

Projectile vomiting + olive mass = pyloric stenosis. Red jelly stool + sudden cramping = intussusception. No meconium 24h = Hirschsprung. Cleft palate post-op = elbow restraints. Appendicitis pain suddenly gone = rupture alert. TEF = frothy secretions + choking = NG tube + NPO + surgery. Celiac = lifelong no gluten. GERD = small feedings, upright positioning.
