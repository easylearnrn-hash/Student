Pediatric Orthopaedics

DDH, Clubfoot, and Scoliosis — NCLEX overview

1. Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

DDH = the hip joint is dislocated or unstable at birth due to abnormal development of the acetabulum (hip socket) or femoral head.

Risk Factors
  • Mostly occurs in girls, first-born, breech position, or family history
  • Tight swaddling

Signs & Symptoms

Infants (under 2–3 months):

  • Asymmetrical gluteal/thigh folds (folds don't match on both sides)
  • Uneven leg length
  • Limited hip abduction (cannot move thigh outward fully)
  • Positive Ortolani sign: "Click" felt or heard when hip is gently abducted
  • Positive Barlow sign: Hip dislocates when adducted with pressure

Older infants/children:

  • Limping, waddling gait
  • Positive Trendelenburg sign (hip drops on unaffected side when standing on affected leg)

Treatment

Pavlik Harness (infants <6 months)
  • Soft brace holding hips in flexed and abducted position
  • Worn 24/7 for 6–12 weeks
  • Do not adjust straps — HCP only
  • Place diaper under harness; no lotions or powders under straps
Spica Cast (if Pavlik fails or older infants)
  • Rigid cast holding hips and legs in position
  • Requires special car seat
⚠️
NCLEX Tips — DDH

Never double-diaper or remove harness unless ordered. Neurovascular checks every shift: color, temperature, cap refill, sensation, movement. Assess skin under straps for breakdown.

2. Clubfoot (Talipes Equinovarus)

A congenital deformity where the foot is twisted inward and downward. Involves shortened tendons and ligaments. Can be unilateral or bilateral. Associated with neuromuscular disorders or oligohydramnios (low amniotic fluid).

Treatment

  • Serial casting (Ponseti method): Begin within first 1–2 weeks of life; casts changed weekly
  • May require Achilles tenotomy (tendon cut to allow correction)
  • Followed by bracing (e.g., Denis Browne bar)
💡
NCLEX Tips — Clubfoot

Teach parents proper skin care, cast care, and bracing schedules. If toes are pale or cap refill > 3 seconds, report immediately. Do not stick anything inside the cast.

3. Scoliosis Screening

Lateral (sideways) curvature of the spine >10°, often accompanied by rotation.

Screening
  • Performed during school-age or early adolescence
  • Adam's Forward Bend Test: Child bends forward; look for asymmetry, rib hump, or shoulder/hip elevation
Types
  • Idiopathic (most common, adolescent girls)
  • Congenital
  • Neuromuscular

Management

  • Mild (<20°) → observation & exercises
  • Moderate (20–40°) → bracing (e.g., Boston brace): worn 18–23 hrs/day; remove only for bathing; not curative — prevents worsening
  • Severe (>40°) → surgery (spinal fusion with rods)
⚠️
NCLEX Tips — Scoliosis

Monitor post-op for neurovascular status, pain, and signs of spinal cord compromise. Log roll when repositioning. Encourage ambulation and deep breathing post-op. Bracing requires psychosocial support — body image concerns are common.
