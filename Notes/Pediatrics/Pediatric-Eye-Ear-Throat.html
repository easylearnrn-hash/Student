Strabismus & Amblyopia

Strabismus

Misalignment of the eyes (crossing or wandering). Can cause amblyopia if untreated. Treatment: corrective glasses, eye patching, eye muscle surgery.

Amblyopia ("Lazy Eye")

Reduced vision in one eye (brain suppresses the weaker eye). Often caused by strabismus or significant refractive difference. Best treated before age 6–7 (critical visual development period). Treatment: patch the strong eye to force use of weak eye.

NCLEX Link

Untreated strabismus → amblyopia → permanent vision loss. Early detection (pediatrician vision screening) is key. Child compliance with patching is a nursing education priority.

Ear — Otitis Media (Middle Ear Infection)

Most common childhood illness requiring medical treatment. Fluid and infection behind tympanic membrane. Most common: 6 months – 2 years.

Signs: Ear pain (pulling/tugging at ear in non-verbal children), fever, irritability, poor sleep, poor feeding, decreased hearing, fluid draining from ear.

Risk factors: Bottle feeding in supine position, secondhand smoke, daycare attendance, cleft palate, formula feeding (vs breastfeeding).

Treatment: Pain management first (acetaminophen/ibuprofen). Antibiotics if: child <2 years, severe symptoms, bilateral, or fails to improve. "Watchful waiting" for 48–72h in mild cases in older children.

Recurrent OM: Tympanostomy tubes (PE tubes) to drain fluid and ventilate middle ear.

Otitis Externa ("Swimmer's Ear")

Infection of the external ear canal. Signs: ear pain worsened by pulling on outer ear (pinna), itching, discharge. Treatment: topical antibiotic ear drops; keep ear dry; no swimming until resolved.

Hearing Impairment

Conductive (outer/middle ear) or sensorineural (inner ear/nerve). Leading cause of speech/language delays.

Infant red flags: No startle (Moro) to sound, no babbling by 7 months, no response to name by 12 months, no words by 15 months.

Support: Hearing aids, cochlear implants, early speech therapy, sign language, FM systems in school.

Throat — Tonsillitis

Inflammation of tonsils; viral or bacterial (Group A Strep). Signs: sore throat, fever, difficulty swallowing, muffled voice, tonsillar exudates (white patches), tender cervical lymph nodes.

Diagnosis: Rapid strep test or throat culture. Treatment: Fluids and analgesics; penicillin (or amoxicillin) if strep positive (full 10-day course).

Tonsillectomy if: recurrent strep (>6/year or >3/year × 3 years), chronic airway obstruction, sleep apnea.

Post-Tonsillectomy Care
  • Watch for bleeding: frequent swallowing, clearing throat, coughing blood = EMERGENCY
  • Cool fluids (popsicles, ice chips, cool water)
  • Avoid red/dark liquids (looks like blood if vomited)
  • Avoid straws (suction → dislodges clot), hot foods, hard/crunchy foods for 2 weeks
  • Ice collar for comfort
  • Pain peaks on day 5–7 (clot separates) — teach family to expect this
  • Avoid strenuous activity for 2 weeks
🚨
Post-Tonsillectomy Hemorrhage

Frequent swallowing or spitting of bright red blood after tonsillectomy = hemorrhage. Return to ER immediately. This can be life-threatening.

NCLEX Summary

Post-tonsillectomy bleeding = EMERGENCY (frequent swallowing = hemorrhage). Strabismus → amblyopia if untreated. Ear tugging + fever = otitis media. No babbling by 7 months = hearing loss red flag. Bacterial conjunctivitis = yellow pus = antibiotics, stay home 24h. Croup = cool mist; Epiglottitis = DO NOT examine throat, prepare airway, IV antibiotics.
