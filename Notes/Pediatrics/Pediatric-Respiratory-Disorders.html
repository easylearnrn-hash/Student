Pediatrics — Respiratory

Pediatric Respiratory Disorders

Croup, epiglottitis, bronchiolitis, asthma, cystic fibrosis, RSV

Why Pediatric Airways Are Different

  • Smaller airway diameter — even small swelling causes significant obstruction
  • Larger tongues relative to airway; shorter tracheas
  • Respiratory muscles tire faster
  • Infants are obligate nose breathers

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

Key Features
  • Viral (parainfluenza most common)
  • Upper airway inflammation (larynx + trachea)
  • Ages 6 months – 3 years
Signs & Symptoms
  • Barking (seal-like) cough
  • Inspiratory stridor
  • Hoarse voice
  • Worse at night

Treatment: Cool mist or humidified air, dexamethasone (steroids to reduce swelling), racemic epinephrine (nebulized) for severe cases, O₂ as needed, keep calm.

Epiglottitis (EMERGENCY)

Immediate Danger

Bacterial infection (Haemophilus influenzae type b) causing rapid, severe epiglottis swelling. Can completely obstruct the airway within hours.

Signs: Sudden high fever, drooling, tripod position (leaning forward on hands to open airway), muffled "hot potato" voice, severe sore throat, stridor.

🚨
DO NOT examine the throat!

Throat inspection can trigger complete airway spasm. Keep child calm. Notify provider STAT. Prepare for intubation. Give IV antibiotics immediately in a controlled setting (OR/ICU).

Prevention: Hib vaccine has dramatically reduced cases.

Bronchiolitis (RSV)

  • Viral lower airway infection — most common cause: RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus)
  • Most common in infants and toddlers, especially <2 years

Signs: Wheezing, cough, tachypnea, retractions, poor feeding, nasal congestion, low-grade fever.

Treatment: Supportive only — nasal suctioning, O₂, IV fluids if unable to feed, upright positioning. No antibiotics (viral). Hospitalize if SpO₂ <90%, dehydrated, or respiratory distress.

Prevention: Synagis (palivizumab) monthly injections for high-risk infants (premature, heart/lung disease).

Asthma

Chronic inflammatory airway disease with reversible bronchoconstriction and mucus production. Triggers: allergens, cold air, exercise, tobacco smoke, viral infections.

Signs: Wheezing (expiratory), chest tightness, persistent cough (especially at night or with exercise), prolonged expiratory phase.

💡
NCLEX Tip — Asthma Inhaler

Always use a spacer with metered-dose inhalers in children. Rinse mouth after inhaled corticosteroids to prevent oral candidiasis (thrush). Albuterol = rescue; steroid inhaler = controller (not for acute attacks).

Cystic Fibrosis (CF)

Autosomal recessive genetic disorder → defective CFTR protein → abnormally thick, sticky mucus throughout body (lungs, GI, pancreas, sinuses, reproductive tract).

Signs: Chronic productive cough, recurrent lung infections (Pseudomonas common), poor weight gain/failure to thrive, greasy/bulky stools (steatorrhea), abdominal distension, salty skin (positive sweat chloride test >60 mEq/L).

Management:

  • Airway clearance: chest physiotherapy (CPT), flutter valve, vest therapy
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement with every meal and snack
  • High-calorie, high-protein diet (up to 120–150% of normal daily intake)
  • Bronchodilators, inhaled DNase (dornase alfa), antibiotics, CFTR modulators (ivacaftor)
  • Fat-soluble vitamin supplements (A, D, E, K)

NCLEX Summary

Croup = barking cough + stridor → cool mist + dexamethasone. Epiglottitis = drooling + tripod + high fever → DO NOT examine throat, prepare airway. Bronchiolitis (RSV) = suction + O₂ + hydration, NO antibiotics. Asthma = albuterol rescue + ICS controller. CF = airway clearance + enzymes + high calories. Synagis = monthly prevention for high-risk RSV.
