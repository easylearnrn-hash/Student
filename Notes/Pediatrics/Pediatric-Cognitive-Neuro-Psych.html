Pediatric Cognitive, Neurologic & Psychosocial Nursing

Seizures, hydrocephalus, meningitis, cerebral palsy, Down syndrome, ASD, ADHD, anxiety, depression

Seizures

Seizure Nursing Care
  • Stay with the child — never leave alone
  • Position side-lying (recovery position) to protect airway
  • Do NOT restrain limbs or put anything in the mouth
  • Clear the area of hard objects; cushion the head
  • Time the seizure — >5 min = status epilepticus (emergency)
  • Assess LOC and breathing after seizure (postictal phase)
  • Document: type, duration, body parts involved, behavior after

Medications: Acute: diazepam lorazepam (stop active seizure). Maintenance: phenytoin levetiracetam valproate. Febrile seizures: treat fever (antipyretics), no routine prophylaxis.

Hydrocephalus

Excess accumulation of CSF → increased intracranial pressure (ICP). Can be congenital or acquired (meningitis, hemorrhage).

Infant signs of increased ICP: Bulging anterior fontanel, high-pitched cry, vomiting (projectile), increasing head circumference ("sunset eyes" — eyes deviate downward), irritability, poor feeding.

Older child signs: Headache (especially morning), vomiting, blurred vision, papilledema, personality changes.

Treatment: Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt — drains excess CSF from brain ventricle to peritoneal cavity.

VP Shunt Nursing Care
  • Position: flat or slightly elevated per MD order (avoid rapid pressure changes)
  • Watch for shunt failure: return of increased ICP signs — headache, vomiting, irritability, deteriorating neuro status
  • Watch for shunt infection: fever, redness along shunt tract, neck stiffness, altered LOC
  • Measure and document head circumference (infants)
  • Shunt may require revision as child grows

Meningitis

Inflammation of meninges (membranes surrounding brain/spinal cord). Bacterial = most serious. Viral = more common, less severe.

Classic signs: Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck), photophobia, phonophobia, fever, headache, vomiting. Infant: bulging fontanel, high-pitched cry, poor feeding, seizures.

Kernig's Sign

Flex hip to 90°, then try to extend knee → pain and resistance = positive (meningeal irritation)

Brudzinski's Sign

Flex neck → involuntary flexion of knees and hips = positive (meningeal irritation)

Treatment: IV antibiotics ASAP for bacterial (do not delay for LP if critically ill); IV steroids (dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation; droplet isolation for bacterial meningitis (until 24h on antibiotics).

Complications: Hearing loss, seizures, cognitive impairment, SIADH, septicemia, Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (meningococcal), DIC.

Cerebral Palsy (CP)

Group of permanent movement/posture disorders caused by non-progressive brain injury before, during, or after birth. Many have intellectual disability, seizures, speech/vision/hearing impairment.

Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

Extra copy of chromosome 21. Most common chromosomal disorder.

Features: Intellectual disability (variable), hypotonia (low muscle tone), flat facial profile, upslanting palpebral fissures, single palmar (simian) crease, small ears, protruding tongue, short stature.

Associated conditions: Congenital heart defects (AV canal most common — 40–50%), hearing loss, vision problems, hypothyroidism, celiac disease, leukemia, Alzheimer's risk (adult).

⚠️
Atlantoaxial Instability

Instability at C1–C2 in ~15% of Down syndrome patients. Screen before sports/surgery. Avoid high-risk neck flexion activities (tumbling, diving, certain gymnastic moves).

Nursing support: Early intervention (PT, OT, speech), monitor growth/hearing/vision/thyroid/heart, genetic counseling, support groups for family.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Neurodevelopmental disorder affecting communication, social interaction, and behavior. Appears before age 3. Wide spectrum (mild to severe).

Signs: No eye contact, delayed/absent speech, failure to respond to name by 12 months, no shared attention, repetitive movements (stimming — hand flapping, rocking), rigid routines (extreme distress with change), sensory sensitivities (hyper- or hypo-sensitivity to touch/sound/light).

Nursing Care & Teaching
  • Consistent, predictable routines (reduces anxiety)
  • Provide advance warning before transitions ("In 5 minutes we will…")
  • Use visual aids, simple direct language, visual schedules
  • Create calm, low-stimulation environments when possible
  • Behavioral therapy (ABA), speech therapy, OT for sensory
  • No single medication for core symptoms; treat comorbidities (anxiety, ADHD, seizures)

ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder)

Subtypes: Predominantly Inattentive, Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive, Combined (most common).

Signs appear before age 12, present in ≥2 settings (home AND school). Inattention: easily distracted, forgetful, loses items, doesn't finish tasks. Hyperactivity: fidgets, can't sit still, talks excessively. Impulsivity: blurts out, interrupts, can't wait turn.

Treatment
  • Behavioral therapy (first-line in young children <6 years)
  • Stimulant medications: methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta), amphetamine salts (Adderall)
  • Non-stimulant: atomoxetine, guanfacine
  • School accommodations (504/IEP)
Medication Teaching
  • Give AFTER meals (reduces appetite suppression)
  • Monitor height and weight regularly (growth suppression risk)
  • Monitor sleep (stimulants can cause insomnia)
  • Monitor blood pressure and heart rate
  • Drug holidays (weekends/summers) may be used to promote growth

Anxiety Disorders & Depression

Anxiety Disorders

GAD, social anxiety disorder, separation anxiety, specific phobias. Very common in children. Often somatic (stomach aches, headaches) → missed school.

Signs: excessive worry, school refusal, sleep disturbance, irritability, somatic complaints, social withdrawal. Treatment: cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) is first-line; SSRIs (fluoxetine) if moderate-severe.

Depression

In children, may present as irritability rather than sadness. Signs: low mood, loss of interest, poor concentration, sleep/appetite changes, fatigue, social withdrawal, declining school performance.

Treatment: therapy (CBT), SSRIs (fluoxetine is FDA-approved in children ≥8 years).

🚨
Suicide Risk Assessment

Always ask directly about suicidal ideation — it does NOT plant the idea. Red flags: giving away prized possessions, saying goodbye, talking about death or being a burden, researching methods. Initiate safety plan; involve family; do not leave alone. Monitor for increased suicidal ideation when starting SSRIs (FDA black-box warning in adolescents).
