Pediatrics — GI Surgery

Omphalocele

Congenital abdominal wall defect — immediate nursing priorities

Definition

A congenital defect where the intestines, liver, and other abdominal organs protrude outside the body through the umbilical ring, covered by a thin, transparent sac (composed of peritoneum and amnion).

Caused by the failure of intestines to return to the abdomen during embryonic development by week 10.

Associated Conditions

  • Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome)
  • Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome)
  • Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome
  • Cardiac defects common

Immediate Nursing Care (Pre-Surgical)

  1. Protect the sac: Cover with sterile saline-soaked gauze + plastic wrap to prevent drying/rupture
  2. Prevent hypothermia: Place under radiant warmer or incubator immediately
  3. IV fluids: Organs outside body = high fluid/heat loss
  4. NPO status: Nothing by mouth before surgical repair
  5. Insert NG tube: Decompress stomach and prevent aspiration
  6. Positioning: Supine with knees slightly flexed; do not apply pressure on the sac
  7. Monitor for infection: Fever, tachycardia, foul odor, redness around sac
  8. Vital signs / SpO₂: Continuous monitoring
🚨
Never let the sac dry out or rupture

A ruptured sac = contamination risk + massive fluid/heat loss. Keep moist and covered until OR.

Surgical Repair

Primary Closure

Used for small defects. Organs returned and abdominal wall closed in one surgery.

Staged Repair / Silo Technique

Used for large defects. A plastic pouch (silo) is placed around protruding organs. Organs gradually reduced back into abdomen over days to weeks. Final closure when all organs returned.

NCLEX Key Points

Priority = keep sac moist + intact, prevent hypothermia, ensure IV access, NPO, NG tube. Differentiate from gastroschisis (no sac, right side). Monitor for genetic anomalies. Post-op: watch for respiratory compromise (abdominal pressure when organs returned).
