Pediatrics — Eye Disorders

Amblyopia ("Lazy Eye")

Vision development disorder — leading cause of vision impairment in children

Definition

Amblyopia, commonly known as "lazy eye," is a vision development disorder in which one eye fails to achieve normal visual acuity, even with prescription glasses or contact lenses. It is the leading cause of vision impairment in children.

Pathophysiology

  • During early childhood, the brain learns to process visual information from both eyes
  • If one eye provides a blurred or misaligned image, the brain starts to ignore that eye and favors the stronger one
  • Over time, the ignored eye fails to develop proper vision → permanent vision loss if untreated
  • Most effective treatment before age 6–8, when visual pathways are still developing

Causes of Amblyopia

TypeCauseExplanation
StrabismicStrabismus (misalignment of eyes)Brain ignores image from misaligned eye
RefractiveUnequal refractive error (e.g., high farsightedness in one eye)Brain favors the eye with clearer vision
DeprivationBlocked vision (e.g., congenital cataract, ptosis)Brain doesn't get clear input from that eye

Signs & Symptoms

Children may not complain — watch for these signs!

  • Poor vision in one eye (discovered on screening)
  • Eyes that do not appear to work together
  • Squinting, closing one eye, or tilting head to see
  • Strabismus (crossed eyes or wandering eye)
  • Difficulty with depth perception
  • Frequent eye rubbing or blinking
  • Poor performance in school (if vision is uncorrected)

Diagnosis

  • Visual acuity test — measures clarity in each eye
  • Cover-uncover test — detects eye misalignment
  • Retinoscopy or autorefractor — checks refractive errors
  • Exam by pediatric ophthalmologist
  • Screening starts at age 3 or earlier if risk factors present

Treatment — NCLEX Focused

Goal: Force the brain to use the weaker eye and improve its vision

TreatmentHow It Works
Patching (occlusion therapy)Covers the stronger eye for several hours a day so the weaker eye works harder
Atropine drops in stronger eyeTemporarily blurs vision in strong eye to stimulate weaker one
Corrective lensesGlasses or contacts to correct refractive error
Treating underlying causeE.g., removal of cataract or correcting ptosis (drooping eyelid)
Vision therapyEye exercises to improve coordination and focus (optional adjunct)

Treatment usually continues for months to years, depending on severity and age.

NCLEX Tips & Teaching

  • Early detection is critical — vision loss can become permanent
  • Screen vision in preschool-aged children
  • Adherence to patching or drops is essential for success
  • Reward systems may help children tolerate patching
  • Avoid calling it "lazy eye" in a way that shames the child
  • Treat the underlying cause if due to cataracts, ptosis, etc.
  • Monitor for psychological effects (social impact of eye patching)

Complications If Untreated

🚨
Permanent Vision Loss

Poor depth perception • Visual disability later in life • Increased risk of injury due to impaired binocular vision

NCLEX Practice Q&A

Q: A 5-year-old is diagnosed with amblyopia. What should discharge instructions include?
A: "Patching the stronger eye helps stimulate the weaker eye to develop."

Q: A parent says "the drop blurs the stronger eye." Does this indicate correct understanding?
A: Yes — atropine blurs the strong eye so the brain uses the weaker eye.
