Newborn Head-to-Toe Assessment — Simulation Script
Introduction
"Hi, my name is ________ and I'll be your student nurse today. I'm going to be performing a newborn head-to-toe assessment."
"Before I start, I've washed my hands and I have my equipment ready — stethoscope, thermometer, measuring tape, and gloves."
"I've reviewed the chart: mom is ______ weeks gestation, delivered via ______. Apgar scores were ______ at one minute and ______ at five minutes. No resuscitation was required."
"I'm going to verify identity using two identifiers before I begin. I'm checking the armband… name is ________, date of birth is ________. That matches."
"Throughout this assessment I'll be keeping baby warm and limiting exposure as much as possible."
1 — General Observation
"Okay, I'm going to start the assessment now."
"Before I touch anything, I'm just going to take a moment and observe."
"Baby is in a flexed position — that's normal, that's what I want to see. I'm watching for spontaneous movement… I'm seeing symmetric movement of all four extremities."
"I want to get my respiratory rate now, before the baby starts crying, because that'll artificially raise it. Looking at the chest and abdomen… respirations look unlabored. No grunting, no flaring, no retractions."
"Skin color looks pink overall. No central cyanosis, no pallor, no significant jaundice at this time."
2 — Vital Signs
"Alright, let me get the vital signs while baby is still calm."
"I'm counting respirations for a full minute by watching abdominal movement… the rate is ______ breaths per minute. Normal range is 30 to 60, so that's within range."
"I'm going to warm my stethoscope before I put it on the skin — I don't want to startle baby with a cold diaphragm. Listening at the apex for a full minute… heart rate is ______, regular rhythm."
"For temperature, I'm going axillary — that's the safest route for a newborn. Tucking the thermometer in snugly and holding the arm down… temperature is ______°C. Normal range is 36.5 to 37.5 — we're good."
3 — Measurements
"Good. Now I'll move on to measurements."
"Getting the weight now. I'm making sure the scale is zeroed, removing the diaper… placing baby gently on the scale. Weight is ______ grams. I'll document that."
"For length, I'm going to gently extend one leg while keeping the other as a reference. Measuring crown to heel… ______ centimeters."
"Head circumference — I'm going right above the eyebrows and ears, around the widest part of the occiput… ______ centimeters. All three measurements are appropriate for gestational age."
"I'm re-covering baby right away — thermoregulation is a priority in the newborn."
4 — Skin
"Now I'll do a thorough skin assessment."
"Moving to skin. Color looks pink and well-perfused. Skin feels warm to touch — no cool or clammy areas."
"I can see some vernix in the skin folds and some lanugo on the shoulders — both expected, especially at this gestational age. I'm noting and documenting those."
"Checking for jaundice — I'm lightly blanching the forehead… no yellow tinge. Same over the chest… looks clear. No generalized petechiae, no open lesions, no signs of infection."
5 — Head & Scalp
"Moving up to the head and scalp."
"Palpating the skull now. I can feel some molding — that's expected after a vaginal delivery and it resolves on its own over the first few days."
"Checking the fontanelles — anterior fontanelle is soft and flat, not bulging, not sunken. The posterior fontanelle is also soft, flat, and smaller, which is normal."
"I'm running my fingers along the sutures… no significant overriding. No boggy, diffuse swelling suggesting caput, no firm localized swelling suggesting a cephalohematoma."
6 — Face
"Now the face."
"Looking at facial features — everything looks symmetric at rest. I'll watch during a cry to confirm both sides of the face move equally… yes, symmetric movement. No drooping, no dysmorphic features noted."
7 — Eyes
"Moving to the eyes."
"Eyes are symmetrically placed. Sclerae are clear — no redness, no discharge, no purulent drainage."
"I'm going to check the red reflex using the ophthalmoscope… I'm seeing a red reflex bilaterally and it looks equal. That's reassuring — an absent red reflex can indicate a cataract or retinoblastoma."
8 — Ears
"Now the ears."
"Ears are symmetrically positioned — the top of each ear aligns with the outer canthus of the eye. I'm folding the cartilage… it recoils back right away, which tells me gestational maturity is appropriate. No pits, no tags, no drainage."
9 — Nose
"And the nose."
"Nose is midline. I'm checking patency by gently occluding one nostril at a time… both are patent. No flaring, no unusual secretions. Remember, newborns are obligate nose breathers, so choanal atresia would present as immediate respiratory distress."
10 — Mouth
"Moving to the mouth — putting my gloves on now."
"Lips are pink and moist. Opening the mouth gently… tongue is midline, no tongue tie. I'm running my finger along the hard palate — intact. Now the soft palate… also intact. No cleft."
"Testing the suck reflex — placing my gloved finger in the mouth… baby has a strong, well-coordinated suck. That's a great sign for feeding."
"Removing and disposing of my gloves."
11 — Neck & Clavicles
"Gloves are off. Now neck and clavicles."
"Baby is moving the head side to side — good range of motion, no torticollis. No masses, no webbing palpable along the neck."
"I'm carefully palpating both clavicles from medial to lateral… both feel smooth and intact. No crepitus, no step-off, no swelling. I'm also watching arm movement — both arms are moving symmetrically, so I have no concern for a birth fracture."
12 — Chest & Lungs
"Moving down to the chest and lungs."
"Chest looks symmetric. Expansion is equal bilaterally. Still no retractions — I'm checking subcostal, intercostal, and supraclavicular areas."
"Auscultating lung fields — I'll go side to side so I'm comparing. Anterior first… then I'll lift baby slightly for the posterior fields. Breath sounds are clear and equal bilaterally. No crackles, no wheeze."
"I do notice a little breast engorgement here — that's completely normal in both male and female newborns from maternal estrogen exposure. I'm not going to touch it — we never squeeze or massage a newborn's breast tissue."
13 — Cardiovascular
"Now the cardiovascular assessment."
"Listening to heart sounds at all landmarks… S1 and S2 are clear. Rate is regular. I'm listening carefully in each area for murmurs… nothing abnormal at this time. I'll document no murmur auscultated."
"Checking pulses now. Brachial pulses bilaterally… equal and strong. Femoral pulses bilaterally… present and equal. I always compare brachial to femoral — if the femorals were diminished compared to the brachials, that's a red flag for coarctation of the aorta."
"Capillary refill over the sternum… less than two seconds. Good peripheral perfusion."
14 — Abdomen
"Moving to the abdomen."
"The abdomen looks soft and rounded — that's normal in a newborn. Before I palpate, I'm going to auscultate all four quadrants… I'm hearing bowel sounds throughout."
"Now light palpation… abdomen is soft, nondistended, no visible masses, no guarding. I can feel the liver edge just under the right costal margin — up to 2 centimeters below is normal in a newborn, so I'm not concerned."
15 — Umbilical Cord
"Now I'll check the cord."
"Cord clamp is secure. I'm going to count vessels — I can see two arteries and one vein. Three vessels is normal. A two-vessel cord would need to be flagged because it can be associated with renal anomalies."
"Checking the base of the cord… no bleeding, no redness, no swelling, no odor. The cord is clean and dry. I'll make sure the diaper stays folded below it to keep it dry and promote healing."
16 — Genitalia
"Moving on to the genitalia."
Female: "The labia look mildly enlarged — that's from maternal estrogen exposure and it resolves on its own. Both the urethral and vaginal openings are visible. I'm also noticing a small amount of white mucousy discharge — that's called pseudomenstruation, it's expected, and I would document it as a normal finding. No lesions, no redness."
Male: "The urethral meatus is at the tip of the penis — that rules out hypospadias. Both testes are palpable in the scrotum. The scrotum may look a little enlarged from fluid or maternal hormones, which is normal. No significant swelling, no bruising, no discoloration."
17 — Anus & Elimination
"And now the anus and elimination status."
"The anal opening is present, patent, and in a normal position. I'm checking the chart — has baby passed meconium? Has baby voided? I'll document the output. If we're approaching 24 hours with no meconium, that needs to be reported to the provider because it can indicate a GI obstruction."
18 — Extremities
"Alright, now the extremities."
"Looking at all four extremities. Five fingers on each hand, five toes on each foot — no polydactyly, no syndactyly. Nails are present. All extremities are moving spontaneously and symmetrically."
"Testing the palmar grasp — placing my finger in baby's palm… baby grips right away. Grasp is strong and equal bilaterally."
"I'm also checking the feet — no clubfoot deformity. Feet look neutral. I'll note if there are any positional findings."
19 — Hips
"Now I'll check the hips."
"Legs are equal in length. Thigh folds and gluteal folds look symmetric — asymmetric folds can be a sign of developmental dysplasia of the hip."
"The Barlow and Ortolani maneuvers are performed by the provider or a trained clinician per facility policy. I would be present to assist and would document who performed the assessment and what the result was."
20 — Back & Spine
"I'm carefully turning baby prone now, supporting the head and neck the whole time… spine is straight and midline. I'm running my fingers down the vertebrae — no gaps, no protrusions."
"Skin over the spine is intact. No open lesions, no abnormal hair tufts, no deep sacral dimples that would need probing. Gluteal crease is midline and symmetric."
"Returning baby to supine now, still supporting the head. Always support the head."
21 — Neurologic & Reflexes
"Moving into reflexes. Baby is in a flexed resting posture with symmetric spontaneous movement — that's a good neurologic baseline."
Rooting: "I'm gently stroking the right cheek… baby is turning toward the stimulus and opening the mouth. Rooting reflex is intact."
Sucking: "Already assessed during the oral exam — strong, well-coordinated suck confirmed."
Moro: "Supporting the head and shoulders… I'm going to create a slight, controlled drop… baby symmetrically extended and abducted both arms, hands opened wide, then flexed back in. That's a normal, symmetric Moro. Asymmetry here would make me think about a brachial plexus injury or clavicle fracture."
Babinski: "Stroking the lateral sole from heel toward the toes… I'm seeing the great toe dorsiflexing and the other toes fanning out. That's the expected Babinski response in a newborn — it's actually abnormal in an adult, but normal here."
Stepping: "Holding baby upright with full support, letting the soles contact the surface… I can see alternating stepping movements. Stepping reflex present."
22 — Feeding Assessment
"Last area — the feeding assessment."
"Baby is showing feeding cues — rooting, hands moving toward the mouth. I've already confirmed a strong suck."
"If I'm observing a breastfeeding attempt, I'm watching the latch — I want areola in the mouth, not just the nipple. I'm listening for swallowing, watching for any coughing, choking, color changes, or signs of fatigue during the feed."
"If bottle feeding, I'm using paced feeding — holding baby semi-upright, tilting the bottle just enough, following baby's cues. No propping the bottle."
23 — Completion
"That completes my newborn head-to-toe assessment."
"My overall impression: this is a healthy-appearing newborn with pink color, stable vital signs, symmetric movement, appropriate muscle tone, and intact reflexes. No acute concerns identified at this time."
"I'm placing baby safely in supine position in the bassinet. Checking that the ID band and security band are both intact. Environment looks safe."
"Performing hand hygiene. I'll document all findings in the medical record. No abnormal findings to report to the RN at this time — but if I had found anything concerning, I would be reporting that now and staying with the infant until the RN assessed."
"Thank you."
Pediatrics — Newborn

Newborn Head-to-Toe Assessment

Systematic examination of the neonate — birth through 28 days — covering all body systems, reflexes, screenings, and NCLEX priorities

1 Overview & Purpose

A newborn head-to-toe assessment evaluates the newborn's transition to extrauterine life, identifies normal variations, detects congenital abnormalities, and recognizes signs of respiratory, cardiovascular, neurologic, metabolic, or infectious complications.

Assessment Components
  • Review of maternal and birth history
  • General appearance, vital signs, and growth measurements
  • Skin, head, and face
  • Eyes, ears, nose, and mouth
  • Neck, clavicles, chest, lungs, and heart
  • Abdomen and umbilical cord
  • Genitalia and anus
  • Extremities, hips, and spine
  • Neurologic status and newborn reflexes
  • Feeding, elimination, and newborn screening
💡
Observe Before Touching

Assess the newborn before disturbing or touching — crying and activity can alter respiratory rate, heart rate, skin color, muscle tone, and neurologic responses.

2 Preparation Before the Assessment

Review Maternal and Birth History

Prenatal Factors
  • Gestational age
  • Maternal diabetes or hypertension
  • Maternal infections, medications, substance exposure
  • Group B Streptococcus status
  • Blood type and Rh factor
  • Complications during pregnancy
Intrapartum Factors
  • Type and duration of labor
  • Vaginal or cesarean birth; forceps or vacuum
  • Rupture of membranes timing
  • Color and character of amniotic fluid; meconium
  • Apgar scores; need for resuscitation
  • Maternal temperature during labor

Safe Environment

  • Perform hand hygiene
  • Use a prewarmed examination surface
  • Keep the newborn covered except for the area being examined
  • Use warm hands and equipment
  • Avoid drafts or air-conditioning vents
  • Never leave the newborn unattended on an examination surface

Recommended Assessment Sequence

Assess least disturbing findings first:

  1. General appearance, respirations, and color
  2. Posture and spontaneous movement
  3. Heart and lung sounds
  4. Abdomen
  5. Head-to-toe physical examination
  6. Reflexes and other stimulating procedures (perform last)

4 Vital Signs

ParameterExpected RangeNotes
Temperature (axillary)36.5–37.5°C (97.7–99.5°F)Axillary preferred — noninvasive, no rectal injury risk
Respiratory Rate30–60 breaths/minCount for full minute; periodic breathing may occur
Heart Rate (apical)110–160 beats/minCount apical for full minute; may rise with crying, fall in deep sleep
Blood PressureNot routinely obtained unless indicatedObtain all 4 extremities if cardiac abnormality suspected

Temperature Abnormalities

Hypothermia Causes
  • Cold environment, wet skin, delayed drying
  • Prematurity, hypoglycemia, sepsis, poor perfusion

Signs of cold stress: Acrocyanosis (bluish hands and feet), tachypnea (rapid breathing), grunting, lethargy, poor feeding, hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), metabolic acidosis

Hyperthermia Causes
  • Environmental overheating, dehydration
  • Infection, maternal fever, excessive bundling

Temperature instability may be an early sign of neonatal sepsis — especially with poor feeding, lethargy, or respiratory changes.

⚠️
NCLEX — Count Respirations Before Touching

Count respirations before stimulating the newborn. Crying may falsely elevate the respiratory rate. Observe abdominal movement — newborns primarily use the diaphragm.

Periodic Breathing vs. Apnea

Periodic Breathing (Expected)

Brief pauses followed by several more rapid breaths. No color change, no bradycardia, no loss of muscle tone.

Apnea (Abnormal)

Cessation of breathing for ~20 seconds, OR a shorter pause associated with bradycardia, cyanosis, pallor, or decreased oxygen saturation.

Birth Weight Classifications

  • Low birth weight <2,500 g
  • Very low birth weight <1,500 g
  • Extremely low birth weight <1,000 g
  • Macrosomia Generally >4,000–4,500 g
Expected Weight Loss

A loss of approximately 5–7% is common after birth due to fluid loss.

Loss approaching or exceeding 10% requires evaluation of feeding, hydration, milk transfer, and elimination.

6 Gestational Age Assessment

Estimated using maternal dating, prenatal ultrasound, and the New Ballard Score (physical + neuromuscular maturity; ±2 weeks).

Preterm Findings
  • Thin, transparent skin; more visible veins
  • Abundant lanugo (fine, soft hair covering the body)
  • Few plantar creases
  • Soft ear cartilage (slow recoil)
  • Decreased flexion and tone
  • Reduced breast tissue; immature genitalia
Post-Term Findings
  • Dry, cracked, peeling skin
  • Long fingernails
  • Reduced vernix (waxy protective coating on the skin) and subcutaneous fat
  • Meconium staining (greenish discoloration from the first bowel movement)
  • Alert appearance

7 Skin Assessment

Inspect the entire skin surface including skin folds, back, buttocks, scalp, and diaper area. Assess color, temperature, moisture, texture, perfusion, birthmarks, bruising, petechiae (tiny nonblanching red or purple spots), rashes, and jaundice.

Expected Skin Findings

FindingDescriptionClinical Notes
Vernix caseosaThick, white, creamy substance protecting fetal skin from amniotic fluidRemains in skin folds; more in preterm newborns
LanugoFine soft hair on shoulders, back, forehead, faceMore prominent in preterm; disappears naturally

MottlingLacy/blotchy skin — unstable peripheral circulationMay occur when cold; persistent mottling with poor perfusion is concerning

Findings Requiring Further Assessment

Central Cyanosis

Blue/gray of lips, tongue, and mucous membranes. Always abnormal — may indicate inadequate oxygenation.

Petechiae

Pinpoint nonblanching spots. Localized on presenting part = birth pressure. Generalized = thrombocytopenia, infection, or coagulation abnormality.

Pallor

May indicate anemia, blood loss, shock, poor perfusion, or hypoxia.

Jaundice

Yellow discoloration from bilirubin. Within first 24 hours = pathological — requires prompt evaluation. Blanch skin over forehead/nose/chest to see underlying color.

8 Head Assessment

Fontanelles

Anterior Fontanelle

Diamond shaped; where frontal and parietal bones meet. Larger than posterior fontanelle. Expected: soft and flat.

Bulging (while calm) = ↑ICP, meningitis, intracranial bleeding

Sunken = dehydration

Posterior Fontanelle

Triangular; where parietal and occipital bones meet. Smaller than anterior fontanelle. Expected: soft and flat.

May briefly bulge during crying — this can be expected.

Scalp Swelling — Critical Comparison

FindingMechanismSuture LinesTimingResolutionRisk
Caput succedaneum Diffuse scalp edema (pressure against cervix) Crosses sutures Present at birth Hours to a few days Minimal
Cephalohematoma Bleeding between skull bone and periosteum (the membrane covering the skull bone) Does NOT cross sutures May appear hours–days after birth Weeks to months Increased jaundice risk; do not aspirate or massage
Subgaleal hemorrhage 🚨 Bleeding beneath scalp aponeurosis (fibrous tissue layer just beneath the scalp) Crosses sutures May enlarge rapidly Emergency Shock, pallor, tachycardia, falling hematocrit — more common after vacuum birth
🚨
Subgaleal Hemorrhage = Medical Emergency

Diffuse boggy scalp swelling crossing sutures, increasing head circumference, pallor, tachycardia, hypotension, or signs of shock — activate emergency response immediately.

9 Eyes Assessment

Red Reflex

Expected

Reddish-orange reflection through the pupil when light is directed into the eye. Present and symmetric in both eyes.

Unexpected

Absent, white, dull, or asymmetric red reflex may indicate cataract, retinal abnormality, or other serious eye disorder — requires evaluation.

  • Small subconjunctival hemorrhage may be present after birth trauma — usually resolves without treatment
  • Eyelid edema common after birth
  • Purulent drainage, marked redness, or persistent discharge requires evaluation (infection or nasolacrimal obstruction)
💉
NCLEX Safety — Eye Prophylaxis

Erythromycin 0.5% ophthalmic ointment is given to prevent ophthalmia neonatorum (serious bacterial eye infection in newborns). Do not wipe the medication away immediately after administration.

10 Ears Assessment

  • Top of pinna (outer visible part of the ear) should align with or rise above an imaginary line from inner to outer canthus (corner) of the eye
  • Low-set or unusually shaped ears may be associated with congenital or genetic conditions, especially combined with other abnormalities
  • Term newborn: firm ear cartilage with prompt recoil when folded
  • Preterm: softer cartilage, slower recoil, flatter ear shape
  • Document preauricular tags or pits; assess for other abnormalities

11 Nose Assessment

Newborns are primarily nose breathers — nasal obstruction may interfere with breathing and feeding.

Expected
  • Both nostrils patent
  • No persistent nasal flaring
  • Small amount of clear mucus may be present
Unexpected
  • Persistent nasal flaring
  • Inability to pass air through one or both nostrils
  • Cyanosis relieved by crying (suggests bilateral obstruction)
🚨
Choanal Atresia

Congenital blockage of posterior nasal passage. Bilateral obstruction causes severe respiratory distress. Cyanosis relieved by crying is a hallmark finding — crying forces oral breathing.

12 Mouth & Throat Assessment

Use a gloved finger and adequate light to inspect lips, tongue, gums, hard palate, soft palate, uvula, oral mucosa, frenulum, and sucking ability.

13 Neck & Clavicle Assessment

Newborn neck appears short due to skin folds and flexed posture. Newborn should turn head to both sides.

Clavicle Fracture

Most common birth injury. Suspect with:

  • Decreased movement of the affected arm
  • Asymmetric Moro reflex
  • Crepitus (crackling or grating sensation under the skin), swelling, or pain with movement

Torticollis

Abnormal tightening of a neck muscle causing head tilt to one side. May be associated with birth trauma or intrauterine positioning.

14 Chest & Respiratory Assessment

Signs of Respiratory Distress

Retractions

Inward movement of soft tissues during inspiration (subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal, supraclavicular). Indicates increased work of breathing.

Grunting

Short expiratory sound — newborn partially closes glottis (the opening between the vocal cords) to maintain lung pressure. Persistent grunting is abnormal.

Nasal Flaring

Widening of nostrils during breathing. Persistent flaring = increased respiratory effort.

🚨
NCLEX Priority — Hold Feeding With Tachypnea

A respiratory rate above 60/min increases aspiration risk. Assess respiratory status before feeding and hold oral feeding when significant tachypnea or respiratory distress is present.

Breast Enlargement

Temporary breast enlargement may occur in male and female newborns due to maternal hormones. A small amount of milky fluid may be present. Do not squeeze or massage — may cause inflammation or infection.

15 Cardiovascular Assessment

Key Assessment Points

  • Count apical pulse for a full minute
  • Palpate brachial and femoral pulses — weak or absent femoral pulses may indicate aortic obstruction
  • Assess capillary refill over sternum or forehead — expected <3 seconds
Signs Suggesting Cardiac Disease
  • Central cyanosis
  • Weak femoral pulses
  • Persistent tachypnea
  • Tachycardia or bradycardia
  • Poor feeding
  • Sweating or fatigue during feeding
  • Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
  • Poor oxygen saturation
  • Failed critical congenital heart disease screen
  • Abnormal pulse oximetry

16 Abdomen Assessment

Sequence: Inspection → Auscultation → Palpation (auscultate before palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds)

  • Expected: rounded/slightly protuberant, soft, symmetric, bowel sounds present, liver edge may be slightly palpable below right costal margin
  • Marked or progressive distention may indicate obstruction, ileus (temporary absence of normal bowel movement), infection, organ enlargement, mass, or perforation
🚨
Scaphoid Abdomen with Respiratory Distress

Unusually sunken abdomen + respiratory distress may suggest diaphragmatic hernia (abdominal organs in chest cavity). Do not use bag-mask ventilation if strongly suspected — air entering GI tract worsens lung compression.

17 Umbilical Cord Assessment

Normal Cord — 3 Vessels

Two umbilical arteries + one umbilical vein. Initially bluish-white, moist, gelatinous → becomes brown, dry, shriveled → black before separating.

Two-Vessel Cord (Single Artery)

One artery and one vein. May be associated with congenital abnormalities — document and report.

Omphalitis (Cord Infection)

Report immediately:

  • Spreading redness or warmth around the stump
  • Purulent drainage or foul odor
  • Fever or temperature instability
  • Lethargy or poor feeding
Cord Care
  • Keep clean and dry; fold diaper below cord
  • Do not pull the cord off; do not cover with tight clothing
  • Observe for bleeding after the clamp is removed

18 Genitourinary Assessment

Female — Expected Findings
  • Enlarged labia and swollen vulva (from maternal hormones)
  • White mucus discharge
  • Small blood-tinged discharge (pseudomenstruation — normal, self-limited)
  • Term: labia majora generally cover labia minora
Male — Expected Findings
  • Both testes descended into scrotum (term)
  • Scrotum has rugae (natural folds or ridges of scrotal skin)
  • Urethral opening at tip of penis

Urethral Abnormalities

Hypospadias

Urethral opening on the underside of the penis.

Epispadias

Urethral opening on the upper surface of the penis.

🚨
NCLEX Priority — Circumcision Contraindication

Do NOT circumcise a newborn with hypospadias or epispadias. The foreskin tissue is preserved and used as a graft for surgical repair (urethroplasty), typically performed between 6–18 months of age — before toilet training begins.

Other Male Genitalia Findings

  • Hydrocele: Fluid surrounding a testicle; scrotum may transilluminate. Many resolve spontaneously.
  • Cryptorchidism: One or both testes undescended. Document location and notify provider.

19 Anal & Rectal Assessment

  • Anus visibly open, normally positioned
  • Most newborns pass meconium within the first 24 hours
🚨
Imperforate Anus

No visible anal opening, failure to pass meconium, abdominal distention, vomiting, or stool from vagina/urethra. Do not insert thermometer, suppository, or any object when anal patency is uncertain.

Hip Assessment — Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

Barlow Maneuver

Evaluates whether a hip can be dislocated from the socket. Gently adducts (moves the leg toward the body's midline) the hip and applies posterior pressure.

Ortolani Maneuver

Evaluates whether a dislocated hip can be reduced into the socket. Abducts (moves the leg away from the midline) the hip while lifting the femoral head (rounded top of the thigh bone) forward.

A palpable clunk is abnormal.

⚠️
Hip Maneuver Safety

Performed only by trained clinicians. Repeated or forceful manipulation can injure the joint. Skin-fold asymmetry alone is not diagnostic — correlate with complete examination.

Back & Spine

Expected
  • Spine straight, vertebral column intact
  • No open lesions
  • Symmetric gluteal crease
  • A small shallow sacral dimple with visible base may be benign
Concerning Sacral Dimple Features
  • Deep dimple with base not visible
  • Location high above the anus
  • Hair tuft, skin tag, discoloration, or drainage
  • Deviated gluteal crease
  • Neurologic abnormalities

Spinal Defects

Nursing Priorities — Open Spinal Defect (Myelomeningocele)
  • Place newborn prone
  • Cover lesion with sterile, moist, nonadherent dressing (sterile normal saline)
  • Prevent pressure on the sac; prevent contamination with urine or stool
  • Maintain body temperature
  • Use latex precautions — increased risk for latex sensitivity
  • Notify neonatal and surgical team immediately
  • Do not apply dry gauze directly to exposed neural tissue

21 Neurologic Assessment & Newborn Reflexes

Interpret according to gestational age and the newborn's current behavioral state.

Behavioral States

Deep sleep → Light sleep → Drowsy → Quiet alert → Active alert → Crying

The quiet-alert state is best for feeding and parent interaction.

Asymmetric Moro Reflex — Causes
  • Clavicle fracture
  • Brachial plexus injury
  • Hemiparesis (weakness or paralysis on one side of the body)
  • Neurologic injury

Jitteriness vs. Seizures

Jitteriness
  • Stimulus-sensitive
  • Stops when extremity is gently held
  • No abnormal eye movement

Causes: hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), hypocalcemia (low blood calcium), drug withdrawal, neurologic irritation

Seizure Activity
  • May continue when limb is held
  • Repetitive eye deviation or lip smacking
  • Chewing, cycling, or paddling movements
  • Apnea or sudden autonomic changes

Requires immediate evaluation.

22 Feeding & Elimination Assessment

Feeding Readiness Cues

  • Early cues: Hand-to-mouth movement, rooting, sucking movements, increased alertness, lip smacking
  • Crying is a late hunger cue

Unexpected Feeding Findings

Hold Oral Feeding — Report to Provider
  • Weak or absent suck; poor latch
  • Repeated coughing, choking, or cyanosis
  • Nasal regurgitation or vomiting
  • Excessive fatigue or sweating during feeding
  • Tachypnea or abdominal distention after feeding

Poor feeding may indicate infection, hypoglycemia, neurologic impairment, respiratory disease, cardiac disease, or structural abnormalities.

23 Newborn Screening & Preventive Care

ScreenMethodTimingKey Points
Metabolic (heel stick) Dried blood spot After 24–48 h of feeding (false negative if done too early) PKU, congenital hypothyroidism, galactosemia, and others. Verify feeding status; warm heel; avoid layering blood drops.
Hearing screening OAE or automated ABR Before discharge Failed initial screen ≠ permanent hearing loss — requires repeat and follow-up.
Critical CHD screen Pulse oximetry At 24 h (or close to discharge) Right hand (preductal) + one foot (postductal). Saturation <90% in either = failed screen → immediate evaluation.
Bilirubin assessment Transcutaneous or serum When jaundice suspected or routinely Interpret by age in hours, gestational age, risk factors, and rate of increase. Visual inspection alone is insufficient.

Routine Medications at Birth

Vitamin K

IM injection into vastus lateralis. Prevents vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB). Newborns have limited stores and immature intestinal production.

Eye Prophylaxis

Erythromycin 0.5% ophthalmic ointment — prevents ophthalmia neonatorum from organisms acquired during birth.

Hepatitis B Vaccine

Per parental consent and maternal HBsAg status. Time-sensitive prophylaxis required when mother is HBsAg-positive.

Safe Sleep Teaching

AAP Safe Sleep Guidelines
  • Place newborn supine for every sleep
  • Use a firm, flat sleep surface
  • Keep pillows, blankets, bumpers, stuffed toys, and loose objects out of sleep area
  • Avoid overheating and smoke exposure
  • Use a separate approved infant sleep surface

Car Seat Safety

  • Rear-facing car seat, correctly installed
  • Harness positioned correctly; chest clip at armpit level
  • No bulky clothing under the harness

Bulb Syringe Teaching

  • Compress bulb before insertion
  • Suction mouth before nose
  • Insert only the tip; release pressure slowly
  • Clean device after use; avoid excessive or deep suctioning

27 Documentation

Document objective findings clearly and specifically. Avoid vague terms like "newborn normal."

Example of Specific Documentation

"Newborn awake and quiet, flexed posture, symmetric spontaneous movement, axillary temperature 36.8°C, apical pulse 136/min, respirations 44/min and unlabored, breath sounds clear and equal bilaterally, skin warm and pink with acrocyanosis of feet, anterior fontanelle soft and flat, abdomen soft with active bowel sounds, three-vessel cord dry and clamped, voided once, meconium passed, strong coordinated suck."

28 NCLEX-RN Priority Points

Top 20 NCLEX Points
  1. Observe before touching the newborn.
  2. Count respirations and apical pulse for a full minute.
  3. Airway and breathing take priority over routine measurements.
  4. Central cyanosis is always abnormal.
  5. Acrocyanosis (blue hands/feet only) may be temporarily expected.
  6. Persistent grunting, flaring, and retractions indicate respiratory distress.
  7. A soft, flat fontanelle is expected; bulging while calm = ↑ICP.
  8. Caput crosses sutures; cephalohematoma does not.
  9. Diffuse boggy scalp swelling with signs of shock = subgaleal hemorrhage — emergency.
  10. A normal cord contains two arteries and one vein.
  11. Hypospadias or epispadias = contraindication to circumcision.
  12. Confirm anal patency and passage of meconium within 24 hours.
  13. Assess symmetric movement and reflexes; asymmetric Moro = fracture or nerve injury.
  14. A positive hip clunk requires evaluation (DDH).
  15. Poor feeding may be an early sign of serious illness.
  16. A cold newborn is at risk for hypoglycemia and respiratory distress.
  17. Jaundice within first 24 hours is pathological — requires prompt evaluation.
  18. Bilious (green) vomiting is an emergency finding.
  19. Complete newborn screening and confirm follow-up for abnormal results.
  20. Never delegate initial comprehensive assessment, interpretation, teaching, or care of an unstable newborn.
🌡️
Temperature + Glucose Are Closely Related

A cold newborn uses more glucose and oxygen to produce heat. For hypothermia: rewarm safely → assess blood glucose → evaluate for respiratory distress → consider infection.

💤
Newborn Deterioration May Be Subtle

Early signs: poor feeding, temperature instability, lethargy, irritability, weak cry, tachypnea, apnea, color change, glucose instability, decreased urine output. A newborn may not show the obvious signs seen in older patients.
