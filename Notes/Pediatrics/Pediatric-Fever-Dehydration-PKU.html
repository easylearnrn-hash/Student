Pediatric Fever, Dehydration & PKU

Management of fever, dehydration assessment, and phenylketonuria nursing care

Fever

Fever = temperature ≥ 100.4°F (38°C). Usually a normal immune response to infection.

Measuring Temperature by Age

  • <3 months → rectal (most accurate)
  • 3 months – 3 years → rectal or axillary or temporal
  • >3 years → oral or axillary

When to Report Immediately

  • Any fever in infant <3 months (possible serious bacterial infection)
  • Fever lasting >3 days in any child
  • Child is lethargic, vomiting, inconsolable, or not drinking
  • Seizure with fever (febrile seizure)
  • Stiff neck, petechiae, difficulty breathing

Treatment

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — 10–15 mg/kg q4–6h
  • Ibuprofen (Motrin) — ≥6 months, 5–10 mg/kg q6–8h
  • NEVER aspirin in children (Reye's syndrome risk)
  • Encourage fluids, light clothing
  • No cold/alcohol sponge baths

Febrile seizures: Occur in ages 6 months – 5 years due to rapid temperature rise. Usually brief and harmless. Side-lying position, do not restrain, time the seizure.

Dehydration

Common causes: vomiting, diarrhea, fever, poor intake, DKA. Children are at higher risk due to higher body water percentage and faster metabolic rate.

SignMildModerate–Severe
MouthDry mucous membranesExtremely dry, cracked lips
Eyes/FontanelSlightly dry eyesSunken eyes, sunken fontanel
TearsDecreasedNo tears when crying
Wet diapersFewer than normalGreatly decreased or absent
Skin turgorNormalTented (poor turgor, pinched skin stays up)
Mental statusAlert, slightly thirstyIrritable, lethargic, or inconsolable
Vital signsNormalTachycardia, low BP, delayed cap refill

Treatment

  • Mild: Oral rehydration solution (ORS) — Pedialyte, Enfalyte; small frequent amounts
  • Avoid sugary drinks, sports drinks, soda, juice (high sugar worsens diarrhea)
  • Moderate–Severe: IV fluid replacement (Normal saline bolus if shock)
  • Monitor I&Os, weight, urine output, electrolytes
⚠️
NCLEX Tip — Dehydration

Sunken fontanel + decreased wet diapers + no tears = moderate dehydration. Give ORS first if child is alert and not vomiting. IV fluids for severe cases or vomiting. Monitor K⁺ with diarrhea.

Phenylketonuria (PKU)

Autosomal recessive genetic disorder → missing or deficient phenylalanine hydroxylase enzyme → phenylalanine (Phe) accumulates → brain damage if untreated.

Newborn Screening

Heel stick blood test done after 24–48 hours of feeding (must have ingested protein to detect elevated Phe). If done too early = false negative.

Signs If Untreated

  • Intellectual disability (severe)
  • Seizures
  • Musty/mousy body odor (distinctive)
  • Lighter skin and hair than family members (Phe needed for melanin production)
  • Behavioral and attention problems
Foods to AVOID (High Phenylalanine)
  • Meat, poultry, fish
  • Dairy products (milk, cheese, eggs)
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Aspartame (artificial sweetener) — contains phenylalanine (this is why diet sodas have "contains phenylalanine" warning)
Treatment
  • Lifelong low-phenylalanine diet
  • Special PKU formula (Phe-free amino acid formula) — provides protein without Phe
  • Allowed: most fruits and vegetables, some grains (low-protein)
  • Some patients may benefit from sapropterin (Kuvan) — increases enzyme activity
  • Regular blood Phe monitoring
📝
NCLEX Summary

Baby <3 months + fever = emergency always. No aspirin in children (Reye's). Dehydration = ↓ wet diapers, sunken fontanel, no tears → give Pedialyte first. PKU = no high-protein foods, musty odor, heel stick after 24h of feeding, lifelong diet. Febrile seizures = usually benign but monitor.
