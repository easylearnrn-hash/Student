Pediatrics — Hematology & Oncology

Pediatric Hematological & Oncological Disorders

Anemia, sickle cell, hemophilia, leukemia, Wilms tumor, neuroblastoma, Hodgkin lymphoma

Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Most common nutritional deficiency in children. Low hemoglobin from insufficient iron.

Causes: Low dietary intake (too much cow's milk in toddlers replaces iron-rich food), premature birth (depleted iron stores), blood loss.

Signs: Pallor, fatigue, tachycardia, irritability, poor concentration, spoon-shaped nails (koilonychia in severe cases), pica (craving non-food items).

Treatment
  • Ferrous sulfate supplementation between meals
  • Give with vitamin C (orange juice) to enhance absorption
  • Avoid giving with milk or antacids (reduce absorption)
  • Stool may turn dark/tarry — expected; reassure family
  • Increase iron-rich foods: red meat, legumes, fortified cereals, leafy greens

Sickle Cell Disease

Autosomal recessive. HbS causes RBCs to become rigid, crescent-shaped (sickle) when deoxygenated → occlude blood vessels → ischemia and pain.

Triggers: Dehydration, cold temperatures, high altitude, stress, infection, low O₂.

Vaso-Occlusive (Pain) Crisis — Nursing Priorities
  1. Hydration — IV fluids + encourage oral fluids (prevents sickling)
  2. Pain management — opioids, NSAIDs, heating pad (NO cold)
  3. O₂ if SpO₂ <95%
  4. Rest — decrease O₂ demand
  5. Prevent infection — vaccines, prophylactic penicillin until age 5

Signs: Severe pain crises (bones, abdomen, chest), dactylitis (swollen hands/feet in infants), chronic anemia, jaundice, splenic infarction, acute chest syndrome (emergency = chest pain + respiratory distress).

Hemophilia

X-linked recessive bleeding disorder. Males affected; females carry. Deficiency of clotting factor VIII (Hemophilia A, most common) or factor IX (Hemophilia B).

Signs: Easy bruising, prolonged bleeding after cuts, hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints — especially knees, elbows, ankles), deep muscle hematomas.

Nursing Care
  • Administer clotting factor concentrates as prescribed
  • RICE for joint bleeds (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation)
  • Avoid aspirin/NSAIDs (increase bleeding risk)
  • Use soft toothbrush; avoid contact sports
  • Teach family to administer factor at home
  • Medical alert bracelet

Leukemia (ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia)

Most common childhood cancer. Bone marrow overproduces immature WBCs (blasts) → crowds out RBCs, platelets, and normal WBCs.

Signs: Fever, fatigue/pallor (anemia), petechiae/bruising (thrombocytopenia), bone/joint pain, lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly.

Nursing Priorities
  • Infection risk (neutropenia) → reverse isolation, hand hygiene, avoid live vaccines, monitor for fever
  • Bleeding risk (thrombocytopenia) → soft toothbrush, no rectal temps, report petechiae
  • Anemia → rest, monitor Hgb, transfuse as ordered
  • Central line care — sterile technique
  • Mucositis from chemotherapy → oral hygiene, soft foods

Wilms Tumor (Nephroblastoma)

Most common kidney tumor in children (peak 3–5 years).

🚨
DO NOT PALPATE the Abdomen!

Palpation can rupture the tumor and spread cancer cells. Post-op nephrectomy + chemotherapy ± radiation. Signs: abdominal mass noticed by parent during bathing, painless, hypertension, hematuria.

Neuroblastoma

Tumor from adrenal medulla or sympathetic nerve tissue. Most common solid tumor in infants. Often found in abdomen (adrenal glands). Frequently diagnosed late (silent until large). Signs: abdominal mass, weight loss, bone pain, periorbital ecchymosis ("raccoon eyes").

Hodgkin Lymphoma

Lymph node malignancy. Common in teens and young adults. Signs: painless, rubbery lymph node swelling (typically cervical), night sweats, fever, weight loss (B symptoms). Diagnosis: biopsy showing Reed-Sternberg cells. Treatment: chemotherapy ± radiation (high cure rate).

NCLEX Summary

Sickle cell crisis = hydration + pain + O₂ + rest. Hemophilia = protect joints, give clotting factors, no ASA/NSAIDs. Leukemia = infection/anemia/bleeding triad; sterile central line.
