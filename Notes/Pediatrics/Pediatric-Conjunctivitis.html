Pediatrics — Eye Disorders

Pediatric Conjunctivitis ("Pink Eye")

Inflammation of the conjunctiva — viral, bacterial, allergic, or chemical

Definition

Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva — the clear membrane that lines the inner eyelids and covers the white part of the eye (sclera). It causes the eye to appear red, irritated, and may produce discharge. It's very common in children and can be viral, bacterial, allergic, or irritant-induced.

Causes of Conjunctivitis in Children

TypeCauseContagious?
ViralUsually adenovirus; often follows a cold or respiratory illness✅ Highly
BacterialStaph, Strep, Haemophilus influenzae, Chlamydia (in newborns)✅ Highly
AllergicPollen, dust, pet dander, seasonal allergies❌ No
Chemical/IrritantChlorine in pools, smoke, shampoo❌ No

Signs & Symptoms by Type

TypeDischargeOther Features
ViralWatery or mucousRed, gritty; preauricular lymph node swelling; often follows cold symptoms
BacterialThick yellow or green pusCrusty eyelids especially in morning; one or both eyes
AllergicClear, stringy mucusItching, both eyes, often with sneezing or runny nose
Chemical/IrritantWateryIrritation, burning, recent exposure to irritant

Treatment Based on Type

TypeTreatment
ViralSupportive care: cool compresses, artificial tears; no antibiotics
BacterialAntibiotic eye drops or ointment (e.g., erythromycin, polymyxin B/trimethoprim)
AllergicAntihistamine or mast cell stabilizer drops (e.g., olopatadine) + remove allergen
Chemical/IrritantIrrigation with saline, avoid exposure

Neonatal Conjunctivitis (Ophthalmia Neonatorum)

🚨
Emergency in Newborns

Occurs in first 30 days of life. Neisseria gonorrhoeae (2–5 days after birth) can cause blindness. Chlamydia trachomatis appears 5–14 days after birth.

Prevented with prophylactic erythromycin ointment at birth. Requires urgent evaluation and systemic antibiotics.

Nursing Interventions & Teaching

  • Hand hygiene is the most important prevention!
  • Clean eye gently with warm water and cotton ball (wipe once per swipe)
  • Do not share towels, pillows, or eye drops
  • Keep child home until discharge has resolved (viral/bacterial types)
  • Use separate towels and washcloths for infected child
  • Dispose of eye makeup or contact lenses used during infection
  • Teach proper eye drop/ointment administration: apply to conjunctival sac, not directly on eye
  • If using both drops and ointment: give drops first, wait 5–10 minutes

When to Refer or Reevaluate

  • No improvement after 2–3 days of treatment
  • Severe pain, photophobia (light sensitivity), vision changes
  • Recurrent infections
  • Suspected herpes simplex virus — needs antiviral immediately
NCLEX Final Tips
  • Contagious? Yes — viral and bacterial types
  • Handwashing = key to prevention
  • Don't give antibiotics for viral pink eye
  • Treat neonatal eye discharge seriously — refer immediately
  • Crusty discharge = bacterial, itchy = allergic, watery + URI = viral
  • Return to school: after 24 hrs on antibiotics (if bacterial), or when discharge resolves
NCLEX Practice Q&A

Q: A child has thick yellow discharge and crusted eyelids in morning. Best action?
A: Recommend the child see a provider for antibiotics — signs of bacterial conjunctivitis

Q: A newborn on day 3 has bilateral eye swelling and thick discharge. Priority action?
A: Notify the provider immediately — may be gonococcal conjunctivitis (Neisseria gonorrhoeae; presents 2–5 days after birth, can cause blindness — emergency)
