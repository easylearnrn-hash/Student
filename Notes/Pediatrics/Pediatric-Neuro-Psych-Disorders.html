Pediatrics — Neurologic & Psychosocial

Pediatric Neurologic, Cognitive & Psychosocial Disorders

Seizures, hydrocephalus, meningitis, CP, Down syndrome, ASD, ADHD, anxiety, depression

Seizures

TypeDescriptionKey Notes
Tonic-ClonicBody stiffens (tonic), then rhythmic jerking (clonic)Most recognizable seizure type
AbsenceBrief blank stare; child pauses and resumes activityCommon in school-age children; may be misunderstood as daydreaming
FebrileTriggered by rapid fever rise; ages 6 months – 5 yearsUsually harmless, brief. Evaluate for fever source. Scary for parents but rarely cause brain damage.

Nursing During Seizure

  • Stay with child; call for help
  • Position: side-lying to prevent aspiration
  • Do NOT restrain or put anything in mouth
  • Time the seizure; document length, movements, post-ictal state
  • Loosen clothing; clear area of hazards
  • Check airway, breathing, and LOC after seizure

Emergency meds: Lorazepam (Ativan) or diazepam (Valium) IV/rectal. Maintenance meds: Phenytoin (Dilantin), levetiracetam (Keppra), valproic acid.

Hydrocephalus

Excess CSF accumulates in the brain ventricles → increased intracranial pressure (ICP).

Infant Signs of Increased ICP
  • Bulging/tense fontanel
  • High-pitched, shrill cry
  • Rapidly increasing head circumference
  • Sunset sign (eyes deviated downward)
  • Vomiting, irritability, lethargy
Treatment: VP Shunt

Ventriculoperitoneal shunt: drains CSF from brain ventricle to peritoneal cavity (abdomen).

Watch for shunt complications: fever, vomiting, headache, decreased LOC → shunt infection or blockage.

Meningitis

Inflammation of meninges (bacterial = serious; viral = usually self-limiting).

Signs: Fever, severe headache, nuchal rigidity (stiff neck), photophobia, vomiting, altered LOC, petechiae/purpura (bacterial meningococcal = emergency).

  • Kernig sign: Pain/resistance when extending knee while hip is flexed (positive = meningeal irritation)
  • Brudzinski sign: Flexing neck causes involuntary knee flexion (positive = meningeal irritation)

Nursing care: Droplet precautions, IV antibiotics, quiet/dim room, monitor neuro status and ICP signs, maintain head of bed slightly elevated.

Cerebral Palsy (CP)

Group of non-progressive motor disorders from brain damage/abnormal development before, during, or after birth.

Types
  • Spastic (most common) — increased muscle tone, stiff movements
  • Ataxic — poor balance and coordination
  • Dyskinetic — involuntary, uncontrolled movements
Nursing Care
  • PT/OT/speech therapy referrals
  • Baclofen (muscle relaxant), botulinum toxin
  • Assistive devices for mobility/communication
  • Safe positioning and feeding techniques (aspiration risk)

Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

Extra chromosome 21. Most common chromosomal disorder.

Features: Low muscle tone (hypotonia), flat facial profile, upward slanting eyes, single palmar crease, short stature, intellectual disability (variable severity).

Watch for: Congenital heart defects (AV canal most common), hearing and vision problems, hypothyroidism, atlantoaxial instability (caution with neck activities), leukemia risk.

Nursing focus: Promote early intervention therapies, monitor growth/development, support family, screen for complications.

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Neurodevelopmental disorder affecting social communication, behavior, and sensory processing. Signs typically appear before age 3.

Signs: No eye contact, delayed/absent speech, repetitive behaviors (hand flapping, rocking), rigid routines, sensory sensitivities (over- or under-reactive to stimuli).

Nursing care:

  • Maintain consistent routines and structured environment
  • Use visual schedules and simple language
  • Prepare child in advance for changes
  • Reduce sensory stimulation in the environment
  • Early referral to speech/behavioral therapy (ABA)

ADHD

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder. Types: Inattentive, Hyperactive-Impulsive, or Combined.

Treatment: Behavioral therapy + stimulant medications (methylphenidate/Ritalin, amphetamine/Adderall).

💡
NCLEX Tip — ADHD Medications

Give medications early in the day (avoid insomnia). Monitor appetite, weight, and growth. Watch for cardiovascular effects. No stopping abruptly. SSRIs are adjuncts; stimulants are first-line.

Anxiety & Depression in Children

Anxiety: Generalized anxiety, social anxiety, school refusal, phobias. Signs: stomach aches, headaches, clinginess, excessive worry. Treatment: CBT + SSRIs (fluoxetine).

Depression: May present as irritability, withdrawal, poor school performance, sleep and appetite changes.

🔴
Suicide Risk Assessment

Always ask directly about suicidal thoughts. Red flags: giving away belongings, talking about death, saying goodbye. SSRIs (especially when starting) → monitor closely for increased suicidal ideation in adolescents (black box warning). Ensure safe environment.

📝
NCLEX Summary

Hydrocephalus = big head + bulging fontanel = VP shunt. Seizure = side-lying, no restraints, don't put anything in mouth. Meningitis = stiff neck + photophobia + fever + droplet isolation. Down syndrome = screen for heart defects and atlantoaxial instability. ASD = structure, routine, therapy. ADHD meds = give early in day, monitor weight and growth. CP = not progressive; focus on therapy and safe swallowing.
