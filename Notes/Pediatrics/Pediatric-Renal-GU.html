Pediatrics — Renal & GU

Pediatric Renal, Urinary & GU Disorders

UTI, glomerulonephritis, nephrotic syndrome, hypospadias, cryptorchidism, enuresis

UTI (Urinary Tract Infection)

Most common: E. coli. More common in girls (shorter urethra). Infants and young children may not show classic urinary symptoms.

Infant Signs
  • Fever (without obvious source)
  • Irritability
  • Poor feeding
  • Vomiting
  • Foul-smelling urine
Older Child Signs
  • Dysuria (burning with urination)
  • Urinary frequency/urgency
  • Foul-smelling or cloudy urine
  • Suprapubic or flank pain

Diagnosis: Urinalysis + urine culture. Treatment: Antibiotics (oral or IV). Encourage fluids.

Prevention teaching: Wipe front to back (girls), cotton underwear, avoid bubble baths, adequate fluid intake, complete voiding, regular toilet schedules.

💡
NCLEX Tip

Fever without a source in an infant → suspect UTI. Collect urine by catheter or suprapubic aspiration (not bag specimen) for accurate culture in infants.

Acute Glomerulonephritis (AGN)

Post-streptococcal kidney inflammation (follows strep throat or impetigo by 1–3 weeks). Antigen-antibody complexes deposit in glomeruli → inflammation.

Classic Signs — "Nephritic"
  • Hematuria — tea-colored, cola-colored, or rusty urine
  • Edema — especially periorbital (around eyes) on waking
  • Hypertension (↑ renin → ↑ blood pressure)
  • Decreased urine output (oliguria)
  • Mild proteinuria (less than nephrotic syndrome)

Treatment: Usually self-resolving. Monitor BP (antihypertensives if needed), fluid restriction, low sodium diet, daily weight, I&Os, bedrest during acute phase.

Nephrotic Syndrome

Kidney damage → massive loss of protein in urine. Most common in children: Minimal Change Disease (MCD), responds well to steroids.

Classic Signs — "Nephrotic"
  • Massive proteinuria (frothy urine — protein loss >3.5 g/day)
  • Severe generalized edema (periorbital, ascites, scrotal/labial, anasarca)
  • Hypoalbuminemia (low protein → low oncotic pressure → fluid leaks)
  • Normal to low blood pressure (opposite of AGN)
  • Hyperlipidemia (liver compensates by making more lipoproteins)

Treatment: Corticosteroids (prednisone), diuretics, albumin IV + fluid restriction for severe cases, prevent infection (immunosuppressed), daily weight, I&Os, skin care for edematous areas.

Hypospadias & Epispadias

Hypospadias

Urethral opening on underside (ventral surface) of the penis. Most common. Treated with surgery at 6–12 months.

Epispadias

Urethral opening on top (dorsal surface) of the penis. Less common. Requires surgical repair.

🚨
Do NOT circumcise before surgical repair

The foreskin may be needed for the reconstructive surgery. Circumcision would eliminate this tissue. Educate parents at birth.

Cryptorchidism (Undescended Testicle)

One or both testes fail to descend into the scrotum before birth.

  • Common in premature infants; may self-correct by 6 months
  • Risks if untreated: infertility, testicular cancer, psychological concerns
  • Treatment: orchiopexy (surgical fixation) — recommended by age 1 year

Enuresis (Bedwetting)

Involuntary urination after the age of expected bladder control (age 5 for nighttime).

  • Primary enuresis: child has never been consistently dry
  • Secondary enuresis: child was dry >6 months then started wetting → evaluate for stress, UTI, DM

First-line treatment: Limit fluids 2 hours before bedtime, void before sleep, positive reinforcement, motivational charts, bedwetting alarms.

Pharmacological (if needed): Desmopressin (DDAVP) or imipramine. Avoid punishment; reassure family it is very common.

📝
NCLEX Summary

AGN = post-strep, cola urine, HIGH BP → monitor BP/weight. Nephrotic = massive protein loss, NORMAL BP, severe edema → steroids. UTI in infants = fever + poor feeding. Hypospadias = do NOT circumcise. Cryptorchidism → orchiopexy by 1 year. Enuresis normal until age 5 → lifestyle first.
