Emergency, Shock & Surgical Medications

Shock types, preoperative medications, and postoperative complication management

1. Shock Types & Medications

Hypovolemic Shock

Caused by bleeding, burns, dehydration

  • Treatment: IV Fluids (NS, LR), Blood Products (Packed RBCs)
  • Risks: fluid overload, allergic reaction to blood

Cardiogenic Shock

Heart fails to pump — often post-MI

  • Dobutamine: ↑ heart contractility
  • Dopamine: ↑ BP & cardiac output
  • Diuretics: if fluid overload present
  • Risks: arrhythmias, low BP

Distributive Shock — 3 Types

  1. Septic Shock (infection → low BP)
    • IV Antibiotics (first priority!)
    • IV Fluids (30 mL/kg bolus)
    • Vasopressors: Norepinephrine, Dopamine
  2. Anaphylactic Shock (severe allergy)
    • Epinephrine IM — FIRST LINE
    • Diphenhydramine (antihistamine)
    • Corticosteroids
    • IV Fluids, Oxygen
    • Monitor: airway, allergic response
  3. Neurogenic Shock (spinal cord injury)
    • Atropine (for bradycardia)
    • Vasopressors (Dopamine)
    • Monitor: HR, temperature regulation

Obstructive Shock

Block to blood flow (PE, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax)

  • Needle decompression (tension pneumothorax)
  • Pericardiocentesis (tamponade)
  • Anticoagulants or thrombolytics (PE)
  • Emergency — monitor oxygenation closely

3. Postoperative Complications & Medications

Bleeding / Hypotension

Signs: Low BP, fast HR

  • IV Fluids
  • Blood transfusion

Pain / Hypertension

  • Opioids (Morphine, Hydromorphone) — monitor for respiratory depression
  • NSAIDs (Ketorolac) — risk of bleeding and kidney injury

Urinary Retention

No urine output after surgery:

  • Bladder scan first
  • Straight catheterization if needed

Respiratory Issues (Atelectasis, Pneumonia)

  • Incentive spirometer
  • Early ambulation
  • Oxygen therapy if needed
