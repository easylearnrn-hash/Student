Section 32 HOW TO INTERPRET LABORATORY VALUES ON THE NCLEX

Do not memorize laboratory values alone. Always connect the value to the client's full clinical picture.

Consider: symptoms, diagnosis, current medications, recent procedures, fluid status, cardiac rhythm, neurological status, bleeding risk, and respiratory status.

A laboratory result becomes more urgent when it causes:

  • Airway compromise
  • Respiratory failure
  • Cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Seizures
  • Severe bleeding
  • Altered level of consciousness
  • Shock
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Severe weakness or paralysis

Reference ranges may vary slightly by laboratory. On the NCLEX, focus on the overall pattern and clinical danger.

White Blood Cell Count

White blood cell count (WBC; the number of infection-fighting cells in the blood).

Elevated WBC may indicate: infection, inflammation, stress, trauma, corticosteroid use, or leukemia.

Low WBC may indicate: bone marrow suppression, chemotherapy effect, severe infection, immunosuppression, or certain medications.

Neutropenia

Neutropenia (a reduced number of neutrophils, which are white blood cells that fight bacterial infection) increases infection risk. The most important value is often the absolute neutrophil count (ANC; the estimated number of neutrophils available to fight infection).

ANCInterpretation
Above 1,500/mm³Usually adequate
1,000–1,500/mm³Mild neutropenia
500–1,000/mm³Moderate neutropenia
Below 500/mm³Severe infection risk
EMERGENCY: Fever with Severe Neutropenia

A fever in a severely neutropenic client is a medical emergency. Notify the provider immediately.

Neutropenic Precautions

  • Perform strict hand hygiene.
  • Avoid sick visitors.
  • Avoid fresh flowers and standing water when required by policy.
  • Avoid raw or undercooked food.
  • Monitor temperature closely.
  • Avoid rectal temperatures, suppositories, and enemas.
  • Use aseptic technique for all procedures.
  • Report fever immediately.
  • Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.
  • Monitor for subtle infection — inflammation may be reduced.

Hemoglobin

Hemoglobin (the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells). Low hemoglobin may cause: fatigue, weakness, pallor, tachycardia, shortness of breath, reduced activity tolerance, and chest pain in severe cases. Priority depends on symptoms, bleeding, and the degree of reduction.

Hematocrit

Hematocrit (the percentage of blood volume made up of red blood cells).

Low hematocrit may indicate anemia, bleeding, or fluid overload. High hematocrit may indicate dehydration, chronic hypoxia, or polycythemia.

Platelets

Platelets (blood components that help form clots). Thrombocytopenia (a low platelet count) increases bleeding risk.

Platelet CountClinical Significance

Below 50,000/mm³Significant bleeding risk
Below 20,000/mm³Risk of spontaneous bleeding
Below 10,000/mm³Severe risk of spontaneous or intracranial bleeding

Thrombocytopenia Precautions

  • Avoid intramuscular injections.
  • Avoid rectal temperatures, suppositories, and enemas.
  • Use a soft toothbrush and electric razor.
  • Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs unless prescribed.
  • Apply prolonged pressure after venipuncture.
  • Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.
  • Monitor urine, stool, skin, gums, and neurological status.
  • Prevent falls and injury. Avoid forceful nose blowing or straining.
Notify the provider for:
  • New headache or confusion
  • Decreased level of consciousness
  • Blood in urine or stool
  • Uncontrolled bleeding
  • Petechiae with worsening platelet count

Prothrombin time (PT; a test that measures the time required for blood to clot through part of the coagulation pathway) is often used with warfarin therapy.

International normalized ratio (INR; a standardized measurement of blood-clotting time) is commonly monitored in clients receiving warfarin. The exact target depends on the diagnosis and prescription.

High INR — Increased Bleeding Risk. Assess for:
  • Bruising, hematuria, melena, gum bleeding
  • Severe headache, weakness, hypotension
Antidote for Warfarin: Vitamin K

Vitamin K (a medication used to reverse warfarin's anticoagulant effect) may be prescribed for high INR or active bleeding.

Activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT; a test used to measure one part of the blood-clotting process) is often used to monitor unfractionated heparin.

Antidote for Heparin: Protamine Sulfate

Protamine sulfate (a medication used to reverse heparin) is the antidote for unfractionated heparin.

Sodium (Na⁺)

Sodium (Na⁺; the main extracellular electrolyte that helps regulate fluid balance and nerve function).

Hyponatremia

Hyponatremia (serum sodium below the normal range) causes water to move into cells. Clinical manifestations: headache, nausea, muscle cramps, confusion, lethargy, seizures, coma.

Severe or rapidly developing hyponatremia is a neurological emergency.

Avoid rapid correction — it may cause osmotic demyelination syndrome.

Nursing Priorities — Hyponatremia

  • Seizure precautions and frequent neurological assessment
  • Fluid restriction when caused by water excess
  • Sodium replacement as prescribed
  • Monitor intake, output, and daily weight

Hypertonic saline (a concentrated sodium solution) may be used for severe symptomatic hyponatremia. Administer carefully with close monitoring.

Hypernatremia

Hypernatremia (serum sodium above the normal range) usually reflects water loss or excess sodium. Clinical manifestations: intense thirst, dry mucous membranes, restlessness, irritability, weakness, confusion, seizures, coma.

Nursing Priorities — Hypernatremia

  • Replace water gradually — avoid rapid correction
  • Monitor neurological status and intake/output
  • Provide oral care and monitor sodium closely

Potassium (K⁺)

Potassium (K⁺; the main intracellular electrolyte required for muscle and cardiac function).

Potassium abnormalities are dangerous — they may cause fatal dysrhythmias.

Hypokalemia

Hypokalemia (serum potassium below the normal range) may result from: diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, nasogastric suction, insulin administration, alkalosis, or poor intake.

Clinical manifestations: muscle weakness, fatigue, leg cramps, reduced bowel sounds, constipation, ileus, weak pulse, dysrhythmias, respiratory muscle weakness.

ECG findings: flattened T waves, ST depression, U waves.

Nursing Priorities — Hypokalemia

  • Cardiac monitoring for severe deficiency
  • Potassium replacement as prescribed
  • Verify adequate urine output before replacement
  • Monitor renal function, bowel sounds, and respiratory status
  • Review diuretic use

Potassium IV Safety — NEVER Give by IV Push

  • Always dilute intravenous potassium.
  • Use an infusion pump.
  • Verify adequate urine output before administration.
  • Monitor the IV site and cardiac rhythm when indicated.
  • Follow the prescribed maximum rate.
Oral potassium: give with food; dilute liquid forms; do not crush extended-release tablets; monitor for GI irritation.

Hyperkalemia

Hyperkalemia (serum potassium above the normal range) may result from: kidney failure, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, tissue destruction, acidosis, excess potassium intake, or adrenal insufficiency.

Clinical manifestations: muscle weakness, paresthesia, abdominal cramping, diarrhea, dysrhythmias, cardiac arrest.

ECG findings: tall peaked T waves, widened QRS, prolonged PR interval, bradycardia, sine-wave pattern in severe cases.

Hyperkalemia Treatment

  • Calcium gluconate — stabilizes the cardiac membrane; does not lower potassium
  • Regular insulin + dextrose — insulin moves K⁺ into cells; dextrose prevents hypoglycemia
  • Sodium bicarbonate — when acidosis is present
  • Nebulized beta-agonist
  • Potassium-binding medication
  • Loop diuretic
  • Dialysis

Calcium (Ca²⁺)

Calcium (Ca²⁺; an electrolyte needed for bone strength, muscle contraction, nerve function, and clotting). Total calcium normal range: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL. Calcium must be interpreted with albumin because much of circulating calcium is protein-bound.

Hypocalcemia

Hypocalcemia (serum calcium below the normal range) may cause: numbness around the mouth, tingling, muscle cramps, tetany, hyperactive reflexes, seizures, laryngospasm, prolonged QT interval.

Classic Clinical Signs of Hypocalcemia:
  • Chvostek sign (facial muscle twitching when the facial nerve is tapped) — suggests neuromuscular irritability
  • Trousseau sign (carpal spasm caused by inflating a blood pressure cuff) — suggests hypocalcemia

Nursing Priorities — Hypocalcemia

  • Seizure precautions
  • Airway monitoring (risk of laryngospasm)
  • Cardiac monitoring (prolonged QT)
  • Calcium replacement as prescribed
  • Fall precautions

Hypercalcemia

Hypercalcemia (serum calcium above the normal range) may cause: weakness, fatigue, constipation, reduced reflexes, kidney stones, bone pain, confusion, dysrhythmias, shortened QT interval.

Classic NCLEX phrase: "Stones, bones, abdominal groans, and psychiatric overtones"

Nursing Priorities — Hypercalcemia

  • Encourage fluids when not contraindicated
  • Promote mobility
  • Monitor cardiac rhythm and kidney function
  • Avoid unnecessary calcium intake
  • Administer prescribed medications

Magnesium (Mg²⁺)

Magnesium (Mg²⁺; an electrolyte needed for neuromuscular stability and cardiac function). Normal range: 1.7–2.2 mg/dL.

Hypomagnesemia

Hypomagnesemia (serum magnesium below the normal range) may cause: tremors, hyperactive reflexes, muscle cramps, seizures, tachycardia, dysrhythmias, and torsades de pointes (a potentially fatal ventricular dysrhythmia associated with a prolonged QT interval).

Nursing Priorities — Hypomagnesemia

  • Cardiac monitoring and seizure precautions
  • Magnesium replacement as prescribed
  • Monitor potassium and calcium — they may also be low

Hypermagnesemia

Hypermagnesemia (serum magnesium above the normal range) may cause: lethargy, reduced reflexes, muscle weakness, hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression, cardiac arrest.

Common causes: kidney failure, excess magnesium-containing antacids or laxatives, magnesium sulfate therapy.

Magnesium Toxicity — Warning Signs

  • Absent deep tendon reflexes
  • Respiratory rate below 12/min
  • Reduced urine output
  • Hypotension and bradycardia
  • Decreased level of consciousness
Antidote for Magnesium Toxicity: Calcium Gluconate

Calcium gluconate is the antidote for magnesium toxicity.

Phosphorus

Phosphorus (an electrolyte needed for energy production, bone health, and cellular function). Normal range: 2.5–4.5 mg/dL. Calcium and phosphorus often move in opposite directions.

Hypophosphatemia may cause muscle weakness, respiratory weakness, confusion, bone pain, and reduced cardiac function. Common causes: malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, refeeding syndrome, hyperparathyroidism.

Hyperphosphatemia commonly occurs with kidney failure and may cause signs of hypocalcemia. Phosphate binders are usually taken with meals.

Chloride

Chloride (Cl⁻; an extracellular electrolyte that helps regulate fluid and acid-base balance). Normal range: 98–106 mEq/L. Chloride often follows sodium and may change with acid-base imbalance.

Blood urea nitrogen (BUN; a measurement of urea waste in the blood). Elevated BUN may occur with dehydration, kidney dysfunction, GI bleeding, high-protein intake, or increased tissue breakdown.

Creatinine (a waste product produced by muscle metabolism and filtered by the kidneys). Elevated creatinine suggests reduced kidney filtration and is generally more specific for kidney function than BUN.

Section 37 LIVER FUNCTION VALUES

Aspartate aminotransferase (AST; an enzyme found in the liver and other tissues) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT; an enzyme found mainly in the liver) may rise with liver injury. ALT is generally more liver-specific.

Bilirubin (a yellow pigment produced from red blood cell breakdown and processed by the liver). Elevated bilirubin may cause: jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, pruritus.

Albumin (the main blood protein produced by the liver that helps maintain oncotic pressure). Normal range: 3.5–5.0 g/dL. Low albumin may cause: edema, ascites, poor wound healing, medication-binding changes, and malnutrition concerns.

Ammonia (a toxic waste product normally converted by the liver into urea). Elevated ammonia may cause confusion, personality changes, asterixis, reduced consciousness, and hepatic encephalopathy.

Asterixis (a flapping tremor of the hands) may occur with liver failure and elevated ammonia. It is a sign of hepatic encephalopathy.

Troponin (a cardiac protein released when heart muscle is injured) is the most important biomarker for myocardial injury. Interpret the trend and the client's symptoms together.

B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP; a hormone released when the ventricles are stretched by increased pressure or volume) may be elevated in heart failure. Higher values may suggest worsening fluid overload or ventricular strain.

Creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB; an enzyme found in heart muscle) may rise after myocardial injury but is less specific than troponin.

Section 39 GLUCOSE VALUES

Approximate fasting blood glucose: 70–99 mg/dL.

Hypoglycemia

Usually below 70 mg/dL.

Manifestations of Hypoglycemia:
  • Sweating and tremors
  • Hunger and tachycardia
  • Irritability and confusion
  • Headache
  • Seizures and loss of consciousness

Hyperglycemia

Manifestations: polyuria (excessive urination), polydipsia (excessive thirst), polyphagia (excessive hunger), blurred vision, fatigue, dehydration, weight loss.

Hemoglobin A1C

Hemoglobin A1C (a blood test that estimates average blood glucose over approximately 2–3 months).

A1C ValueInterpretation
Below 5.7%Normal
5.7%–6.4%Prediabetes
6.5% or higherConsistent with diabetes (when confirmed)

Treatment goals are individualized.

Section 41 RESPIRATORY ACIDOSIS

Occurs when carbon dioxide is retained. Typical pattern: low pH, high PaCO₂.

Common causes: opioid overdose, sedation, airway obstruction, COPD, severe pneumonia, respiratory muscle weakness, hypoventilation.

Clinical manifestations: headache, confusion, drowsiness, warm flushed skin, reduced level of consciousness.

Treatment focuses on improving ventilation. Address the underlying cause. May require supplemental oxygen, bronchodilators, reversal agents, or mechanical ventilation.

Section 42 RESPIRATORY ALKALOSIS

Occurs when excessive carbon dioxide is exhaled. Typical pattern: high pH, low PaCO₂.

Common causes: anxiety, pain, fever, hypoxemia, pulmonary embolism, mechanical overventilation, early sepsis.

Clinical manifestations: lightheadedness, tingling, numbness, muscle cramps, anxiety, palpitations.

Treatment focuses on correcting the underlying cause.

Section 43 METABOLIC ACIDOSIS

Occurs when acid accumulates or bicarbonate is lost. Typical pattern: low pH, low HCO₃⁻.

Common causes: diabetic ketoacidosis, kidney failure, severe diarrhea, lactic acidosis, shock, sepsis.

Clinical manifestations: Kussmaul respirations, confusion, weakness, dysrhythmias, hypotension.

Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing used to reduce acid by removing carbon dioxide) are commonly seen in diabetic ketoacidosis.

Section 44 METABOLIC ALKALOSIS

Occurs when acid is lost or bicarbonate increases. Typical pattern: high pH, high HCO₃⁻.

Common causes: prolonged vomiting, nasogastric suction, excess antacid use, diuretic therapy, hypokalemia.

Clinical manifestations: weakness, confusion, muscle cramps, paresthesia, dysrhythmias, reduced respiratory rate.

Treatment focuses on correcting fluid and electrolyte losses.

Isotonic Fluids

Isotonic fluids (solutions with a concentration similar to blood plasma) expand the circulating volume without causing major fluid shifts between cells.

Risks: fluid overload, pulmonary edema, worsening heart failure, worsening kidney failure.

Monitor: lung sounds, oxygenation, edema, blood pressure, urine output, and daily weight.

Hypotonic Fluids

Hypotonic fluids (solutions with a lower concentration than blood plasma) move water into cells.

Risks: cerebral edema, hypotension, worsening increased intracranial pressure.

Use cautiously in: head injury, stroke, burns, increased ICP.

Hypertonic Fluids

Hypertonic fluids (solutions with a higher concentration than blood plasma) pull water from cells into the bloodstream.

Risks: pulmonary edema, circulatory overload, rapid sodium correction, vein irritation.

Hypertonic saline requires close neurological and sodium monitoring.

Section 46 INTRAVENOUS COMPLICATIONS

Infiltration

Infiltration (leakage of a nonvesicant IV fluid into surrounding tissue). Findings: cool skin, swelling, pallor, discomfort, slowed infusion, no blood return.

Actions

  • Stop the infusion.
  • Remove the catheter as indicated.
  • Elevate the limb.
  • Apply warm or cold compress according to the solution and policy.
  • Restart IV access at another site. Document findings.

Extravasation

Extravasation (leakage of a vesicant medication into surrounding tissue, causing potential tissue injury). Findings: pain, burning, swelling, blistering, skin discoloration, reduced infusion flow.

Actions — Extravasation

  1. Stop the infusion immediately.
  2. Leave the catheter in place initially if aspiration or antidote administration may be required.
  3. Aspirate residual medication when indicated.
  4. Notify the provider and pharmacy.
  5. Administer the prescribed antidote.
  6. Apply the recommended compress and elevate the limb.
  7. Follow the extravasation protocol. Document and monitor the site.
Do NOT flush the line.

Phlebitis

Phlebitis (inflammation of a vein). Findings: redness, warmth, tenderness, swelling, red streak along the vein, palpable venous cord.

Actions

  • Stop the infusion and remove the catheter.
  • Apply a warm compress. Restart at another site. Monitor for infection.

Local Infection

Findings: redness, drainage, warmth, tenderness, fever.

Actions

  • Remove the catheter and notify the provider.
  • Culture the site or catheter if prescribed.
  • Apply a sterile dressing. Monitor for systemic infection.

Fluid Overload

Findings: crackles, dyspnea, tachycardia, hypertension, jugular venous distention, edema, anxiety, reduced oxygen saturation.

Actions

  • Slow or stop the infusion. Elevate the head of the bed.
  • Administer oxygen. Notify the provider.
  • Prepare to administer a diuretic. Monitor vital signs and oxygenation.

Air Embolism

Air embolism (air entering the vascular system and blocking blood flow). Possible findings: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, hypotension, cyanosis, confusion, sense of impending doom.

Actions — Air Embolism (EMERGENCY)

  • Clamp the catheter immediately.
  • Place the client on the left side in Trendelenburg position when appropriate.
  • Administer oxygen.
  • Notify the emergency response team and provider.
  • Monitor cardiac and respiratory status.

Section 47 CENTRAL VENOUS CATHETER SAFETY

Central venous catheter (a catheter inserted into a large central vein) may be used for long-term IV therapy, parenteral nutrition, chemotherapy, vesicant medications, and hemodynamic monitoring.

Nursing Priorities — CVC

  • Use sterile technique for all access and dressing changes.
  • Scrub the hub before every access.
  • Maintain an occlusive dressing. Assess for infection.
  • Check external catheter length. Avoid unnecessary disconnection.
  • Clamp the line when required. Confirm placement before initial use.
  • Monitor for pneumothorax after insertion.

Possible complications: infection, air embolism, catheter occlusion, thrombosis, pneumothorax, catheter migration.

High-Protein Diet (Section 50)

May be needed for: burns, pressure injuries, major surgery, malnutrition, wound healing, severe infection.

Use cautiously in severe kidney or liver dysfunction, depending on the treatment plan.

Low-Sodium Diet (Section 51)

Commonly used for: heart failure, hypertension, ascites, edema, kidney disease.

Teach the client to avoid: processed meats, canned soups, chips, fast food, frozen meals, pickled foods, soy sauce, salted snacks. Encourage label reading.

High-Potassium Foods (Section 52)

Include: bananas, oranges, potatoes, tomatoes, avocados, spinach, dried fruits, beans, melons.

Clients with hyperkalemia or advanced kidney disease may need to restrict these foods.

Calcium-Rich Foods (Section 53)

Sources: milk, yogurt, cheese, fortified foods, leafy green vegetables, sardines, tofu. Vitamin D supports calcium absorption.

Iron-Rich Foods (Section 54)

Sources: red meat, liver, beans, lentils, dark leafy vegetables, fortified cereals. Vitamin C increases iron absorption.

Oral Iron Teaching Points

  • Stool may become dark — this is expected.
  • Constipation may occur.
  • Take with vitamin C when appropriate to enhance absorption.
  • Avoid taking with antacids at the same time.
  • Liquid iron may stain teeth — use a straw and rinse the mouth.

Section 55 ENTERAL NUTRITION

Enteral nutrition (feeding through the gastrointestinal tract using a tube) is preferred when the GI tract functions.

Nursing Priorities — Enteral Nutrition

  • Verify tube placement according to policy before feeding.
  • Elevate the head of the bed 30–45 degrees.
  • Assess bowel function. Monitor glucose and electrolytes.
  • Flush the tube to prevent clogging. Provide oral care.
  • Monitor for aspiration.

Signs of Aspiration

  • Coughing, choking, wet voice, dyspnea, crackles
  • Reduced oxygen saturation, fever

Stop the feeding and assess the client if aspiration is suspected.

Section 56 PARENTERAL NUTRITION

Parenteral nutrition (intravenous administration of nutrients when the GI tract cannot be used adequately) contains glucose, amino acids, electrolytes, vitamins, and lipids. Total parenteral nutrition is usually administered through a central venous catheter.

Nursing Priorities — Parenteral Nutrition

  • Use sterile technique and an infusion pump.
  • Monitor blood glucose and electrolytes.
  • Monitor liver function, daily weight, and intake/output.
  • Assess for infection. Use a dedicated line.
  • Do not stop abruptly unless required for an emergency.

If parenteral nutrition is suddenly unavailable, dextrose-containing IV fluid may be prescribed to prevent hypoglycemia.

Section 57 REFEEDING SYNDROME

Refeeding syndrome (dangerous electrolyte shifts that occur when nutrition is restarted rapidly after severe malnutrition) may cause:

  • Severe hypophosphatemia (most characteristic finding)
  • Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia
  • Fluid overload, dysrhythmias, respiratory failure, neurological changes
High-risk clients:
  • Prolonged starvation or severe malnutrition
  • Alcohol use disorder, anorexia nervosa
  • Major weight loss

Nutrition should be restarted gradually with close electrolyte monitoring.

Section 58 COMMON DIAGNOSTIC TEST PRECAUTIONS

CT With Contrast

Computed tomography (CT; an imaging test that uses x-rays to create detailed cross-sectional images) may use iodinated contrast.

Before Contrast

  • Assess kidney function.
  • Ask about previous contrast reactions and allergies.
  • Check for pregnancy when relevant. Ensure hydration when appropriate.
  • Review metformin use according to current protocol.

After Contrast

  • Encourage fluids when not contraindicated.
  • Monitor for allergic reaction.
  • Monitor kidney function in high-risk clients.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI; an imaging test that uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves) does not use ionizing radiation.

Before MRI

  • Screen for metal implants; remove all metal objects.
  • Assess for pacemakers, aneurysm clips, cochlear implants, and implanted devices.
  • Assess for claustrophobia. Evaluate kidney function if gadolinium contrast is used.

The client must not enter the MRI area with unsafe metal equipment.

Ultrasound

Ultrasound (an imaging test that uses sound waves) does not use ionizing radiation. Preparation depends on the body area.

  • Pelvic ultrasound may require a full bladder.
  • Abdominal ultrasound may require fasting.

Endoscopy (Upper)

Endoscopy (visual examination of an internal organ using a flexible scope) often requires sedation.

Before

  • Confirm nothing-by-mouth status and verify informed consent.
  • Remove dentures when indicated. Review anticoagulants. Establish IV access.

After Upper Endoscopy

  • Monitor airway. Assess gag reflex before allowing oral intake.
  • Monitor for bleeding and perforation.
Signs of Perforation: severe pain, fever, tachycardia, rigid abdomen, difficulty swallowing, subcutaneous emphysema.

Colonoscopy

Colonoscopy (endoscopic examination of the colon).

Before

Bowel preparation, clear-liquid diet, nothing by mouth as prescribed, medication review, informed consent.

After — Expected

Mild gas, bloating, cramping.

After — Report Immediately

  • Severe abdominal pain and rigid abdomen
  • Fever and tachycardia
  • Heavy rectal bleeding

These may indicate perforation or hemorrhage.

Lumbar Puncture

Lumbar puncture (insertion of a needle into the lower spinal canal to obtain cerebrospinal fluid or measure pressure).

Before

  • Verify consent and assess coagulation status.
  • Position correctly: side-lying with knees flexed, or sitting leaning forward.
  • Explain the need to remain still throughout the procedure.

After

  • Monitor neurological status and puncture site.
  • Monitor for CSF leakage. Encourage fluids if prescribed.

A severe headache that worsens when upright may be a post-lumbar-puncture headache caused by CSF leakage.

Bone Marrow Biopsy

Bone marrow biopsy (removal of a small sample of bone marrow for examination). Common site: posterior iliac crest.

After

  • Apply pressure and monitor for bleeding.
  • Keep the dressing dry. Assess pain. Monitor for infection.

Liver Biopsy

Liver biopsy (removal of a small sample of liver tissue).

Before

  • Assess coagulation studies. Verify consent. Obtain baseline vital signs.
  • Teach the client to hold the breath during needle insertion.

After

  • Position on the right side to tamponade the biopsy site.
  • Monitor for bleeding and vital signs. Assess abdominal and shoulder pain.
  • Monitor for hypotension and tachycardia.

Thoracentesis

Thoracentesis (removal of fluid or air from the pleural space using a needle).

During

Sit upright, lean over a bedside table, remain still, avoid coughing.

After

  • Assess breath sounds and oxygen saturation.
  • Monitor respiratory status and puncture site. Watch for pneumothorax.
Signs of Pneumothorax After Thoracentesis:
  • Sudden dyspnea and chest pain
  • Reduced or absent breath sounds on the affected side
  • Tachycardia and reduced oxygen saturation

Paracentesis

Paracentesis (removal of fluid from the abdominal cavity).

Before

  • Ask the client to void. Obtain weight and abdominal girth.
  • Check coagulation status. Obtain baseline vital signs.

After

  • Monitor blood pressure and for fluid shifts.
  • Monitor for bleeding. Recheck weight and abdominal girth.
  • Assess the puncture site.
  • Albumin may be prescribed after large-volume removal.

Electroencephalogram (EEG)

Electroencephalogram (EEG; a test that records electrical activity in the brain).

Before

  • Wash hair; avoid hair products.
  • Avoid stimulants when prescribed.
  • Do not stop seizure medications unless directed.

The test is painless. No electrical current enters the body.

Electrocardiogram (ECG)

Electrocardiogram (ECG; a recording of the electrical activity of the heart).

  • No electrical current enters the body — the test is painless.
  • The client should remain still during the recording.
  • Remove oils or lotions from electrode sites.

Section 59 RAPID LABORATORY PRIORITY RULES

Memorize these 35 rules. Each one reflects a testable NCLEX principle.

  1. Potassium abnormalities may cause fatal dysrhythmias.
  2. Severe sodium abnormalities may cause seizures and cerebral edema.
  3. Low calcium may cause tetany, seizures, and laryngospasm.
  4. High magnesium may cause absent reflexes and respiratory depression.
  5. Low magnesium may cause torsades de pointes.
  6. Severe thrombocytopenia creates spontaneous bleeding risk.
  7. Fever with severe neutropenia is an emergency.
  8. Elevated troponin indicates myocardial injury until fully evaluated.
  9. Rising creatinine with reduced urine output suggests worsening kidney function.
  10. Elevated ammonia with confusion suggests hepatic encephalopathy.
  11. Never give intravenous potassium by push.
  12. Verify urine output before administering potassium.
  13. Calcium gluconate stabilizes the heart in hyperkalemia but does not remove potassium.
  14. Insulin moves potassium into cells.
  15. Protamine sulfate reverses heparin.
  16. Vitamin K reverses warfarin.
  17. Calcium gluconate reverses magnesium toxicity.
  18. Soap and water are required for Clostridioides difficile hand hygiene.
  19. FFP replaces clotting factors, not platelets.
  20. Cryoprecipitate replaces fibrinogen.
  21. Hypertonic saline requires slow correction and close monitoring.
  22. Isotonic fluid expands vascular volume.
  23. Hypotonic fluid moves water into cells.
  24. Hypertonic fluid pulls water into the bloodstream.
  25. Stop an infusion immediately when infiltration or extravasation is suspected.
  26. Do not flush an extravasated vesicant.
  27. Sudden dyspnea after central-line manipulation may indicate air embolism.
  28. Never stop parenteral nutrition abruptly unless required for safety.
  29. Refeeding syndrome commonly causes severe hypophosphatemia.
  30. After upper endoscopy, confirm gag reflex before giving oral intake.
  31. After liver biopsy, position the client on the right side.
  32. After thoracentesis, sudden respiratory distress may indicate pneumothorax.
  33. After colonoscopy, severe abdominal pain and rigidity may indicate perforation.
  34. When an abnormal laboratory value is given, assess the client — not only the number.
  35. The most dangerous laboratory value is the one causing an immediate cardiac, neurological, respiratory, or bleeding complication.
