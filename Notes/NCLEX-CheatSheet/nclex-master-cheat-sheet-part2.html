Section 2 THE NCLEX PRIORITY FRAMEWORK

Use the following hierarchy when deciding what to do first.

Priority Level 1 — Immediate Threats to Life

  • Airway obstruction; severe breathing difficulty; absent breathing; severe hypoxemia
  • Uncontrolled bleeding; shock; cardiac arrest
  • Severe allergic reaction; sudden altered consciousness
  • Seizure lasting longer than expected; signs of increased intracranial pressure
  • Severe medication reaction; suicidal intent with a specific plan

Priority Level 2 — Actual Acute Problems

  • New chest pain; sudden shortness of breath; new confusion; acute neurological changes
  • Severe uncontrolled pain; abnormal vital signs with symptoms
  • Postoperative complications; new bleeding; reduced urine output
  • Acute infection with instability

Priority Level 3 — Potential Problems

Risk for falls, infection, pressure injury, aspiration, or impaired skin integrity.

An actual problem usually takes priority over a risk for problem, unless the risk represents an immediate life-threatening danger.

Priority Level 4 — Long-Term and Psychosocial Needs

Knowledge deficits, chronic coping difficulties, long-term mobility needs, discharge teaching, stable emotional concerns.

Psychosocial needs become the priority when there is no immediate physiological threat or when the psychological problem creates an immediate safety risk.

  • Acute before chronic; unstable before stable
  • Unexpected before expected; actual before potential
  • Systemic before localized; new symptoms before old symptoms
  • Life-threatening before non-life-threatening
  • Safety before comfort
  • Least invasive before more invasive, when clinically appropriate

Section 5 ASSESS BEFORE YOU ACT

Usually Assess First When:

  • The problem is unclear or information is incomplete.
  • The client reports a new symptom but is stable.
  • The nurse must determine whether an intervention is needed.
  • The client's baseline status is unknown.
  • The nurse must verify a finding before notifying the provider.

Examples: assess pain before analgesic; check glucose when hypoglycemia is suspected; assess lung sounds for shortness of breath; verify blood pressure before antihypertensive.

Act First When:

  • The client is in immediate danger or the emergency is obvious.
  • Delaying treatment would cause harm.
  • The intervention stabilizes airway, breathing, circulation, or safety.

Examples: stop a blood transfusion when a reaction is suspected; stop an infusion when extravasation is identified; apply oxygen during severe respiratory distress; place client on side during a seizure; remove source of electrical injury before touching the client; apply direct pressure to active external bleeding; administer emergency glucose for severe confirmed hypoglycemia.

Section 7 CLINICAL JUDGMENT

Step 1
Recognize Cues

Identify important findings from history, assessment, vitals, labs, meds, and nursing notes. Not every detail is equally important.

Step 2
Analyze Cues

Connect related findings. Fever + tachycardia + hypotension + confusion + elevated lactate = severe infection with impaired perfusion.

Step 3
Prioritize Hypotheses

Determine which explanation is most dangerous, best supported by findings, and requires immediate action.

Step 4
Generate Solutions

Select safe interventions that address the priority problem.

Step 5
Take Action

Perform the highest-priority appropriate intervention.

Step 6
Evaluate Outcomes

Determine whether the client improved, worsened, remained unchanged, or requires another intervention.

Section 8 SAFETY RULES

General Safety — Always

  • Identify the client using at least two approved identifiers.
  • Verify allergies before administering medications.
  • Perform hand hygiene before and after client contact.
  • Maintain privacy and confidentiality.
  • Keep the bed in the lowest position when appropriate; lock wheels on beds, wheelchairs, and stretchers.
  • Place the call light within reach; use nonskid footwear for fall-risk clients.
  • Follow facility policy for alarms and restraints. Document objectively and promptly.

Never use a room number as the sole client identifier.

Fall Prevention — High-Risk Client

  • Keep the bed low with wheels locked. Provide nonskid footwear and adequate lighting.
  • Remove clutter; ensure assistive devices are available. Assist with toileting.
  • Place call light within reach. Use alarms per policy. Perform frequent rounding.
  • Do not rely on restraints as a routine fall-prevention measure.

Seizure Precautions

  • Pad side rails when indicated. Keep oxygen and suction equipment available.
  • Maintain IV access if prescribed. Keep the bed low. Remove hazardous objects.
  • During a seizure: turn client onto the side, protect the head, loosen restrictive clothing, time the seizure, observe movements and level of consciousness.
  • Assess after the seizure.

During a Seizure — Never

  • Restrain the client's movements.
  • Insert anything into the mouth or force the mouth open.
  • Offer food, fluid, or oral medication until swallowing is safe.

Aspiration Precautions

  • Elevate the head of the bed. Assess swallowing ability.
  • Keep suction equipment available when indicated. Use prescribed food and liquid consistency.
  • Encourage small bites and slow eating. Place food on the stronger side of the mouth after a stroke.
  • Keep the client upright during and after meals. Verify feeding-tube placement before use.
  • Stop feeding and assess when coughing, choking, wet voice, respiratory distress, or reduced SpO₂ occurs.

Section 9 RESTRAINTS

Restraints are used only when less restrictive interventions have failed and the client presents a risk of harm.

Try These First

  • Reorientation, family presence, sitter or observation, pain relief, toileting
  • Reduced stimulation, bed alarm, moving client closer to nurses' station
  • Covering or protecting tubes; addressing hunger, thirst, or discomfort

Restraint Principles

  • Use the least restrictive restraint. Obtain the required prescription — no standing or PRN restraint orders.
  • Assess circulation, skin, nutrition, hydration, elimination, and continued need regularly.
  • Remove at required intervals. Secure to the bed frame — not the side rail.
  • Use a quick-release knot. Allow space for circulation. Document alternatives attempted.

A Restraint Is NEVER Used For:

  • Staff convenience or punishment
  • Routine fall prevention or as a substitute for observation
  • A client who is merely confused but not dangerous

Section 11 STERILE TECHNIQUE

A Sterile Object Becomes Contaminated When:

  • It touches a nonsterile object or falls below waist level.
  • It is held outside the nurse's view or becomes wet through a nonsterile surface.
  • It is left unattended or the nurse reaches across it improperly.

The outer edge of a sterile field is considered contaminated.

Opening Sterile Supplies

Open flaps in order: away from body → side flap → other side → toward body. Avoid reaching over the sterile field.

Pouring Sterile Solution

  • Check the label and expiration. Remove cap without touching the inside; place cap with inner surface facing up.
  • Pour a small amount away first when required by policy. Prevent splashing. Do not allow the bottle to touch the sterile field.

  • Assessment, teaching, evaluation, triage, nursing judgment, or care planning
  • Medication administration (unless specifically authorized)
  • Care of unstable clients; interpretation of findings
  • Sterile invasive procedures outside their role

Five Rights of Delegation

  • Right task · Right circumstance · Right person · Right direction and communication · Right supervision and evaluation

Ask: Is the client stable? Is the outcome predictable? Is the task routine? Does it require assessment, teaching, judgment, or evaluation? Is the person legally trained and authorized? Can the nurse appropriately supervise?

Section 14 INFORMED CONSENT

Provider's ResponsibilityNurse's Responsibility
Explain the procedure; discuss risks, benefits, and alternatives; answer procedure-specific questions; obtain informed consent Witness the signature; confirm signing is voluntary; verify client appears competent and understands the general purpose; notify provider if client has unanswered questions; use a qualified interpreter when needed. The nurse should not provide the provider's detailed procedural explanation.

Consent Is Invalid When:

  • The client is heavily sedated or impaired by premedication.
  • The client is being forced or coerced.
  • The client lacks decision-making capacity and no authorized representative is involved.
  • The client does not understand the information.
  • A qualified interpreter is required but not used.

Emergency exception: Emergency treatment may proceed without formal consent when immediate treatment is necessary to preserve life and an authorized decision-maker is unavailable, according to law and policy.

Section 15 CLIENT RIGHTS

Clients generally have the right to: receive information, participate in decisions, refuse treatment, receive privacy and confidential care, access interpretation services, receive emergency care, review their records, know who is providing care, receive care without discrimination, complete advance directives, and change their mind about treatment.

A competent adult may refuse treatment even when the decision appears unwise.

The nurse should: assess understanding, explain consequences within the nursing role, notify the provider, document objectively, and continue appropriate care.

Never threaten, coerce, abandon, or secretly treat a competent client who refuses care.

Section 16 ADVANCE DIRECTIVES

Living will: Describes the medical treatments a person does or does not want if unable to communicate.

Healthcare power of attorney: Identifies a person authorized to make healthcare decisions when the client cannot.

Important Rules

  • A competent client's current wishes take priority over any advance directive.
  • Advance directives become relevant when the client cannot communicate or make decisions.
  • Family members do not automatically override a competent client.
  • The nurse should advocate for the documented wishes of the client.

Do-not-resuscitate status does not mean "do not treat." Clients with DNR status still receive appropriate assessment, medications, oxygen, hygiene, pain management, and other prescribed care.

Section 17 CONFIDENTIALITY

Protect client information in: conversations, electronic records, printed documents, photographs, social media, telephone communication, handoffs, and public areas.

Do Not:

  • Discuss clients in elevators or public areas.
  • Access a chart without a work-related reason. Share passwords or leave records visible.
  • Post client information online. Confirm that someone is a client without authorization.
  • Give information to family members without permission, unless legally authorized.

Share only the minimum necessary information with people involved in the client's care.

Section 18 DOCUMENTATION

Documentation Should BeDocumentAvoid
Accurate, objective, timely, complete, legible, factual, chronological What you observed; what the client stated; what you did and when; the client's response; who was notified; new prescriptions received; follow-up actions Blaming language, personal opinions, vague statements, unapproved abbreviations, altered records, charting before care is provided, mentioning an incident report in the chart

Correcting an Error

  • Do not erase, use correction fluid, or destroy the original entry.
  • Mark the error according to facility policy; add the correct information.
  • Date, time, and authenticate the correction.

Late entry: Label it as a late entry; include the current date and time while identifying when the care occurred.

Section 19 INCIDENT REPORTS

Complete an incident report for: medication errors, client falls, equipment injuries, needlestick injuries, treatment errors, client elopement, property loss, unexpected injuries.

Incident Report Rules

  • Is not part of the medical record.
  • Is used for quality improvement and risk management.
  • Should contain objective facts and should not assign blame.
  • Should not be copied into the chart.

In the medical record, document the event, assessments, interventions, notifications, and client response. Do not write that an incident report was completed.

Stay present, provide privacy, acknowledge the emotion, and allow silence. Do not immediately change the topic or tell the client to stop crying.

When the Client Is Angry

Remain calm. Maintain personal space. Listen. Acknowledge the concern. Set respectful limits. Offer realistic options. Do not argue or become defensive.

When a Medication Error Occurs

  • Assess the client immediately. Protect the client from harm.
  • Notify the provider and appropriate nursing leadership.
  • Follow prescriptions for monitoring or treatment.
  • Document the client's condition and care. Complete an incident report.
  • Do not hide the error or alter documentation.

Client safety comes before paperwork.

High-Alert Medications — Use Extra Caution

  • Insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, chemotherapy
  • Concentrated electrolytes, neuromuscular blocking agents
  • IV vasoactive medications, sedatives

Independent double checks may be required according to policy.

Section 23 ORAL, ENTERAL, AND PARENTERAL MEDICATION RULES

Do Not Crush:

  • Enteric-coated tablets; extended-release, sustained-release, or timed-release formulations
  • Sublingual or buccal tablets
  • Hazardous medications unless specifically directed

Do Not Give Oral Medications to a Client Who:

  • Has an absent gag reflex or cannot swallow safely
  • Has severely reduced consciousness or is actively vomiting
  • Is prescribed nothing by mouth or has a contraindication to oral administration

Enteral Tube Medications

  • Verify tube placement per policy. Elevate the head of the bed. Use liquid form when appropriate.
  • Administer medications separately. Flush before, between, and after.
  • Do not mix medications into feeding formula. Do not crush prohibited formulations.
  • Pause feeding when required for absorption or compatibility.

Intramuscular Injection

The ventrogluteal site is generally considered safe for many adult IM injections. Consider muscle size, medication volume, client age, needle length, and bleeding risk.

Subcutaneous Injection

Rotate sites appropriately. Do not massage a heparin injection site — this may increase bruising.

Section 24 BLOOD TRANSFUSION SAFETY

Before Transfusion

  • Verify the prescription and confirm informed consent per policy.
  • Ensure appropriate IV access. Obtain baseline vital signs.
  • Verify the blood product with the required licensed professional: client identity, blood type, compatibility, unit number, and expiration.
  • Use compatible tubing and solution. Begin promptly after receiving.
  • Stay with the client during the initial period. Monitor closely for a reaction.

Suspected Transfusion Reaction — Immediately

  1. Stop the transfusion.
  2. Keep the IV line open with new tubing and normal saline.
  3. Assess airway, breathing, circulation, and vital signs.
  4. Notify the provider and blood bank.
  5. Follow prescriptions for treatment. Send required specimens.
  6. Document findings and interventions. Complete the appropriate safety report.

Never restart the same blood product after a suspected reaction unless specifically directed under an approved protocol.

Possible Reaction Findings

Fever, chills, flushing, dyspnea, wheezing, chest tightness, back or flank pain, hypotension, tachycardia, anxiety, hives, itching, hemoglobinuria.

Section 25 OXYGEN SAFETY

Oxygen Safety Rules

  • Post oxygen-in-use signs. Keep oxygen away from flames and sparks. Do not allow smoking.
  • Avoid petroleum-based products near oxygen equipment. Secure oxygen cylinders upright.
  • Use water-based lubricants when needed. Assess skin around oxygen devices.
  • Humidify oxygen when indicated. Ensure prescribed flow settings are correct.

A client who needs oxygen should receive it based on clinical need. Do not withhold oxygen from a client with chronic lung disease who is severely hypoxemic — administer it carefully and monitor the response.

Section 27 POSTOPERATIVE PRIORITIES

Immediately after surgery, assess: airway, breathing, oxygenation, circulation, level of consciousness, surgical site, drainage, pain, urine output, and temperature.

Expected Postoperative Findings

  • Mild incisional pain; temporary drowsiness; small amounts of expected drainage
  • Reduced bowel sounds immediately after abdominal surgery; mild nausea; gradual return of activity

Unexpected Findings — Report Immediately

  • Stridor, severe respiratory distress
  • Rapidly expanding drainage or bright-red bleeding
  • Sudden hypotension, new confusion, chest pain, or severe restlessness
  • Rigid abdomen, absent urine output, wound separation, evisceration
  • Sudden unilateral leg swelling or sudden shortness of breath

Wound Dehiscence — Wound Separation

  • Position to reduce tension on the incision.
  • Cover with sterile saline-moistened dressings. Notify the surgeon.
  • Instruct the client not to cough or strain.

Evisceration — Organ Protrusion Through the Wound (EMERGENCY)

  • Stay with the client. Position with knees bent to reduce abdominal tension.
  • Cover the organs with sterile saline-moistened dressings.
  • Do not push the organs back inside.
  • Notify the surgeon immediately. Keep the client NPO. Prepare for emergency surgery.

Section 28 PAIN

Pain is whatever the client says it is. Assess: location, quality, intensity, timing, aggravating and relieving factors, associated symptoms, effect on function, and previous interventions.

Important Principles

  • Do not rely only on vital signs to determine whether pain exists.
  • A sleeping client may still have pain.
  • Chronic pain may not produce dramatic vital-sign changes.
  • Reassess after every intervention. Use both medication and nonpharmacological interventions.
  • Treat severe acute pain promptly while monitoring safety.
  • Do not use placebo medication to test whether pain is "real."

Opioid Priority Assessment

Before and after administering an opioid, assess: respiratory rate, level of consciousness, oxygenation, pain level, and blood pressure when relevant.

Excessive sedation often appears before severe respiratory depression.

Section 30 URINE OUTPUT

Adult urine output below approximately 30 mL/hour is commonly considered concerning and should be evaluated in the clinical context.

First Consider

  • Is the catheter kinked? Is the drainage bag positioned correctly?
  • Is the client dehydrated? Is blood pressure low?
  • Has the client received nephrotoxic medication?
  • Is there urinary retention or obstruction? Is renal perfusion reduced?

Do not irrigate a urinary catheter without an appropriate prescription or protocol unless emergency policy permits it.

Part 1 Summary RAPID NCLEX RULES TO MEMORIZE

These foundational rules apply to almost every NCLEX question involving priority, safety, delegation, or clinical judgment.

  • Airway usually comes before breathing and circulation.
  • Unstable clients come before stable clients.
  • Acute problems come before chronic problems.
  • Unexpected findings come before expected findings.
  • Assess before acting unless there is an obvious emergency.
  • Stop the harmful treatment first.
  • Client safety comes before documentation.
  • Never delegate assessment, teaching, evaluation, or nursing judgment.
  • Assistive personnel receive routine tasks for stable clients.
  • LPN/LVNs generally care for stable clients with predictable outcomes.
  • The registered nurse handles unstable, complex, and unpredictable clients.
  • Use the least restrictive safety intervention.
  • Never place anything in the mouth during a seizure.
  • Never leave a high-risk client alone in immediate danger.
  • Never push eviscerated organs back into the abdomen.
  • Stop a blood transfusion first when a reaction is suspected.
  • Stop an IV infusion when extravasation is suspected.
  • Never administer oral medication to a client who cannot swallow safely.
  • A competent client has the right to refuse treatment.
  • Do-not-resuscitate does not mean do not treat.
  • Use a qualified interpreter — not a child or untrained family member.
  • Do not discuss an incident report in the medical record.
  • Document facts, not opinions.
  • Reassess after every important intervention.
  • New confusion is an urgent physiological finding until proven otherwise.
  • Restlessness may be an early sign of hypoxia.
  • Sudden shortness of breath is always significant.
  • Rapid weight gain commonly indicates fluid retention.
  • Urine output reflects kidney perfusion and must be monitored closely.
  • When two answers appear correct, select the one that protects the client from the greatest immediate harm.
PART 2 — LABORATORY VALUES, ELECTROLYTES, ACID-BASE, IV FLUIDS, NUTRITION & DIAGNOSTICS

Section 32 HOW TO INTERPRET LABORATORY VALUES ON THE NCLEX

Do not memorize laboratory values alone. Always connect the value to the client's full clinical picture.

Consider: symptoms, diagnosis, current medications, recent procedures, fluid status, cardiac rhythm, neurological status, bleeding risk, and respiratory status.

A laboratory result becomes more urgent when it causes:

  • Airway compromise
  • Respiratory failure
  • Cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Seizures
  • Severe bleeding
  • Altered level of consciousness
  • Shock
  • Acute kidney injury
  • Severe weakness or paralysis

Reference ranges may vary slightly by laboratory. On the NCLEX, focus on the overall pattern and clinical danger.

White Blood Cell Count

White blood cell count (WBC; the number of infection-fighting cells in the blood).

Elevated WBC may indicate: infection, inflammation, stress, trauma, corticosteroid use, or leukemia.

Low WBC may indicate: bone marrow suppression, chemotherapy effect, severe infection, immunosuppression, or certain medications.

Neutropenia

Neutropenia (a reduced number of neutrophils, which are white blood cells that fight bacterial infection) increases infection risk. The most important value is often the absolute neutrophil count (ANC; the estimated number of neutrophils available to fight infection).

EMERGENCY: Fever with Severe Neutropenia

A fever in a severely neutropenic client is a medical emergency. Notify the provider immediately.

Neutropenic Precautions

  • Perform strict hand hygiene.
  • Avoid sick visitors.
  • Avoid fresh flowers and standing water when required by policy.
  • Avoid raw or undercooked food.
  • Monitor temperature closely.
  • Avoid rectal temperatures, suppositories, and enemas.
  • Use aseptic technique for all procedures.
  • Report fever immediately.
  • Avoid unnecessary invasive procedures.
  • Monitor for subtle infection — inflammation may be reduced.

Hemoglobin

Hemoglobin (the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells). Low hemoglobin may cause: fatigue, weakness, pallor, tachycardia, shortness of breath, reduced activity tolerance, and chest pain in severe cases. Priority depends on symptoms, bleeding, and the degree of reduction.

Hematocrit

Hematocrit (the percentage of blood volume made up of red blood cells).

Low hematocrit may indicate anemia, bleeding, or fluid overload. High hematocrit may indicate dehydration, chronic hypoxia, or polycythemia.

Platelets

Platelets (blood components that help form clots). Thrombocytopenia (a low platelet count) increases bleeding risk.

Sodium (Na⁺)

Sodium (Na⁺; the main extracellular electrolyte that helps regulate fluid balance and nerve function).

Hyponatremia

Hyponatremia (serum sodium below the normal range) causes water to move into cells. Clinical manifestations: headache, nausea, muscle cramps, confusion, lethargy, seizures, coma.

Severe or rapidly developing hyponatremia is a neurological emergency.

Avoid rapid correction — it may cause osmotic demyelination syndrome.

Nursing Priorities — Hyponatremia

  • Seizure precautions and frequent neurological assessment
  • Fluid restriction when caused by water excess
  • Sodium replacement as prescribed
  • Monitor intake, output, and daily weight

Hypertonic saline (a concentrated sodium solution) may be used for severe symptomatic hyponatremia. Administer carefully with close monitoring.

Hypernatremia

Hypernatremia (serum sodium above the normal range) usually reflects water loss or excess sodium. Clinical manifestations: intense thirst, dry mucous membranes, restlessness, irritability, weakness, confusion, seizures, coma.

Nursing Priorities — Hypernatremia

  • Replace water gradually — avoid rapid correction
  • Monitor neurological status and intake/output
  • Provide oral care and monitor sodium closely

Potassium (K⁺)

Potassium (K⁺; the main intracellular electrolyte required for muscle and cardiac function).

Potassium abnormalities are dangerous — they may cause fatal dysrhythmias.

Hypokalemia

Hypokalemia (serum potassium below the normal range) may result from: diuretics, vomiting, diarrhea, nasogastric suction, insulin administration, alkalosis, or poor intake.

Clinical manifestations: muscle weakness, fatigue, leg cramps, reduced bowel sounds, constipation, ileus, weak pulse, dysrhythmias, respiratory muscle weakness.

ECG findings: flattened T waves, ST depression, U waves.

Nursing Priorities — Hypokalemia

  • Cardiac monitoring for severe deficiency
  • Potassium replacement as prescribed
  • Verify adequate urine output before replacement
  • Monitor renal function, bowel sounds, and respiratory status
  • Review diuretic use

Potassium IV Safety — NEVER Give by IV Push

  • Always dilute intravenous potassium.
  • Use an infusion pump.
  • Verify adequate urine output before administration.
  • Monitor the IV site and cardiac rhythm when indicated.
  • Follow the prescribed maximum rate.
Oral potassium: give with food; dilute liquid forms; do not crush extended-release tablets; monitor for GI irritation.

Hyperkalemia

Hyperkalemia (serum potassium above the normal range) may result from: kidney failure, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors, tissue destruction, acidosis, excess potassium intake, or adrenal insufficiency.

Clinical manifestations: muscle weakness, paresthesia, abdominal cramping, diarrhea, dysrhythmias, cardiac arrest.

ECG findings: tall peaked T waves, widened QRS, prolonged PR interval, bradycardia, sine-wave pattern in severe cases.

Hyperkalemia Treatment

  • Calcium gluconate — stabilizes the cardiac membrane; does not lower potassium
  • Regular insulin + dextrose — insulin moves K⁺ into cells; dextrose prevents hypoglycemia
  • Sodium bicarbonate — when acidosis is present
  • Nebulized beta-agonist
  • Potassium-binding medication
  • Loop diuretic
  • Dialysis

Calcium (Ca²⁺)

Calcium (Ca²⁺; an electrolyte needed for bone strength, muscle contraction, nerve function, and clotting). Total calcium normal range: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL. Calcium must be interpreted with albumin because much of circulating calcium is protein-bound.

Hypocalcemia

Hypocalcemia (serum calcium below the normal range) may cause: numbness around the mouth, tingling, muscle cramps, tetany, hyperactive reflexes, seizures, laryngospasm, prolonged QT interval.

Classic Clinical Signs of Hypocalcemia:
  • Chvostek sign (facial muscle twitching when the facial nerve is tapped) — suggests neuromuscular irritability
  • Trousseau sign (carpal spasm caused by inflating a blood pressure cuff) — suggests hypocalcemia

Nursing Priorities — Hypocalcemia

  • Seizure precautions
  • Airway monitoring (risk of laryngospasm)
  • Cardiac monitoring (prolonged QT)
  • Calcium replacement as prescribed
  • Fall precautions

Hypercalcemia

Hypercalcemia (serum calcium above the normal range) may cause: weakness, fatigue, constipation, reduced reflexes, kidney stones, bone pain, confusion, dysrhythmias, shortened QT interval.

Classic NCLEX phrase: "Stones, bones, abdominal groans, and psychiatric overtones"

Nursing Priorities — Hypercalcemia

  • Encourage fluids when not contraindicated
  • Promote mobility
  • Monitor cardiac rhythm and kidney function
  • Avoid unnecessary calcium intake
  • Administer prescribed medications

Magnesium (Mg²⁺)

Magnesium (Mg²⁺; an electrolyte needed for neuromuscular stability and cardiac function). Normal range: 1.7–2.2 mg/dL.

Hypomagnesemia

Hypomagnesemia (serum magnesium below the normal range) may cause: tremors, hyperactive reflexes, muscle cramps, seizures, tachycardia, dysrhythmias, and torsades de pointes (a potentially fatal ventricular dysrhythmia associated with a prolonged QT interval).

Nursing Priorities — Hypomagnesemia

  • Cardiac monitoring and seizure precautions
  • Magnesium replacement as prescribed
  • Monitor potassium and calcium — they may also be low

Hypermagnesemia

Hypermagnesemia (serum magnesium above the normal range) may cause: lethargy, reduced reflexes, muscle weakness, hypotension, bradycardia, respiratory depression, cardiac arrest.

Common causes: kidney failure, excess magnesium-containing antacids or laxatives, magnesium sulfate therapy.

Magnesium Toxicity — Warning Signs

  • Absent deep tendon reflexes
  • Respiratory rate below 12/min
  • Reduced urine output
  • Hypotension and bradycardia
  • Decreased level of consciousness
Antidote for Magnesium Toxicity: Calcium Gluconate

Calcium gluconate is the antidote for magnesium toxicity.

Phosphorus

Phosphorus (an electrolyte needed for energy production, bone health, and cellular function). Normal range: 2.5–4.5 mg/dL. Calcium and phosphorus often move in opposite directions.

Hypophosphatemia may cause muscle weakness, respiratory weakness, confusion, bone pain, and reduced cardiac function. Common causes: malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, refeeding syndrome, hyperparathyroidism.

Hyperphosphatemia commonly occurs with kidney failure and may cause signs of hypocalcemia. Phosphate binders are usually taken with meals.

Chloride

Chloride (Cl⁻; an extracellular electrolyte that helps regulate fluid and acid-base balance). Normal range: 98–106 mEq/L. Chloride often follows sodium and may change with acid-base imbalance.

Section 37 LIVER FUNCTION VALUES

Aspartate aminotransferase (AST; an enzyme found in the liver and other tissues) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT; an enzyme found mainly in the liver) may rise with liver injury. ALT is generally more liver-specific.

Bilirubin (a yellow pigment produced from red blood cell breakdown and processed by the liver). Elevated bilirubin may cause: jaundice, dark urine, pale stool, pruritus.

Asterixis (a flapping tremor of the hands) may occur with liver failure and elevated ammonia. It is a sign of hepatic encephalopathy.

Section 41 RESPIRATORY ACIDOSIS

Occurs when carbon dioxide is retained. Typical pattern: low pH, high PaCO₂.

Common causes: opioid overdose, sedation, airway obstruction, COPD, severe pneumonia, respiratory muscle weakness, hypoventilation.

Clinical manifestations: headache, confusion, drowsiness, warm flushed skin, reduced level of consciousness.

Treatment focuses on improving ventilation. Address the underlying cause. May require supplemental oxygen, bronchodilators, reversal agents, or mechanical ventilation.

Section 42 RESPIRATORY ALKALOSIS

Occurs when excessive carbon dioxide is exhaled. Typical pattern: high pH, low PaCO₂.

Common causes: anxiety, pain, fever, hypoxemia, pulmonary embolism, mechanical overventilation, early sepsis.

Clinical manifestations: lightheadedness, tingling, numbness, muscle cramps, anxiety, palpitations.

Treatment focuses on correcting the underlying cause.

Section 43 METABOLIC ACIDOSIS

Occurs when acid accumulates or bicarbonate is lost. Typical pattern: low pH, low HCO₃⁻.

Common causes: diabetic ketoacidosis, kidney failure, severe diarrhea, lactic acidosis, shock, sepsis.

Clinical manifestations: Kussmaul respirations, confusion, weakness, dysrhythmias, hypotension.

Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing used to reduce acid by removing carbon dioxide) are commonly seen in diabetic ketoacidosis.

Section 44 METABOLIC ALKALOSIS

Occurs when acid is lost or bicarbonate increases. Typical pattern: high pH, high HCO₃⁻.

Common causes: prolonged vomiting, nasogastric suction, excess antacid use, diuretic therapy, hypokalemia.

Clinical manifestations: weakness, confusion, muscle cramps, paresthesia, dysrhythmias, reduced respiratory rate.

Treatment focuses on correcting fluid and electrolyte losses.

Isotonic Fluids

Isotonic fluids (solutions with a concentration similar to blood plasma) expand the circulating volume without causing major fluid shifts between cells.

Risks: fluid overload, pulmonary edema, worsening heart failure, worsening kidney failure.

Monitor: lung sounds, oxygenation, edema, blood pressure, urine output, and daily weight.

Hypotonic Fluids

Hypotonic fluids (solutions with a lower concentration than blood plasma) move water into cells.

Risks: cerebral edema, hypotension, worsening increased intracranial pressure.

Use cautiously in: head injury, stroke, burns, increased ICP.

Hypertonic Fluids

Hypertonic fluids (solutions with a higher concentration than blood plasma) pull water from cells into the bloodstream.

Risks: pulmonary edema, circulatory overload, rapid sodium correction, vein irritation.

Hypertonic saline requires close neurological and sodium monitoring.

Section 46 INTRAVENOUS COMPLICATIONS

Infiltration

Infiltration (leakage of a nonvesicant IV fluid into surrounding tissue). Findings: cool skin, swelling, pallor, discomfort, slowed infusion, no blood return.

Actions

  • Stop the infusion.
  • Remove the catheter as indicated.
  • Elevate the limb.
  • Apply warm or cold compress according to the solution and policy.
  • Restart IV access at another site. Document findings.

Extravasation

Extravasation (leakage of a vesicant medication into surrounding tissue, causing potential tissue injury). Findings: pain, burning, swelling, blistering, skin discoloration, reduced infusion flow.

Actions — Extravasation

  1. Stop the infusion immediately.
  2. Leave the catheter in place initially if aspiration or antidote administration may be required.
  3. Aspirate residual medication when indicated.
  4. Notify the provider and pharmacy.
  5. Administer the prescribed antidote.
  6. Apply the recommended compress and elevate the limb.
  7. Follow the extravasation protocol. Document and monitor the site.
Do NOT flush the line.

Phlebitis

Phlebitis (inflammation of a vein). Findings: redness, warmth, tenderness, swelling, red streak along the vein, palpable venous cord.

Actions

  • Stop the infusion and remove the catheter.
  • Apply a warm compress. Restart at another site. Monitor for infection.

Local Infection

Findings: redness, drainage, warmth, tenderness, fever.

Actions

  • Remove the catheter and notify the provider.
  • Culture the site or catheter if prescribed.
  • Apply a sterile dressing. Monitor for systemic infection.

Fluid Overload

Findings: crackles, dyspnea, tachycardia, hypertension, jugular venous distention, edema, anxiety, reduced oxygen saturation.

Actions

  • Slow or stop the infusion. Elevate the head of the bed.
  • Administer oxygen. Notify the provider.
  • Prepare to administer a diuretic. Monitor vital signs and oxygenation.

Air Embolism

Air embolism (air entering the vascular system and blocking blood flow). Possible findings: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, hypotension, cyanosis, confusion, sense of impending doom.

Actions — Air Embolism (EMERGENCY)

  • Clamp the catheter immediately.
  • Place the client on the left side in Trendelenburg position when appropriate.
  • Administer oxygen.
  • Notify the emergency response team and provider.
  • Monitor cardiac and respiratory status.

Section 47 CENTRAL VENOUS CATHETER SAFETY

Central venous catheter (a catheter inserted into a large central vein) may be used for long-term IV therapy, parenteral nutrition, chemotherapy, vesicant medications, and hemodynamic monitoring.

Nursing Priorities — CVC

  • Use sterile technique for all access and dressing changes.
  • Scrub the hub before every access.
  • Maintain an occlusive dressing. Assess for infection.
  • Check external catheter length. Avoid unnecessary disconnection.
  • Clamp the line when required. Confirm placement before initial use.
  • Monitor for pneumothorax after insertion.

Possible complications: infection, air embolism, catheter occlusion, thrombosis, pneumothorax, catheter migration.

Section 55 ENTERAL NUTRITION

Enteral nutrition (feeding through the gastrointestinal tract using a tube) is preferred when the GI tract functions.

Nursing Priorities — Enteral Nutrition

  • Verify tube placement according to policy before feeding.
  • Elevate the head of the bed 30–45 degrees.
  • Assess bowel function. Monitor glucose and electrolytes.
  • Flush the tube to prevent clogging. Provide oral care.
  • Monitor for aspiration.

Signs of Aspiration

  • Coughing, choking, wet voice, dyspnea, crackles
  • Reduced oxygen saturation, fever

Stop the feeding and assess the client if aspiration is suspected.

Section 56 PARENTERAL NUTRITION

Parenteral nutrition (intravenous administration of nutrients when the GI tract cannot be used adequately) contains glucose, amino acids, electrolytes, vitamins, and lipids. Total parenteral nutrition is usually administered through a central venous catheter.

Nursing Priorities — Parenteral Nutrition

  • Use sterile technique and an infusion pump.
  • Monitor blood glucose and electrolytes.
  • Monitor liver function, daily weight, and intake/output.
  • Assess for infection. Use a dedicated line.
  • Do not stop abruptly unless required for an emergency.

If parenteral nutrition is suddenly unavailable, dextrose-containing IV fluid may be prescribed to prevent hypoglycemia.

Section 57 REFEEDING SYNDROME

Refeeding syndrome (dangerous electrolyte shifts that occur when nutrition is restarted rapidly after severe malnutrition) may cause:

  • Severe hypophosphatemia (most characteristic finding)
  • Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia
  • Fluid overload, dysrhythmias, respiratory failure, neurological changes
High-risk clients:
  • Prolonged starvation or severe malnutrition
  • Alcohol use disorder, anorexia nervosa
  • Major weight loss

Nutrition should be restarted gradually with close electrolyte monitoring.

Section 58 COMMON DIAGNOSTIC TEST PRECAUTIONS

CT With Contrast

Computed tomography (CT; an imaging test that uses x-rays to create detailed cross-sectional images) may use iodinated contrast.

Before Contrast

  • Assess kidney function.
  • Ask about previous contrast reactions and allergies.
  • Check for pregnancy when relevant. Ensure hydration when appropriate.
  • Review metformin use according to current protocol.

After Contrast

  • Encourage fluids when not contraindicated.
  • Monitor for allergic reaction.
  • Monitor kidney function in high-risk clients.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI; an imaging test that uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves) does not use ionizing radiation.

Before MRI

  • Screen for metal implants; remove all metal objects.
  • Assess for pacemakers, aneurysm clips, cochlear implants, and implanted devices.
  • Assess for claustrophobia. Evaluate kidney function if gadolinium contrast is used.

The client must not enter the MRI area with unsafe metal equipment.

Ultrasound

Ultrasound (an imaging test that uses sound waves) does not use ionizing radiation. Preparation depends on the body area.

  • Pelvic ultrasound may require a full bladder.
  • Abdominal ultrasound may require fasting.

Endoscopy (Upper)

Endoscopy (visual examination of an internal organ using a flexible scope) often requires sedation.

Before

  • Confirm nothing-by-mouth status and verify informed consent.
  • Remove dentures when indicated. Review anticoagulants. Establish IV access.

After Upper Endoscopy

  • Monitor airway. Assess gag reflex before allowing oral intake.
  • Monitor for bleeding and perforation.

Colonoscopy (endoscopic examination of the colon).

Before

Bowel preparation, clear-liquid diet, nothing by mouth as prescribed, medication review, informed consent.

After — Expected

Mild gas, bloating, cramping.

After — Report Immediately

  • Severe abdominal pain and rigid abdomen
  • Fever and tachycardia
  • Heavy rectal bleeding

These may indicate perforation or hemorrhage.

Lumbar Puncture

Lumbar puncture (insertion of a needle into the lower spinal canal to obtain cerebrospinal fluid or measure pressure).

Before

  • Verify consent and assess coagulation status.
  • Position correctly: side-lying with knees flexed, or sitting leaning forward.
  • Explain the need to remain still throughout the procedure.

After

  • Monitor neurological status and puncture site.
  • Monitor for CSF leakage. Encourage fluids if prescribed.

A severe headache that worsens when upright may be a post-lumbar-puncture headache caused by CSF leakage.

Bone Marrow Biopsy

Bone marrow biopsy (removal of a small sample of bone marrow for examination). Common site: posterior iliac crest.

After

  • Apply pressure and monitor for bleeding.
  • Keep the dressing dry. Assess pain. Monitor for infection.

Liver Biopsy

Liver biopsy (removal of a small sample of liver tissue).

Before

  • Assess coagulation studies. Verify consent. Obtain baseline vital signs.
  • Teach the client to hold the breath during needle insertion.

After

  • Position on the right side to tamponade the biopsy site.
  • Monitor for bleeding and vital signs. Assess abdominal and shoulder pain.
  • Monitor for hypotension and tachycardia.

Thoracentesis

Thoracentesis (removal of fluid or air from the pleural space using a needle).

During

After

  • Assess breath sounds and oxygen saturation.
  • Monitor respiratory status and puncture site. Watch for pneumothorax.

  • Sudden dyspnea and chest pain
  • Reduced or absent breath sounds on the affected side
  • Tachycardia and reduced oxygen saturation

Paracentesis

Paracentesis (removal of fluid from the abdominal cavity).

Before

  • Ask the client to void. Obtain weight and abdominal girth.
  • Check coagulation status. Obtain baseline vital signs.

After

  • Monitor blood pressure and for fluid shifts.
  • Monitor for bleeding. Recheck weight and abdominal girth.
  • Assess the puncture site.
  • Albumin may be prescribed after large-volume removal.

Electroencephalogram (EEG)

Electroencephalogram (EEG; a test that records electrical activity in the brain).

Before

  • Wash hair; avoid hair products.
  • Avoid stimulants when prescribed.
  • Do not stop seizure medications unless directed.

Electrocardiogram (ECG)

Electrocardiogram (ECG; a recording of the electrical activity of the heart).

  • No electrical current enters the body — the test is painless.

Section 59 RAPID LABORATORY PRIORITY RULES

Memorize these 35 rules. Each one reflects a testable NCLEX principle.

  1. Potassium abnormalities may cause fatal dysrhythmias.
  2. Severe sodium abnormalities may cause seizures and cerebral edema.
  3. Low calcium may cause tetany, seizures, and laryngospasm.
  4. High magnesium may cause absent reflexes and respiratory depression.
  5. Low magnesium may cause torsades de pointes.
  6. Severe thrombocytopenia creates spontaneous bleeding risk.
  7. Fever with severe neutropenia is an emergency.
  8. Elevated troponin indicates myocardial injury until fully evaluated.
  9. Rising creatinine with reduced urine output suggests worsening kidney function.
  10. Elevated ammonia with confusion suggests hepatic encephalopathy.
  11. Never give intravenous potassium by push.
  12. Verify urine output before administering potassium.
  13. Calcium gluconate stabilizes the heart in hyperkalemia but does not remove potassium.
  14. Insulin moves potassium into cells.
  15. Protamine sulfate reverses heparin.
  16. Vitamin K reverses warfarin.
  17. Calcium gluconate reverses magnesium toxicity.
  18. Soap and water are required for Clostridioides difficile hand hygiene.
  19. FFP replaces clotting factors, not platelets.
  20. Cryoprecipitate replaces fibrinogen.
  21. Hypertonic saline requires slow correction and close monitoring.
  22. Isotonic fluid expands vascular volume.
  23. Hypotonic fluid moves water into cells.
  24. Hypertonic fluid pulls water into the bloodstream.
  25. Stop an infusion immediately when infiltration or extravasation is suspected.
  26. Do not flush an extravasated vesicant.
  27. Sudden dyspnea after central-line manipulation may indicate air embolism.
  28. Never stop parenteral nutrition abruptly unless required for safety.
  29. Refeeding syndrome commonly causes severe hypophosphatemia.
  30. After upper endoscopy, confirm gag reflex before giving oral intake.
  31. After liver biopsy, position the client on the right side.
  32. After thoracentesis, sudden respiratory distress may indicate pneumothorax.
  33. After colonoscopy, severe abdominal pain and rigidity may indicate perforation.
  34. When an abnormal laboratory value is given, assess the client — not only the number.
  35. The most dangerous laboratory value is the one causing an immediate cardiac, neurological, respiratory, or bleeding complication.

60 Cardiovascular Assessment Priorities

Immediately assess a client reporting or displaying:
  • New chest pain
  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Palpitations with dizziness
  • Syncope
  • New confusion
  • Cool, clammy skin
  • Weak or absent pulses
  • Sudden hypotension
  • New irregular heart rhythm
  • Unilateral leg swelling
  • Sudden weight gain
  • Reduced urine output
  • Severe fatigue with exertion
Important cardiovascular assessment findings:
  • Heart rate and rhythm
  • Blood pressure
  • Peripheral pulses and capillary refill
  • Skin color and temperature
  • Level of consciousness
  • Urine output
  • Lung sounds
  • Edema and jugular venous distention
  • Chest pain characteristics
  • Oxygen saturation
Perfusion is inadequate when the client develops: altered mental status, cool extremities, weak pulses, delayed capillary refill, hypotension, reduced urine output, chest pain, elevated lactate, or cyanosis.

61 Chest Pain Priorities

For new or suspected cardiac chest pain:
  • Stop activity; assess airway, breathing, circulation, and vital signs
  • Obtain a 12-lead electrocardiogram
  • Apply continuous cardiac monitoring; establish intravenous access
  • Administer oxygen when hypoxemic or in respiratory distress
  • Administer prescribed aspirin if not contraindicated
  • Administer prescribed nitroglycerin when blood pressure permits
  • Obtain cardiac laboratory tests; prepare for urgent reperfusion when indicated
  • Do not leave an unstable client with chest pain alone
Cardiac pain may be described as pressure, squeezing, tightness, heaviness, burning, or crushing. It may radiate to the arm, shoulder, neck, jaw, back, or epigastric area. Associated findings include dyspnea, diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting, anxiety, weakness, and syncope.
Atypical presentations — Women, older adults, and clients with diabetes may present with unusual fatigue, indigestion, shortness of breath, back pain, nausea, weakness, or minimal chest pain.

62 Acute Coronary Syndrome

Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) — sudden reduction of blood flow to the heart — includes unstable angina, non-ST-elevation MI (NSTEMI), and ST-elevation MI (STEMI).
Unstable angina is a medical emergency. It may occur at rest, with minimal activity, more frequently than usual, for a longer duration, or with reduced response to nitroglycerin. It may progress to myocardial infarction.
Myocardial infarction (MI) — death of heart muscle from prolonged lack of blood flow — may produce persistent chest pressure, dyspnea, diaphoresis, nausea, anxiety, dysrhythmias, hypotension, and elevated troponin.

71 Deep Vein Thrombosis

Do not massage the affected leg.

81 Asthma

Findings: wheezing, cough, chest tightness, dyspnea, prolonged expiration.

82 Pneumonia

86 Chest Tube Management

Keep drainage system upright, below chest level, free of dependent loops, and securely connected. Water-seal chamber: tidaling with breathing is expected; continuous bubbling = air leak; intermittent bubbling may be expected when air is leaving the pleural space.
If tube disconnects: place end in sterile water per emergency protocol; reconnect with sterile technique; notify provider.
If tube comes out: apply sterile occlusive dressing taped on three sides; call for help; assess breathing; administer oxygen; notify provider.
Do NOT routinely: clamp the chest tube (risk of tension pneumothorax), strip or milk the tubing, or raise the system above chest level. Clamping only under specific direction.

108 Renal Assessment Priorities

Immediately evaluate:
  • Urine output below expected level or sudden inability to urinate
  • New blood in the urine or rapidly rising creatinine
  • Severe flank pain
  • New confusion in a client with kidney disease
  • Crackles, dyspnea, or pulmonary edema
  • Severe hyperkalemia or new dysrhythmia
  • Swelling with hypertension
  • Fever with flank pain
  • Pain, redness, drainage, or absent vibration over a dialysis access
Always assess the client and check for simple mechanical causes (e.g., kinked urinary-catheter tubing) before assuming kidney failure.

Urgent dialysis may be required for: refractory metabolic acidosis, severe hyperkalemia, certain poisonings, fluid overload unresponsive to treatment, severe uremic complications.

110 Chronic Kidney Disease

CKD — persistent kidney damage lasting at least 3 months — is assessed with eGFR and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR). Findings should be confirmed rather than diagnosed from one isolated test.
Common findings: fatigue, hypertension, edema, anemia, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, bone disease, uremia (confusion, nausea, pruritus, metallic taste, pericarditis, neuropathy, reduced consciousness).
CKD nursing priorities: control BP; monitor kidney function, potassium, and fluid balance; avoid nephrotoxic substances; adjust medication doses; manage diabetes carefully; monitor hemoglobin; follow dietary restrictions; protect future dialysis-access sites.
Anemia of CKD: damaged kidneys produce less erythropoietin. Treatment may include iron, erythropoiesis-stimulating medications, or blood transfusion. Monitor BP and Hgb — these medications increase thrombotic and hypertensive risks when Hgb rises excessively.
Phosphate binders should generally be taken with meals.

111 Hemodialysis

Access types: arteriovenous fistula (preferred for long-term), AV graft, or central venous catheter.
Assess the fistula/graft for: thrill (palpable vibration — should be felt), bruit (swishing sound — should be heard), redness, warmth, swelling, drainage, distal circulation. Absent thrill or bruit requires immediate reporting.
Protect the dialysis arm — never use for: blood pressure measurements, IV lines, blood draws, tight clothing, or constrictive jewelry. Teach the client not to sleep on the access arm or carry heavy objects with that arm.

112 Peritoneal Dialysis

Process: inflow → dwell → drainage. Expected: clear light-yellow outflow; mild fullness during inflow; outflow approximately equal to or greater than inflow. Poor outflow causes: kinked tubing, closed clamps, constipation, incorrect position, catheter obstruction. Reposition client; ensure drainage bag is below abdominal level.
Peritonitis (major complication): cloudy outflow, abdominal pain, fever, chills, nausea, rebound tenderness. Cloudy dialysate should be reported immediately and a sample sent for culture. Maintain strict aseptic technique during exchanges.

116 Kidney Stones

Findings: severe flank pain radiating toward groin, hematuria, nausea, vomiting, restlessness, urinary urgency. Clients with kidney stones are often restless and unable to remain still (unlike peritonitis, where clients lie very still).
Nursing care: administer analgesics and antiemetics; encourage fluids when appropriate; strain all urine; save the stone for analysis; promote mobility when tolerated; monitor for obstruction and infection.
Report immediately: fever, chills, anuria, uncontrolled pain, or persistent vomiting. An infected obstructed urinary system is an emergency.

117 Urinary Retention

Findings: suprapubic fullness, lower abdominal discomfort, frequent small voids, dribbling, restlessness, reduced urine output, overflow incontinence. Actions: assess bladder distention; use bladder scanner; provide privacy; assist to normal voiding position; run water or use other noninvasive measures; catheterize when prescribed. After relief of severe retention, monitor for hematuria, hypotension, large urine output, and electrolyte imbalance.

118 Urinary Catheter Safety

Use only when clinically indicated. Nursing care:
  • Maintain a closed system; keep bag below bladder level
  • Keep tubing free of kinks; secure the catheter
  • Perform routine perineal hygiene
  • Obtain specimens through the sampling port
  • Empty bag using a clean container; remove catheter as soon as possible
Do not: disconnect unnecessarily; place bag on floor; irrigate routinely without order; obtain sterile sample from drainage bag. After catheter removal, monitor time and amount of first void.

120 Transurethral Resection of the Prostate

Expected after TURP with CBI: light-pink urine, small clots initially, bladder spasms. Unexpected — report: bright-red bleeding, large clots, reduced drainage, distended bladder, severe spasms, hypotension, tachycardia.

122 Hypoglycemia

ADA levels: Level 1 = <70 but ≥54 mg/dL; Level 2 = <54 mg/dL; Level 3 = severe event requiring assistance. Findings: sweating, tremors, hunger, tachycardia, palpitations, irritability, headache, confusion, slurred speech, seizure, loss of consciousness.
Conscious client who can swallow: give ~15 g rapid-acting carbohydrate (glucose tablets, juice, regular soda). Recheck glucose in ~15 minutes; repeat if still low. Avoid high-fat foods initially. After recovery, provide snack with carbohydrate and protein.
Unconscious client: do not give oral food or fluid. Maintain airway. Give IV dextrose when access available. Give glucagon when prescribed and IV access unavailable. After glucagon, place client on the side — nausea and vomiting may occur.

124 Diabetic Ketoacidosis

DKA — severe insulin deficiency causing hyperglycemia, ketone production, dehydration, and metabolic acidosis — occurs most commonly in type 1 diabetes. Findings: hyperglycemia, ketones, metabolic acidosis, dehydration, polyuria, polydipsia, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath, Kussmaul respirations, altered consciousness.
DKA treatment sequence:
  1. Begin isotonic IV fluids
  2. Check potassium
  3. Start IV regular insulin when appropriate
  4. Replace potassium as prescribed
  5. Add dextrose to fluids as glucose falls
  6. Treat the precipitating cause
  7. Monitor glucose, electrolytes, anion gap, and neurological status
Critical potassium rule: serum K⁺ may appear normal or high even though total body potassium is depleted. Do not begin insulin when K⁺ is dangerously low until replacement has started — insulin drives K⁺ into cells and will worsen hypokalemia.

125 Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State

HHS — extreme hyperglycemia with profound dehydration and little or no ketoacidosis — occurs most often in type 2 diabetes. Findings: extremely high glucose, profound dehydration, neurological changes, weakness, seizures, coma, minimal or absent ketones, no major Kussmaul breathing. Treatment: aggressive but monitored fluid replacement, electrolyte replacement, IV insulin, treat the cause.
HHS often has more severe dehydration and neurological dysfunction than DKA.

126 Diabetes Sick-Day Rules

Teach the client to:
  • Continue basal insulin unless specifically directed otherwise
  • Check glucose more often; check ketones when instructed
  • Drink fluids; consume carbohydrate-containing liquids if unable to eat
  • Contact provider for persistent hyperglycemia, ketones, vomiting, dehydration, or altered consciousness
  • Seek emergency care for breathing difficulty, severe weakness, confusion, or inability to keep fluids down

128 Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuresis (SIADH)

Mechanism: excessive ADH causing water retention and dilutional hyponatremia. Findings: low serum sodium and osmolality, concentrated urine, low urine output, weight gain without major peripheral edema, headache, confusion, seizures.
Nursing care: restrict fluids (first-line treatment); monitor sodium; measure I&O; obtain daily weights; perform neurological assessments; use seizure precautions; administer hypertonic saline for severe symptomatic cases as prescribed. Avoid correcting sodium too rapidly.
Memory aid: SIADH = water stays inside (concentrated urine, low serum sodium).

129 Diabetes Insipidus

Mechanism: inadequate ADH activity causing excessive dilute urine. Findings: large amounts of dilute urine, low urine specific gravity, intense thirst, dehydration, hypernatremia, high serum osmolality, hypotension, tachycardia.
Nursing care: replace fluids; monitor sodium and I&O; obtain daily weights; provide free access to water when safe; administer desmopressin for central DI as prescribed; monitor for fluid overload and hyponatremia during treatment.
Memory aid: DI = dry inside (dilute urine, high serum sodium).

130 Hypothyroidism

Findings: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, bradycardia, constipation, dry skin, slow speech, depression, reduced reflexes.
Levothyroxine teaching: take at the same time each day on an empty stomach; separate from iron, calcium, and certain antacids; take for life when permanent hypothyroidism is present; therapeutic improvement occurs gradually. Excessive dosage may cause tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, heat intolerance, or weight loss.
Myxedema coma — life-threatening decompensated hypothyroidism: hypothermia, bradycardia, hypotension, hypoventilation, hyponatremia, hypoglycemia, reduced consciousness. Requires intensive care, airway support, thyroid-hormone replacement, and treatment of precipitating cause. Avoid aggressive external rewarming.

131 Hyperthyroidism

Findings: weight loss, heat intolerance, tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, anxiety, diarrhea, sweating, insomnia, muscle weakness.
Report during antithyroid therapy (methimazole, propylthiouracil): fever, sore throat (may indicate agranulocytosis — severe reduction in granulocytes), jaundice, dark urine, unusual bleeding.
Radioactive iodine teaching: avoid pregnancy for prescribed period; follow radiation-safety instructions; limit close contact as directed; expect possible permanent hypothyroidism.
Thyroid storm — acute life-threatening hypermetabolic crisis: very high fever, severe tachycardia, hypertension followed by hypotension, agitation, delirium, vomiting, diarrhea, heart failure. Treatment sequence: antithyroid medication → iodine (after antithyroid med) → beta blocker → corticosteroid → cooling → fluids → treat precipitating cause. Do not administer aspirin for fever — it can increase free thyroid hormone. Use acetaminophen.

132 Thyroidectomy Care

After thyroidectomy: maintain airway; keep oxygen, suction, and emergency tracheostomy set available; position in Semi-Fowler; support head and neck; monitor incision and behind neck for bleeding; assess voice quality; monitor for hypocalcemia.
Report immediately:
  • Stridor or difficulty breathing — airway emergency
  • Rapid neck swelling or frequent swallowing — may indicate bleeding
  • Tingling around mouth, muscle twitching, positive Chvostek or Trousseau sign — may indicate hypocalcemia

133 Adrenal Insufficiency

Findings: fatigue, weight loss, hypotension, dehydration, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, abdominal symptoms, hyperpigmentation in primary adrenal insufficiency.
Corticosteroid teaching: take exactly as prescribed; do not stop abruptly; increase doses during illness or stress when instructed; carry medical identification; keep emergency medication available; report vomiting that prevents medication intake.
Adrenal crisis — acute cortisol deficiency: severe hypotension, shock, vomiting, abdominal pain, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, confusion. Immediate treatment: IV hydrocortisone and fluid resuscitation. Do not delay treatment while awaiting test results when crisis is strongly suspected.

134 Cushing Syndrome

Findings: truncal obesity, moon-shaped face, buffalo hump (fat pad between shoulders), thin extremities, purple striae, fragile skin, poor wound healing, hypertension, hyperglycemia, increased infection risk, osteoporosis, muscle weakness, mood changes.
Nursing care: monitor glucose and BP; prevent infection; protect fragile skin; prevent falls; encourage adequate protein; monitor electrolytes. Avoid abrupt corticosteroid withdrawal.

136 Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease

Findings: heartburn, regurgitation, chronic cough, hoarseness, symptoms after meals or lying down.
Teaching: eat smaller meals; avoid lying down after eating; elevate head of bed; avoid trigger foods; reduce weight; stop smoking; avoid tight clothing; take medications as prescribed.

137 Peptic Ulcer Disease

Associated with: H. pylori infection, NSAIDs, smoking. Treatment: proton-pump inhibitor, antibiotics for H. pylori, discontinuation of harmful medications. Complete the entire antibiotic course.
Report immediately: sudden severe abdominal pain, rigid abdomen, hematemesis, melena, dizziness, syncope. Sudden severe pain with rigid abdomen may indicate perforation.

138 Appendicitis

Findings: periumbilical pain moving to right lower quadrant (McBurney’s point), nausea, vomiting, fever, rebound tenderness, guarding, loss of appetite.
Do not: apply heat; give laxatives; administer enemas; or repeatedly palpate the abdomen. A sudden decrease in pain followed by generalized severe abdominal pain may indicate rupture and peritonitis.

139 Peritonitis

Findings: severe abdominal pain, rigid board-like abdomen, guarding, rebound tenderness, fever, tachycardia, absent bowel sounds, nausea, shock. Clients often lie very still — movement worsens pain.
Priorities: maintain NPO; establish IV access; administer fluids and antibiotics; insert nasogastric tube when prescribed; monitor for shock; prepare for surgery.

142 Pancreatitis

Findings: severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, vomiting, abdominal tenderness, elevated lipase, tachycardia. Pain may improve when leaning forward. Grey Turner and Cullen signs may indicate internal bleeding and severe disease.
Nursing priorities: manage pain; provide fluids (early monitored hydration preferred); monitor respiratory status, glucose, calcium, BP, and urine output; provide early enteral nutrition as prescribed; avoid alcohol; treat gallstone disease when present.
Complications: shock, ARDS, hypocalcemia, hyperglycemia, kidney injury, pancreatic necrosis. Avoid prophylactic antibiotics in uncomplicated acute pancreatitis.

143 Cholecystitis

Findings: right upper-quadrant pain, radiation to right shoulder, nausea, vomiting, fever, pain after fatty food, positive Murphy sign (inspiratory arrest with pain during palpation of RUQ).
Nursing care: NPO during acute attack; administer fluids, analgesics, and antibiotics when prescribed; use low-fat diet after acute symptoms improve; prepare for cholecystectomy when indicated. After laparoscopic cholecystectomy, shoulder pain from retained gas may occur — ambulation often helps.

144 Cirrhosis

Findings: ascites, edema, jaundice, pruritus, bruising, varices, low albumin, coagulation abnormalities, hepatic encephalopathy. Decompensated cirrhosis is marked by ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, or portal-hypertensive GI bleeding.
Nursing priorities: monitor neurological status and bleeding; measure abdominal girth; obtain daily weights; follow sodium restrictions; monitor fluid and electrolytes; avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic medications; prevent infection; provide skin care; provide adequate nutrition. Protein is not routinely eliminated — individualized, especially during encephalopathy.

145 Hepatic Encephalopathy

Findings: confusion, personality change, sleep-pattern changes, asterixis (flapping tremor), slurred speech, coma. Possible triggers: GI bleeding, infection, constipation, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, excess sedatives.
Lactulose — lowers intestinal ammonia and promotes its removal through stool — is adjusted to produce the prescribed number of soft stools. Assess: mental status, stool frequency, hydration, potassium, sodium. Excessive diarrhea may cause dehydration and electrolyte imbalance. Rifaximin may be added for recurrent or persistent encephalopathy.

146 Esophageal Varices

Ruptured varices — emergency: hematemesis, melena, hypotension, tachycardia, shock. Priorities: protect airway; establish large-bore IV access; prepare blood products; administer prescribed vasoactive medication and antibiotics; prepare for urgent endoscopy; monitor for hepatic encephalopathy. Keep scissors at the bedside with a balloon tamponade tube per policy — airway obstruction can occur if the balloon migrates.

147 Ascites

Findings: abdominal distention, weight gain, dyspnea, early satiety, reduced mobility. Nursing care: measure daily weight; measure abdominal girth consistently at the same location; restrict sodium; administer diuretics (spironolactone, furosemide); monitor potassium and kidney function; position for breathing comfort; prepare for paracentesis when prescribed.
After large-volume paracentesis: monitor blood pressure, assess puncture site, administer albumin when prescribed, monitor for fluid shifts.

153 Pregnancy Terminology

Gravida — total number of pregnancies (including current).
Para — number of pregnancies that reached the age of viability, regardless of whether the infant was born alive.
GTPAL system:
  • G — Gravida: total pregnancies
  • T — Term: births at ≥37 weeks
  • P — Preterm: births after viability but before 37 weeks
  • A — Abortions: pregnancy losses before viability
  • L — Living: currently living children
A multiple pregnancy counts as one under gravida and one delivery under T or P, but each surviving infant is counted under L.

Expected changes: mild increase in heart rate, increased blood volume, mild physiological anemia, increased urinary frequency, constipation, heartburn, mild dependent edema, increased vaginal discharge, skin pigmentation changes, mild shortness of breath with exertion.

  • Severe abdominal pain or heavy vaginal bleeding

  • Markedly reduced fetal movement; fever
  • Thoughts of harming oneself or the infant
  • Severe nausea and vomiting with inability to retain fluids

157 Fetal Heart Rate Basics

Normal FHR baseline: 110–160 beats/min.
Evaluate: baseline rate, variability, accelerations, decelerations, and contraction pattern.
Moderate variability is generally reassuring. Absent or minimal variability may occur with fetal sleep, maternal medications, prematurity, hypoxia, or acidosis. Always interpret with the complete tracing and clinical situation.
Accelerations (temporary increases in FHR) are generally reassuring.

Priority actions:
  • Call for emergency assistance
  • Insert a gloved hand and lift the presenting part off the cord — do not remove the hand until delivery or instructed by the provider
  • Position in knee-chest or Trendelenburg position
  • Stop oxytocin; administer oxygen when indicated
  • Prepare for emergency cesarean delivery
  • Cover an exposed cord with sterile saline-moistened gauze
  • Do not attempt to push the cord back into the uterus

160 Uterine Tachysystole

Uterine tachysystole — more than 5 contractions in 10 minutes averaged over 30 minutes — can reduce fetal oxygenation. Actions: stop oxytocin; reposition laterally; increase IV fluid as prescribed; notify provider; administer prescribed tocolytic if needed; continue fetal monitoring.
Terbutaline (beta₂-adrenergic medication that relaxes uterine muscle) adverse effects: maternal tachycardia, tremor, palpitations, hyperglycemia.

161 Oxytocin Safety

Oxytocin is a high-alert medication. Administer with an infusion pump and per protocol. During labor, monitor: FHR, contraction frequency, duration, intensity, resting uterine tone, and maternal vital signs.
Stop oxytocin for: persistent late decelerations, severe variable decelerations, uterine tachysystole, prolonged contraction, abnormal resting tone, signs of uterine rupture, or significant fetal distress.

162 Preeclampsia

Findings: hypertension, proteinuria (not required when other organ dysfunction is present), severe headache, vision changes, hyperreflexia, epigastric or RUQ pain, low platelets, elevated liver enzymes, kidney dysfunction, pulmonary edema, reduced fetal growth. May develop or first appear after delivery.
Nursing priorities: monitor BP, neurological status, deep tendon reflexes, urine output, headache and vision changes, epigastric pain, and fetal status; reduce environmental stimulation; implement seizure precautions; administer antihypertensive medication and magnesium sulfate as prescribed.

163 Magnesium Sulfate in Preeclampsia

Monitor during magnesium sulfate infusion: respiratory rate, deep tendon reflexes, urine output, level of consciousness, blood pressure, magnesium level when prescribed, fetal heart rate.
Magnesium toxicity signs: absent reflexes, respiratory depression, reduced urine output, bradycardia, hypotension, reduced consciousness.
Antidote: Calcium gluconate — keep readily available at bedside during magnesium treatment.
Magnesium is eliminated through the kidneys; reduced urine output increases toxicity risk.
Magnesium sulfate prevents seizures in preeclampsia/eclampsia. It is NOT primarily an antihypertensive.

164 Eclampsia

Eclampsia (seizures in a preeclamptic client) is an obstetric emergency.
During the seizure: stay with client; turn onto the side; protect the airway; protect from injury; do not restrain; do not place anything in the mouth.
After the seizure: administer oxygen; suction secretions; administer magnesium sulfate as prescribed.
Delivery is the definitive treatment, but maternal stabilization occurs first.

165 HELLP Syndrome

HELLP = Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets. May occur with preeclampsia.
Findings: RUQ or epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting, malaise, hypertension, headache, low platelets, elevated liver enzymes, evidence of hemolysis.
Major risks: bleeding, liver rupture, placental abruption, DIC, maternal and fetal instability. Prepare for urgent stabilization and delivery.

166 Placenta Previa

Placenta previa (placental implantation over or near the cervical opening) classically causes: painless, bright-red vaginal bleeding. The uterus is usually soft and nontender.
Do not perform a digital vaginal examination until placenta previa has been excluded. Management may include: pelvic rest, activity restriction, fetal monitoring, blood-loss monitoring, and cesarean delivery when the placenta covers the cervical opening.

167 Placental Abruption

Placental abruption (premature separation of the placenta) classically causes: painful vaginal bleeding, sudden abdominal or back pain, uterine tenderness, firm or board-like uterus, frequent contractions, fetal distress, maternal shock. Visible bleeding may underestimate total blood loss (can be concealed).
Key comparison: Placenta previa = painless bleeding, soft uterus. Placental abruption = painful bleeding, firm uterus.

168 Uterine Rupture

Uterine rupture — life-threatening emergency. Findings: sudden severe abdominal pain, abnormal FHR, cessation of contractions, loss of fetal station, vaginal bleeding, maternal hypotension, easily palpable fetal parts.
Actions: stop oxytocin; activate emergency response; administer oxygen; begin fluid and blood-product resuscitation; prepare for emergency surgery.

169 Amniotic Fluid Embolism

Amniotic fluid embolism — rare catastrophic maternal collapse — may cause sudden dyspnea, cyanosis, hypotension, seizure, cardiac arrest, DIC, and severe bleeding.
Priorities: CPR; oxygenation and ventilation; hemodynamic support; blood products; emergency delivery when indicated.

170 Preterm Labor

Preterm labor (regular contractions causing cervical change before 37 weeks) may present as: menstrual-like cramps, pelvic pressure, low backache, increased vaginal discharge, fluid leakage, regular contractions.
Management may include: hydration when appropriate; corticosteroids for fetal lung maturation; short-term tocolytic therapy; magnesium sulfate for fetal neuroprotection at selected gestational ages; treatment of infection.
Betamethasone (corticosteroid given before expected preterm birth) accelerates fetal lung maturation. It may temporarily increase maternal blood glucose.

171 Rupture of Membranes

After rupture of membranes, immediately assess (FHR first — cord prolapse can occur): fetal heart rate, fluid color, fluid odor, time of rupture, contraction pattern, maternal temperature.
Infection findings: maternal fever and tachycardia, fetal tachycardia, uterine tenderness, foul-smelling fluid.

172 Meconium-Stained Amniotic Fluid

Meconium-stained fluid may be associated with fetal stress or normal maturity.
Nursing care: notify the neonatal team; continue fetal monitoring; prepare for neonatal resuscitation if needed.
Routine suctioning is not performed in a vigorous newborn solely because meconium is present. Initial support is based on respiratory effort, muscle tone, and heart rate.

A normal postpartum uterus should be: firm, midline, and gradually descending.
Boggy uterus (uterine atony): massage the fundus; assess bladder fullness; assist to void or catheterize as prescribed; assess lochia and blood loss; administer oxytocin or another uterotonic medication; notify provider if bleeding continues.

175 Postpartum Hemorrhage

The 4 Ts of postpartum hemorrhage:
  • Tone — uterine atony (most common)
  • Trauma — laceration, hematoma, rupture
  • Tissue — retained placental tissue
  • Thrombin — coagulation disorder
Boggy uterus: massage fundus first. Firm uterus with bright-red continuous bleeding: suspect laceration. Hematoma: severe perineal pain, pressure, swelling, minimal visible bleeding, signs of shock.

180 Newborn Immediate Priorities

Immediately after birth: maintain warmth; position and maintain airway; dry the newborn; stimulate breathing when needed; assess respirations and heart rate; promote skin-to-skin contact when stable; identify the newborn correctly.
Routine preventive care: intramuscular vitamin K (prevents vitamin K deficiency bleeding — most reliable when given within 6 hours), ocular prophylaxis per local requirements, hepatitis B vaccination with parental consent.

181 Apgar Score

Apgar score — rapid assessment of newborn adaptation — completed at 1 and 5 minutes. Five components: Appearance, Pulse, Grimace, Activity, Respirations. Each scored 0, 1, or 2. Apgar describes transition but does not replace resuscitation decisions. Resuscitation should begin when clinically indicated rather than waiting for the Apgar score.

182 Normal Newborn Findings

Expected findings: flexed posture, strong cry, irregular respirations with brief pauses, heart rate 110–160/min, acrocyanosis shortly after birth, vernix caseosa, lanugo, molding, mild caput succedaneum, milia, Mongolian spots, transient breast enlargement, vaginal mucus or small bloody discharge in female newborns.
Acrocyanosis (bluish hands and feet with a pink central body) is expected shortly after birth.
Central cyanosis (involving the tongue or trunk) is abnormal.

184 Newborn Respiratory Distress

Report immediately: grunting, nasal flaring, retractions, central cyanosis, persistent tachypnea, apnea, poor muscle tone, reduced oxygen saturation.
Grunting — sound produced during expiration to maintain lung pressure — indicates respiratory difficulty.

185 Newborn Temperature Control

Routes of heat loss: evaporation (moisture to vapor), conduction (contact with cold surface), convection (exposure to cool air), radiation (heat transfer to nearby cold objects without contact).
Prevent heat loss by: drying immediately; removing wet linens; skin-to-skin contact; applying a hat; using warm blankets; prewarming equipment; avoiding drafts.
Cold stress increases oxygen consumption, glucose use, and metabolic demand — may lead to hypoglycemia, respiratory distress, and metabolic acidosis.

186 Newborn Hypoglycemia

Risk factors: maternal diabetes, prematurity, small or large for gestational age, cold stress, infection, difficult delivery.
Findings: jitteriness, poor feeding, weak cry, hypothermia, lethargy, apnea, seizure.
Actions: check blood glucose; maintain warmth; feed early when safe; administer glucose gel or IV dextrose as prescribed; reassess glucose. Do not assume all jitteriness is normal.

187 Newborn Jaundice

Physiological jaundice: appears after the first 24 hours; results from normal bilirubin processing; resolves with feeding and maturation.
Pathological jaundice: present during the first 24 hours, rises rapidly, becomes severe, persists unusually long, or occurs with anemia or illness.
Kernicterus — permanent neurological injury from bilirubin deposition in brain tissue — may result from high unconjugated bilirubin.
Phototherapy care: protect the eyes; expose as much skin as safely possible; reposition regularly; monitor temperature and hydration; encourage feeding; monitor stool and urine; do not apply lotions unless prescribed. Loose green stools may occur.

188 Breastfeeding Basics

Effective breastfeeding signs: deep latch, audible swallowing, rhythmic sucking, minimal nipple pain after initial latch, adequate urine and stool output, appropriate weight pattern.
Teaching: feed based on hunger cues; bring infant to the breast; break suction before removing; alternate starting breast; allow infant to finish first breast; avoid strict time limits when feeding is effective.
Hunger cues: rooting, hand-to-mouth movement, lip smacking, increased alertness. Crying is a late hunger cue.

189 Mastitis

Mastitis — inflammation or infection of breast tissue — may cause: unilateral breast pain, redness, warmth, fever, flu-like symptoms.
Teaching: continue breastfeeding or emptying the breast unless specifically contraindicated; begin feeding on the affected side when tolerable; ensure correct latch; rest and hydrate; complete prescribed antibiotics; apply warm compresses before feeding; report abscess signs.

190 Safe Infant Sleep

Teach caregivers to: place infant supine for every sleep; use a firm, flat sleep surface; keep soft objects and loose bedding out of the sleep area; avoid pillows, bumper pads, stuffed toys, and positioners; avoid overheating; use a separate infant sleep surface near the caregiver; avoid smoking exposure.
The prone position may be used during supervised awake “tummy time,” not routine sleep.

191 Pediatric Assessment Principles

Children often compensate well and then deteriorate rapidly.
Assess: general appearance, work of breathing, skin circulation, interaction with caregiver, hydration, urine output, level of consciousness, age-appropriate behavior.
Early deterioration signs: tachycardia, irritability, restlessness, reduced feeding, reduced urine output, increased respiratory effort.
Hypotension is often a late sign of pediatric shock.

194 Pediatric Pain

Use age-appropriate scales: FLACC (face, legs, activity, cry, consolability); Faces scale; numeric scale for children who understand numbers.
Pain behaviors: crying, withdrawal, irritability, reduced activity, changes in sleep or feeding, guarding, increased heart rate.
A quiet child is not necessarily pain-free.

195 Pediatric Dehydration

Signs: dry mucous membranes, reduced tears, sunken eyes, sunken fontanel (in infants), reduced urine output, tachycardia, delayed capillary refill, weight loss, lethargy. Daily weight is the most reliable indicator.
Mild to moderate: use oral rehydration solution in small, frequent amounts. Avoid plain water as the only replacement, soda, undiluted juice, or sports drinks in young children.
Severe dehydration: weak pulses, markedly delayed capillary refill, altered consciousness, minimal urine output, hypotension — requires IV isotonic fluid resuscitation.

196 Pediatric Medication Safety

Safety rules: calculate doses in kilograms; verify maximum safe dose; use accurate measuring devices; never call medication “candy”; do not mix medication into a large amount of essential food; direct liquid toward side of infant’s mouth; do not force medication while child is lying flat; double-check high-alert medications.
Convert pounds to kilograms: weight in lb ÷ 2.2 = weight in kg

197 Croup

Croup (viral upper-airway inflammation with subglottic swelling): barking cough, inspiratory stridor, hoarseness, symptoms worse at night.
Care: keep the child calm; minimize agitation; administer corticosteroids; administer nebulized epinephrine for moderate or severe symptoms; monitor for increasing respiratory distress. Do not force a throat examination.

198 Epiglottitis

Epiglottitis — rapid epiglottic swelling that may obstruct the airway — findings: high fever, severe sore throat, drooling, difficulty swallowing, muffled voice, tripod positioning, stridor. Priority actions:
  • Keep the child calm; do not separate unnecessarily from caregiver
  • Do not inspect the throat with a tongue blade
  • Do not obtain a throat culture before the airway is secured
  • Keep emergency airway equipment available
  • Prepare for controlled intubation
  • Administer antibiotics; do not place child flat

199 Bronchiolitis

Bronchiolitis (viral small-airway inflammation, commonly RSV): wheezing, cough, tachypnea, retractions, poor feeding, apnea in young infants. Treatment is mainly supportive: nasal suctioning, hydration, oxygen when needed, respiratory monitoring.
Monitor feeding carefully — infants breathe primarily through the nose and may tire quickly during feeds.

200 Pediatric Asthma

Severe asthma signs: silent chest, inability to speak, severe retractions, exhaustion, cyanosis, reduced consciousness, falling respiratory rate despite distress. A silent chest is more dangerous than loud wheezing — it suggests minimal airflow.
Use: short-acting bronchodilator for acute symptoms; corticosteroids to reduce inflammation; oxygen when needed; peak-flow monitoring in children able to perform it.

201 Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis (inherited disorder causing abnormally thick secretions): recurrent lung infections, thick mucus, poor weight gain, bulky greasy stool, salty-tasting skin, malabsorption.
Nursing care: airway-clearance therapy; high-calorie high-protein nutrition; pancreatic-enzyme replacement; fat-soluble vitamins; increased salt when prescribed; infection prevention. Give pancreatic enzymes with every meal and snack, immediately before or with food, without crushing enteric-coated beads. Do not mix into hot food.

203 Tetralogy of Fallot

Tetralogy of Fallot involves four structural abnormalities reducing pulmonary blood flow. May cause hypercyanotic “tet spells.”
During a tet spell:
  • Place child in knee-chest position (increases systemic vascular resistance, reduces right-to-left shunting)
  • Administer oxygen; keep child calm
  • Administer morphine or other prescribed medication
  • Notify the provider
Older children may instinctively squat during cyanotic episodes.

204 Kawasaki Disease

Kawasaki disease (inflammatory childhood illness affecting medium-sized vessels): fever lasting several days, red eyes without drainage, strawberry tongue, red cracked lips, rash, swollen hands and feet, enlarged cervical lymph node, irritability.
Major complication: coronary artery aneurysm.
Treatment: IV immune globulin and aspirin.
Aspirin is generally avoided in children (Reye syndrome risk), but Kawasaki disease is an important exception under provider supervision.

205 Rheumatic Fever

Rheumatic fever (inflammatory complication following inadequately treated group A strep infection) may affect: heart, joints, skin, nervous system. Findings: migratory joint pain, carditis, chorea, rash, subcutaneous nodules. Prevent by promptly treating streptococcal pharyngitis and completing antibiotics.

Febrile seizure — usually self-limited. During seizure: protect from injury; place on side; do not restrain; do not put anything in mouth; time the seizure. Seek emergency care for prolonged seizure, breathing difficulty, repeated seizure, or abnormal recovery.

208 Child Abuse and Neglect

Possible warning signs: injury inconsistent with history; delay in seeking treatment; injuries in different stages of healing; patterned burns or bruises; injuries uncommon for developmental age; fearful behavior around caregiver; repeated unexplained injuries.
Nursing responsibilities: ensure immediate safety; treat injuries; interview caregivers and child separately when appropriate; document objective findings and exact statements; report suspicion per mandatory-reporting law. Do not confront or investigate beyond the nursing role. Proof is not required before making a mandated report — reasonable suspicion is sufficient.

209 Pediatric Immunization Principles

General NCLEX principles: mild illness is usually not a reason to delay vaccination; inactivated vaccines cannot cause the disease they prevent; live vaccines are generally avoided during pregnancy and severe immunosuppression; do not restart a series merely because doses were delayed; observe for anaphylaxis after vaccination; previous severe allergic reaction to a vaccine component is a major contraindication. Use the current official immunization schedule — recommendations may change.

210 Fracture Assessment

The 6 Ps of neurovascular assessment:
  • Pain — especially severe, increasing, or unrelieved by medication
  • Pallor
  • Pulse
  • Paresthesia
  • Paralysis
  • Poikilothermia — inability of extremity to regulate temperature (coolness) suggests impaired circulation
Compare both extremities.

211 Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome — dangerous pressure buildup within a closed muscle compartment — is an emergency.
Early findings: severe pain out of proportion to injury, pain with passive movement, paresthesia, tight swollen compartment.
Late findings: pallor, paralysis, absent pulse (a pulse may still be present early).
Actions: notify provider immediately; keep extremity at heart level; loosen restrictive dressings as prescribed; prepare for fasciotomy.
Do not: elevate far above heart; apply ice when compartment syndrome is suspected; delay treatment while waiting for pulse loss.

212 Cast Care

Immediately after cast application: support a wet plaster cast with palms (not fingertips); allow full drying; keep uncovered while drying; assess circulation and sensation; elevate during initial swelling period as prescribed.
Teach: keep cast dry; do not insert objects inside; do not apply powders or lotions under cast; report hot spots, foul odor, drainage, severe pain, numbness, or tightness; move exposed fingers or toes.
A “window” should not be cut without a prescription — it can create pressure points.

213 Traction

Maintain: correct body alignment, unobstructed ropes, weights hanging freely, pulleys functioning correctly, countertraction, skin integrity, neurovascular status. Traction must remain continuous unless specifically prescribed otherwise.
Do not: remove weights without a prescription; allow weights to rest on the floor; place knots against pulleys; release traction during routine repositioning.
Skeletal traction pin-site care: report purulent drainage, increasing redness, fever, loose pins, severe pain.

214 Hip Replacement Precautions

After posterior hip replacement, avoid: hip flexion beyond 90°, crossing the legs, internal rotation, low chairs, bending toward the feet. Use: abduction pillow, elevated toilet seat, firm chair, assistive devices.
Report immediately: sudden severe hip pain, shortened leg, abnormal leg rotation, inability to move the leg — may indicate dislocation.

215 Amputation Care

Monitor: bleeding, infection, residual-limb skin, contracture risk, phantom sensations, emotional adjustment. Prevent contractures: avoid prolonged flexion; encourage prescribed prone positioning; perform ROM exercises; avoid pillows beneath residual limb for prolonged periods.
Phantom-limb pain (pain perceived in the missing body part) is real and requires treatment.

216 Osteoporosis

Often no symptoms until a fracture occurs.
Prevention and treatment: weight-bearing exercise; adequate calcium and vitamin D; fall prevention; smoking cessation; limit alcohol; prescribed medication.
Bisphosphonate teaching (e.g., alendronate):
  • Take first thing in the morning with a full glass of plain water
  • Remain upright for at least 30 minutes
  • Do not eat or take other medications immediately afterward
  • Report difficulty swallowing or severe heartburn (esophageal irritation)

222 Burns — Electrical and Chemical

Immediate priorities (all burns): stop burning process; assess airway; support breathing and circulation; remove jewelry and constrictive items; cover with clean dry material; prevent hypothermia; manage pain; begin fluid resuscitation; monitor urine output. Inhalation injury signs: facial burns, singed nasal hair, soot, hoarseness, stridor, carbonaceous sputum, enclosed-space fire. Airway swelling can progress rapidly; early intubation may be required.
Electrical Burns: possible complications include cardiac dysrhythmias, deep internal injury, kidney injury from muscle breakdown (myoglobinuria), and fractures. The external wound may appear small despite severe internal damage.
Chemical Burns: remove contaminated clothing; brush off dry chemicals before irrigation; irrigate with large amounts of water unless the chemical requires another protocol; protect healthcare personnel from exposure.

223 Cancer Warning Signs

Possible warning signs: unexplained weight loss, persistent fatigue, unusual bleeding, nonhealing sore, change in bowel or bladder habits, persistent cough, difficulty swallowing, new or changing mole, unexplained lump. These require evaluation but do not automatically indicate cancer.

224 Chemotherapy Safety

Chemotherapy may affect: bone marrow, GI lining, hair follicles, reproductive cells, and organ function.
Common complications: neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia, nausea, mucositis, diarrhea or constipation, fatigue, alopecia, infertility, organ toxicity.
Handle hazardous medications and body fluids according to protective-equipment and disposal protocols.

225 Neutropenia

Teach the client to: perform frequent hand hygiene; avoid sick contacts; avoid crowds when advised; use safe food preparation; avoid raw or undercooked meat and eggs; monitor temperature; report fever immediately; avoid rectal temperatures and suppositories; maintain oral care; protect skin from cuts.
Infection during cancer treatment can become life-threatening rapidly. Report fever, chills, cough, mouth sores, catheter redness, urinary symptoms, or diarrhea promptly.

226 Thrombocytopenia in Cancer

Bleeding precautions: soft toothbrush; electric razor; avoid aspirin and ibuprofen unless approved; avoid IM injections; avoid rectal procedures; apply prolonged pressure after needle sticks; prevent falls and cuts; monitor stool, urine, gums, and neurological status.
Report: uncontrolled bleeding, severe headache, confusion, vision changes, black stool, red urine, vomiting blood.

227 Tumor Lysis Syndrome

Tumor lysis syndrome — rapid release of intracellular contents after cancer-cell destruction — may cause:
  • Hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, hyperuricemia
  • Acute kidney injury, dysrhythmias, seizures
Highest risk: large tumor burden, highly treatment-sensitive cancers, initiation of chemotherapy.
Prevention and treatment: aggressive hydration; allopurinol (reduces new uric-acid formation — does not remove existing uric acid); rasburicase (rapidly breaks down uric acid); cardiac monitoring; frequent lab monitoring; dialysis if severe.

228 Superior Vena Cava Syndrome

SVC syndrome (obstruction of blood flow through the SVC): facial/neck swelling, upper-extremity edema, distended neck and chest veins, dyspnea, headache, cyanosis, difficulty swallowing.
Actions: elevate head of bed; administer oxygen; notify provider urgently; avoid upper-extremity IV access when possible; prepare for radiation, chemotherapy, steroids, or stenting.

229 Spinal-Cord Compression from Cancer

Oncological emergency. Findings: new back pain, limb weakness, numbness, difficulty walking, bowel or bladder dysfunction.
Notify the provider immediately and prepare for corticosteroids, imaging, radiation, or surgery.

230 Hypercalcemia of Malignancy

Findings: confusion, weakness, constipation, nausea, polyuria, dehydration, dysrhythmias.
Treatment: IV fluids, bisphosphonates, calcitonin, treatment of the cancer. Encourage mobility and hydration when not contraindicated.

231 Radiation Therapy

External radiation — the client is not radioactive after treatment.
Skin care: wash gently; pat dry; do not remove treatment markings; avoid lotions, powders, deodorants, or heat unless approved; protect from sunlight; wear loose clothing.
Internal radiation (brachytherapy): limit time near client; maximize distance; use shielding; private room; radiation warning sign; dosimeter; limit visitors; restrict pregnant visitors and young children; cluster care.
If a sealed source becomes dislodged: do not touch it directly; use long-handled forceps; place in designated lead container; notify radiation safety personnel.

235 Systemic Lupus Erythematosus

SLE (chronic autoimmune disease) may cause: fatigue, joint pain, butterfly-shaped facial rash, photosensitivity, kidney involvement, blood-cell abnormalities, neurological symptoms.
Teaching: balance rest and activity; avoid excessive sun exposure; use sunscreen; report infection; take medications consistently; avoid smoking; monitor kidney function. Immunosuppressive medications increase infection risk.

236 HIV and AIDS

HIV transmission occurs through: blood, semen, vaginal and rectal fluids, breast milk, perinatal exposure.
HIV is not transmitted through: hugging, sharing dishes, toilets, casual contact, or insect bites.
Antiretroviral therapy (ART) should be taken consistently — preserves immune function, reduces opportunistic infections, reduces transmission, improves survival. Do not stop ART abruptly without medical direction.
Use Standard Precautions for all clients. No special isolation is required solely because a client has HIV.

245 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder

Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD; recurring unwanted thoughts and repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety) may involve contamination fears, checking, counting, repeated washing, repeated reassurance-seeking, and intrusive thoughts.

Nursing Principles

  • Do not abruptly prevent a ritual during severe anxiety.
  • Allow reasonable time for rituals initially.
  • Gradually establish limits.
  • Do not participate extensively in the ritual.
  • Encourage alternative coping strategies.
  • Focus on the anxiety rather than debating whether the obsession is logical.
  • Ensure that rituals do not prevent nutrition, hygiene, sleep, or treatment.

246 Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD; persistent trauma-related symptoms after exposure to a traumatic event) may include intrusive memories, nightmares, avoidance, hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, emotional numbness, sleep disturbance, and irritability.

Nursing Care

  • Establish safety and trust.
  • Avoid forcing detailed discussion of the trauma.
  • Allow the client to control the pace.
  • Teach grounding techniques.
  • Identify triggers.
  • Encourage trauma-focused treatment.
  • Assess for depression, substance use, and suicide risk.

248 Depression

Major depressive disorder (persistent depressed mood or loss of interest associated with impaired functioning) may cause sadness, loss of pleasure, fatigue, sleep changes, appetite changes, poor concentration, guilt, hopelessness, psychomotor slowing or agitation, and suicidal thoughts.

Nursing Care

  • Assess suicide risk directly.
  • Offer simple choices.
  • Assist with hygiene when needed.
  • Provide small, frequent, high-calorie meals when intake is poor.
  • Schedule important activities when energy is highest.
  • Avoid overwhelming the client.
  • Recognize small accomplishments.
  • Do not demand cheerfulness.
  • Encourage gradual activity.

Important NCLEX Pattern

Energy may improve before mood and hopelessness improve after treatment begins. This can increase the ability to act on suicidal thoughts. Continue close safety monitoring.

249 Suicide Assessment

Ask Directly

  • “Are you thinking about killing yourself?”
  • “Do you have a plan?”
  • “What method would you use?”
  • “Do you have access to it?”
  • “When would you do it?”
  • “Have you attempted suicide before?”
  • “What has stopped you so far?”

Asking directly does not create suicidal thoughts.

Highest Risk

Risk is especially urgent when the client has: current suicidal thoughts, a detailed plan, access to a lethal method, a selected time, previous attempts, command hallucinations, severe hopelessness, substance intoxication, a recent major loss, sudden unexplained calmness after severe depression, giving away possessions, or a farewell message.

Nursing Actions for Imminent Risk

  • Stay with the client.
  • Initiate the required observation level.
  • Remove dangerous objects.
  • Search belongings according to policy.
  • Notify the provider and treatment team.
  • Arrange emergency psychiatric evaluation.
  • Document statements exactly.
  • Develop a safety plan when clinically appropriate.

A “no-suicide contract” is not a substitute for assessment, observation, removal of lethal means, or a collaborative safety plan. NIMH guidance specifically distinguishes a safety plan from an ineffective “safety contract.”

250 Mania

Mania (a period of abnormally elevated or irritable mood with increased energy and impaired judgment) may include minimal sleep, rapid speech, flight of ideas, grandiosity, distractibility, impulsivity, hypersexuality, excessive spending, poor nutrition, and agitation.

Nursing Priorities

  • Reduce stimulation.
  • Provide a structured environment.
  • Set clear, consistent limits.
  • Use short directions.
  • Avoid power struggles.
  • Redirect inappropriate behavior.
  • Protect from financial, sexual, and physical risk.
  • Monitor sleep, hydration, and nutrition.
  • Offer high-calorie finger foods.
  • Provide fluids that can be consumed while moving.
  • Encourage rest periods.
  • Do not engage in lengthy group activities during severe mania.

251 Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia (a chronic psychotic disorder affecting thought, perception, emotion, and functioning) may cause positive and negative symptoms.

CategoryDescriptionExamples
Positive Symptoms Symptoms added to normal functioning Hallucinations · Delusions · Disorganized speech · Disorganized behavior
Negative Symptoms Loss or reduction of normal functioning Flat affect · Reduced speech · Lack of motivation · Social withdrawal · Reduced pleasure

Hallucinations

Hallucination (a sensory perception without an external stimulus) may involve hearing, seeing, smelling, tasting, or feeling something.

Nursing response: Assess the content. Ask whether the hallucination gives commands. Assess danger to self or others. Present reality briefly. Focus on the client’s feelings. Redirect toward reality-based activity.

Example: “I understand that you hear voices. I do not hear them. What are they telling you?”

Do not: Pretend to hear the voice · Argue · Whisper near the client · Touch the client unexpectedly.

Delusions

Delusion (a fixed false belief that is not changed by evidence) may be persecutory, grandiose, somatic, or referential.

Nursing response: Do not argue. Do not reinforce the belief. Present reality briefly. Focus on feelings. Redirect to concrete activity.

Example: “That sounds frightening. I do not see evidence that anyone is trying to harm you. You are safe here.”

258 Electroconvulsive Therapy

Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT; treatment using controlled electrical stimulation under anesthesia to produce a therapeutic seizure) may be used for severe depression, severe suicidal depression, catatonia, treatment-resistant mood disorders, and selected severe manic or psychotic conditions.

Before ECT

  • Obtain informed consent.
  • Maintain nothing-by-mouth status.
  • Remove dentures, glasses, hearing aids, and hair accessories.
  • Obtain baseline vital signs.
  • Administer prescribed medications.
  • Have emergency equipment available.

During ECT

  • The client receives short-acting anesthesia.
  • A muscle relaxant reduces physical movement.
  • Oxygenation and cardiac status are monitored.

After ECT

  • Position on the side until awake.
  • Monitor airway and vital signs.
  • Reorient frequently.
  • Assess memory and confusion.
  • Maintain fall precautions.

Temporary confusion, headache, muscle soreness, and memory impairment may occur. NIMH describes ECT as a procedure performed with general anesthesia and an intravenous muscle relaxant.

259 Alcohol Withdrawal

Alcohol withdrawal may cause tremors, anxiety, sweating, nausea, insomnia, tachycardia, hypertension, hallucinations, and seizures.

Delirium Tremens

Delirium tremens (severe alcohol withdrawal with delirium and autonomic instability) may cause severe confusion, agitation, hallucinations, fever, tachycardia, hypertension, and seizures. Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening. Benzodiazepines are the primary medication class used to control symptoms and prevent seizures and delirium tremens.

Nursing Care

  • Use seizure precautions.
  • Monitor vital signs.
  • Reduce stimulation.
  • Administer benzodiazepines according to protocol.
  • Administer thiamine before or with glucose when prescribed.
  • Replace fluids and electrolytes.
  • Assess for injury, infection, and gastrointestinal bleeding.

270 Leadership and Chain of Command

Use the Chain of Command When

  • A prescription appears unsafe.
  • A provider does not respond to a significant change.
  • A concern remains unresolved.
  • Staffing creates immediate danger.
  • A client’s rights are threatened.

Sequence Commonly Includes

  1. Clarify with the person involved.
  2. Notify the charge nurse.
  3. Notify the nursing supervisor.
  4. Notify the provider’s supervisor or medical chain.
  5. Continue escalating according to policy until safety is addressed.

Do not carry out a prescription that the nurse believes will cause harm merely because a provider prescribed it.

278 Dosage Calculation Basics

Desired Over Have

Dose to give = Desired dose ÷ Dose on hand × Quantity

Example: Prescription: 500 mg · Available: 250 mg per tablet

500 ÷ 250 × 1 = 2 tablets

279 Weight Conversion

Kilograms = pounds ÷ 2.2

Example: A client weighs 154 lb. → 154 ÷ 2.2 = 70 kg

Do not calculate weight-based medication doses using pounds.

280 Milligram and Gram Conversion

  • 1 g = 1,000 mg
  • 1 mg = 1,000 mcg
  • 1 L = 1,000 mL
  • 1 kg = 1,000 g

To convert a larger unit to a smaller unit, multiply. To convert a smaller unit to a larger unit, divide.

281 Oral Liquid Calculation

Prescription: 375 mg · Available: 250 mg per 5 mL

375 ÷ 250 × 5 = 7.5 mL

Always include the unit in the final answer.

282 Intravenous Pump Rate

mL/hour = total volume in mL ÷ time in hours

Example: 1,000 mL over 8 hours → 1,000 ÷ 8 = 125 mL/hour

283 Gravity Infusion Rate

Drops/minute = volume in mL × drop factor ÷ time in minutes

Example: 500 mL over 4 hours with tubing delivering 15 drops/mL:

4 hours × 60 = 240 minutes → 500 × 15 ÷ 240 = 31.25

Round to: 31 drops/minute. Drops are counted as whole numbers.

284 Medication Infusion Rate

Prescription: 800 units/hour · Available: 25,000 units in 500 mL

First calculate units per mL: 25,000 ÷ 500 = 50 units/mL

Then: 800 ÷ 50 = 16 mL/hour

285 mg/kg/day Calculations

Prescription: 30 mg/kg/day divided into three equal doses · Child weighs: 44 lb

Step 1: Convert weight. 44 ÷ 2.2 = 20 kg

Step 2: Calculate daily dose. 30 mg × 20 kg = 600 mg/day

Step 3: Divide doses. 600 ÷ 3 = 200 mg per dose

Always determine whether the prescription is mg/kg/dose, mg/kg/day, or a total daily dose divided into several doses.

286 Safe-Dose Range

Safe range: 10–15 mg/kg/dose · Child weighs: 20 kg

Minimum safe dose: 10 × 20 = 200 mg · Maximum safe dose: 15 × 20 = 300 mg

A prescribed dose of 250 mg is within the safe range. A prescribed dose of 350 mg should be clarified.

287 Insulin and Heparin Calculations

For Concentrated Medications

  • Verify the concentration carefully.
  • Use the correct syringe.
  • Perform an independent double-check when required.
  • Confirm the pump setting.
  • Reassess relevant laboratory values.

Never confuse: units with milliliters · U-100 insulin with other concentrations · heparin units/mL with the total units in the bag.

  • Do not use a trailing zero. Correct: 5 mg. Incorrect: 5.0 mg.

292 Final NCLEX Priority Formula

When Unsure, Ask

  • Is there an immediate threat? Airway? Breathing? Circulation? Severe bleeding? Neurological deterioration? Suicide? Seizure? Severe medication reaction?
  • Is the problem actual or only a risk? Actual generally comes first.
  • Is the finding new or expected? New and unexpected generally comes first.
  • Is the client stable or unstable? Unstable comes first.
  • Should I assess or act? Assess when the situation is unclear and the client is stable. Act when the emergency is obvious.
  • Can the task be delegated? Do not delegate assessment, teaching, evaluation, judgment, or unstable care.
  • What causes the greatest harm if delayed? Choose the action that prevents death or irreversible injury.

294 Test-Day Strategy

Before Selecting an Answer

  • Read the final sentence of the question carefully.
  • Identify whether it asks for first, priority, expected, or incorrect.
  • Identify whether the client is stable or unstable.
  • Identify the main clinical problem.
  • Eliminate unsafe options.
  • Eliminate options outside the nursing role.
  • Apply airway, breathing, circulation, safety, and acute-versus-chronic principles.
  • Select the most direct action addressing the problem.
  • Do not add information not present in the question.
  • Do not change an answer only because another option sounds more complicated.

For Select All That Apply

  • Evaluate each option independently.
  • Do not search for a specific number of correct answers.
  • Select only options that directly answer the question.

For Case Studies

  • Recognize cues.
  • Analyze cues.
  • Prioritize the likely problem.
  • Generate appropriate solutions.
  • Take the safest action.
  • Evaluate whether the client improved.

The NCLEX is testing whether the entry-level registered nurse can provide safe care—not whether the candidate can select the most advanced or physician-level intervention.
