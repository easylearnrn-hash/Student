Intussusception

Bowel-Within-Bowel Obstruction

1. Definition

  • One part of the intestine slides/telescopes into another → bowel obstruction
  • Cuts off blood supply → ischemia → necrosis if untreated
  • Most common in infants and young children 3 months–6 years
💡 Think: intestine folding into itself like a telescope collapsing

2. Classic Symptom Triad

Currant Jelly Stools

Bloody + mucus stool — from intestinal ischemia and mucosal sloughing

⚠️ Hallmark NCLEX finding!

Severe Intermittent Abdominal Pain

Child draws knees to chest during pain episodes, then appears normal between episodes

Palpable Sausage-Shaped Mass

Typically felt in the right lower quadrant (RLQ)

3. Additional Symptoms

  • Vomiting (may be bilious)
  • Abdominal distension
  • Lethargy (child becomes quiet/limp between pain episodes)
  • Fever (if bowel necrosis or perforation occurs)

4. Diagnosis

MethodDetails
UltrasoundFirst-line imaging; shows "target sign" or "doughnut sign"
Contrast EnemaBoth diagnoses AND treats (can reduce intussusception)
Physical ExamSausage-shaped mass RLQ, Dance's sign (empty RLQ)

5. Treatment

First-Line (Nonsurgical)

✅ Air or Barium Enema — reduces intussusception non-surgically
Both diagnoses AND treats simultaneously!

Surgical (If Enema Fails or Complications)

  • Surgery if enema is unsuccessful
  • Surgery if bowel perforation or necrosis is suspected
  • Bowel resection if necrotic segment is present

6. Critical Warning Sign

PAIN SUDDENLY STOPS + CHILD BECOMES LETHARGIC

If the intermittent pain episodes suddenly stop and the child becomes quiet/lethargic — this is NOT improvement.

This may indicate bowel perforation or worsening necrosis → Immediate surgical emergency!

🚨 Pain stopping + lethargy = perforation suspected — call surgeon immediately!

7. Nursing Care

  • Assess pain pattern — note frequency, duration, and character of episodes
  • Monitor stools — report currant jelly appearance immediately
  • Prepare child and family for enema procedure or surgery
  • Monitor vital signs — watch for signs of perforation (fever, tachycardia, ↑ WBC)
  • NPO status for procedure/surgery preparation
  • IV fluids for hydration
  • Post-procedure: monitor bowel sounds, first normal stool, tolerance of feeds

8. NCLEX Must-Know Summary

✅ Currant jelly stool = hallmark sign
✅ Child draws knees to chest during pain
✅ Sausage-shaped mass in RLQ
✅ First-line = air/barium enema (diagnoses + treats)
⚠️ Pain stops + lethargy = perforation = emergency!
⚠️ Surgery if enema fails or perforation/necrosis
⚠️ Never ignore sudden calm — it's a red flag!
