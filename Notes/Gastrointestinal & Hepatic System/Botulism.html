Botulism

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · note_gi_13

Definition

A rare but serious paralytic illness caused by a toxin produced by Clostridium botulinum.

The toxin blocks acetylcholine (ACh) release at neuromuscular junctions → flaccid (descending) paralysis.

Key: muscles cannot contract because the nerve signal is never transmitted.

Causes & Transmission

TypeMechanismHigh-Yield Detail
FoodborneIngestion of preformed toxin in improperly canned/preserved foodsAnaerobic environment → home-canned vegetables, fermented fish
Infant (most common in US)Ingestion of spores (e.g., honey) → colonize intestines → release toxinNo honey under 12 months
WoundSpores enter puncture wound or IV drug use siteIV drug users; treat with penicillin/metronidazole
IatrogenicOverdose or improper Botox injectionRare; mimics botulism

Pathophysiology

Toxin inhibits ACh release → descending flaccid paralysis:

  • Starts in cranial nerves (face, eyes, throat)
  • Progresses to arms → legs → diaphragm
  • Respiratory failure if diaphragm involved
⚠️ NCLEX: No fever and normal mental status — distinguishes botulism from other neurological infections.

Clinical Manifestations

Adults

  • Diplopia (double vision)
  • Ptosis (drooping eyelids)
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
  • Dysarthria (slurred speech)
  • Dry mouth
  • Descending paralysis (face → down)
  • Respiratory failure
  • No fever · Alert mental status

Infants — "Floppy Baby Syndrome"

  • Constipation — first sign
  • Poor feeding
  • Weak cry
  • Generalized hypotonia (reduced tone)
  • Lethargy
  • Loss of head control
Emergency: Floppy + poor feeding + weak cry = suspect botulism

Diagnosis

  • Clinical signs + exposure history (often sufficient to start treatment)
  • Confirmed by detecting toxin in serum, stool, or food samples
  • Infant botulism: stool culture for C. botulinum spores
🚨 Do not delay antitoxin while waiting for lab confirmation!

Treatment

Antitoxins

PatientAntitoxin
Adults & older childrenHeptavalent Botulinum Antitoxin (HBAT)
InfantsBotulism Immune Globulin (BabyBIG)

Supportive Care

  • Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure → #1 priority
  • Nutritional support (NG tube or TPN)
  • Wound botulism: IV penicillin or metronidazole
⛔ Avoid aminoglycosides — they worsen neuromuscular blockade!
⛔ No antibiotics for foodborne botulism in adults — may increase toxin release from lysed bacteria.

Prevention

  • Boil home-canned foods before eating (kills toxin, not spores)
  • Never give honey to infants under 12 months
  • Proper wound care and sterile injection techniques

⚡ NCLEX Priorities

  • Cranial nerve dysfunction + no fever = suspect botulism
  • Respiratory status = #1 priority → monitor O₂, ABGs, prepare for intubation
  • Infants: constipation is the first sign; ask about honey exposure
  • Do not delay antitoxin for lab confirmation
  • No aminoglycosides · No antibiotics for foodborne form (adults)
Classic NCLEX Q: 3-month-old with constipation, weak cry, and poor feeding → Ask: "Has the baby consumed honey?"
