Ulcerative Colitis

Chronic Inflammatory Bowel Disease — Colon & Rectum

1. What is Ulcerative Colitis?

  • Chronic autoimmune inflammatory bowel disease (IBD)
  • Causes inflammation and ulcers in the lining of the colon and rectum
  • Occurs in flares (active disease) and remissions (symptom-free)
  • Starts at the rectum and spreads upward — continuous (not patchy)
  • More common in young adults aged 15–35 years
💡 UC only affects the colon and rectum — Crohn's can affect anywhere from mouth to anus!

2. Pathophysiology

  1. Immune system attacks the colon's lining
  2. Chronic inflammation → ulcers and bleeding
  3. Colon loses ability to absorb water → diarrhea
  4. Repeated inflammation → scarring → narrowed, stiff colon → Toxic Megacolon in severe cases

3. Symptoms

GI Symptoms

  • 🩸 Frequent bloody diarrhea — hallmark! (10–20 stools/day in severe cases)
  • 😣 Abdominal cramping (worse before bowel movements)
  • 🚽 Urgency to defecate
  • Mucus in stool

Systemic Symptoms

  • ⚖️ Weight loss (poor absorption)
  • 😴 Fatigue & weakness
  • 🌡️ Fever (during severe flares)
  • Anemia (from blood loss)
🚨 Bloody diarrhea = key symptom of Ulcerative Colitis!

4. Complications

  • ⚠️ Toxic Megacolon — emergency! Colon becomes overstretched → may rupture
  • ⚠️ Severe GI bleeding
  • ⚠️ Colon Cancer — risk increases after 10+ years of UC
  • ⚠️ Malnutrition
  • ⚠️ Anemia
🚨 Toxic Megacolon signs: severe abdominal pain, distension, fever, NO bowel movements — EMERGENCY!
💡 Patients with UC need regular colonoscopies to screen for colon cancer!

5. Diagnosis

TestWhat It Detects
Colonoscopy with BiopsyGold standard! Confirms UC — shows continuous inflammation in the colon
Stool SampleRules out infections (bacterial, viral, parasitic)
CBCAnemia (↓ RBCs), elevated WBCs (inflammation)
ESR & CRPElevated inflammatory markers
⚠️ Colonoscopy should be avoided during severe flares — risk of perforation!

6. Treatment — Goal: Reduce Inflammation & Prevent Flares

A. Medications

Drug ClassExamplesWhat It DoesNCLEX Note
Aminosalicylates (first-line)Sulfasalazine, MesalamineReduces colon inflammationTake with food; monitor kidney & liver
Corticosteroids (acute flares only)Prednisone, HydrocortisoneSuppresses immune responseShort-term only — osteoporosis, infection risk
ImmunosuppressantsAzathioprine, MethotrexatePrevents attacksMonitor for infections
Biologics (severe cases)Infliximab (Remicade), Adalimumab (Humira)Targets specific inflammation proteinsMonitor for allergic reactions & infections
Antidiarrheals (use caution)Loperamide (Imodium)Reduces diarrheaAvoid in severe flares — risk of toxic megacolon!

B. Diet

✅ Low-fiber diet (reduces colon irritation)
✅ High-calorie, high-protein diet
⚠️ Avoid dairy (lactose worsens symptoms)
⚠️ No high-fiber foods during flares!

C. Surgery

  • Proctocolectomy — removal of entire colon & rectum → permanent cure!
  • Ileostomy — surgical opening for waste to exit through a stoma
✅ Unlike Crohn's Disease, UC CAN be cured with surgery!

8. Nursing Care

  • Monitor for bloody diarrhea — risk of anemia
  • Encourage hydration & proper nutrition
  • Assess for Toxic Megacolon: severe abdominal pain, distension, fever, no bowel movements
  • Promote stress reduction (stress triggers flares)
  • Educate on medication adherence — prevents flares
  • Remind: no antidiarrheals during severe flares

9. NCLEX Must-Know Summary
