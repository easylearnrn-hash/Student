Phenylketonuria (PKU)

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · note_gi_23

Definition

Phenylketonuria (PKU) is an autosomal recessive genetic disorder in which the body cannot metabolize phenylalanine (Phe) — an essential amino acid found in protein-rich foods.

  • Deficient enzyme: Phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH)
  • Phenylalanine accumulates in the blood and brain → neurotoxic
  • If untreated: severe intellectual disability, seizures, behavioral problems
Maintain phenylalanine blood levels at 2–6 mg/dL to prevent neurologic damage.

Newborn Screening

  • Heel stick blood test — mandatory in the U.S. for all newborns
  • First exposure to phenylalanine: breast milk (high in Phe) and formula
  • Early detection is critical — treatment before symptoms = normal development
⚠️ If PKU is untreated:
• Severe intellectual disability
• Seizures
• Behavioral problems
• Musty urine odor (phenylacetate)

Dietary Management

Foods to AVOID

  • Breast milk (first exposure)
  • Dairy products
  • Meat, poultry, fish
  • Eggs
  • Nuts and legumes
  • Aspartame (artificial sweetener — contains phenylalanine)
  • High-protein foods generally
⚠️ Aspartame = phenylalanine — products containing aspartame are labeled "Contains Phenylalanine"

Safe Foods

  • Fruits
  • Vegetables
  • Specially formulated low-phenylalanine products
  • Rice, corn
  • Some cereals (low-protein)
Special formula: Phenyl-Free or similar — provides essential amino acids without phenylalanine

Monitoring & Treatment

ParameterGoal
Phenylalanine blood level2–6 mg/dL
Diet durationStrict diet for life
Monitoring frequencyRegular blood checks — especially important in pregnancy
Growth & developmentRegular checkups for dietary adjustment
Maternal PKU: Women with PKU who become pregnant must maintain strict phenylalanine control throughout pregnancy — high Phe levels cause fetal brain damage.

Important Nursing Notes

  • Teach family about strict lifelong dietary restrictions
  • Read all food labels — watch for "Contains Phenylalanine" (aspartame)
  • Provide dietitian referral for meal planning
  • Regular monitoring of blood Phe levels (goal: 2–6 mg/dL)
  • Support groups and psychological support for patient/family
  • Main risk is brain damage (not just acidosis)
