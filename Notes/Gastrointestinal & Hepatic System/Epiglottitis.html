Epiglottitis

Life-threatening airway emergency — epiglottis swells and can cause complete upper airway obstruction

Airway Emergency No Tongue Blade Tripod Position Hib Vaccine Prevention

1. What Is Epiglottitis?

  • Inflammation of the epiglottis (flap-like cartilage behind the tongue, above the larynx)
  • Epiglottis closes over the trachea during swallowing; opens during breathing
  • When inflamed → swells → blocks airflow → severe respiratory distress
  • More common in children, but adults can be affected

2. Causes

Infectious (Most Common)

  • Haemophilus influenzae type B (Hib) — most common (preventable with Hib vaccine)
  • Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A Strep)
  • Staphylococcus aureus

Non-Infectious

  • Burns from hot liquids
  • Throat trauma
  • Inhalation of toxic substances

3. Symptoms — Mnemonic: A.I.R. N.U.R.S.E.

A Abnormal Position Tripod position — leaning forward, chin up, mouth open I Increased Temperature High fever R Rapid Onset Sudden, acute symptoms N Nasal Flaring Difficulty breathing U Using Accessory Muscles Chest retractions visible R Retractions Suprasternal, intercostal retractions S Stridor High-pitched inspiratory noise E Enlarged Epiglottis "Thumb sign" on lateral neck X-ray

Also: Drooling (dysphagia), muffled/hoarse voice, irritability and anxiety

🛑 NO COUGH — key distinguishing feature from croup!

4. Diagnosis

  • Lateral neck X-ray → classic "thumb sign" (swollen epiglottis)
  • Laryngoscopy — only in controlled airway settings
  • Blood cultures — identify causative organism
🚫 Do NOT use tongue blades or perform oral exams — can trigger complete airway obstruction due to reflex spasm!

5. Emergency Nursing Interventions

✅ DO THIS
  • Prepare emergency airway equipment
  • Stay with the patient at all times
  • Keep child calm (crying worsens swelling)
  • Allow tripod positioning
  • Administer humidified oxygen as needed
  • Monitor O₂ sat, respiratory effort, LOC
🚫 DO NOT DO THIS
  • No throat swabs
  • No oral temperatures
  • No tongue depressors
  • No oral exams
  • Do not leave the child alone

6. Medical Treatment

  • IV fluids — prevent dehydration
  • IV antibiotics — Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime
  • Antipyretics — Acetaminophen, Ibuprofen
  • Corticosteroids — Dexamethasone to reduce swelling
  • Airway management — may need intubation or tracheostomy (anesthesiologist/ENT involved)

7. Prevention — Hib Vaccine

Most effective prevention strategy. Vaccination schedule:

  • 2 months
  • 4 months
  • 6 months (if applicable)
  • Booster: 12–15 months

9. NCLEX-Focused Tips

🔺 Priority Nursing Action: If epiglottitis is suspected → Notify provider immediately and prepare airway equipment. Do NOT assess the throat!
  • ✅ Initiate droplet precautions for first 24 hours after antibiotics are started
  • ✅ Administer humidified O₂
  • ✅ Keep child calm and allow tripod positioning
🔑 Key Takeaway: Epiglottitis = TRUE airway emergency. High fever + drooling + muffled voice + tripod + NO cough = epiglottitis. Thumb sign on lateral neck X-ray. NEVER use tongue blade. Prepare airway equipment. IV ceftriaxone + dexamethasone. Hib vaccine prevents it.
