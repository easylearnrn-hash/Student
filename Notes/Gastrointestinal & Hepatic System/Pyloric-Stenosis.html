Pyloric Stenosis

Gastric Outlet Obstruction in Infants

1. Definition

  • Thickening of the pylorus causing gastric outlet obstruction
  • Affects infants 2–8 weeks old
  • Food cannot pass from the stomach into the small intestine
💡 Think: young infant (2–8 weeks) — NOT older children, teenagers, or adults!

2. Key Symptoms

Classic GI Signs

  • 🌊 Projectile, non-bilious vomiting after feeding — hallmark!
  • 🫒 Olive-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant
  • 👁️ Visible peristaltic waves across the abdomen
  • 🍽️ Constant hunger (infant feeds eagerly despite vomiting)

Systemic Signs (Dehydration)

  • 🔵 Sunken fontanelle
  • 💧 Dry mouth / decreased tears
  • 🚽 Decreased urine output
  • ⚖️ Weight loss
🚨 Non-bilious (no green) vomiting = obstruction is above the ampulla of Vater (bile can't mix in yet)

3. Electrolyte & Lab Findings

ImbalanceWhy It Happens
Hypokalemia (↓ K⁺)Lost in vomit + kidneys retain H⁺ instead → K⁺ excreted
Hypochloremia (↓ Cl⁻)HCl lost in gastric vomit
Metabolic AlkalosisLoss of HCl → excess HCO₃⁻ (base) accumulates
↑ Hematocrit / ↑ BUNHemoconcentration from dehydration
🚨 Classic triad: hypokalemia + hypochloremia + metabolic alkalosis — think pyloric stenosis!

4. Diagnosis

  • 🔊 Abdominal Ultrasound — first-line, confirms pyloric thickening
  • 🧪 Labs: elevated Hct/BUN, electrolyte imbalances
  • Physical exam: olive-shaped mass in RUQ, visible peristalsis
✅ Ultrasound = gold standard diagnostic tool for pyloric stenosis

5. Management

Pre-operative Stabilization (PRIORITY FIRST)

  • NPO — nothing by mouth
  • IV fluids — correct dehydration and electrolyte imbalances
  • NG tube — decompression if needed (prevents aspiration)
  • Monitor electrolytes: correct K⁺, Cl⁻, and metabolic alkalosis before surgery
💡 Electrolyte correction BEFORE surgery — operating on an alkalotic infant is dangerous!

Surgical Treatment

✂️ Pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure)
Cutting the thickened pyloric muscle lengthwise → relieves obstruction without removing tissue

Post-operative Care

  • Gradual refeeding — start with small volumes, increase slowly
  • Some vomiting is normal post-op (residual swelling)
  • Monitor for persistent vomiting → may indicate incomplete pyloromyotomy
  • Monitor incision site for signs of infection

6. NCLEX Must-Know Summary

✅ Age: 2–8 weeks old — never older
✅ Projectile non-bilious vomiting = hallmark
✅ Olive-shaped RUQ mass + visible peristalsis
✅ Ultrasound = first-line diagnosis
⚠️ Metabolic alkalosis + hypokalemia + hypochloremia
⚠️ Correct electrolytes BEFORE surgery
⚠️ Treatment = Pyloromyotomy (surgical)
⚠️ Some post-op vomiting = normal
