Alcoholism

GI & Nutritional Complications of Chronic Alcohol Use

1. GI Complications

🚨 Acute Pancreatitis — very common in chronic alcohol abusers!
  • Alcohol directly damages pancreatic acinar cells
  • Triggers premature enzyme activation → autodigestion
  • Can progress to chronic pancreatitis with irreversible damage

2. Important Lab Values

Hypomagnesemia (Low Mg)

  • Cause: Poor absorption due to chronic alcohol use
  • Risk: Arrhythmia
📈 EKG finding: Torsades de Pointes
(Polymorphic ventricular tachycardia — deadly if untreated!)

Hypocalcemia (Low Ca²⁺)

  • Symptoms:
    • Hyperactive reflexes
    • Positive Chvostek's Sign — facial twitch on cheek tap
    • Positive Trousseau's Sign — hand spasm on BP cuff inflation
    • Seizures
💡 Low Mg + Low Ca in alcoholic = monitor EKG for Torsades de Pointes!

3. Vitamin Deficiencies in Alcoholism

Magnesium (Mg)
Arrhythmia risk, muscle cramps
Calcium (Ca)
Tetany, seizures, Chvostek's/Trousseau's signs
Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)
Wernicke's encephalopathy — confusion, ataxia, eye movement issues
Vitamin B12 (Cobalamin)
Neuropathy, anemia

4. Wernicke's Encephalopathy

HIGH PRIORITY NCLEX TOPIC

  • Caused by Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) deficiency
  • Classic triad:
    • 🧠 Confusion
    • 🚶 Ataxia (unsteady gait)
    • 👁️ Ophthalmoplegia (abnormal eye movements / nystagmus)
🚨 ALWAYS give IV Thiamine BEFORE glucose in suspected alcoholism!
Giving glucose first depletes thiamine → can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's!
💡 If untreated → Korsakoff Syndrome (irreversible memory loss + confabulation)

5. Nursing Monitoring & Interventions

  • Regularly monitor Mg and Ca levels
  • Monitor EKG for changes — watch for Torsades de Pointes (↑ QT interval)
  • Watch closely for signs of Wernicke's encephalopathy: confusion, ataxia, eye movement abnormalities
  • IV Thiamine BEFORE IV glucose — always!
  • Monitor for seizure activity (low Ca, low Mg)
  • Monitor for signs of pancreatitis: epigastric pain radiating to back, ↑ lipase/amylase

6. Alcohol Withdrawal Timeline

Time After Last DrinkSymptomsSeverity
6–24 hoursTremors, anxiety, nausea, insomniaMild
24–48 hoursSeizures (MOST DANGEROUS early phase), hallucinationsSevere
48–72 hoursDelirium Tremens (DTs) — confusion, hyperthermia, HTN, tachycardia, diaphoresisLife-Threatening
🚨 Delirium Tremens = life-threatening emergency! Treat with benzodiazepines (e.g., lorazepam, diazepam)!

7. NCLEX Must-Know Summary

✅ B1 deficiency → Wernicke's (confusion + ataxia + eye problems)
✅ IV Thiamine BEFORE glucose — always!
✅ Low Mg → Torsades de Pointes on EKG
⚠️ Chvostek's + Trousseau's signs = hypocalcemia
⚠️ DTs at 48–72 hrs = life-threatening — give benzodiazepines
⚠️ Alcohol → pancreatitis, B12 deficiency, neuropathy
