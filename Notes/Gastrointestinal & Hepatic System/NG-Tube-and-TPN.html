NG Tube & TPN

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · Enteral & Parenteral Nutrition

1. Nasogastric (NG) Tube

Definition

  • Flexible tube inserted through the nose → esophagus → stomach
  • Used for: decompression, feeding, medication delivery, gastric lavage

Indications

  • Bowel obstruction / gastric decompression
  • Enteral feeding (nutrients directly into stomach)
  • Medication administration
  • Gastric lavage (e.g., overdose)

Placement Verification (NCLEX Critical)

Must verify placement BEFORE every use!
  • Gold Standard Initial placement → X-ray
  • pH Test Gastric contents = pH < 5 (acidic)
  • Aspirate Green, off-white, or brown color expected
  • ❌ Outdated Air bolus + auscultation alone is no longer best practice

Nursing Interventions

  • Elevate HOB 30–45° during and after feeding (prevents aspiration)
  • Check residual volume before each feeding — hold if > 500 mL
  • Flush with 30 mL water before/after meds & feedings
  • Clamp NG tube after meds to allow absorption

Contraindications

  • Basilar skull fracture (tube could enter brain)
  • Esophageal varices (fragile veins can rupture)

Complications

  • Aspiration (stomach contents into lungs)
  • Tube displacement
  • Nasal erosion
  • Metabolic imbalances

NG tube path: nose → esophagus → stomach

2. Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

Definition

  • TPN = IV administration of nutrients — bypasses the GI tract entirely
  • Delivered via central venous catheter (CVC) — PICC or subclavian line

Contents of TPN

  • Dextrose (glucose)
  • Amino acids (proteins)
  • Lipids (fats)
  • Electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻)
  • Vitamins & trace elements

Indications

  • Non-functioning GI tract (bowel obstruction, short bowel syndrome)
  • Severe malnutrition
  • Prolonged NPO
  • Absorptive disorders (e.g., Crohn's disease)

Administration Guidelines

  • Central line only — high glucose damages peripheral veins
  • Dedicated line — no piggybacking other medications
  • Use a micron filter on the IV line
  • Start/stop gradually to prevent hypo/hyperglycemia

Nursing Responsibilities

  • Monitor blood glucose every 4–6 hours
  • Daily weights
  • Monitor I&O (intake & output)
  • Change TPN bag and tubing every 24 hours
  • Sterile technique for central line dressing changes
  • Watch for CLABSI (central line associated bloodstream infection)

Complications of TPN

  • Infection / sepsis (from central line)
  • Hypoglycemia (if TPN suddenly stopped)
  • Hyperglycemia (especially diabetic patients)
  • Fluid overload (edema, crackles)
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Refeeding syndrome (rapid electrolyte shift in malnourished patients)
