Peritonitis & Sepsis

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · Life-Threatening Emergencies

Peritonitis

What Is It?

Peritonitis is inflammation of the peritoneum (the thin lining of the abdominal cavity) — a life-threatening emergency often caused by infection from a ruptured organ.

Causes

  • Perforated peptic ulcer (hole in stomach/duodenal wall)
  • Ruptured appendix (burst appendix leaking pus)
  • Diverticulum rupture (bulging colon pouch tears)
  • Abdominal trauma
  • Peritoneal dialysis catheter infection
  • Bowel obstruction with perforation
  • Post-op abdominal infection
NCLEX: Sudden severe abdominal pain after perforation = emergency!

Signs & Symptoms

  • Rigid, board-like abdomen (hallmark sign)
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Rebound tenderness (pain increases when pressure is RELEASED)
  • Fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Decreased/absent bowel sounds
  • Abdominal distension

Diagnosis

  • WBC count ↑ (leukocytosis)
  • Blood cultures (identify organism)
  • Abdominal X-ray or CT — free air = perforation
  • Paracentesis — needle aspiration of peritoneal fluid for testing

Treatment

  • NPO (nothing by mouth)
  • IV fluids (correct dehydration & hypotension)
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics
  • Analgesics (pain management)
  • NG tube (decompress bowel)
  • Surgery (repair cause, remove infected material)

Nursing Interventions

  • Monitor vital signs closely (BP, HR, Temp, RR)
  • Keep patient NPO
  • Administer IV fluids & antibiotics
  • Prepare for possible surgery
  • Monitor for signs of sepsis

Sepsis

What Is It?

Sepsis is a systemic, life-threatening response to infection that can lead to organ dysfunction and shock — when the immune system's reaction to an infection spreads throughout the whole body.

Any infection (like peritonitis) can cause sepsis if not treated early.

Signs & Symptoms — S.E.P.S.I.S. Mnemonic

S

E

P
Pale or discolored skin
S

"I feel like I might die"
S

Diagnostic Criteria (qSOFA / SIRS)

  • Temp > 38°C (100.4°F) or < 36°C (96.8°F)
  • HR > 90 bpm
  • RR > 22/min
  • WBC ↑ or ↓
  • Lactic acid ↑ (indicator of poor perfusion)
  • Altered mental status

Septic Shock

Septic shock = severe sepsis + hypotension persisting despite fluids + poor organ perfusion
  • BP stays low despite IV fluid resuscitation
  • Requires vasopressors to maintain BP
  • Lactate > 2 mmol/L despite fluids

Sepsis Bundle — Treatment Within First Hour

  1. Blood cultures — draw BEFORE antibiotics
  2. Broad-spectrum antibiotics — start immediately
  3. IV fluids (NS or LR) — 30 mL/kg
  4. Lactate level — check to assess perfusion
  5. Vasopressors — if fluids don't restore BP

Nursing Priorities for Sepsis

  • Early recognition → continuous vital sign monitoring
  • Administer oxygen (improve perfusion)
  • Start IV access + fluids immediately
  • Monitor urine output (kidney perfusion)
  • Notify provider STAT if sepsis suspected
  • Educate on signs of infection and early treatment
