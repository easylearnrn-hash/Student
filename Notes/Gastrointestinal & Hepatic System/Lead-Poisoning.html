Lead Poisoning

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · Toxicology

What Is Lead Poisoning?

Lead poisoning (plumbism) is a toxic condition caused by lead accumulation in the body.

  • Lead is a heavy metal that damages the brain, nervous system, kidneys, and blood-forming organs
  • Most common in children under 6 — absorb lead more easily, developing nervous systems

Pathophysiology

  • Entry: ingestion (swallowing) or inhalation (dust/fumes)
  • Binds to red blood cells → distributes to soft tissues and bones
  • Interferes with:
    • Heme synthesis → anemia
    • Neurotransmitter function → neurological and behavioral issues
    • Kidney function → damages renal tubules
    • Bone growth → stored in bones for years

Common Sources of Lead Exposure

SourceExplanation / Example
Lead-based paintCommon in homes built before 1978
Contaminated dust or soilOld paint, industrial sites, near highways
Water pipesLead plumbing or solder leaches into drinking water
Imported toys/jewelryMay contain unsafe lead paint
Occupational exposureBattery factories, smelting, construction
Folk remedies & cosmeticsAzarcon, greta — imported products may contain lead

Risk Factors

  • Age under 6 years
  • Living in older housing (pre-1978)
  • Low socioeconomic status
  • Poor nutrition — iron or calcium deficiency increases lead absorption
  • Immigrant/refugee children (possible prior exposure)

Laboratory Diagnosis

TestNCLEX Notes
Blood Lead Level (BLL)Diagnostic test — ≥5 µg/dL in children is concerning
CBCMay show microcytic hypochromic anemia
Peripheral smearBasophilic stippling (blue granules in RBCs) — hallmark finding
Erythrocyte protoporphyrinElevated in chronic exposure
X-rays"Lead lines" at bone growth plates in children

Treatment

InterventionPurpose
Remove source of exposurePrimary prevention — home inspection, lead abatement
Chelation therapyBinds lead for excretion — given if BLL ≥45 µg/dL in children
Chelating agentsSuccimer (DMSA), EDTA, Dimercaprol (BAL)
HydrationPromotes renal excretion
Nutritional supportIron, calcium, Vitamin C — reduce lead absorption

Nursing Priorities (NCLEX Focus)

  1. Identify & remove exposure source — ask about home age, occupation, imported items
  2. Monitor developmental milestones — catch early neuro deficits in children
  3. Administer chelation therapy — ensure adequate hydration before and during
  4. Educate parents — wash hands before eating, wet-mop floors (reduce dust), avoid peeling paint
  5. Nutritional counseling — encourage calcium & iron-rich foods (milk, leafy greens, lean meats)
  6. Follow-up testing — recheck BLL after intervention to ensure decline
  7. Report to public health authorities — lead poisoning is a reportable condition

A
Anemia, abdominal pain

Drops — foot drop, wrist drop from peripheral neuropathy

Complications

  • Encephalopathy → seizures, coma
  • Cognitive impairment (irreversible if severe and prolonged)
  • Hearing loss
  • Kidney damage
  • Behavioral disorders
  • Growth retardation

NCLEX Sample Questions

Q: A 3-year-old has a BLL of 48 µg/dL. Priority?
A: Begin chelation therapy and remove the source of exposure
Q: What food helps reduce lead absorption?
A: Milk (high calcium) and lean meat (iron-rich) — both decrease lead absorption
Q: Correct teaching for parents in an older home?
A: Wet-mop floors instead of sweeping — prevents airborne lead dust

Final NCLEX Takeaways

  • ✅ Most dangerous to children under 6
  • ✅ Screening & prevention are key
  • ✅ Chelation therapy if BLL ≥45 µg/dL in children
  • ✅ Never sweep dust — wet-mop instead
  • ✅ Calcium, iron, Vitamin C reduce absorption
  • ✅ Monitor neurodevelopment and hematologic status
  • ✅ Lead poisoning is a reportable condition
