Key Definitions

TermDefinition
Substance Use DisorderA pattern of substance use causing significant impairment or distress (≥2 of 11 DSM-5 criteria in 12 months)
AddictionCompulsive drug-seeking behavior despite harmful consequences; loss of control
ToleranceNeed for increasing amounts to achieve the same effect
DependencePhysical adaptation — withdrawal symptoms when substance stopped
WithdrawalPhysiologic response to abrupt cessation or dose reduction of a substance
IntoxicationReversible substance-specific syndrome from recent use

Alcohol

Intoxication Signs
  • Slurred speech; unsteady gait
  • Impaired judgment and coordination
  • Disinhibition; euphoria
  • Nausea/vomiting; flushed face
  • Respiratory depression (high BAC)
Withdrawal Timeline
  • 6–12 hours: Tremors, anxiety, N/V, tachycardia, diaphoresis
  • 12–24 hours: Hallucinations (visual, auditory, tactile)
  • 24–48 hours: Seizures — peak risk
  • 48–72 hours: Delirium Tremens (DTs) — life-threatening
Delirium Tremens (DTs) — Medical Emergency
  • Severe autonomic instability: fever, hypertension, tachycardia, diaphoresis
  • Confusion, agitation, hallucinations, seizures
  • Can be fatal if untreated
  • Treatment: Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Diazepam) — PRIORITY
  • Also: IV fluids, thiamine (B1) BEFORE glucose to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy
🚨
NCLEX Alert — Alcohol Withdrawal
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose — prevents Wernicke's encephalopathy
  • Benzodiazepines = drug of choice for alcohol withdrawal
  • CIWA-Ar scale used to assess severity
  • DTs = most severe, life-threatening stage

Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome

SyndromeFeatures
Wernicke's EncephalopathyThiamine (B1) deficiency — Classic triad: Confusion, Ataxia, Ophthalmoplegia (eye movement disorder). Reversible if treated with thiamine.
Korsakoff SyndromeChronic thiamine deficiency — Confabulation (making up memories), severe anterograde amnesia. Often irreversible.

Opioids

Intoxication — "Opioid Triad"
  • Pinpoint pupils (miosis)
  • Respiratory depression
  • Altered consciousness (CNS depression)
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation, slurred speech
  • Treatment: Naloxone (Narcan) — opioid antagonist
Withdrawal (Not Life-Threatening)
  • Flu-like: yawning, rhinorrhea, myalgia
  • Anxiety, restlessness, irritability
  • Dilated pupils (mydriasis)
  • Piloerection ("goose flesh")
  • N/V/D, abdominal cramping
  • Peaks at 36–72 hours; resolves in 1–2 weeks
💡
Opioid Overdose — First Priority

Administer Naloxone (Narcan) IM or IV immediately. Maintain airway; monitor respiratory rate. Short duration of Naloxone — may need repeated doses; watch for re-sedation.

Stimulants (Cocaine, Amphetamines)

Intoxication
  • Euphoria, agitation, hyperactivity
  • Dilated pupils
  • Tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia
  • Paranoia, hallucinations ("formication" — bugs crawling on skin)
  • Risk: MI, stroke, cardiac arrhythmias
Withdrawal ("Crash")
  • Fatigue, lethargy, hypersomnia
  • Increased appetite
  • Dysphoria, depression
  • Intense cravings
  • Not life-threatening; supportive care

Sedatives/Hypnotics/Anxiolytics (Benzodiazepines, Barbiturates)

Intoxication
  • CNS depression, sedation
  • Slurred speech, ataxia, confusion
  • Respiratory depression
  • Anterograde amnesia
Withdrawal — LIFE-THREATENING
  • Tremors, anxiety, diaphoresis
  • Seizures
  • Delirium
  • Very similar to alcohol withdrawal
  • Treatment: Slow taper; another benzodiazepine

Cannabis

Intoxication
  • Euphoria, relaxation, altered time perception
  • Conjunctival injection (red eyes), increased appetite ("munchies")
  • Tachycardia, dry mouth
  • Impaired coordination, memory, judgment
  • Possible: paranoia, anxiety, psychosis (high THC strains)

Pharmacotherapy for Substance Use Disorders

SubstanceMedicationMechanism / Notes
AlcoholDisulfiram (Antabuse)Causes severe reaction when alcohol consumed (flushing, vomiting, palpitations). Patient must be fully sober before starting. Teach to avoid alcohol in ALL forms (mouthwash, sauces).
AlcoholNaltrexoneReduces craving and reward from alcohol. Also used for opioid use disorder.
AlcoholAcamprosateReduces craving; helps maintain abstinence
OpioidMethadoneLong-acting opioid agonist; reduces withdrawal and cravings; dispensed daily at clinic
OpioidBuprenorphine (Subutex/Suboxone)Partial opioid agonist; ceiling effect reduces overdose risk; office-based prescribing
Opioid OverdoseNaloxone (Narcan)Opioid antagonist — reverses respiratory depression; short-acting, may need repeat dosing
💡
Disulfiram (Antabuse) Teaching

Avoid ALL alcohol: mouthwash, cough syrups, cooking wine, vinegars, aftershave. Even small amounts cause a severe disulfiram reaction: flushing, nausea, vomiting, hypotension, tachycardia.

Nursing Interventions — General

  • Non-judgmental, therapeutic approach — addiction is a disease, not a moral failure
  • Monitor vital signs during withdrawal — q1–4h depending on severity
  • Assess using validated scales: CIWA-Ar (alcohol), COWS (opioid)
  • Seizure precautions for alcohol and sedative withdrawal
  • Maintain safety — fall risk, confusion, agitation
  • Provide IV fluids, electrolyte replacement as needed
  • Encourage participation in AA, NA, support groups
  • Screen for co-occurring mental health disorders (depression, PTSD)
  • Respect confidentiality — substance abuse records have extra protections (42 CFR Part 2)

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Alcohol withdrawal seizures: peak at 24–48 hours; DTs at 48–72 hours
  • Thiamine BEFORE glucose in alcohol patients (prevents Wernicke's)
  • Benzodiazepines = treatment of choice for alcohol/sedative withdrawal
  • Opioid overdose: pinpoint pupils + respiratory depression → Naloxone immediately
  • Opioid withdrawal = flu-like + dilated pupils; NOT life-threatening
  • Stimulant intoxication: dilated pupils, tachycardia, hyperthermia
  • Sedative/benzo withdrawal can be life-threatening (like alcohol)
  • Disulfiram: patient must be completely alcohol-free before starting
