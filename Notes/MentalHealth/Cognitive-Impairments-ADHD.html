Overview of Neurodevelopmental Disorders

Neurodevelopmental disorders manifest early in development and are characterized by deficits in personal, social, academic, or occupational functioning. They include intellectual disability, communication disorders, ADHD, learning disorders, and autism spectrum disorder.

Intellectual Disability (ID)

Definition

Significant limitations in intellectual functioning (IQ ≤70) AND adaptive behavior (conceptual, social, practical skills). Onset during developmental period (before age 18).

Levels of Intellectual Disability

LevelIQ RangeCharacteristicsIndependence
Mild50–70Slow academic progress; some difficulty with abstract concepts; most can live independently with minimal supportUsually independent or semi-independent
Moderate35–50Limited academic skills; need support with complex tasks; can hold jobs with supervisionSupervised living
Severe20–35Very limited communication; limited self-care; require significant daily supportRequires daily support
Profound<20Minimal verbal communication; severe limitations in all areas; may have significant motor impairmentTotal care required

Common Causes of Intellectual Disability

  • Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) — most common genetic cause
  • Fragile X syndrome — most common inherited cause
  • Fetal alcohol syndrome (FASD)
  • Prenatal infections (CMV, rubella, toxoplasmosis)
  • Perinatal hypoxia
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU) — preventable with newborn screening
  • Hypothyroidism (congenital, untreated)
💡
Nursing Approach for Intellectual Disability
  • Use simple, concrete language
  • Allow extra time for processing and responses
  • Teach in short sessions with repetition
  • Focus on maximizing independence and self-care skills
  • Involve family/caregivers in care planning

Learning Disabilities

Dyslexia

Reading disorder — difficulty decoding words, reading fluency, and spelling despite adequate intelligence and instruction

Dyscalculia

Math disorder — difficulty with number sense, arithmetic facts, calculation, and math reasoning

Dysgraphia

Written expression disorder — difficulty with handwriting, spelling, and organizing written language

Auditory Processing Disorder

Difficulty processing and interpreting auditory information despite normal hearing ability

ADHD — Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder

DSM-5 Criteria

Persistent pattern of inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning/development. Symptoms present in ≥2 settings (home, school, work). Several symptoms before age 12.

Types of ADHD

TypeFeatures
Predominantly InattentiveDifficulty sustaining attention, easily distracted, forgetful, loses items, does not follow through on tasks, poor organization
Predominantly Hyperactive-ImpulsiveFidgeting, leaves seat inappropriately, runs/climbs excessively, talks excessively, blurts out answers, difficulty waiting turn
Combined PresentationMeets criteria for both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive types — most common presentation

Treatment for ADHD

Non-Pharmacological (Try First in Young Children)
  • Behavioral therapy — reinforcement, structure, routines
  • Parent training in behavior management
  • Environmental modifications
  • Educational accommodations (IEP/504 plan)
  • Social skills training
Pharmacological
  • Stimulants (first-line for ≥6 years): Methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta), Amphetamine (Adderall, Vyvanse)
  • Non-stimulants: Atomoxetine (Strattera), Guanfacine (Intuniv), Clonidine
  • Monitor: appetite, growth, sleep, BP, HR, mood
💡
ADHD Stimulant Medication Teaching
  • Give medication early in day to avoid insomnia
  • Monitor height and weight (growth suppression)
  • May decrease appetite — offer nutritious snacks after medication wears off
  • Schedule medication-free holidays (e.g., summer) when school performance not critical
  • Controlled substance — assess for misuse potential

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

Core Features
  • Deficits in social communication and interaction across multiple contexts
  • Restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities
  • Symptoms present in early developmental period (though may not be recognized until demands exceed capacity)
  • Severity levels: Level 1 (requiring support), Level 2, Level 3 (requiring very substantial support)

Key Features of ASD

DomainManifestations
Social CommunicationPoor eye contact; difficulty understanding nonverbal cues; challenges in developing/maintaining relationships; one-sided conversations; may not respond to name being called
Repetitive BehaviorsStereotyped movements (hand-flapping, rocking); insistence on sameness; highly restricted fixated interests; hyper/hyposensitivity to sensory input
LanguageRanges from non-verbal to highly verbal; echolalia; may take language literally; difficulty with pragmatic language

Nursing Care for ASD

  • Maintain consistent routines — changes in routine cause significant distress
  • Warn of upcoming changes in advance
  • Reduce sensory overstimulation (noise, lighting, touch)
  • Use visual schedules, clear instructions, simple language
  • Respect communication preferences — may use AAC devices
  • Recognize that behaviors (meltdowns) are often responses to overwhelm, not willful defiance
  • Support family caregivers; provide resources and referrals
💡
Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)

The most evidence-based treatment for ASD. Uses behavioral principles to improve communication, social skills, and adaptive behaviors. Most effective when started early.

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Intellectual disability: IQ ≤70 + deficits in adaptive behavior + onset before 18
  • Most common genetic cause of ID: Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
  • Most common inherited cause: Fragile X syndrome
  • ADHD: inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity in ≥2 settings before age 12
  • ADHD stimulants: give early in day, monitor growth and appetite
  • ASD: social communication deficits + restricted/repetitive behaviors
  • ASD nursing: maintain consistent routines, reduce sensory stimulation
  • ABA therapy: gold standard for ASD — start early
