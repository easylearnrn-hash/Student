Serotonin Syndrome

NCLEX-focused — causes · classic triad · drugs implicated · treatment · NMS comparison

What Is Serotonin Syndrome?

A life-threatening, drug-induced excess of serotonergic activity in the central and peripheral nervous system. Results from overstimulation of serotonin receptors, typically due to drug interactions or overdose of serotonergic agents.

Onset is typically rapid — within 24 hours of starting a new drug or increasing a dose. Can progress quickly to death if not treated.

Classic Clinical Triad

Hunter's Criteria — The Classic Triad
Mental Status Changes
Agitation, anxiety, confusion, restlessness
Autonomic Instability
Hyperthermia, diaphoresis, tachycardia, hypertension, diarrhea
Neuromuscular Abnormalities
Clonus, hyperreflexia, tremor, incoordination, myoclonus

Key differentiator: Clonus (rhythmic muscle contractions) and hyperreflexia — these distinguish serotonin syndrome from NMS.

Common Drug Causes

Serotonergic Drugs (Combinations Are Dangerous)
  • SSRIs — Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Paroxetine, Escitalopram
  • SNRIs — Venlafaxine, Duloxetine
  • MAOIs — Phenelzine, Tranylcypromine, Linezolid, Methylene blue
  • TCAs — Amitriptyline, Imipramine
  • Triptans — Sumatriptan (for migraines)
  • Opioids — Tramadol, Fentanyl, Meperidine (Pethidine)
  • Linezolid (antibiotic with MAOI properties)
  • Lithium
  • Dextromethorphan (DXM) — in cough medicines
  • MDMA (Ecstasy), LSD, cocaine
  • St. John's Wort (herbal supplement)
Most Dangerous Combinations
  • MAOI + any SSRI/SNRI — most dangerous; can be fatal
  • MAOI + Tramadol/Meperidine
  • MAOI + Linezolid
  • Two SSRIs together
  • SSRI + Triptans
  • SSRI + St. John's Wort
🚨
MAOI Safety Rule — NCLEX Critical
  • Wait 14 days after stopping an SSRI before starting an MAOI (5 weeks for fluoxetine due to long half-life)
  • Wait 14 days after stopping an MAOI before starting an SSRI
  • Violating this rule can cause fatal serotonin syndrome

Memory Trick — Key Differences

Serotonin Syndrome: Hypere-FLEX-ia (hyperreflexia), CLONus, RAPID onset
NMS: LEAD-pipe rigidity, SLOW onset, HIGH CK

Treatment of Serotonin Syndrome

  1. Discontinue ALL serotonergic agents immediately — most critical step
  2. Supportive care — IV fluids, oxygen, cardiac monitoring
  3. Benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Diazepam) — for agitation, hyperthermia, seizures, muscle rigidity
  4. Temperature management — cooling measures for hyperthermia (ice packs, cooling blankets)
  5. Cyproheptadine (Periactin) — serotonin antagonist; may be given orally or by NG tube
  6. Antihypertensives as needed for severe hypertension
  7. Intubation and mechanical ventilation — if respiratory compromise in severe cases
💡
Priority Nursing Action

First priority: Stop the offending drug(s) immediately. Notify the provider. Begin supportive measures. Most mild cases resolve within 24 hours of stopping the offending drug.

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Serotonin syndrome = excess serotonin; triad: mental changes + autonomic instability + neuromuscular (clonus/hyperreflexia)
  • Onset: rapid (within 24 hours)
  • Most dangerous: MAOI + SSRI combination
  • MAOI washout: wait 14 days before/after SSRI; 5 weeks for fluoxetine
  • Key differentiator from NMS: clonus and hyperreflexia (vs. lead-pipe rigidity in NMS)
  • Treatment: stop all serotonergic drugs immediately + supportive care
  • Cyproheptadine = serotonin antagonist used in treatment
  • NMS = antipsychotics; Serotonin Syndrome = serotonergic drugs — different causes!
