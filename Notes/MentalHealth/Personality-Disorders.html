What Is a Personality Disorder?

An enduring pattern of inner experience and behavior that deviates markedly from cultural expectations, is pervasive and inflexible, and causes significant impairment or distress. Onset typically in adolescence or early adulthood.

Organized into 3 Clusters (A, B, C) based on descriptive similarities.

Cluster A — "Odd or Eccentric"

Cluster A
Paranoid PD
  • Pervasive distrust and suspicion
  • Interprets motives as malevolent
  • Holds grudges; reluctant to confide
  • Suspects infidelity without cause
Schizoid PD
  • Detachment from social relationships
  • Restricted emotional expression
  • Prefers solitary activities
  • Little interest in sex; few close friends
Schizotypal PD
  • Odd beliefs, magical thinking
  • Ideas of reference
  • Peculiar speech and appearance
  • Social anxiety (paranoid type)
💡
Cluster A Nursing Approach

Be consistent and non-threatening. Do not challenge paranoid beliefs directly. Respect need for personal space. Build trust slowly over time.

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) — NCLEX Focus

Core Features
  • Splitting — sees people as all good or all bad ("staff splitting")
  • Self-harm — cutting, burning; often as tension relief (not always suicidal)
  • Impulsivity — substance use, spending, sexual behavior, reckless driving
  • Fear of abandonment — frantic efforts to avoid real or imagined abandonment
  • Identity disturbance — unstable self-image
🚨
BPD Nursing Priority

Self-harm behavior must be taken seriously — assess for suicidal intent EVERY time. Treatment: Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is the evidence-based treatment of choice.

💡
Handling Splitting

Staff should meet regularly to maintain consistency. Do not let a patient divide staff into "good" and "bad" nurses. Present a unified treatment approach.

Cluster C — "Anxious or Fearful"

Cluster C
Avoidant PD
  • Social inhibition; feelings of inadequacy
  • Hypersensitive to criticism
  • Avoids occupational activities due to fear of rejection
  • Views self as inferior; unwilling to try new things
Dependent PD
  • Excessive need to be taken care of
  • Submissive and clinging behavior
  • Fear of separation; difficulty making decisions alone
  • Urgently seeks new relationship when one ends
Obsessive-Compulsive PD (OCPD)
  • Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, control
  • Inflexible; rigid morality
  • Difficulty delegating; hoarding
  • Different from OCD — ego-syntonic (consistent with self-image)
💡
Cluster C Nursing Approach

For Avoidant — encourage small social steps; validate accomplishments. For Dependent — foster independence; avoid over-helping. For OCPD — allow some control; help identify priorities.

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Cluster A = Odd (Paranoid, Schizoid, Schizotypal)
  • Cluster B = Dramatic (Antisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, Narcissistic)
  • Cluster C = Anxious (Avoidant, Dependent, OCPD)
  • BPD: splitting, self-harm, fear of abandonment → consistent staff, set limits, DBT
  • ASPD: manipulation, no remorse → firm limits, do not be manipulated
  • OCPD ≠ OCD — OCPD is ego-syntonic (patient does not see it as a problem)
  • Self-harm in BPD → assess for suicidal intent EVERY time; never minimize
