What Is a Therapeutic Relationship?

A professional, goal-directed connection between nurse and patient that promotes healing and well-being. Based on trust, respect, and clear boundaries.

💡
NCLEX Tip

A therapeutic relationship is patient-centered — the nurse focuses on helping the patient, not forming a personal connection.

The Four Phases

1
Pre-Interaction Phase — Before meeting the patientReview patient history. Self-reflect on personal biases before the first encounter.
2
Orientation Phase — Establish trust & define the relationshipIntroduce self, set professional boundaries, establish treatment goals.
3
Working Phase — Active problem-solvingEncourage expression of feelings, provide education, support coping skills.
4
Termination Phase — End of the relationshipSummarize progress, prepare for discharge, allow patient to express feelings about ending care.
⚠️
Termination Phase — NCLEX Alert

Patient may feel abandoned. Encourage them to express feelings about the relationship ending. This is expected and therapeutic.

Characteristics of a Therapeutic Relationship

Trust
Patient feels safe and respected.
Empathy
Understanding feelings without judgment.
Respect
Acknowledging the patient's dignity and worth.
Genuineness
Being honest — not pretending to care.
💡
Boundaries

Nurses should never share personal problems or accept gifts from patients. Therapeutic relationships are different from friendships.

Therapeutic vs. Non-Therapeutic Communication

Therapeutic Techniques

TechniqueExampleWhy It Works
Active Listening"I hear that you're feeling overwhelmed. Tell me more."Shows interest, encourages expression
Open-Ended Questions"How are you feeling today?"Encourages detailed responses
Reflection"It sounds like you're feeling very frustrated."Helps patient recognize emotions
Clarification"Can you explain what you mean by that?"Ensures accurate understanding
Silence(Allowing quiet moments)Gives patient time to think and express
Exploring"Tell me more about what happened."Encourages patient to provide details

Non-Therapeutic Responses

ResponseExampleWhy It's Wrong
✗ False Reassurance"Don't worry, everything will be fine."Dismisses patient's feelings; may be untrue
✗ Advising"You should just leave that relationship."Takes away patient autonomy
✗ Minimizing"Everyone feels sad sometimes."Dismisses the patient's emotions
✗ Asking "Why?""Why do you feel that way?"Sounds judgmental; creates defensiveness
✗ Changing SubjectPatient: "I feel depressed." Nurse: "What did you eat?"Avoids the patient's concern
✗ Agreeing/Disagreeing"I think your doctor is wrong."Creates division within the care team

Setting & Maintaining Boundaries

ViolationExampleWhy It's Wrong
Sharing Personal Problems"I went through a divorce too."Shifts focus from patient to nurse
Accepting GiftsPatient gives expensive necklaceCreates favoritism / ethical issues
Touching Without ConsentHugging without askingCan trigger trauma; misinterpreted
Keeping Secrets"I won't tell anyone if you hurt yourself."Safety risk — must report self-harm

Transference vs. Countertransference

Transference

Patient projects feelings about someone else onto the nurse.

Example: Patient treats nurse like their abusive parent and becomes angry.

Countertransference

Nurse projects personal feelings onto the patient.

Example: Nurse becomes overly protective because patient reminds them of their child.

Handling Difficult Situations

Patient Becomes Angry

Remain calm, set limits, offer choices: "I need you to lower your voice so we can talk calmly." Do NOT argue or threaten. Call for help if needed.

Patient Says "I Want to Die"

Ask directly: "Are you thinking of harming yourself?" Assess plan, means, intent. Stay with patient. Alert provider immediately.

Patient Develops Romantic Feelings

Set firm professional boundaries: "I am here to support your health, not a personal relationship." Redirect to treatment goals.

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Therapeutic relationship = professional, goal-oriented, time-limited
  • Never accept gifts — blurs professional boundaries
  • Open-ended questions = best for communication
  • Best therapy = CBT for anxiety, depression, PTSD
  • Suicidal thoughts → stay with the patient & notify provider immediately
