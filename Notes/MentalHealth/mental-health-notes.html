Mental Health – NCLEX RN

Mental health nursing is about caring for patients with emotional, behavioral, or psychiatric disorders (conditions that affect thinking, mood, or behavior).

The focus is on:

  • Safety
  • Therapeutic communication
  • Medication management
  • Crisis intervention
  • Coping support
🧠 Remember:

You help patients function, not just treat their illness.

1. Therapeutic Communication (Building Trust)

What To Do Example
Open-ended questions "Can you tell me more about that?"
Use silence Let patient think and talk
Reflect feelings "It sounds like you feel scared."
Restate "You're saying you don't feel safe?"
Never give advice ❌ "You should…"
Don't say "Why?" ❌ "Why did you do that?" (feels judgmental)
🧠 Goal:

Build trust and let the patient explore feelings safely.

2. Safety First – Suicide, Violence, and Crisis

Suicide Risk (Life-Threatening Concern)

ALWAYS assess risk directly:

Ask: "Are you thinking of hurting yourself?"

Red flags:

  • Plan, means, past attempts
  • Hopelessness, withdrawal

🧠 Important: Patient says "I want it to end" = stay with them + notify team

Keep patient safe:

  • One-to-one supervision (someone with them at all times)
  • Remove sharp objects, belts, cords, etc.

Assault Risk / Violence Risk

  • Signs: clenched fists, pacing, yelling
  • Stay calm, give space
  • Use clear, short statements
  • Never turn your back

Crisis Intervention

Crisis = moment of breakdown/loss of coping

Goal = stabilize first, then help regain control

3. Mental Status Exam (MSE)

Mental Status Exam = structured way to assess brain function.

Area What You Check
Appearance Clean? Dressed? Groomed?
Behavior Calm? Agitated?
Mood How they say they feel ("I'm sad")
Affect What you see (smiling? blank?)
Thought process Clear or disorganized?
Thought content Any delusions (false beliefs)?
Perception Any hallucinations (seeing/hearing things not real)?
Insight Do they understand they're ill?
Judgment Can they make safe choices?

4. Psychiatric Medications

🧠 Remember:

Always teach about side effects and when to call the provider.

Type Used For Examples Side Notes
SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors) Depression, anxiety Fluoxetine, Sertraline Can take weeks to work
Benzodiazepines Anxiety Lorazepam, Diazepam Sedating, habit-forming
Antipsychotics Schizophrenia, mania Risperidone, Haloperidol Watch for EPS (extra movements) and NMS (muscle rigidity + fever)
Mood stabilizers Bipolar disorder Lithium Check levels (0.6–1.2), avoid dehydration
MAOIs Depression Phenelzine Avoid tyramine foods (aged cheese, wine) — risk of hypertensive crisis (very high BP)

5. Common Psychiatric Disorders

Depression

  • Low mood, loss of interest, fatigue, suicidal thoughts
  • Encourage small goals and self-care
  • Assess for suicide risk

Bipolar Disorder

  • Mania = high energy, no sleep, risky behavior
  • Depression = low energy, sadness
  • Protect during mania (limit stimuli, rest, safe environment)

Schizophrenia

  • Positive symptoms = hallucinations (seeing or hearing things), delusions (false beliefs), disorganized speech
  • Negative symptoms = flat affect, poor hygiene
  • Stay calm, do not argue with delusions, focus on feelings

Anxiety Disorders

  • Excess worry, fear, physical signs (racing heart, tense muscles)
  • Use calm voice, breathing techniques
  • Teach coping skills (journaling, deep breathing)

OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder)

  • Obsessions = unwanted thoughts
  • Compulsions = repeated actions to relieve stress
🧠 Important:

Don't stop rituals right away — help reduce them slowly.

PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder)

  • Flashbacks, nightmares, startle easily
  • Avoid triggers, use grounding techniques

6. Therapeutic Milieu (Safe Healing Environment)

  • Structure, safety, support
  • Clear rules and roles
  • Encourage group participation
  • Limit stimulation if needed
🧠 Purpose:

Helps patient feel secure and build social skills

NCLEX High-Yield Mental Health Tips

Critical Points:
  • Never ignore suicidal words — assess and stay with patient
  • Set limits clearly but kindly for manic or aggressive patients
  • Do not argue with delusions or hallucinations
  • Use therapeutic communication to build trust
  • Safety comes first, even before feelings
