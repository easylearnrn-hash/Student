Patient Rights in Psychiatric Care

Core Rights
  • Right to treatment — Patients must receive appropriate, individualized care
  • Right to refuse treatment — Competent adults can decline medication or procedures (exception: immediate danger)
  • Right to informed consent — Must understand risks, benefits, alternatives before agreeing to treatment
  • Right to confidentiality — Information cannot be shared without written consent (HIPAA)
  • Right to the least restrictive environment — Use minimal restrictions necessary for safety
  • Right to communicate — Patients can send and receive mail, make phone calls
  • Right to legal counsel — May consult an attorney at any time
  • Right to vote — Institutionalization does not remove voting rights

⚠️
NCLEX Alert — Involuntary Commitment

Must meet criteria: danger to self, danger to others, OR grave disability (cannot meet basic needs). The patient still retains rights even under involuntary hold.

Confidentiality & HIPAA

CAN Share Without Consent
  • Court-ordered disclosure
  • Duty to warn third parties (Tarasoff rule)
  • Mandatory child/elder abuse reporting
  • Communicable disease reporting
  • Suicidal threats to a named target
CANNOT Share Without Written Consent
  • Information to family (even parents of adults)
  • Employer requests
  • Insurance companies (general rule)
  • Substance abuse records (extra protection — 42 CFR Part 2)
⚠️
Tarasoff Rule — Duty to Warn

If a patient makes a specific, credible threat against an identifiable person, the provider has a legal duty to warn the potential victim AND notify law enforcement. Confidentiality yields to public safety.

Least Restrictive Interventions

Always use the least restrictive intervention first. Escalate only when necessary:

  1. Verbal de-escalation — Calm tone, open body language, offer choices
  2. Voluntary medication — Offer oral PRN medications
  3. Voluntary room restriction / quiet area
  4. Show of force — Several staff present without physical contact
  5. Physical holds — Last resort before restraints
  6. Chemical restraint — IM medication when patient is a danger
  7. Physical restraints — Only when all else fails

Restraints & Seclusion

Use Only When...
  • Patient is an immediate danger to self or others
  • Less restrictive measures have failed or are not feasible
  • Ordered by a physician or licensed independent practitioner (LIP)
RequirementStandard
Order requiredWritten order within 1 hour of application; renewed every 4 hrs (adults), 2 hrs (9–17 yrs), 1 hr (<9 yrs)
In-person evaluationWithin 1 hour of restraint application
MonitoringEvery 15 minutes minimum — check circulation, breathing, ROM, hygiene
DocumentationBehavior requiring restraint; less restrictive alternatives tried; patient response
ReleaseAs soon as patient is calm and no longer a danger; offer food/water/bathroom, assess skin
PositioningNever prone restraint (aspiration/asphyxiation risk)
🚨
NCLEX Priority: Restraint Safety
  • Restraints are NEVER used as punishment or for staff convenience
  • Tied to fixed part of bed frame — not side rails
  • Quick-release knot or quick-release clip ONLY

Safety Priorities — Suicide & Self-Harm

Immediate Safety Steps
  1. Stay with the patient — Never leave alone
  2. Remove all dangerous items — sharps, ligatures, medications, belts, cords
  3. Notify provider immediately
  4. 1:1 constant observation for high-risk patients
  5. Document all assessments and interventions
💡
Safe Environment Checklist
  • Remove belts, shoelaces, neckties, cords
  • Check rooms for sharps, medications hoarded
  • Plastic utensils during meals
  • Non-breakable room fixtures
  • Locked doors to stairwells, exits, outdoor areas

Mandatory Reporting Requirements

Must Report
  • Child abuse or neglect (suspected)
  • Elder abuse or neglect
  • Intimate partner violence (varies by state)
  • Gunshot wounds
  • Communicable diseases
  • Specific threats against identifiable person (Tarasoff)
Report To
  • Child Protective Services (CPS)
  • Adult Protective Services (APS)
  • Law enforcement (as required)
  • State health department (communicable disease)
  • Identified victim AND law enforcement (Tarasoff)

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Involuntary commitment = danger to self, others, OR grave disability
  • Patient rights remain intact even under involuntary hold
  • Always use least restrictive intervention first
  • Restraints: never punishment, never prone, monitor q15min
  • Tarasoff = break confidentiality to protect identifiable third party
  • Suicidal patient → stay, remove hazards, notify provider, document
  • Substance abuse records have extra confidentiality protections (42 CFR Part 2)
