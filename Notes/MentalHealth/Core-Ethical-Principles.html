Core Ethical Principles

Nursing Ethics — Autonomy · Beneficence · Nonmaleficence · Justice · Fidelity · Veracity · ANA Code

Overview

Nursing ethics is grounded in a set of core moral principles that guide clinical decision-making, patient care, and professional conduct. These principles form the foundation of the ANA Code of Ethics and are tested heavily on NCLEX.

The 6 Core Ethical Principles

1. Autonomy
From Latin: "autos" (self) + "nomos" (law) — self-governance

Definition: The patient's right to make their own decisions about their health care — even if the nurse or provider disagrees with the choice.

Clinical Examples:

  • A competent adult refuses a blood transfusion for religious reasons — the nurse must respect this decision
  • Informed consent — patient must receive full information before agreeing to treatment
  • Advance directives — patient's pre-stated wishes guide care even when unconscious
  • The right to refuse medication or treatment

Mental Health Application: Voluntary vs. involuntary commitment. Competent patients have the right to refuse psychiatric medication. Informed consent for ECT, medications.

2. Beneficence
Latin: "bene" (good) + "facere" (to do) — do good

Definition: The duty to act in the patient's best interest and promote their well-being. Doing good actively.

Clinical Examples:

  • Administering pain medication to relieve suffering
  • Providing education so the patient can make informed decisions
  • Recommending a treatment that offers the best chance of recovery
  • Advocating for the patient when they cannot advocate for themselves

Mental Health Application: Initiating suicide precautions to protect a patient at risk. Recommending a therapist for a patient who needs support.

3. Nonmaleficence
Latin: "non" (not) + "maleficence" (wrongdoing) — do no harm

Definition: The duty to avoid causing harm — whether intentional or unintentional. The foundation of the Hippocratic oath: Primum non nocere ("First, do no harm").

Clinical Examples:

  • Verifying the six rights of medication administration to prevent medication errors
  • Reporting a colleague who is practicing unsafely
  • Weighing the risks vs. benefits of a treatment or procedure
  • Avoiding unnecessary or harmful interventions

Mental Health Application: Using restraints as a last resort — restraints cause harm (psychological trauma, physical injury). Avoiding overmedication. Not leaving a suicidal patient alone.

4. Justice
Latin: "justitia" — fairness, equal treatment

Definition: The duty to treat all patients fairly and equitably — distributing resources, care, and treatment without discrimination.

Clinical Examples:

  • Providing equal care regardless of race, religion, gender, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status
  • Fair allocation of limited resources (organ transplant lists, ICU beds)
  • Advocating for the underserved and marginalized populations
  • Triage in emergencies — most critical first, regardless of identity

Mental Health Application: Ensuring patients with mental illness receive the same quality of care as those with physical illness. Fighting stigma in healthcare settings.

5. Fidelity
Latin: "fidelitas" — faithfulness, loyalty

Definition: The duty to keep promises and commitments to patients; to be loyal and trustworthy; to honor obligations.

Clinical Examples:

  • Following through on promises made to patients ("I will check on your pain medication in 30 minutes")
  • Maintaining confidentiality — honoring the commitment of privacy
  • Advocating for patients even when it is inconvenient or unpopular
  • Following through on delegation and professional commitments

Mental Health Application: Being consistent and reliable with psychiatric patients — especially important for therapeutic relationships. Patients with borderline personality disorder or attachment issues particularly rely on fidelity.

6. Veracity
Latin: "verax" — truthfulness

Definition: The duty to tell the truth to patients — providing honest, complete, and accurate information.

Clinical Examples:

  • Telling a patient the truth about their diagnosis (even terminal diagnoses)
  • Accurately reporting medication errors rather than concealing them
  • Providing honest information about side effects of medications
  • Not giving false hope ("everything will be fine" when it may not be)

Mental Health Application: Honesty is foundational to the therapeutic relationship. Lying or withholding truth destroys trust. However, delivery of truth should be compassionate and thoughtful.

Ethical Conflicts in Mental Health Nursing

Patient with dementia refuses careAutonomy vs. BeneficenceDetermine decisional capacity; if lacking, use advance directives or surrogate decision-maker

Ethical Decision-Making Process

  1. Identify the ethical dilemma — What principles are in conflict?
  2. Gather information — Patient values, clinical facts, legal requirements, institutional policies
  3. Identify stakeholders — Patient, family, healthcare team, society
  4. Consider all options — What are the possible courses of action?
  5. Apply ethical principles — Which principles support each option?
  6. Consult resources — Ethics committee, chaplain, social worker, legal counsel
  7. Make and implement a decision — Guided by patient best interest and ethical principles
  8. Evaluate the outcome — Did the action align with ethical principles? What can be learned?

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Autonomy = right to decide for self — even if nurse disagrees
  • Beneficence = do good; Nonmaleficence = do no harm (different principles!)
  • Justice = fair, equal treatment for all
  • Fidelity = keep promises, be trustworthy
  • Veracity = tell the truth
  • On NCLEX, when autonomy conflicts with beneficence, support informed patient autonomy if patient is competent
  • ANA Code of Ethics Provision 1: The nurse practices with compassion and respect for every individual regardless of background
  • Ethics committee = resource for complex dilemmas, not everyday decisions
