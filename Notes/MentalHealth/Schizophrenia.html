Mental Health

Schizophrenia

NCLEX-focused — positive & negative symptoms · antipsychotics · EPS · NMS · nursing care

Overview

A severe, chronic mental illness characterized by distorted thinking, perception, emotions, and behavior. Onset typically late teens to early 30s. Males earlier onset than females. Requires lifelong treatment.

Positive vs. Negative Symptoms

Positive Symptoms (Excess / Distortion)

Symptoms that are added to normal behavior — responsive to antipsychotics

  • Hallucinations — false sensory perceptions (most common: auditory)
  • Delusions — fixed, false beliefs (paranoid most common)
  • Disorganized speech — loose associations, word salad, tangentiality, clang associations
  • Disorganized/catatonic behavior
  • Illusions — misinterpretation of real stimuli
Negative Symptoms (Absence / Diminishment)

Loss of normal function — harder to treat; respond better to atypical antipsychotics

  • Alogia — poverty of speech; brief, empty replies
  • Avolition — lack of motivation; inability to initiate goal-directed activity
  • Anhedonia — inability to experience pleasure
  • Affect (flat/blunted) — diminished emotional expression
  • Asociality — social withdrawal, reduced social interaction
💡
Memory Tip: The "5 A's" of Negative Symptoms

Alogia, Avolition, Anhedonia, Affect (flat), Asociality

Nursing Interventions

With Hallucinations

  • Do NOT deny that the patient hears/sees something — acknowledge their experience
  • Do NOT play along or validate the hallucination as real
  • Say: "I understand you hear a voice. I don't hear it, but I can see it's frightening you."
  • Redirect to reality; engage in simple, grounding activities
  • Assess if voices are commanding harm to self or others — safety priority

With Delusions

  • Do NOT argue with or reinforce the delusion
  • Acknowledge the feeling: "I can see you feel frightened. I'm here to help keep you safe."
  • Do NOT play along: never reinforce paranoid beliefs
  • Redirect to a different topic or activity
  • If delusion involves refusing food (food is poisoned): offer sealed packaged foods or allow patient to open their own food
⚠️
Command Hallucinations — Priority

If patient reports hearing voices commanding them to hurt self or others — this is a safety emergency. Notify provider immediately, initiate safety protocols, increase monitoring level.

Antipsychotic Medications

First-Generation (Typical) Antipsychotics

Typical Antipsychotics — Block D2 Dopamine Receptors

Examples: Haloperidol (Haldol), Chlorpromazine (Thorazine), Fluphenazine

  • Effective for positive symptoms; limited effect on negative symptoms
  • High risk for Extrapyramidal Side Effects (EPS)
  • Haloperidol: available as IM depot (long-acting) — good for non-compliant patients

Second-Generation (Atypical) Antipsychotics

Atypical Antipsychotics — Block D2 + Serotonin Receptors

Examples: Clozapine, Risperidone, Olanzapine, Quetiapine, Aripiprazole, Ziprasidone

  • Effective for both positive AND negative symptoms
  • Lower risk of EPS
  • Higher risk of metabolic syndrome (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia)
  • Clozapine: most effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; risk of agranulocytosis — weekly WBC monitoring required

Critical Side Effects — NCLEX High Yield

🚨
Tardive Dyskinesia — NCLEX Alert

TD is potentially irreversible. Report involuntary movements immediately. Do NOT give anticholinergics — they worsen TD. Assess regularly using AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale).

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) — Life-Threatening Emergency

NMS — Rare but Life-Threatening

Idiosyncratic reaction to antipsychotics, most often typical antipsychotics. Can occur at any time, even after years of use.

Classic presentation — "FEVER":

  • Fever — hyperthermia (often >40°C / 104°F)
  • Encephalopathy — altered consciousness, confusion
  • Vitals unstable — tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis
  • Elevated CK (creatine kinase) — from muscle breakdown
  • Rigidity — severe muscle rigidity ("lead pipe")

Treatment: Stop antipsychotic immediately; supportive care; Dantrolene (muscle relaxant); Bromocriptine (dopamine agonist)

🚨
NMS vs. Serotonin Syndrome

NMS: develops slowly (days–weeks); lead-pipe rigidity; bradyreflexia. Caused by antipsychotics. Serotonin Syndrome: develops rapidly (hours); clonus/hyperreflexia; more agitation. Caused by serotonergic drugs.

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Positive symptoms: hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior
  • Negative symptoms: the 5 A's — Alogia, Avolition, Anhedonia, flat Affect, Asociality
  • Most common hallucination in schizophrenia: AUDITORY
  • Do NOT argue with delusions; do NOT deny hallucinations; do NOT play along
  • Clozapine: most effective but requires weekly WBC — agranulocytosis risk
  • EPS: acute dystonia → Benztropine IM immediately
  • Tardive dyskinesia: irreversible; anticholinergics worsen it
  • NMS: fever + lead-pipe rigidity + elevated CK → stop antipsychotic immediately
