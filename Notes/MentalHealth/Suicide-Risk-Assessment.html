Suicide Risk Assessment

NCLEX Critical — risk factors · protective factors · assessment · nursing interventions · safety planning

Key Principle — Always Ask Directly

🚨
NCLEX Critical — Ask About Suicide Directly

Asking a patient directly about suicidal ideation does NOT plant the idea. Research shows direct questioning reduces risk by allowing patients to express thoughts. Always ask: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" or "Are you having thoughts of suicide?"

Risk Factors for Suicide

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
  • Previous suicide attempt (strongest predictor)
  • Family history of suicide
  • Male gender (more lethal methods)
  • Age: adolescents and elderly (≥65)
  • History of trauma or abuse
  • Chronic pain or terminal illness
Modifiable / Situational Risk Factors
  • Current major depression, bipolar disorder
  • Schizophrenia, psychosis
  • Substance abuse (alcohol = major risk factor)
  • Social isolation, lack of support
  • Recent significant loss (job, relationship, death)
  • Hopelessness (stronger predictor than depression alone)
  • Access to lethal means (firearms, medications)
  • Recent discharge from psychiatric facility
  • Impulsivity
💡
Hopelessness — The Strongest Predictor

Research by Aaron Beck found that hopelessness is a stronger predictor of suicide than depression. The belief that things will never get better = highest risk indicator. Assess with: "Do you see any hope for your future?"

Protective Factors

Factors That Reduce Suicide Risk
  • Reasons for living (children, family, pets)
  • Strong social support network
  • Religious or spiritual beliefs that discourage suicide
  • Sense of purpose or meaning in life
  • Access to mental health care
  • Positive therapeutic relationship
  • Problem-solving skills and resilience
  • Means restriction (no firearms in home)

Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)

C-SSRS Overview

The C-SSRS is a validated, evidence-based tool widely used in clinical settings and research to assess suicidal ideation and behavior. It categorizes severity on a 1–5 scale:

  1. Wish to be dead — passive ideation
  2. Active suicidal ideation — no plan
  3. Active ideation with some intent — no plan
  4. Active ideation with plan — but no intent to act
  5. Active ideation with plan and intent to act — high risk, requires immediate intervention

Also assesses suicidal behavior (actual attempt, aborted attempt, preparatory actions) and lethality of past attempts.

Nursing Interventions

Immediate / High-Risk Interventions

Priority Actions — Active Suicidal Risk
  1. Stay with the patient — do not leave alone under any circumstances
  2. Notify the provider immediately — it is a medical emergency
  3. Remove all potential means — sharps, medications, belts, cords, call buttons, plastic bags — complete environmental safety sweep
  4. Initiate 1:1 continuous observation (1:1 sitter or constant monitoring)
  5. Administer medications as ordered (sedation, antipsychotics as appropriate)
  6. Document all observations, interventions, and patient statements thoroughly
  7. Communicate in handoff — always include suicide risk in SBAR/handoff reports

Ongoing Nursing Interventions

  • Build a therapeutic relationship — non-judgmental, consistent, hopeful
  • Frequent check-ins (every 15 minutes minimum, more if risk is high)
  • Safety planning — collaboratively identify warning signs, coping strategies, support people, crisis resources
  • Means restriction counseling — discuss removing firearms and medications from home (extremely effective)
  • Involve family/support system in safety planning with patient consent
  • Provide crisis line numbers (National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988)
  • Monitor for changes in mood, behavior, statements
  • Be especially vigilant when depression begins to lift — patient may have energy to act

Safety Planning

Elements of a Safety Plan (Stanley-Brown Safety Planning Intervention)
  1. Warning signs that a crisis is developing
  2. Internal coping strategies (things I can do alone)
  3. Social contacts and settings that provide distraction
  4. People I can ask for support
  5. Professional and crisis resources I can contact
  6. Making the environment safe — removing access to means
⚠️
No-Suicide Contracts — NOT Recommended as Sole Intervention

Research shows that "no-suicide contracts" are NOT evidence-based and should never be used as the sole or primary safety intervention. They may provide false reassurance, may not prevent attempt, and can harm the therapeutic relationship. Use structured safety planning instead.

Highest Risk Periods

  • When depression begins to lift (first few weeks of antidepressant treatment) — patient now has energy to act
  • Shortly after discharge from inpatient psychiatric care — highest risk within 30–90 days of discharge
  • Following a significant loss (relationship, job, loved one)
  • Anniversaries of traumatic events
  • During periods of hopelessness — not necessarily deep depression

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Always ask directly about suicidal ideation — does NOT increase risk
  • Previous attempt = strongest single predictor of future attempt
  • Hopelessness = strongest psychological predictor (stronger than depression)
  • Immediate action when patient is suicidal: Stay, Remove means, Notify provider, 1:1 observation
  • Ideation with a specific plan + intent + access to means = highest risk → immediate psychiatric hospitalization
  • Risk increases when antidepressants start working (energy returns before mood fully lifts) — first 1–4 weeks
  • Post-discharge risk is highest in first 30–90 days after inpatient discharge
  • No-suicide contracts are NOT evidence-based — use structured safety planning
  • Means restriction (removing firearms from home) is one of the most effective suicide prevention strategies
  • National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988
