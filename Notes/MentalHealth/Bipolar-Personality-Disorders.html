Bipolar Disorder

NCLEX-focused — mania · depression · types · lithium toxicity · nursing interventions

What Is Bipolar Disorder?

A mood disorder characterized by cycling between manic and depressive episodes. Significant functional impairment during both phases. Requires lifelong management.

Types of Bipolar Disorder

TypeDefinitionEpisodes
Bipolar IAt least one full manic episode (≥7 days or hospitalization required); depressive episodes common but not required for diagnosisMania + Depression (or Mania alone)
Bipolar IIAt least one hypomanic episode (≥4 days) + at least one major depressive episode; NO full maniaHypomania + Depression
Cyclothymic DisorderChronic, fluctuating mood with hypomanic and depressive symptoms for ≥2 years; does not meet full criteria for mania or MDDSubsyndromal cycling

Manic Episode — Features

DSM-5: Manic Episode = ≥7 days (or any duration if hospitalized)

Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood + increased goal-directed activity/energy, plus ≥3 of the following ("DIG FAST"):

DIG FAST Mnemonic
LetterFeature
DDistractibility
IImpulsivity / Indiscretion (reckless behaviors)
GGrandiosity (inflated self-esteem; feels special, powerful)
FFlight of ideas (racing thoughts)
AActivity increase (goal-directed or psychomotor agitation)
SSleep decreased (needs little sleep, does not feel tired)
TTalkativeness (pressured speech)

Manic Episode Behaviors

  • Hypersexuality; excessive spending; reckless driving
  • Decreased need for sleep (feels rested after 2–3 hours)
  • Poor judgment; impulsive decisions (gambling, investments)
  • May have psychotic features (grandiose delusions, hallucinations) in severe mania

Nursing Care During Mania

PriorityInterventionRationale
1Safety first — remove dangerous objects; monitor behaviorImpulsivity and poor judgment = safety risk
2Ensure nutrition and hydration — high-calorie finger foods, frequent small mealsPatient too busy/distracted to sit and eat; high metabolic demand
3Reduce stimulation — quiet environment, limited visitorsOverstimulation worsens agitation
4Set firm, clear limits — calm, consistent approachEstablishes boundaries without power struggle
5Short, simple interactionsPatient cannot concentrate on long conversations
6Channel energy into activitiesRedirects energy safely; prevents harm
7Monitor sleep and I&OSleep deprivation worsens mania; dehydration risk

Medications — Bipolar Disorder

MedicationUseKey Points
LithiumGold standard mood stabilizer; Bipolar I & IINarrow therapeutic range (0.6–1.2 mEq/L maintenance). Monitor levels, renal function, thyroid. Lithium toxicity = NCLEX priority.

Atypical antipsychoticsAcute mania; adjunctOlanzapine, Quetiapine, Aripiprazole, Risperidone. Monitor metabolic syndrome.
BenzodiazepinesShort-term: acute agitation in maniaLorazepam IM for acute agitation; not long-term mood stabilizer

Lithium Toxicity — NCLEX HIGH PRIORITY

Therapeutic vs. Toxic Levels
RangeLevel (mEq/L)Meaning
Therapeutic (acute mania)0.8 – 1.4 mEq/LTherapeutic range for acute treatment
Therapeutic (maintenance)0.6 – 1.2 mEq/LLong-term maintenance
Mild Toxicity1.5 – 2.0 mEq/LCoarse tremor, N/V/D, drowsiness, confusion
Moderate Toxicity2.0 – 2.5 mEq/LAtaxia, blurred vision, tinnitus, slurred speech
Severe Toxicity>2.5 mEq/LSeizures, coma, cardiac dysrhythmias, death
🚨
Lithium Toxicity Risk Factors
  • Dehydration or low sodium diet → increased lithium reabsorption by kidneys
  • NSAIDs and thiazide diuretics → raise lithium levels
  • Sweating, vomiting, diarrhea → dehydration → toxicity risk
  • Teach: maintain consistent fluid/salt intake; report N/V/D or tremors immediately
💡
Lithium Patient Teaching
  • Take with food to reduce GI upset
  • Do NOT stop lithium abruptly — rebound mania
  • Adequate fluid (2–3 L/day) and sodium intake is essential
  • Early signs of toxicity: coarse tremor, N/V/D, ataxia
  • Blood levels drawn 12 hours after last dose

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Bipolar I = at least one full manic episode
  • Bipolar II = hypomania + major depression; no full mania
  • Mania: DIG FAST — grandiosity, decreased sleep, pressured speech, impulsivity
  • Mania nursing priorities: safety, nutrition (finger foods), reduce stimulation
  • Lithium: narrow range; toxicity signs = coarse tremor, N/V/D, ataxia, seizures
  • Low sodium = ↑ lithium reabsorption = toxicity risk
  • Draw lithium level 12 hours after last dose
  • NSAIDs and thiazides raise lithium levels
