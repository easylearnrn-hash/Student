What Is a Therapeutic Relationship?

A therapeutic relationship is a professional, goal-directed connection between a nurse and a patient that promotes healing and well-being. It is based on trust, respect, empathy, and clear professional boundaries.

🚨
NCLEX Tip — Patient-Centered, Not Personal

A therapeutic relationship is patient-centered — the nurse focuses on helping the patient, not forming a personal friendship. The nurse must never share personal problems, accept gifts, or shift focus to their own needs.

The Four Phases of a Therapeutic Relationship

Pre-Interaction
Phase
Before Meeting the Patient
The nurse reviews the patient's history, chart, and available information. Reflects on personal biases, feelings, or attitudes that could interfere with care. Self-reflection is the key nursing activity before the first contact.
Orientation
Phase
Establishing Trust & Defining the Relationship
The nurse introduces themselves, explains their role, and begins building rapport. Goals and expectations are set collaboratively — including the treatment goals and boundaries of the relationship. The patient begins to trust the nurse as a safe, consistent presence.
Working
Phase
Active Problem-Solving & Goal Achievement
The longest and most productive phase. The nurse encourages expression of feelings, identifies coping patterns, provides education, and supports development of healthy coping skills. The patient explores thoughts, behaviors, and feelings — the nurse guides but does not lead.
Termination
Phase
Ending the Relationship
The nurse summarizes progress, reviews goals achieved, and prepares the patient for discharge or transition. Patients may feel abandoned, sad, or regressive — the nurse must allow the patient to express feelings about the relationship ending and normalize these responses.
🚨
NCLEX Tip — Termination Phase

Termination phase = patient may feel abandoned. The correct nursing response is to encourage them to express their feelings about the relationship ending — do NOT avoid the topic or minimize their feelings.

Characteristics of a Therapeutic Relationship

Core Qualities
  • Trust — patient feels safe, respected, and consistently cared for
  • Empathy — understanding patient feelings without judgment (not sympathy, which merges feelings)
  • Respect — acknowledging the patient's dignity, worth, and autonomy
  • Genuineness — being honest and authentic, not pretending to care
  • Boundaries — keeping the relationship professional, not personal

🚨
NCLEX Tip — Open-Ended Questions

Always use open-ended questions to encourage the patient to express themselves — avoid yes/no questions. Never say "Don't worry" — instead, acknowledge the emotion and encourage discussion.

Transference & Countertransference

Transference

The patient projects feelings about someone from their past (parent, partner, sibling) onto the nurse. The nurse reminds the patient of someone from their past, and the patient responds to the nurse with those old feelings — often anger, dependency, or affection.

Example: Patient treats the nurse like their abusive parent and becomes hostile for no apparent reason.

Countertransference

The nurse projects their own personal feelings onto the patient. The nurse's past experiences or unresolved emotions affect their professional judgment and reactions.

Example: The patient reminds the nurse of their own child — the nurse becomes overly protective and has difficulty maintaining therapeutic boundaries.

🚨
NCLEX Tip — Quick Memory Aid

Transference = Patient's feelings toward the nurse (patient transfers feelings from their past onto the nurse).
Countertransference = Nurse's feelings toward the patient (nurse transfers their own feelings onto the patient).

Handling Difficult Situations

If a Patient Becomes Angry or Aggressive
  • Remain calm; do NOT argue or threaten
  • Set limits: "I need you to lower your voice so we can talk calmly."
  • Offer choices; maintain patient dignity
  • Ensure safety — call for help if needed
If a Patient Expresses Suicidal Thoughts
  • Ask directly: "Are you thinking of harming yourself?"
  • Assess for plan, means, and intent
  • Stay with the patient — do NOT leave them alone
  • Notify the provider immediately
If a Patient Develops Romantic Feelings
  • Set firm, professional boundaries immediately
  • "I am here to support your health — this is a professional relationship."
  • Redirect focus to treatment goals
  • Document and report to supervisor if needed

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Therapeutic relationship = professional, goal-oriented, and time-limited
  • Four phases: Pre-Interaction → Orientation → Working → Termination
  • Pre-interaction = nurse reviews chart and self-reflects before meeting patient
  • Orientation = build trust; establish treatment goals and boundaries
  • Working phase = active problem-solving; encourage feelings; support coping
  • Termination = allow patient to express feelings about relationship ending; patient may feel abandoned
  • Transference = patient's feelings toward nurse; Countertransference = nurse's feelings toward patient
  • Never accept gifts from patients — blurs professional boundaries
  • Open-ended questions = best for communication; never say "Don't worry"
  • Best evidence-based therapy = Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for anxiety, depression, PTSD
  • If a patient expresses suicidal thoughts → Stay with them & notify the provider immediately
