What Is ECT?

Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is a medical procedure in which a brief electric current is passed through the brain to intentionally trigger a seizure. Performed under general anesthesia with muscle relaxants to prevent injury.

Despite its controversial history, ECT is considered a safe, effective, evidence-based treatment when used appropriately. Modern ECT does NOT cause the dramatic convulsions depicted in popular media.

Indications for ECT

When ECT Is Used
  • Severe, treatment-resistant Major Depressive Disorder — failed 2+ adequate antidepressant trials
  • Acute suicidal risk — when immediate response needed (faster than medication)
  • Severe Bipolar Depression or acute mania unresponsive to medications
  • Catatonia — especially when unresponsive to benzodiazepines
  • Schizophrenia — when medication-resistant; especially with mood component
  • Postpartum psychosis
  • Patients unable to tolerate medications — elderly, pregnant (pregnancy is NOT a contraindication; ECT may be safer than some medications for the fetus)

Contraindications

Absolute / Relative Contraindications
  • No absolute contraindications — benefit/risk must be weighed for each patient
  • Relative contraindications: Recent MI, severe cardiac arrhythmias, increased intracranial pressure, space-occupying brain lesion (tumor, AVM), aortic aneurysm, recent stroke, severe hypertension, severe COPD

Pre-Procedure Nursing Care

Pre-ECT Checklist
  1. Informed consent — patient (or legal guardian) must give written informed consent; explain procedure, risks, benefits, alternatives
  2. NPO — nothing by mouth for 6–8 hours (anesthesia)
  3. Remove hairpins, jewelry, nail polish, contact lenses, dentures
  4. Void — empty bladder before procedure
  5. Vital signs — baseline; withhold antihypertensives if ordered
  6. IV access established
  7. Medications held: Lithium, benzodiazepines, anticonvulsants may interfere with seizure threshold — hold as ordered
  8. Explain to patient: they will be asleep, won't feel the seizure, may wake with brief confusion and headache — this is normal
💡
Medication Considerations Pre-ECT
  • Hold: Benzodiazepines, anticonvulsants (raise seizure threshold, reduce effectiveness)
  • Hold: Lithium (increases CNS effects post-ECT)
  • Continue: Antihypertensives usually continued (physician decision)
  • MAOIs: Typically discontinued weeks before ECT

The ECT Procedure

1
Anesthesia induction — short-acting general anesthetic (e.g., methohexital or propofol)Patient is fully unconscious and unaware of the procedure.
2
Muscle relaxant administered — succinylcholinePrevents violent muscular contractions; body movement is minimal (slight twitching in feet).
3
Oxygenation — oxygen via mask before and afterProtects from hypoxia during apnea phase of muscle relaxant.
4
Electrode placement — bilateral or unilateral (right side preferred — less memory impairment)Brief electrical stimulus delivered; EEG confirms seizure ≥ 25 seconds.
5
Recovery monitoring — vital signs, oxygenation, level of consciousnessTypically occurs in recovery area; patient wakes within minutes.

Typical course: 6–12 sessions, given 3 times per week (Monday–Wednesday–Friday)

Post-ECT Confusion — What's Normal

Confusion, disorientation, and short-term memory impairment after ECT are expected and temporary. Reassure patient and family. Memory typically returns within hours; some short-term memory gaps may persist during the treatment course.

Side Effects of ECT

Common (Expected) Side Effects
  • Confusion and disorientation (resolves within hours)
  • Short-term memory impairment (during treatment course)
  • Headache
  • Muscle aches
  • Nausea
  • Fatigue
Rare / Serious Concerns
  • Retrograde amnesia (losing memories before ECT)
  • Anterograde amnesia (difficulty forming new memories)
  • Cardiovascular: transient arrhythmias
  • Risks of general anesthesia
  • Falls (due to confusion post-procedure)
💡
Memory & ECT — Important Clarification

Long-term memory effects of ECT are generally temporary. Memory typically improves after the treatment course ends. Unilateral electrode placement (right side) causes less memory impairment than bilateral placement, though bilateral may be more effective for severe depression.

Patient & Family Teaching

  • ECT is not painful — you will be asleep and won't feel it
  • Some confusion and memory lapses after each treatment are normal and temporary
  • Do not drive or make important decisions on ECT days
  • Have a family member or friend accompany patient
  • Treatment typically done 3 times per week; improvement usually seen after 3–6 sessions
  • Some patients need maintenance ECT (monthly) to prevent relapse
  • NPO 6–8 hours before each treatment — take only approved medications with small sips of water

Key Takeaways
  • ECT is used for severe, treatment-resistant depression and acute suicidal risk
  • Pre-ECT: NPO, consent, remove jewelry, void, hold benzo/anticonvulsants as ordered
  • Post-ECT: airway priority; reorient patient; expect confusion and headache
  • Confusion after ECT is normal and expected — do NOT panic
  • Memory impairment is usually temporary
  • Typical course: 6–12 treatments, 3x/week
  • Pregnancy is NOT an absolute contraindication
