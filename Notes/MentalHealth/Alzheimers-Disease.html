Mental Health

Alzheimer's Disease

NCLEX-focused — pathophysiology · stages · medications · safety priorities · caregiver support

Overview

Alzheimer's disease is the most common form of dementia, accounting for 60–80% of dementia cases. It is a progressive, irreversible neurodegenerative disease that destroys memory and cognitive function. No cure exists — treatment focuses on slowing progression and managing symptoms.

Pathophysiology

Brain Changes
  • Amyloid plaques — abnormal clumps of beta-amyloid protein between neurons; disrupt neuron communication
  • Neurofibrillary tangles — twisted strands of tau protein inside neurons; impair nutrient transport
  • Loss of acetylcholine (ACh) — major neurotransmitter for memory and learning; impaired in Alzheimer's
  • Progressive neuron death and brain atrophy (especially hippocampus — memory center)

Stages of Alzheimer's Disease

Early Stage (Mild)
  • Short-term memory loss (forgets recent events)
  • Misplaces objects frequently
  • Difficulty finding words
  • Gets lost in familiar places
  • Difficulty with complex tasks (finances, planning)
  • Personality/mood changes
  • Still relatively independent
Middle Stage (Moderate)
  • Increasing memory loss — forgets personal history
  • Cannot recall name of spouse or family members
  • Confusion about time, place, current events
  • Wandering behavior
  • Sundowning — increased confusion late afternoon/evening
  • Needs help with ADLs (dressing, bathing, toileting)
  • Behavioral symptoms: agitation, paranoia, repetitive movements
Late Stage (Severe)
  • Loss of ability to communicate verbally
  • Does not recognize family members
  • Total dependence for all ADLs
  • Bedridden or wheelchair-bound
  • Dysphagia — high aspiration risk
  • Incontinence (bowel and bladder)
  • Susceptible to infections (pneumonia, UTIs)
  • Loss of basic reflexes

Safety Priorities — NCLEX Focus

Wandering

Interventions
  • Door alarms and coded locks (not standard door locks — may know how to use them)
  • Identification bracelet with name and phone number
  • GPS tracking devices
  • Secure enclosed outdoor areas for safe wandering
  • Register with Safe Return Program (MedicAlert)
  • Camouflage or cover doors with curtains or mirrors

Sundowning

  • Maintain consistent daily routine
  • Increase activity and sunlight exposure during day
  • Provide soft lighting at dusk — avoid abrupt darkness
  • Avoid stimulants (caffeine) in afternoon
  • Keep environment calm and quiet in evenings

Dysphagia / Aspiration Risk (Late Stage)

  • Upright position for meals (90°); remain upright 30–60 minutes after eating
  • Soft or thickened foods/liquids as ordered by speech therapy
  • Small bites; check for food pocketing in cheeks
  • Suction at bedside

Falls Prevention

  • Remove throw rugs and clutter
  • Install grab bars, handrails
  • Ensure adequate lighting
  • Low bed position; bed alarms

Medications

Drug ClassExamplesMechanism / StageNotes
Cholinesterase InhibitorsDonepezil (Aricept), Rivastigmine, GalantamineInhibit acetylcholinesterase → ↑ ACh; Mild-moderate ADSE: N/V/D, bradycardia; give with food. Do NOT cure — only slow progression.
NMDA AntagonistMemantine (Namenda)Blocks excess glutamate (excitotoxicity); Moderate-severe ADOften combined with donepezil for moderate-severe stage
Atypical AntipsychoticsRisperidone, Olanzapine (low dose)For agitation, psychosis, aggression — use cautiouslyBlack box warning: increased mortality in elderly dementia patients
AntidepressantsSSRIs (Sertraline)For depression and anxiety comorbid with ADAvoid TCAs — anticholinergic effects worsen cognition
⚠️
Antipsychotics in Dementia — Black Box Warning

Atypical antipsychotics carry a black box warning for increased risk of death in elderly patients with dementia. Use only when other interventions have failed and risk/benefit has been carefully considered.

Redirect, not correctGently redirect to another topic or activity when distressed
Use visual cues (pointing, gestures)Compensates for verbal comprehension decline

Caregiver Support

Caregiver Burden Assessment & Support
  • Assess for caregiver burnout — depression, fatigue, social isolation
  • Refer to Alzheimer's Association and local support groups
  • Discuss respite care options (adult day care, short-term residential care)
  • Educate on disease progression and what to expect
  • Assist with legal/financial planning early (advance directives, power of attorney)
  • Validate caregiver emotions — grief (anticipatory), frustration, guilt

Key Takeaways
  • Alzheimer's = most common dementia; progressive and irreversible
  • Pathology: amyloid plaques + neurofibrillary tangles + ↓ ACh
  • Early: short-term memory loss. Late: total dependence, dysphagia
  • Donepezil: slows progression, does NOT cure; SE include bradycardia
  • Memantine: for moderate-severe; blocks excess glutamate
  • Wandering: door alarms, ID bracelet, GPS tracking
  • Sundowning: increase daytime activity and light; routine is key
  • Do NOT argue — use validation + redirection
  • Antipsychotics in dementia: black box warning — use only as last resort
