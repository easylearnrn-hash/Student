Levels of Anxiety

Mild
Heightened awareness; increased learning; restless; tension
Moderate
Narrowed perception; focus on immediate concern; needs direction
Severe
Greatly reduced perception; can't learn; physical symptoms; needs direction for every action
Panic
Loss of rational thought; terror; immobilized; may hallucinate; danger to self
💡
NCLEX Tip

Mild anxiety enhances learning and performance. Severe anxiety and panic impair learning — do not teach at this level. Address anxiety first.

Types of Anxiety Disorders

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

GAD
  • Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple areas of life
  • Symptoms ≥ 6 months
  • Difficulty controlling worry; fatigue; muscle tension; irritability; poor concentration; sleep disturbance
  • Treatment: CBT + SSRI/SNRI; Buspirone for chronic anxiety

Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder
  • Recurrent, unexpected panic attacks + persistent worry about future attacks
  • Panic attack: peaks within 10 minutes; 4+ physical symptoms
  • Symptoms: palpitations, chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, sweating, trembling, paresthesias, feeling of unreality, fear of dying or "going crazy"
  • Often associated with agoraphobia (avoidance of places where escape is difficult)
  • Treatment: SSRIs, CBT, breathing techniques
⚠️
Panic Attack Nursing Intervention
  • Stay calm; speak in short, simple sentences
  • Remain with patient — do NOT leave alone
  • Guide slow, deep breathing (breathe with patient)
  • Move to quiet, low-stimulation area
  • Administer PRN benzodiazepine as ordered

Phobias

Specific Phobia

Intense, irrational fear of a specific object or situation (spiders, heights, needles). Marked, immediate anxiety on exposure. Avoidance behavior.

Social Anxiety Disorder

Fear of social situations where scrutiny may occur. Fear of embarrassment or humiliation. Avoids speaking in public, eating in front of others.

Treatment: Systematic desensitization (gradual exposure), CBT

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD
  • Obsessions — intrusive, unwanted thoughts/images/impulses that cause distress
  • Compulsions — repetitive behaviors or mental acts performed to reduce anxiety
  • Person recognizes obsessions as irrational (ego-dystonic) — unlike OCPD
  • Compulsions are time-consuming (>1 hour/day) and cause significant impairment
  • Treatment: CBT with Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) + SSRI (Fluoxetine, Fluvoxamine)
💡
OCD Nursing Approach

Allow the ritual initially — do NOT interrupt compulsions abruptly (increases anxiety). Set limits on time spent on rituals gradually. Work with therapy team on response prevention.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD
  • Develops after exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence
  • Symptoms ≥ 1 month after the event
  • 4 Core Symptom Clusters:
ClusterSymptoms
Re-experiencingFlashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories, emotional distress at reminders
AvoidanceAvoiding thoughts/feelings related to trauma; avoiding external reminders
Negative alterations in cognition/moodPersistent negative beliefs, distorted blame, emotional numbness, anhedonia
HyperarousalHypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, irritability, sleep disturbance, difficulty concentrating
💡
PTSD Nursing Priorities
  • Establish safety and trust first
  • Do NOT force patient to recall the traumatic event
  • Trauma-informed care — avoid re-traumatization
  • Treatment: Trauma-focused CBT, EMDR; Sertraline or Paroxetine (FDA-approved)

Medications for Anxiety Disorders

Drug ClassExamplesUse / Key Notes
SSRISertraline, Escitalopram, ParoxetineFirst-line for GAD, panic, OCD, PTSD; takes 2–4 weeks
SNRIVenlafaxine, DuloxetineGood for GAD; also treats depression
BenzodiazepinesLorazepam, Diazepam, AlprazolamShort-term anxiety relief; PRN for panic attacks; risk of dependence; do NOT abruptly stop
BuspironeBuSparNon-habit forming; for chronic GAD; takes 2–4 weeks; not effective PRN
Beta-blockersPropranololPerformance anxiety; reduces physical symptoms only
⚠️
Benzodiazepine Safety
  • Never stop abruptly — risk of seizures
  • Additive CNS depression with alcohol
  • Risk of dependence — monitor closely
  • Elderly: increased fall risk

General Nursing Interventions for Anxiety

  • Remain calm; speak slowly and clearly
  • Provide a quiet, safe environment
  • Use therapeutic touch only with consent
  • Teach relaxation techniques: deep breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery
  • Avoid providing excessive reassurance (reinforces anxiety)
  • Acknowledge and validate anxiety without reinforcing avoidance
  • Set realistic, achievable goals to rebuild confidence

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Mild anxiety → helpful; Panic → dangerous
  • GAD → worry ≥ 6 months; SSRIs + CBT first-line
  • Panic attack → stay with patient, calm breathing, move to quiet area
  • OCD → do NOT interrupt rituals abruptly; ERP is gold standard
  • PTSD → do NOT force trauma recall; trauma-informed care; EMDR effective
  • Benzodiazepines → risk of dependence; never abruptly discontinue
  • Buspirone → not PRN; takes 2–4 weeks; no dependence risk
