Reproductive Health

Priapism

Urological emergency — prolonged erection, ischemic vs non-ischemic, causes, treatment

Definition

Priapism = prolonged, painful erection lasting >4 hours without sexual stimulation. It is a urological emergency — ischemia leads to permanent erectile dysfunction if untreated. Time = tissue.

Types

Ischemic (Low-Flow) — Most Common
  • Blood trapped in corpora cavernosa → no outflow
  • Painful, rigid erection
  • Tissue hypoxia — medical emergency
  • Cavernosal blood gas: hypoxic, acidotic
Non-Ischemic (High-Flow) — Rare
  • Unregulated arterial blood flow (usually from trauma)
  • Usually painless or less painful
  • Not immediately life-threatening
  • Often resolves without emergency treatment

Causes / Risk Factors

  • Sickle cell disease — most common cause in children (high-yield NCLEX)
  • Medications: trazodone (antidepressant), sildenafil (Viagra) (erectile dysfunction drug), antipsychotics (risperidone), anticoagulants
  • Spinal cord injury
  • Leukemia (hyperviscosity)
  • Trauma to genitals or pelvis

Signs & Symptoms

  • Persistent erection >4 hours
  • Painful, tender penis
  • Rigid shaft but soft glans (tip)
  • No sexual arousal involved

Diagnosis

  • History + physical exam
  • Cavernosal blood gas — determines ischemia (low O₂, low pH = ischemic)
  • Penile Doppler ultrasound — assesses blood flow

Treatment

Ischemic Priapism (Emergency)
  1. Aspiration of blood from corpora cavernosa using sterile needle
  2. Intracavernosal injection of phenylephrine (vasoconstrictor → reduces arterial inflow)
  3. Surgical shunt if unresponsive to above
Non-Ischemic
  • Often resolves without emergency treatment
  • Ice packs, pressure to perineum
  • Observation and follow-up
🚨
NCLEX High-Yield Tips

Sickle cell + priapism = priority emergency. Painful erection >4 hours = call provider STAT. Trazodone or sildenafil = possible cause. Time = tissue — delayed treatment = permanent erectile dysfunction. Treatment = aspiration + phenylephrine injection.

NCLEX Summary
