Reproductive Complications

Ectopic pregnancy, miscarriage, placenta previa, abruption, preeclampsia, eclampsia, testicular torsion, prostatitis

Female Reproductive Complications

1. Ectopic Pregnancy

Fertilized egg implants outside the uterus — most commonly in the fallopian tube. Risk: tube rupture → internal hemorrhage → emergency.

  • Signs: Unilateral (one-sided) abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, shoulder pain (referred from diaphragm irritation by blood), low BP/high HR if ruptured
  • Treatment: Methotrexate (if unruptured — stops cell division); surgery if ruptured; monitor hCG until zero
🚨
Emergency

Ruptured ectopic = hemorrhagic shock. Priority: IV access, fluids, prep for surgery. Methotrexate is teratogenic — handle with gloves.

2. Miscarriage (Spontaneous Abortion)

Pregnancy loss before 20 weeks.

Nursing: Emotional support, monitor bleeding and vitals, possible D&C. Offer grief counseling and referrals.

3. Placenta Previa

Placenta attaches over or near the cervix (blocking the birth canal).

  • Key sign: Painless bright red bleeding in 2nd or 3rd trimester
  • NEVER perform vaginal exam (can cause massive hemorrhage)
  • Management: Bed rest, monitor fetal heart rate, plan C-section delivery

4. Placental Abruption

Placenta prematurely separates from uterine wall. Life-threatening for mother and baby.

  • Signs: Sudden severe abdominal pain, dark red bleeding, rigid board-like uterus, fetal distress
  • Treatment: Emergency delivery, monitor vitals/fluids/bleeding
Previa vs Abruption — NCLEX Quick Difference
  • Placenta Previa: Painless bright red bleeding — no vaginal exam
  • Abruption: Painful dark red bleeding — rigid uterus — emergency delivery

5. Preeclampsia & Eclampsia

Preeclampsia

High BP + proteinuria after 20 weeks gestation. Signs: headache, blurry vision, swelling of hands/face, high BP.

Treatment: Monitor BP, may give magnesium sulfate to prevent seizures. Delivery is the only cure.

Eclampsia

Preeclampsia + seizures. Emergency: seizure precautions, administer magnesium sulfate IV, prepare for emergency delivery. Antidote for mag toxicity = calcium gluconate.

Male Reproductive Complications

6. Testicular Torsion

Twisting of the spermatic cord → cuts off blood supply to the testicle. Surgical emergency.

  • Signs: Sudden, severe scrotal pain; swelling and redness; nausea/vomiting; absent cremasteric reflex; pain NOT relieved by lifting scrotum
  • Treatment: Immediate surgical detorsion (within 6h to save testicle)

7. Prostatitis

Inflammation or infection of the prostate gland.

  • Signs: Painful urination, low back/pelvic pain, fever and chills, painful ejaculation
  • Treatment: Antibiotics (long course — 4–6 weeks), fluids, NSAIDs for pain

NCLEX Summary

Ectopic = one-sided pain + bleeding = emergency. Previa = painless bright red = NO vaginal exam. Abruption = painful dark red + rigid uterus = deliver NOW. Eclampsia = Mag sulfate + calcium gluconate as antidote. Testicular torsion = sudden scrotal pain = emergency surgery within 6h. Miscarriage types: threatened (closed cervix) vs inevitable (open cervix).
