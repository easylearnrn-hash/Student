Reproductive Health

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Non-cancerous prostate enlargement — symptoms, medications, TURP overview, NCLEX priorities

What Is BPH?

BPH = non-cancerous enlargement of the prostate gland in aging men. The prostate surrounds the urethra, so when it grows, it compresses the urethra → urinary symptoms.

Risk factors: Men over 50, family history, hormonal changes, obesity, sedentary lifestyle.

Signs & Symptoms

SymptomMeaning
Urinary hesitancyTrouble starting urination
Weak stream / dribblingReduced urine flow; leaking after urination
NocturiaWaking at night to urinate
Incomplete emptyingBladder feels full after voiding
Frequency & urgencyNeeding to urinate more often and urgently

Diagnosis

  • DRE (Digital Rectal Exam): Palpate prostate for size and consistency (nodules = suspect cancer)
  • PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen): Blood test — mildly elevated in BPH; markedly elevated in cancer
  • Post-void residual ultrasound: checks how much urine remains after voiding
  • Urinalysis: rules out infection; uroflowmetry: measures urine flow rate

Treatment

Alpha-Blockers

Tamsulosin (Flomax), doxazosin

Relax smooth muscle in prostate/urethra → improved urine flow. Side effects: orthostatic hypotension, dizziness.

5-Alpha-Reductase Inhibitors

Finasteride (Proscar), dutasteride

Shrink prostate by blocking testosterone conversion. Takes 6+ months to see effect. Side effects: decreased libido, erectile dysfunction.

Surgery: TURP (Transurethral Resection of the Prostate) — most common surgical treatment for BPH refractory to medication.

📝
NCLEX Must-Knows

Weak stream + frequency + hesitancy = classic BPH. Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) = relax urethra. Finasteride = shrinks prostate but takes 6+ months. PSA elevated in both BPH and cancer — biopsy confirms cancer. Hard nodular prostate on DRE → suspect cancer. Urinary retention is a key complication.
