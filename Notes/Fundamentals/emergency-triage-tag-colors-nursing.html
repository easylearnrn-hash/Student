EMERGENCY TRIAGE TAG COLORS

🚨 Mass Casualty Incidents (MCIs): Emergency triage tag colors prioritize patients based on injury severity and survivability. This is critical for NCLEX disaster management questions.

Key Principle: Greatest good for the greatest number – focus on survivable cases with immediate needs.

THE FOUR TRIAGE TAG COLORS

RED TAPE – IMMEDIATE Priority 1

Meaning: Life-threatening injuries that are survivable with immediate intervention.

Examples:

  • Airway obstruction (requiring intervention)
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Severe bleeding
  • Open chest/abdominal wounds
  • Unstable vital signs

🎯 NCLEX Tip: Always assess red-tagged patients FIRST in triage priority. Interventions must be initiated immediately.

YELLOW TAPE – DELAYED Priority 2

Meaning: Serious but not immediately life-threatening injuries. Stable enough for care to be delayed without risk.

Examples:

  • Fractures (open or closed without compromised circulation)
  • Burns without airway involvement
  • Moderate blood loss
  • Conscious head injuries (no signs of increased ICP)

🎯 NCLEX Tip: These patients are seen after red-tagged cases. They can wait safely.

GREEN TAPE – MINIMAL / WALKING WOUNDED Priority 3

Meaning: Minor injuries. Can ambulate and care for themselves.

Examples:

  • Minor lacerations
  • Sprains and strains
  • Emotional distress
  • Closed fractures with good distal pulses

🎯 NCLEX Tip: Referred to as "walking wounded". These patients often help in care or evacuating others.

BLACK TAPE – EXPECTANT / DECEASED No Priority

Meaning: No signs of life OR injuries so severe that survival is unlikely even with immediate care.

Examples:

  • Massive head trauma
  • Cardiac arrest without response to CPR
  • Major burns covering >85% body surface

🎯 NCLEX Tip: Do NOT provide extensive interventions. Provide comfort measures only (e.g., pain relief if possible).

TRIAGE ASSESSMENT CRITERIA – START SYSTEM

START System = Simple Triage And Rapid Treatment

Use this systematic approach to quickly categorize patients in an MCI:

1. RESPIRATIONS

  • Not breathing → Reposition airway
  • If breathing starts → RED
  • If still apneic → BLACK
  • RR >30 or <10 → RED

2. PERFUSION (Capillary Refill or Radial Pulse)

  • Cap refill >2 seconds OR no radial pulse → RED

3. MENTAL STATUS

  • Unable to follow commands → RED
  • Follows commands → YELLOW or GREEN

🎯 Assessment Order: Always assess airway → breathing → circulation → consciousness in that order.

QUICK MNEMONICS

REMEMBER THE TAG COLORS:

  • 🟥 Red = "Rescue me NOW!"
  • 🟨 Yellow = "You can wait a bit."
  • 🟩 Green = "Go on your own."
  • ⬛ Black = "Beyond help."

HIGH-YIELD NCLEX TIPS

WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW FOR THE EXAM:

  • ❓ "Who do you treat first in an MCI?"
    → Choose the RED (Immediate) patient.
  • ❓ "Which patient can wait?"
    → Choose YELLOW.
  • ❓ "Who gets no aggressive intervention?"
    → Choose BLACK.
  • Key Principle: In MCI, greatest good for the greatest number applies. Focus on survivable cases.
  • Assessment Order: Always assess airway, breathing, circulation, and consciousness in that order.
  • Walking Wounded: Green-tagged patients can often assist with evacuation or basic care of others.
  • Black Tag ≠ No Care: Provide comfort measures (pain relief) if resources allow, but do not delay care for red/yellow patients.

NCLEX SCENARIO EXAMPLES

SCENARIO 1: Which patient should you assess FIRST?

Options:

  • A) Patient with a closed leg fracture, good pulses
  • B) Patient with severe bleeding from the abdomen
  • C) Patient with minor facial lacerations
  • D) Patient with no pulse, massive head trauma

✅ Answer: B – Severe bleeding from abdomen = RED tag (Priority 1). Life-threatening but survivable with immediate intervention.

SCENARIO 2: Which patient can wait for treatment?

Options:

  • A) Patient with open chest wound
  • B) Patient with closed femur fracture, stable vitals
  • C) Patient with airway obstruction
  • D) Patient in respiratory distress (RR 35)

✅ Answer: B – Closed femur fracture with stable vitals = YELLOW tag (Priority 2). Serious but not immediately life-threatening.

SCENARIO 3: Which patient is considered "walking wounded"?

Options:

  • A) Patient with severe burns covering 50% body
  • B) Patient with minor sprain, able to ambulate
  • C) Patient with tension pneumothorax
  • D) Patient unresponsive with massive head injury

✅ Answer: B – Minor sprain, able to ambulate = GREEN tag (Priority 3). Minimal injury, can self-care and potentially help others.

SCENARIO 4: Which patient receives comfort measures only?

Options:

  • A) Patient with moderate blood loss from arm laceration
  • B) Patient with cardiac arrest, no response to CPR
  • C) Patient with open leg fracture, good pulses
  • D) Patient with emotional distress, crying

✅ Answer: B – Cardiac arrest with no response to CPR = BLACK tag (No Priority). Survival unlikely even with immediate care; provide comfort measures only.
