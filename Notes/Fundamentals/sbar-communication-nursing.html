SBAR COMMUNICATION

SBAR is a standardized communication tool used by nurses and healthcare professionals to ensure clear, concise, and effective communication, especially during handoffs, physician calls, and critical situations.

WHAT DOES SBAR STAND FOR?

Letter Meaning Description
S Situation What is happening right now?
B Background What is the relevant context or history?
A Assessment What do you think is going on?
R Recommendation What do you want or need?

HOW TO USE SBAR

S – SITUATION (CURRENT ISSUE/PROBLEM)

What to Include:

  • State your name, role, unit
  • Identify the patient (name, age, location)
  • State the immediate issue
🧾 Example:

"This is Nurse Kim from 3B. I'm calling about Mr. John Doe, a 76-year-old with CHF (congestive heart failure). He's experiencing new-onset shortness of breath and an O₂ sat (oxygen saturation) of 88%."

B – BACKGROUND (CLINICAL HISTORY AND CONTEXT)

What to Include:

  • Explain the reason for admission
  • Give relevant past medical history, medications, recent procedures, or lab results
🧾 Example:

"He was admitted 3 days ago with an acute CHF exacerbation. He's on Lasix (furosemide), and his BNP (brain natriuretic peptide) yesterday was 740 pg/mL (high)."

A – ASSESSMENT (YOUR NURSING JUDGMENT OR CONCERN)

What to Include:

  • Share your clinical findings
  • State vital signs, mental status, pain score, and your interpretation
🧾 Example:

"His lungs have crackles, RR (respiratory rate) is 28, BP (blood pressure) is 150/90, and he's using accessory muscles. I think he's fluid-overloaded and decompensating."

R – RECOMMENDATION (WHAT YOU SUGGEST OR REQUEST)

What to Include:

  • Clearly state what you need: orders, tests, actions, or clarification
🧾 Example:

"I'd like you to evaluate him and consider increasing his diuretic or ordering a stat chest X-ray."

NCLEX-RN KEY TIPS

✅ WHEN TO USE SBAR:
  • Reporting a patient change to a provider
  • Handing off a patient during shift change or transfer
  • Escalating care concerns (e.g., critical labs, new symptoms)
✅ SBAR IMPROVES:
  • Patient safety 🛡️
  • Communication clarity 💬
  • Clinical decision-making 🧠
✅ NCLEX-STYLE QUESTIONS MAY ASK:
  • Which info belongs in each SBAR section
  • Which part to say first when calling the physician
  • How SBAR promotes safe, effective care

SBAR IN PRIORITY/DELEGATION QUESTIONS

🚨 IMPORTANT:
  • SBAR helps RNs prioritize what to report to the provider
  • In delegation, LPNs or UAPs can report objective data, but only RNs use SBAR to communicate assessments and recommendations

COMPLETE SBAR TEMPLATE

Component What to Say
S – Situation I am (name), (X) nurse on ward (X)
I am calling about (patient X)
I am calling because... I am concerned that...
(eg blood pressure is low/high, pulse is XX, temperature is XX, Early Warning Score is XX)
B – Background Patient (X) was admitted on (XX date) with... (eg MI/chest infection)
They have had (X operation/procedure/investigation)
Patient (X)'s condition has changed in the last (XX mins)
Their last set of observations were (XX)
Patient (X)'s normal condition is... (eg alert/drowsy/confused, pain free)
A – Assessment I think the problem is (XXX)
And I have... (eg given O₂/analgesia, stopped the infusion)
OR I am not sure what the problem is but patient (X) is deteriorating
OR I don't know what is wrong but I am worried
R – Recommendation I need you to...
Come to see the patient in the next (XX mins)
AND Is there anything I need to do in the meantime?
(eg stop the fluid/repeat the observations)
🎯 HIGH-YIELD NCLEX TIPS:
  • Always start with Situation – identify yourself and the problem immediately
  • Background provides context – keep it brief and relevant
  • Assessment is YOUR nursing judgment – use critical thinking
  • Recommendation shows you're proactive – suggest specific actions
  • SBAR keeps communication organized and professional
  • Practice SBAR format for shift handoffs and physician calls
