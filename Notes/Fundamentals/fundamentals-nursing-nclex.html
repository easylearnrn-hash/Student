NCLEX Study Guide

Fundamentals of Nursing

Core Curriculum 9 Sections ACNHS · 2026

Safety is a top NCLEX priority because it protects both the patient and the nurse.

This comprehensive review covers the essential fundamentals every nursing student must master for NCLEX success — from infection control and vital signs to legal ethics and medication administration.

01

Safety & Infection Control

Fall Precautions

  • Apply fall-risk wristband (yellow) and document risk level using the Morse or Hendrich scale
  • Maintain bed in lowest locked position at all times when not providing direct care
  • Ensure call light is within reach before leaving the bedside
  • Provide non-slip footwear; remove all environmental hazards
  • Raise 2 side rails only — raising all 4 constitutes a restraint and requires a physician order
  • Assess for orthostatic hypotension: BP drop ≥ 20 mmHg systolic when moving to standing

Standard vs. Transmission-Based Precautions

Standard Precautions — Applied to All Patients
  • Perform hand hygiene before and after every patient contact
  • Don gloves whenever contact with blood or body fluids is anticipated
  • Decontaminate shared equipment between patients
  • Dispose of sharps in an approved sharps container immediately after use
Precaution Type Diseases / Pathogens PPE Required Room Requirement
Contact MRSA, C. diff, VRE, wound infections Gloves + gown Private room preferred
Droplet Influenza, Mumps, Pertussis, Meningitis Surgical mask within 3 feet Private room or 3-ft separation
Airborne TB, Measles, Chickenpox (Varicella) N95 respirator + gown + gloves Negative-pressure AIIR room
Memory Tip

Airborne = Air = N95 + AIIR room. Think of the three “A”s: Airborne, Air, AIIR.

Hand Hygiene — Most Tested on NCLEX

Critical Rules
  • Use soap and water for C. diff or visibly soiled hands — alcohol does not destroy C. diff spores
  • Use alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR) for all other clinical situations
  • Minimum scrub duration: 15–30 seconds
  • Perform hand hygiene before and after glove removal
02

Vital Signs

Vital signs are the cornerstone of patient monitoring and provide early warning of clinical deterioration. Always assess trending changes, not only single values.

Parameter Normal Range (Adult) Intervene / Report If
Temperature 97.8–99.1 °F (36.5–37.3 °C) > 100.4 °F — possible infection or sepsis
Heart Rate (HR) 60–100 bpm < 60 or > 100 bpm; irregular rhythm
Respiratory Rate (RR) 12–20 breaths / min < 10 or > 24 breaths / min
Blood Pressure (BP) 90/60–120/80 mmHg SBP < 90 or > 180 mmHg
Oxygen Saturation (SpO2) 95–100 % < 90 % — hypoxemia; administer O2 immediately
Pain 0 (none) — 10 (worst) Always assess; reassess 30–60 min after any intervention
Report to Provider Immediately
  • Temp > 100.4 °F in immunocompromised or post-operative patients
  • SpO2 < 90 % — initiate supplemental O2 per protocol and notify provider
  • SBP < 90 mmHg — assess for shock; position supine or Trendelenburg
03

Physical Assessment

Systematic physical assessment follows a structured order to ensure thoroughness and avoid influencing clinical findings.

Standard Assessment Order — I-P-P-A

Inspect → Palpate → Percuss → Auscultate

Critical Exception — Abdominal Assessment

Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate

Palpating before auscultation stimulates bowel motility and produces artifactual, unreliable bowel sounds.

Key Assessment Terms

  • Inspect — Deliberate visual observation: lighting, symmetry, color, size, contour
  • Palpate — Systematic touch to assess texture, tenderness, masses, organ margins, and temperature
  • Percuss — Tapping to elicit sounds: tympany (gas), dullness (fluid or solid), resonance (air-filled lung)
  • Auscultate — Stethoscope to evaluate heart sounds, lung fields, and bowel sounds
  • HEENT — Standardized component: Head, Eyes, Ears, Nose, Throat
  • PERRLA — Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation
04

Documentation

Nursing documentation is a legal and clinical record. Accuracy, timeliness, and objectivity are non-negotiable professional standards.

Best Practice — Always Do
  • Record objective, measurable findings: “Wound measures 2 × 1 cm with erythematous margins and serous drainage.”
  • Quote patients verbatim: “Patient states, ‘I feel dizzy when I stand.’”
  • Document interventions and patient response immediately after providing care
  • Use approved EMR system or black ink; sign with full name, title, and license credentials
Prohibited — Never Do
  • Subjective opinions: “Patient is uncooperative” — document observable behaviors instead
  • Leave blank spaces in any documentation — draw a single line and initial
  • Erase, apply white-out, or overwrite any entry
  • Document care that was not actually performed
  • Backdate or alter a completed entry without proper labeling
Late Entry Protocol

Label clearly as “Late Entry” with the current date, time of entry, and a brief reason for the delay. Document factual findings only.

05

Patient Rights & Ethical Principles

Ethical Principle Definition Clinical Example
Autonomy Right of the competent patient to make self-determined decisions about their care Honoring a patient’s informed refusal of treatment
Beneficence Actively promote the patient’s best interest and well-being Administering prescribed analgesia proactively
Nonmaleficence Do no harm — avoid actions causing unnecessary injury or suffering Verifying medication allergy before every administration
Justice Fair, equitable distribution of care and healthcare resources Equal, unbiased care regardless of race, religion, or socioeconomic status
Fidelity Faithfulness to commitments and professional obligations Following through on a promise to return with pain medication
Veracity Truthfulness and transparency in all communications with patients Disclosing a medication error honestly and promptly

Informed Consent — NCLEX Must-Know

Critical Rules — Informed Consent
  • The provider (MD / NP / PA) is solely responsible for explaining the procedure, risks, benefits, and alternatives
  • The RN’s role is to witness the patient’s signature — not to obtain or explain consent
  • Patient must be competent, uncoerced, and adequately informed at time of signing
  • Consent is invalid if the patient is sedated, confused, intoxicated, or a minor without a legal guardian present
  • A DNR order does not mean withdrawal of all care — continue comfort and nursing care
06

Communication & Patient Teaching

Therapeutic Communication Techniques

  • Use open-ended questions: “Tell me more about how you’re feeling.”
  • Demonstrate active listening: maintain appropriate eye contact, therapeutic silence
  • Reflect and restate: mirror the patient’s own words to confirm understanding
  • Use a certified medical interpreter for language barriers — never rely on family members
  • Avoid “Why” questions — they can feel accusatory and close dialogue
  • Avoid false reassurance: never say “Everything will be fine.”

Effective Patient Teaching

  • Deliver all education at a 5th-grade reading level or lower
  • Use the teach-back method: ask the patient to explain instructions in their own words to confirm comprehension
  • Involve family members or caregivers when clinically appropriate and with patient consent
  • Prioritize discharge teaching: medications, follow-up appointments, warning signs, activity restrictions
07

Patient Positioning

Position Angle / Orientation Clinical Indication
High Fowler’s 90° Dyspnea, SOB, CHF, eating, NG tube insertion
Semi-Fowler’s (Fowler’s) 45–60° Promotes lung expansion; default post-operative positioning; tube feeding
Low Fowler’s 15–30° Post-op lumbar / spinal procedures; prevent aspiration in supine patients
Supine Flat, dorsal Spinal anesthesia recovery; lumbar puncture (LP) post-procedure (1–4 h)
Prone Face-down, ventral ARDS, COVID-19 with severe hypoxemia; posterior wound care
Sims’ (left lateral) Left side, semi-prone Enemas, rectal medications, colonoscopy prep, unconscious patient (airway)
Trendelenburg Head down, feet elevated Hypotension, hypovolemic shock (use with caution if ICP is elevated)
Lithotomy Supine, legs elevated & abducted Pelvic / GYN examinations, urinary catheter insertion
08

Medication Administration — The 6 Rights

Before Every Medication — Verify Allergies First

Check the patient’s allergy record in the MAR before administering any medication. Document the specific allergy and type of reaction clearly.

# Right Verification Method
1 Right Patient Scan wristband barcode; verbally confirm name and date of birth (two identifiers)
2 Right Drug Triple-check medication label against MAR; know generic and brand names
3 Right Dose Calculate carefully; verify against safe therapeutic range; consult pharmacist PRN
4 Right Route Confirm route: PO, IV, IM, SQ, SL, PR, topical — route determines bioavailability
5 Right Time Administer within ±30 minutes of scheduled time; STAT orders immediately
6 Right Documentation Record in MAR immediately after administration — never before
09

Common Nursing Procedures

Procedure / Skill High-Yield Clinical Points
Urinary Catheter (Foley) Strict sterile technique throughout insertion; monitor for CAUTI signs (cloudy urine, fever, suprapubic tenderness); measure UO hourly in critical patients; remove as early as possible
Wound Care Sterile technique for acute / surgical wounds; clean technique for chronic wounds; document dimensions, drainage color and amount, odor, surrounding skin integrity at every dressing change
NG Tube Confirm placement via pH ≤ 5.5 aspirate or chest X-ray before feeding or medications; measure residual volume PRN; maintain HOB ≥ 30° to prevent aspiration
IV Access Assess insertion site Q4–8H; Infiltration = cool, pale, swollen, non-bloody; Phlebitis = redness, warmth, tender cord; discontinue and resite both
Blood Draw / Laboratory Use correct tube: CBC → lavender (EDTA); CMP / BMP → green or SST; follow correct order of draw; label all specimens at the bedside immediately

High-Yield NCLEX Tips
  • Safety first — Prioritize Airway, Breathing, Circulation (ABCs) in every clinical scenario
  • Assess before acting — Always fully assess the patient before implementing any intervention
  • Hand hygiene is the #1 measure to prevent HAIs — perform it between every patient contact
  • 2 side rails only — All 4 rails up is legally classified as a restraint requiring a physician order
  • Teach-back method is the gold standard to confirm patient understanding of all discharge instructions
  • Document objectively — Describe measurable findings and behaviors only; never include personal opinions
  • Informed consent — The nurse witnesses, the provider educates. Confused or sedated patients cannot consent.
