CARE OF A CLIENT WITH A TUBE

What Kind of Tubes?

NCLEX mostly tests these types of tubes used in patient care.

TYPES OF TUBES

Tube What It Does
NG Tube (Nasogastric) Goes through nose into stomach for feeding or suction
G-Tube (Gastrostomy) Goes through belly into stomach for long-term feeding
Chest Tube Removes air, blood, or fluid from pleural space around lungs
Urinary Catheter Drains urine from bladder
Surgical Drains (JP, Hemovac) Remove fluid from surgical sites

GENERAL CARE RULES FOR ANY TUBE

ALWAYS FOCUS ON THESE FIRST:

  • Check placement: Is the tube where it's supposed to be?
  • Secure the tube: Prevent pulling or tension
  • Keep site clean and dry: Prevent infection
  • Monitor output: Amount, Color, Smell
  • Assess for complications: Infection (redness, swelling, fever), Blockage (tube not draining or feeding right), Dislodgement (tube moves or falls out)

🧠 NCLEX Tip: Unexpected changes in output = assess first, then call the doctor.

SPECIFIC TUBES – WHAT TO KNOW

NG TUBE CARE

  • Check placement before using:
    • X-ray (gold standard when first placed)
    • Check pH of stomach aspirate (should be acidic, pH 1-5)
  • Secure at nose to prevent pulling
  • Flush with water before and after meds/feedings
  • Measure output if suctioning
  • Oral care every 2 hours (nose and mouth can dry out)

🧠 NCLEX Tip: If patient coughs a lot or trouble breathing → suspect wrong placement (tube in lungs).

G-TUBE CARE

  • Clean around site daily with soap and water
  • Check for leakage around tube
  • Flush before/after feeding to prevent clogging
  • Rotate tube gently (if ordered) to prevent skin sticking
  • Keep feeding formula at room temp to avoid cramping

CHEST TUBE CARE

  • Keep drainage system below chest (gravity helps drain)
  • Check water seal chamber:
    • Bubbling = air leak (small bubbling ok if new pneumothorax)
    • Tidaling (water moves up/down with breathing) = normal
  • No clamping unless ordered (risk of tension pneumothorax)
  • Assess for sudden change in drainage (blood = bad)

🧠 NCLEX Tip: If chest tube comes out, cover with sterile gauze taped on 3 sides (air can escape but not enter).

URINARY CATHETER CARE

  • Keep bag below bladder (to prevent backflow infection)
  • No kinks in tubing
  • Empty bag regularly (at least every shift or if full)
  • Secure catheter to leg to prevent tugging
  • Hand hygiene before and after touching

🧠 NCLEX Tip: Signs of infection = cloudy urine, fever, foul smell.

SURGICAL DRAIN CARE (JP Drain, Hemovac)

  • Compress bulb (for JP) or spring (for Hemovac) to maintain suction
  • Measure output daily
  • Keep site sterile when emptying or stripping tubing
  • Record color (bloody, serous, cloudy)

NCLEX HIGH-YIELD REMINDERS

Key Points to Remember

  • Unexpected change? → Assess patient, then notify provider
  • Signs of infection → Redness, swelling, fever, pus
  • Keep drainage systems below site (gravity helps)
  • Flush feeding tubes before and after use
  • No clamping chest tubes unless ordered

MEMORY TRICKS

  • NG Tube = "No Gas" (prevents aspiration, decompresses stomach)
  • G-Tube = "Gut Tube" (direct to stomach through abdomen)
  • Chest Tube = "Below Chest, Tidaling Best" (keep low, look for tidaling)
  • Catheter = "Keep it Low, Let it Flow" (bag below bladder)
  • JP Drain = "Just Press" (compress bulb for suction)
