SKIN INTEGRITY & PRESSURE INJURIES - NCLEX FUNDAMENTALS

🎯 NCLEX Big Idea: Breaks in skin integrity allow infection, fluid loss, delayed healing, and increased mortality — especially in immobile or critically ill patients. Pressure injuries are largely preventable — prevention is a nursing priority.

Critical Principle: If skin breakdown is preventable and occurs, it is a failure in nursing care.

A. DEFINITION (What It Is & Why It Matters Clinically)

Pressure Injury Stages 1-4, Unstageable, and Deep Tissue Injury

SKIN INTEGRITY

Skin integrity refers to the intactness, strength, and health of the skin (the body's largest organ that protects against infection, fluid loss, and injury).

Functions of the skin:

  • Protection (barrier against bacteria and trauma)
  • Thermoregulation (temperature control through sweating and blood flow)
  • Sensation (touch, pain, pressure, temperature)
  • Fluid balance (prevents excessive fluid loss)
  • Vitamin D synthesis (important for bone health)

WHY IT MATTERS CLINICALLY:

Breaks in skin integrity allow:

  • Infection (pathogens enter the body)
  • Fluid loss
  • Delayed healing
  • Increased mortality, especially in immobile or critically ill patients

PRESSURE INJURY

A pressure injury (formerly pressure ulcer) is localized damage to the skin and/or underlying tissue, usually over a bony prominence, caused by prolonged pressure, shear, or friction.

Key mechanisms:

  • Pressure (constant force that compresses tissue and reduces blood flow)
  • Shear (sliding of skin layers when the skeleton moves but skin stays in place)
  • Friction (skin rubbing against surfaces, damaging superficial layers)

NCLEX MINDSET

"Pressure injuries are largely preventable — prevention is a nursing priority."

B. PURPOSE / IMPORTANCE (Why Nurses Prioritize Skin Integrity)

NURSES FOCUS ON SKIN INTEGRITY TO:

  • Prevent pressure injuries
  • Reduce hospital-acquired infections
  • Maintain comfort and dignity
  • Promote wound healing
  • Reduce length of hospital stay
  • Prevent sepsis (life-threatening response to infection)

NCLEX PRIORITY RULE:

If skin breakdown is preventable and occurs, it is a failure in nursing care.

C. RISK FACTORS FOR SKIN BREAKDOWN (Always Assess FIRST)

IMMOBILITY

  • Inability to reposition independently
  • Prolonged bedrest or wheelchair use

MOISTURE

  • Urinary incontinence (loss of bladder control)
  • Fecal incontinence (loss of bowel control)
  • Sweating or wound drainage

POOR NUTRITION

  • Protein deficiency (impairs tissue repair)
  • Dehydration (reduces skin elasticity)
  • Low albumin (protein made by liver indicating nutritional status)

DECREASED SENSATION

  • Spinal cord injury
  • Neuropathy (nerve damage)
  • Sedation or decreased level of consciousness

POOR CIRCULATION

  • Peripheral vascular disease (reduced blood flow to extremities)
  • Diabetes mellitus (impaired circulation and sensation)

D. PRESSURE INJURY STAGING (NCLEX MUST-KNOW)

Pressure Injury Staging Diagram (Stages 1-4)

STAGE 1

  • Intact skin
  • Non-blanchable erythema (redness that does NOT turn white when pressed)
  • Skin may feel warm, firm, or painful

⚠️ Key point: Stage 1 = skin is NOT broken.

STAGE 2

  • Partial-thickness skin loss
  • Involves epidermis and part of dermis
  • Appears as:
    • Shallow open ulcer
    • Blister (intact or ruptured)

STAGE 3

  • Full-thickness skin loss
  • Fat tissue visible
  • No bone, tendon, or muscle exposed
  • May have undermining (tissue destruction under intact skin edges)

STAGE 4

  • Full-thickness tissue loss
  • Exposed bone, tendon, or muscle
  • High risk for osteomyelitis (bone infection)

UNSTAGEABLE

  • Base covered by slough (yellow/white dead tissue) or eschar (black dead tissue)
  • Depth cannot be determined until removed

DEEP TISSUE PRESSURE INJURY

  • Purple or maroon discoloration
  • Indicates deep tissue damage
  • Skin may be intact or blistered

E. NURSING PRIORITIES (What NCLEX Wants FIRST)

Priority Action
1. Prevention Turn and reposition at least every 2 hours
2. Pressure relief Offload heels, use pressure-reducing surfaces
3. Moisture management Clean and dry skin promptly
4. Nutrition support Adequate protein and calories
5. Early identification Stage 1 injuries are reversible
6. Infection prevention Open skin = infection risk

PRIORITY RULE

"The best treatment for pressure injuries is prevention."

F. SAFETY & INFECTION CONTROL

Skin Care Safety

SAFE SKIN CARE:

  • Use gentle cleansers
  • Avoid vigorous rubbing
  • Apply moisture barrier creams
  • Do NOT massage over bony prominences (can worsen tissue damage)

Wound Care

WOUND CARE PRIORITIES:

  • Use clean or sterile technique as ordered
  • Cover open wounds appropriately
  • Monitor for infection signs:
    • Redness
    • Warmth
    • Purulent drainage
    • Fever

NCLEX TRAP:

Redness + fever + wound drainage = possible sepsis, not just skin issue.

G. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

OLDER ADULTS

  • Thinner skin
  • Decreased collagen (structural protein)
  • Reduced circulation
  • High pressure injury risk

PEDIATRICS

  • Fragile skin
  • Medical devices can cause pressure injuries

IMMUNOCOMPROMISED PATIENTS

  • Delayed healing
  • Increased infection risk

POST-OPERATIVE PATIENTS

  • Limited mobility
  • Pressure from surgical positioning

H. NCLEX TRAPS & COMMON MISTAKES

COMMON MISTAKES:

  • ❌ Massaging reddened bony areas
  • ❌ Waiting until skin breaks to intervene
  • ❌ Using donut cushions (increase pressure)
  • ❌ Ignoring nutrition in wound healing
  • ❌ Documenting pressure injuries as "inevitable"
  • ❌ Failing to reposition immobile patients

I. DELEGATION (RN vs LPN vs UAP)

Role Responsibilities
UAP • Reposition patients
• Perform basic skin care
• Report redness or breakdown
LPN • Perform wound care per orders
• Apply dressings
• Reinforce skin care education
RN • Perform skin assessments
• Stage pressure injuries
• Develop prevention plan
• Evaluate wound healing
• Coordinate interdisciplinary care

NCLEX RULE:

Assessment, staging, and planning = RN responsibility.

J. NCLEX QUICK MEMORY SECTION

KEY POINTS

Turn every 2 hours

RED FLAGS

  • Non-blanchable redness
  • Open wounds
  • Fever with skin breakdown
  • Black or yellow tissue
