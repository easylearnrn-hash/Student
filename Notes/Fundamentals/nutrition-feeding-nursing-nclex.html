NUTRITION & FEEDING - NCLEX FUNDAMENTALS

🎯 NCLEX Big Idea: Poor nutrition leads to delayed wound healing, infection risk, muscle wasting, electrolyte imbalance, aspiration, and increased mortality — all high-priority NCLEX concerns.

Critical Principle: A patient who cannot eat safely is at risk for airway compromise, not just malnutrition.

A. DEFINITION (What It Is & Why It Matters Clinically)

NUTRITION

Nutrition is the process by which the body ingests, digests, absorbs, transports, metabolizes, and excretes nutrients (substances in food needed for energy, growth, tissue repair, and regulation of body processes).

Key Nutrients:

  • Macronutrients (nutrients needed in large amounts):
    • Carbohydrates
    • Proteins
    • Fats
  • Micronutrients (nutrients needed in small amounts):
    • Vitamins
    • Minerals
  • Water (essential for circulation, temperature regulation, cellular function)

👉 Why it exists: Nutrition maintains cellular metabolism (chemical processes that keep cells alive), immune function, wound healing, muscle strength, and organ function.

WHY IT MATTERS CLINICALLY:

Poor nutrition leads to:

  • Delayed wound healing
  • Infection risk
  • Muscle wasting (loss of muscle mass)
  • Electrolyte imbalance (abnormal mineral levels)
  • Aspiration (inhalation of food or liquid into lungs)
  • Increased morbidity and mortality

FEEDING

Feeding is the method by which nutrition is delivered to the patient:

  • Orally (by mouth)
  • Enterally (through gastrointestinal tract using feeding tube)
  • Parenterally (through intravenous access, bypassing GI tract)

🎯 NCLEX Focus: How a patient is fed is as important as what they are fed.

B. PURPOSE / IMPORTANCE (Why Nurses Prioritize This)

NURSES PRIORITIZE NUTRITION & FEEDING TO:

  • Maintain energy balance (adequate calories for metabolic demands)
  • Prevent malnutrition (deficiency of nutrients)
  • Prevent aspiration pneumonia (lung infection caused by aspirated food)
  • Support growth and development
  • Promote wound healing and tissue repair
  • Maintain electrolyte and fluid balance
  • Ensure medication effectiveness (many meds require food considerations)

C. NUTRITION & FEEDING ASSESSMENT (Always FIRST!)

1. NUTRITIONAL ASSESSMENT

Assess:

  • Height and weight
  • BMI (Body Mass Index) (weight-to-height ratio: underweight, normal, overweight, obesity)
  • Recent weight changes (unintentional weight loss = red flag)
  • Diet history (types of food, frequency, restrictions)
  • Appetite

Laboratory Values:

  • Albumin (protein made by liver; reflects long-term nutritional status)
  • Prealbumin (protein reflecting short-term nutritional changes)
  • Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium)

NCLEX TRAP:

Normal weight does NOT equal adequate nutrition!

2. FEEDING SAFETY ASSESSMENT

Assess:

  • Level of consciousness (alertness and ability to protect airway)
  • Gag reflex (protective reflex preventing aspiration)
    • Note: Absence does NOT automatically mean unsafe
  • Swallowing ability (risk for dysphagia = difficulty swallowing)
  • Dentition (teeth condition affecting chewing)
  • Positioning ability (must sit upright)

D. TYPES OF NUTRITION & FEEDING (Step-by-Step)

1. ORAL NUTRITION (PREFERRED ROUTE)

Oral feeding is the safest and most physiologic method when the patient can eat safely.

Nursing Actions:

  • Assist with feeding if needed
  • Cut food into small pieces
  • Provide adaptive utensils if needed
  • Allow adequate time
  • Ensure upright position (≥ 90 degrees)

SAFETY PRIORITY:

NEVER rush feeding → increases aspiration risk

2⃣ENTERAL NUTRITION (TUBE FEEDING)

Enteral nutrition is delivery of nutrition into the gastrointestinal tract through a feeding tube.

Common Tubes:

  • NG tube (Nasogastric tube) — Nose → Stomach (short-term)
  • OG tube (Orogastric tube) — Mouth → Stomach
  • PEG tube (Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy) — Surgically placed through abdominal wall into stomach
  • PEJ tube (Percutaneous Endoscopic Jejunostomy) — Surgically placed into jejunum (small intestine)

Tube Feeding Routes: NG, ND, NJ, Gastrostomy, and Jejunostomy Tubes

BEFORE FEEDING (CRITICAL STEPS!)

  • Verify tube placement (confirm location of tube tip)
    • Best: X-ray confirmation (gold standard)
    • pH testing (gastric pH < 5)
    • Visual aspiration of gastric contents
  • Check residual volume (amount of formula left in stomach from previous feeding)
    • Hold feeding if residual > 250-500 mL (per facility policy)
  • Assess bowel sounds (presence of intestinal activity)

NCLEX TRAP:

Do NOT use air bolus auscultation as a reliable placement check! (Outdated practice — air sounds can be misleading)

DURING FEEDING

  • Elevate head of bed 30–45 degrees (prevents aspiration)
  • Administer feeding slowly
  • Monitor for:
    • Nausea
    • Vomiting
    • Abdominal distention
    • Diarrhea

AFTER FEEDING

  • Keep head elevated 30–60 minutes (prevents aspiration)
  • Flush tube with water to prevent clogging (usually 30-60 mL)

3. PARENTERAL NUTRITION

Parenteral nutrition provides nutrients directly into the bloodstream, bypassing the GI tract.

Types:

  • TPN (Total Parenteral Nutrition) — Complete nutrition via central line
  • PPN (Peripheral Parenteral Nutrition) — Partial nutrition via peripheral IV

Indications:

  • Non-functioning GI tract
  • Severe malabsorption
  • Bowel obstruction
  • Severe pancreatitis

HIGH-RISK THERAPY:

TPN is hypertonic (high concentration) → infection and glucose risks

Critical Safety Points:

  • Must use central line (peripherally administered TPN damages veins)
  • Monitor blood glucose regularly (hyperglycemia risk)
  • Sterile technique for dressing changes
  • Do NOT stop abruptly (risk of hypoglycemia)
  • Use dedicated lumen (don't mix with other infusions)

E. NURSING PRIORITIES (NCLEX Loves This!)

PRIORITY ORDER:

  1. Airway Protection
    • Dysphagia → aspiration risk
    • If airway not protected, feeding is contraindicated
  2. Positioning
    • Upright before, during, after feeding
  3. Verification
    • Tube placement before enteral feeding
  4. Glucose Control
    • Especially with TPN
  5. Infection Prevention
    • Central line care
  6. Hydration
    • Nutrition without fluids = unsafe

F. SAFETY & INFECTION CONTROL

ASPIRATION PREVENTION (CRITICAL!)

  • Upright positioning (30–45 degrees minimum)
  • Thickened liquids if prescribed
  • Small bites
  • Slow pace
  • Speech-language pathology consult for dysphagia

TUBE FEEDING SAFETY

  • Never lay patient flat during or immediately after feeding
  • Label feeding tubing (enteral ≠ IV — prevent fatal medication errors!)
  • Change tubing per policy
  • Perform oral care (even if NPO — prevents pneumonia)

TPN SAFETY

  • Use dedicated lumen (don't mix with other medications/fluids)
  • Monitor blood glucose regularly (q6h or per policy)
  • Change dressing using sterile technique
  • Do NOT stop abruptly (risk of hypoglycemia — taper if needed)

G. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Population Key Considerations
Older Adults Decreased taste and smell. Dentition issues. Higher aspiration risk. May need softer diets.
Pediatrics Higher caloric needs per kg. Risk for dehydration quickly. Accurate weight-based calculations critical.
Post-Operative Patients Return of bowel sounds before feeding. Risk for ileus (temporary cessation of bowel motility).
Chronic Illness Cancer, renal disease, heart failure → altered nutritional needs. May need protein restriction (renal) or fluid restriction (HF).

H. NCLEX TRAPS & COMMON MISTAKES

COMMON MISTAKES (DON'T DO THIS!):

  • Feeding a lethargic patient without swallow assessment
  • Lowering head of bed during tube feeding
  • Forgetting to flush feeding tubes (causes clogging)
  • Ignoring residual volumes (risk of aspiration)
  • Stopping TPN suddenly (risk of hypoglycemia)
  • Assuming NPO patients don't need oral care (they do! Prevents pneumonia)

J. NCLEX QUICK MEMORY SECTION

MUST-REMEMBER:

  • Oral route preferred (safest, most physiologic)
  • Head of bed up 30–45° (during and after feeding)
  • Check placement before feeding (X-ray = gold standard)
  • Aspiration = AIRWAY emergency
  • TPN = high glucose + infection risk
  • Flush tubes after feeding (prevents clogging)

NCLEX ONE-LINER:

"IF THEY CAN'T SWALLOW SAFELY, THEY CAN'T EAT — NO EXCEPTIONS."

COMMON NCLEX SCENARIOS

SCENARIO 1: Aspiration Risk

Question: A patient with dysphagia is coughing during meals. What is the priority action?

✅ Answer: STOP feeding immediately (airway priority!), position upright, assess respiratory status, notify provider, request speech-language pathology consult for swallow evaluation.

SCENARIO 2: Tube Placement Verification

Question: Before administering tube feeding through a newly inserted NG tube, what is the priority nursing action?

✅ Answer: Verify tube placement via X-ray (gold standard). Do NOT feed until placement confirmed. Air bolus auscultation is NOT reliable.

SCENARIO 3: High Residual Volume

Question: The nurse checks gastric residual before tube feeding and obtains 300 mL. What should the nurse do?

✅ Answer: Hold the feeding, return residual to stomach (per policy), notify provider. High residual (>250-500 mL) indicates delayed gastric emptying = aspiration risk.

SCENARIO 4: TPN Complication

Question: A patient on TPN develops confusion, tremors, and diaphoresis. Blood glucose is 45 mg/dL. What happened?

✅ Answer: Hypoglycemia from abrupt TPN discontinuation. TPN contains high glucose; body produces insulin in response. If stopped suddenly, insulin remains high but glucose drops. Never stop TPN abruptly — taper gradually.

  • Airway protection is THE priority — if they can't swallow safely, they can't eat
  • X-ray confirms NG tube placement (gold standard)

  • Never use air bolus auscultation alone for tube placement
  • High residual (>250-500 mL) = hold feeding
  • Flush tubes after feeding (prevents clogging)
  • TPN = central line + glucose monitoring + sterile technique
  • Never stop TPN abruptly (hypoglycemia risk)
  • Label enteral tubing "For Enteral Use Only"
  • NPO patients still need oral care (prevents pneumonia)
  • Dysphagia = speech pathology consult
  • Normal weight ≠ adequate nutrition (check labs!)
