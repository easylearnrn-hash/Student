ELIMINATION & INTAKE AND OUTPUT (I&O)

🎯 NCLEX Big Idea: Impaired elimination leads to fluid imbalance, electrolyte imbalance, infection, toxicity, skin breakdown, and organ failure — all high-priority NCLEX concerns.

Key Principle: Changes in I&O often appear BEFORE vital signs change.

A. DEFINITION (What It Is & Why It Matters Clinically)

ELIMINATION

Elimination is the body's process of removing waste products (unusable or toxic substances produced by metabolism) through:

Urination (removal of liquid waste via kidneys and bladder) removes:

  • Urea (nitrogenous waste from protein breakdown)
  • Creatinine (waste from muscle metabolism)
  • Excess electrolytes (charged minerals like sodium and potassium)
  • Excess fluid

Defecation (removal of solid waste via intestines and rectum) removes:

  • Feces (solid waste: undigested food, bacteria, cells)
  • Bilirubin metabolites (waste from red blood cell breakdown)

INTAKE AND OUTPUT (I&O)

I&O is the measurement and comparison of all fluids entering the body (intake) versus all fluids leaving the body (output) over a specific time period (usually 8 or 24 hours).

Intake includes:

  • Oral fluids (water, juice, soup)
  • Enteral fluids (tube feedings given through gastrointestinal tract)
  • Parenteral fluids (fluids given intravenously into a vein)

Output includes:

  • Urine
  • Stool (if liquid)
  • Emesis (vomit)
  • Drainage (from tubes, wounds, surgical drains)
  • Excessive perspiration (sweating)

👉 Why it matters: I&O reflects kidney function, circulatory status, and overall fluid balance, which directly affects blood pressure, oxygenation, and tissue perfusion.

B. PURPOSE / IMPORTANCE (Why Nurses Monitor This)

NURSES MONITOR ELIMINATION & I&O TO:

  • Detect dehydration (fluid volume deficit — too little fluid in body)
  • Detect fluid overload (excess fluid volume — too much fluid in body)
  • Evaluate renal function (kidney ability to filter blood and make urine)
  • Prevent urinary retention (inability to empty bladder completely)
  • Prevent constipation (infrequent or difficult bowel movements)
  • Prevent urinary tract infection (UTI) (infection of bladder, urethra, or kidneys)
  • Identify early signs of shock (life-threatening circulatory failure)

C. NORMAL VALUES (NCLEX Must-Know Numbers)

URINARY OUTPUT

  • Normal adult urine output: ≥ 30 mL/hour
  • Minimum daily output: ~ 800–1,200 mL/day

BOWEL ELIMINATION

  • Normal bowel pattern varies: From 3 times/day to 3 times/week
  • Consistency matters more than frequency

FLUID BALANCE

  • Balanced I&O: Intake ≈ Output
  • Positive balance: Intake > Output (risk of fluid overload)
  • Negative balance: Output > Intake (risk of dehydration)

D. STEP-BY-STEP BREAKDOWN (Assessment → Action → Evaluation)

STEP 1: ASSESS ELIMINATION

Urinary Assessment

Assess:

  • Amount (mL/hour)
  • Color (clear, amber, dark, bloody)
  • Clarity (clear vs cloudy — cloudy suggests infection)
  • Odor (strong or foul suggests infection)
  • Frequency (how often)
  • Dysuria (painful urination)

Bowel Assessment

Assess:

  • Frequency
  • Consistency (hard, soft, liquid)
  • Color (brown normal, black suggests bleeding, clay suggests bile obstruction)
  • Straining (increases vagal response — can cause bradycardia)
  • Presence of blood or mucus

STEP 2: MEASURE INTAKE ACCURATELY

Include:

  • Oral fluids (measure cups)
  • IV fluids (rate × time)
  • Tube feedings
  • Flushes (saline used to flush tubes)

NCLEX TRAP:

Ice chips count as intake (usually 50% of volume).

STEP 3: MEASURE OUTPUT ACCURATELY

Include:

  • Urine (use urinal, hat, or Foley catheter bag)
  • Liquid stool
  • Emesis
  • Drainage from surgical drains

NCLEX TRAP:

Solid stool is NOT measured unless liquid.

STEP 4: COMPARE INTAKE VS OUTPUT

  • Identify trends
  • Report output < 30 mL/hour
  • Correlate with:
    • Blood pressure
    • Heart rate
    • Edema (swelling caused by fluid accumulation in tissues)
    • Lung sounds (crackles suggest fluid overload)

E. NURSING PRIORITIES (What Comes FIRST on NCLEX)

PRIORITY ORDER:

  1. Airway & Breathing
    • Fluid overload → pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs) → impaired gas exchange
  2. Circulation
    • Low output → decreased perfusion → shock risk
  3. Kidney Protection
    • Monitor output hourly in critical patients
  4. Skin Integrity
    • Incontinence → moisture → pressure injury
  5. Infection Prevention
    • Catheter care, hygiene

PRIORITY RULE:

"NO URINE = NO PERFUSION UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE"

F. SAFETY & INFECTION CONTROL (Always Tested)

URINARY CATHETERS

  • Use aseptic technique (sterile method to prevent infection) for insertion
  • Maintain closed drainage system
  • Keep bag below bladder level
  • Perform perineal care (cleaning of genital area) daily

BOWEL ELIMINATION SAFETY

❌ DO NOT give laxatives to:

  • Suspected bowel obstruction (blocked intestines)
  • Acute abdominal pain of unknown cause

✅ Encourage FIRST:

  • Fluids
  • Fiber
  • Mobility

Then consider medications if needed.

G. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

Population Key Considerations
Older Adults Decreased thirst sensation. Increased risk for: dehydration, constipation, urinary retention
Pediatrics Higher fluid needs per body weight. Monitor diapers: Weigh diapers (1 gram = 1 mL urine)
Post-Operative Patients Risk for: urinary retention (from anesthesia), ileus (temporary absence of bowel movement)
Immunocompromised Strict catheter care. Early removal of invasive devices

H. NCLEX TRAPS & COMMON MISTAKES

COMMON MISTAKES (DON'T DO THIS!):

  • Ignoring low urine output because vital signs are "normal"
  • Forgetting to count IV flushes as intake
  • Placing Foley bag on bed (causes backflow and infection)
  • Giving laxatives without assessing bowel sounds
  • Assuming dark urine is normal dehydration without assessment

J. NCLEX QUICK MEMORY SECTION

KEY NUMBERS (MEMORIZE THESE!):

  • Urine output ≥ 30 mL/hr (minimum normal)
  • 1 gram diaper = 1 mL urine (pediatric measurement)
  • Ice chips = 50% of volume (for intake calculation)

RED FLAGS (REPORT IMMEDIATELY!):

  • No urine in 6–8 hours
  • Dark, concentrated urine
  • Sudden weight gain (fluid overload)
  • Crackles in lungs (pulmonary edema)
  • Output < 30 mL/hour

NCLEX SHORTCUT:

"LOW URINE = LOW PERFUSION UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE"

COMMON NCLEX SCENARIOS

SCENARIO 1: Low Urine Output

Question: A patient has had only 20 mL urine output in the last 2 hours. Vital signs are stable. What is the priority action?

✅ Answer: Assess for bladder distention (palpate abdomen), check catheter patency if present, notify provider. Don't assume vitals = stable patient. Low output = kidney/perfusion problem until proven otherwise.

SCENARIO 2: Fluid Overload

Question: A patient's intake is 2,400 mL and output is 800 mL over 24 hours. The patient has crackles in lungs and +2 edema. What is the priority?

✅ Answer: Position in High Fowler's (airway first!), notify provider, anticipate diuretic order, restrict fluids, monitor respiratory status closely.

SCENARIO 3: Catheter Care

Question: The UAP places the Foley bag on the bed while transferring the patient. What should the nurse do?

✅ Answer: Immediately place bag below bladder level, educate UAP about infection risk from backflow, document incident per facility policy.

SCENARIO 4: Delegation Question

Question: Which task can the RN delegate to the UAP?

A) Measure and record I&O for stable patient
B) Assess urine color and clarity
C) Interpret I&O trends
D) Notify provider of low output

✅ Answer: A — UAP can measure and record. RN must assess, interpret, and make clinical decisions.

HIGH-YIELD NCLEX POINTS:

  • Low urine output = perfusion problem until proven otherwise
  • I&O changes appear before vital sign changes (early warning!)
  • Ice chips = 50% intake (commonly tested)
  • Catheter bag below bladder (prevents infection)
  • No laxatives with bowel obstruction or acute abdominal pain
  • Weigh pediatric diapers: 1 gram = 1 mL urine
  • Fluid overload → High Fowler's first (airway priority)
  • RN assesses/interprets; UAP measures/records
  • Report output < 30 mL/hour immediately
  • Cloudy, foul-smelling urine = infection
