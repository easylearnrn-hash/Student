FAMILY DYNAMICS

WHAT ARE FAMILY DYNAMICS?

Family dynamics = how family members relate to and affect each other.

🧠 NCLEX TIP:

Family issues can impact patient care, healing, and coping.

TYPES OF FAMILY STRUCTURES

(KINDS OF FAMILIES)

Family Type Meaning
Nuclear family Parents and children living together
Extended family Includes grandparents, aunts, uncles living together
Blended family Step-parents and step-children mixed into one family
Single-parent family One parent raising child(ren) alone
Alternative family Living arrangements outside traditional (example: two friends raising a child)

FAMILY ROLES

(WHO DOES WHAT)

Decision Maker

Chooses important things (like treatments)

Caregiver

Provides direct care (helping dress, feed, etc.)

Financial Provider

Pays for household needs

Emotional Support

Helps with feelings, comfort

🧠 NCLEX TIP:

Role strain (stress) happens when one person has too much responsibility.

DYSFUNCTIONAL FAMILY DYNAMICS

(WHEN THINGS GO WRONG)

🛑 WARNING SIGNS:
  • Poor communication (arguing, not listening)
  • Role confusion (no one knows who's in charge)
  • Power struggles (fighting over control)
  • Neglect or abuse (ignoring needs or harming family members)
🧠 NCLEX TIP:

Family problems can cause stress for the patient and affect healing.

NURSE'S ROLE WITH FAMILY DYNAMICS

Action What to Do
Assess Who's involved? Who makes decisions?
Support Include family in teaching and care if the patient allows
Respect Different families may have different cultures and beliefs
Refer Get social work or counseling if needed

NCLEX HIGH-YIELD REMINDERS

✅ KEY TAKEAWAYS:
  • Family can be a resource OR a stress
  • Always respect the patient's definition of family (not just legal relationships)
  • Support communication and healthy coping
  • Refer to resources (like counseling) when family dynamics cause problems
💡 CLINICAL APPLICATION:
  • Ask patients: "Who do you consider family?" and "Who helps you at home?"
  • Include family in discharge planning and teaching (with patient permission)
  • Watch for signs of caregiver burnout or family conflict
  • Know when to involve social services, case management, or counseling
  • Respect cultural differences in family roles and decision-making
