PAIN ASSESSMENT - NCLEX FUNDAMENTALS

🎯 NCLEX Big Idea: Uncontrolled pain leads to increased stress response, poor oxygenation, delayed wound healing, immobility complications (blood clots, pneumonia, pressure injuries), and decreased quality of life — all high-priority NCLEX concerns.

Critical Principle: Pain is whatever the patient says it is. The patient's self-report is the most reliable indicator.

A. DEFINITION (What It Is & Why It Matters Clinically)

0-10 Numeric Pain Rating Scale

Wong-Baker FACES Pain Rating Scale (for children and non-verbal patients)

PAIN

Pain is an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage.

👉 Pain is subjective (experienced and described by the patient, not measured by the nurse).

Types of Pain:

  • Acute pain — short-term pain related to injury, surgery, or illness (expected to resolve)
  • Chronic pain — pain lasting longer than 3–6 months, often without ongoing tissue damage
  • Nociceptive pain — pain from tissue injury or inflammation (somatic or visceral)
  • Neuropathic pain — pain caused by nerve damage or dysfunction (burning, shooting, tingling)
  • Visceral pain — pain from internal organs (dull, aching, poorly localized)
  • Somatic pain — pain from skin, muscles, bones, or joints (sharp, well-localized)
  • Referred pain — pain felt in a different location than the source (e.g., heart attack causing jaw pain)

WHY PAIN EXISTS:

Pain is a protective mechanism that alerts the body to injury and promotes healing behaviors (rest, immobilization, seeking help).

WHY IT MATTERS CLINICALLY:

Uncontrolled pain leads to:

  • Increased stress response (activation of sympathetic nervous system)
  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure
  • Poor oxygenation
  • Delayed wound healing
  • Immobility → risk of blood clots, pneumonia, pressure injuries
  • Psychological distress and decreased quality of life

NCLEX MINDSET

"Pain is whatever the patient says it is. Period."

B. PURPOSE / IMPORTANCE (Why Nurses Assess Pain)

NURSES ASSESS PAIN TO:

  • Identify cause and type of pain
  • Determine severity and impact on function
  • Guide safe and effective pain management
  • Evaluate response to interventions
  • Prevent complications related to untreated pain
  • Advocate for the patient when pain is underestimated

NCLEX PRIORITY:

Pain assessment is required BEFORE AND AFTER any pain intervention.

C. PAIN ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORKS (NCLEX MUST-KNOW)

1. PQRST PAIN ASSESSMENT

Letter Component What to Ask
P Provocation/Palliation What makes it better or worse?
Q Quality Sharp, dull, burning, throbbing, stabbing, aching?
R Region/Radiation Where is the pain? Does it spread anywhere?
S Severity Rate pain 0-10. How intense is it?
T Timing When did it start? Constant or intermittent?

2. OLDCARTS (ALTERNATIVE FRAMEWORK)

  • O – Onset (when did it start?)
  • L – Location (where is the pain?)
  • D – Duration (how long does it last?)
  • C – Characteristics (what does it feel like?)
  • A – Aggravating factors (what makes it worse?)
  • R – Relieving factors (what makes it better?)
  • T – Timing (constant or intermittent?)
  • S – Severity (how bad is it?)

NCLEX TRAP:

Skipping a structured pain assessment = unsafe practice.

D. PAIN SCALES & MEASUREMENT (How Pain Is Quantified)

Comparative Pain Scale Chart (0-10 with facial expressions)

1. Numeric Rating Scale (NRS)

  • Scale 0–10
  • 0 = no pain
  • 10 = worst pain imaginable
  • Used for alert, oriented adults

2. Wong-Baker FACES Scale

Pain Scale 0-10 with Facial Expressions (Wong-Baker Style)
  • Facial expressions representing pain levels
  • Used for:
    • Pediatrics
    • Patients with language barriers
    • Patients with mild cognitive impairment

3. FLACC Scale

FLACC = Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability

Category 0 Points 1 Point 2 Points
Face No particular expression or smile Occasional grimace or frown Frequent to constant frown, clenched jaw
Legs Normal position or relaxed Uneasy, restless, tense Kicking or legs drawn up
Activity Lying quietly, normal position Squirming, shifting back and forth Arched, rigid, or jerking
Cry No cry (awake or asleep) Moans or whimpers, occasional complaint Crying steadily, screams or sobs
Consolability Content, relaxed Reassured by occasional touching Difficult to console or comfort

Used for: Infants, young children, nonverbal patients

Scoring: 0 = no pain, 1-3 = mild pain, 4-6 = moderate pain, 7-10 = severe pain

4. PAINAD Scale

PAINAD (Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia)

Category 0 Points 1 Point 2 Points
Breathing Normal Occasional labored breathing Noisy labored breathing, hyperventilation
Negative Vocalization None Occasional moan or groan Repeated calls out, loud moaning
Facial Expression Smiling or inexpressive Sad, frightened, frown Facial grimacing
Body Language Relaxed Tense, pacing, fidgeting Rigid, fists clenched, knees pulled up
Consolability No need to console Distracted or reassured by voice/touch Unable to console, distract, or reassure

Used for: Patients with severe cognitive impairment

Scoring: 0 = no pain, 1-3 = mild pain, 4-6 = moderate pain, 7-10 = severe pain

NCLEX RULE:

Choose the appropriate pain scale based on patient ability, not nurse preference.

E. NURSING PRIORITIES (What Comes FIRST on NCLEX)

Priority Action
1. Believe the patient Pain is subjective
2. Assess before intervene Location, severity, type
3. Identify red flags Chest pain, sudden severe headache, abdominal rigidity
4. Treat pain promptly Especially acute pain
5. Reassess effectiveness 30–60 minutes after intervention

PRIORITY RULE:

Unrelieved pain is a patient safety issue.

F. SAFETY & INFECTION CONTROL

Pain vs Emergency Conditions

PAIN MAY SIGNAL:

  • Myocardial infarction (heart attack) — chest pain, jaw/arm radiation
  • Internal bleeding — abdominal pain, hypotension
  • Compartment syndrome — severe pain unrelieved by medication
  • Infection — localized pain with fever

NCLEX TRAP:

Do NOT mask pain before assessing potential life-threatening causes.

Medication Safety

ASSESS:

  • Allergies
  • Renal function (kidney ability to clear drugs)
  • Respiratory status (opioids depress breathing)

MONITOR FOR:

  • Respiratory depression (slow or shallow breathing)
  • Sedation
  • Constipation (especially with opioids)

G. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

OLDER ADULTS

  • Pain often underreported
  • Higher sensitivity to opioids
  • Increased risk for falls and confusion

PEDIATRICS

  • Use age-appropriate scales
  • Behavioral cues matter (crying, withdrawal)

POST-OPERATIVE PATIENTS

  • Acute pain expected
  • Pain control improves mobility and lung expansion

CHRONIC ILLNESS

  • Multimodal pain management needed
  • Risk of tolerance and dependence

H. NCLEX TRAPS & COMMON MISTAKES

COMMON MISTAKES:

  • ❌ Assuming calm patients are not in pain
  • ❌ Using vital signs alone to assess pain
  • ❌ Ignoring nonverbal cues
  • ❌ Failing to reassess after medication
  • ❌ Believing pain complaints must "match" diagnosis
  • ❌ Withholding pain meds due to fear of addiction in acute pain

I. DELEGATION (RN vs LPN vs UAP)

Role Responsibilities
UAP • Report pain complaints
• Observe nonverbal signs
• Assist with comfort measures
LPN • Administer prescribed pain medications
• Reinforce nonpharmacologic measures
• Monitor and report response
RN • Perform comprehensive pain assessment
• Interpret findings
• Develop pain management plan
• Evaluate effectiveness
• Educate patient and family

NCLEX RULE:

Pain assessment and evaluation = RN responsibility.
