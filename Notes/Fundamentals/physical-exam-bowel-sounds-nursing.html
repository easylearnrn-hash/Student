PHYSICAL EXAM & BOWEL SOUNDS

Physical examination is a systematic approach to assessing the patient's body using four key techniques. Understanding the correct order is essential for accurate assessment, especially for the abdomen.

Remember: Most body systems follow I-P-P-A (Inspect, Palpate, Percuss, Auscultate), but the abdomen is different!

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION TECHNIQUES

CRITICAL NCLEX CONCEPT

General Physical Exam: I-P-P-A (Inspect → Palpate → Percuss → Auscultate)

Abdominal Exam: I-A-P-P (Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate)

Why the difference? Touching the abdomen first can alter bowel sounds and cause false findings!

1⃣ INSPECTION

Description:

Looking at the body to spot any obvious problems like swelling, discoloration, or unusual shapes.

Purpose:

  • Get initial information about the patient's condition
  • Identify visible signs of issues before touching
  • Note symmetry, color, contour, and movement

Order:

ALWAYS DONE FIRST to avoid changing any signs that could be affected by touching or tapping the body.

What to Look For:

  • Skin color and condition
  • Swelling (edema) or deformities
  • Scars, lesions, or rashes
  • Body symmetry
  • Respiratory pattern and effort

2⃣ AUSCULTATION

Description:

Listening to the sounds inside the body using a stethoscope.

Purpose:

  • Check the sounds of the heart, lungs, and digestive system
  • Detect abnormalities in organ function
  • Assess bowel sounds before manipulating the abdomen

Order:

Done SECOND for abdomen (before percussion or palpation) to prevent altering internal sounds.

What to Listen For:

  • Lungs: Breath sounds (clear, crackles, wheezes)
  • Heart: Heart rate, rhythm, murmurs
  • Bowel: Gurgling sounds (frequency and quality)
  • Blood vessels: Bruits (abnormal whooshing sounds)

3⃣ PERCUSSION

Description:

Tapping on the body to hear different sounds.

Purpose:

  • Find out if there is fluid, air, or solid masses under the skin
  • Assess organ size and position
  • Detect abnormal fluid accumulation (ascites)

Order:

Done THIRD for abdomen before palpation to avoid causing pain or tenderness that could change the findings.

Sounds You'll Hear:

  • Resonance: Hollow sound (normal over lungs)
  • Dullness: Thud-like sound (organs, fluid, masses)
  • Tympany: Drum-like sound (air-filled structures like stomach)
  • Flatness: Very dull sound (muscle, bone)

4⃣ PALPATION

Description:

Using hands to feel the body, especially the abdomen, for any unusual lumps, pain, or stiffness.

Purpose:

  • Check the size, shape, firmness, and location of body parts
  • Find any pain or discomfort (tenderness)
  • Assess temperature, texture, and mobility
  • Detect masses, organ enlargement, or rigidity

Order:

ALWAYS DONE LAST because it can be painful and may influence other exam results if done first.

Types of Palpation:

  • Light palpation: Gentle pressure to assess surface structures
  • Deep palpation: Deeper pressure to assess organs and masses
  • Always palpate tender areas LAST

BOWEL SOUND AUSCULTATION

NORMAL BOWEL SOUNDS

Description: Gurgling or rumbling noises made by the movement of the intestines.

Frequency: 5-30 sounds per minute (varies widely)

Characteristics:

  • Irregular, intermittent gurgling
  • Occur every 5-15 seconds
  • Sound like "gurgles" or "clicks"
  • Indicate normal peristalsis (intestinal movement)

Assessment Time: Listen in all 4 quadrants for at least 60 seconds before documenting "absent bowel sounds"

ABNORMAL BOWEL SOUNDS

Hyperactive (Increased):

  • Sounds: Loud, frequent, high-pitched
  • Causes: Diarrhea, gastroenteritis, early bowel obstruction
  • Significance: Increased intestinal activity

Hypoactive (Decreased):

  • Sounds: Rare, very quiet
  • Causes: Post-surgery, constipation, paralytic ileus
  • Significance: Decreased intestinal activity

Absent:

  • Sounds: No bowel sounds heard after 5 minutes of listening
  • Causes: Complete bowel obstruction, paralytic ileus, peritonitis
  • Significance: Medical emergency - report immediately!

ABDOMINAL AORTIC ANEURYSM (AAA)

🩺 AUSCULTATION FINDING:

A swishing or "bruit" sound may be heard over the abdomen if an abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) is present.

Cause: Turbulent blood flow through the aneurysm

Location: Typically heard over the aorta (midline, above umbilicus)

⚠️ CRITICAL NURSING ACTION:
  • If bruit detected: Report to provider immediately
  • DO NOT palpate deeply if AAA is suspected (risk of rupture)
  • Monitor for signs of rupture: Severe back/abdominal pain, hypotension, shock

KEY POINTS SUMMARY

Concept Key Information
Bowel Sounds Normally gurgling/rumbling; listen all 4 quadrants
Bruit Swishing sound = potential AAA; report immediately
Abdominal Exam Order Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate (I-A-P-P)
General Exam Order Inspect → Palpate → Percuss → Auscultate (I-P-P-A)
Why Different? Touching abdomen alters bowel sounds
Absent Bowel Sounds Must listen 5 minutes before documenting; emergency!
🎯 NCLEX HIGH-YIELD TIPS:
  • Abdomen = I-A-P-P (inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation)
  • All other areas = I-P-P-A (inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation)
  • Listen for 5 minutes before documenting absent bowel sounds
  • Bruit = AAA until proven otherwise → notify provider
  • Never deep palpate suspected AAA (rupture risk)
  • Hyperactive sounds = early obstruction or diarrhea
  • Absent sounds = complete obstruction or ileus (emergency)
  • Palpate tender areas LAST to get accurate assessment

"IAPP" for Abdomen - "I Always Postpone Palpation"

"IPPA" for Everything Else - "I Palpate, Percuss, Auscultate"
