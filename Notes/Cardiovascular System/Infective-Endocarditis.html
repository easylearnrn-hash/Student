Cardiovascular System

Infective Endocarditis

Valve infection, PATHOGENS mnemonic, embolic signs, Duke criteria, and treatment

Definition & Basics

What Is Infective Endocarditis?

  • Infection of the endocardium — inner lining of the heart, especially the valves
  • Characterized by vegetation (fibrin + platelets + organisms) on valve leaflets
  • Most commonly affects mitral valve (left side) and tricuspid valve (IV drug users → right side)
  • Can also involve chordae tendineae and septal defects
Types

Infective (Bacterial)

  • Staphylococcus aureus — most common overall; associated with skin infections, IV catheters, injection drug use, and acute infective endocarditis
  • Viridans group streptococci — associated with the mouth, poor dentition, dental manipulation, and subacute infective endocarditis
  • Enterococcus faecalis — associated with the GI/GU tract, older adults, and healthcare exposure
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci, especially Staphylococcus epidermidis — associated with prosthetic valves and implanted cardiac devices
  • HACEK organisms — uncommon, slow-growing gram-negative organisms that can cause infective endocarditis

Non-Infective (Sterile)

  • Marantic endocarditis, also called nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE) — most strongly associated with advanced cancer and hypercoagulable states
  • Libman–Sacks endocarditis — associated with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) and antiphospholipid syndrome
  • Vegetations are sterile — no microorganisms are present
  • Vegetations are made mainly of platelets and fibrin and may break off, causing systemic emboli
Pathophysiology — How Infective Endocarditis Develops
  1. Damage to valve — from trauma, turbulent blood flow, or pre-existing disease creates a rough surface
  2. Platelets + fibrin form a sterile clot (thrombus) on the damaged area
  3. Bacteremia — bacteria enter the bloodstream from dental work, IV drug use, procedures, or infection
  4. Bacteria attach to the clot and grow → forming infected vegetations
  5. Embolization — pieces of vegetation break off and travel through the bloodstream → causing organ damage (stroke, renal infarct, PE)

Clinical Signs & Symptoms

Classic Findings

  • Fever — most common sign; persistent, low-grade in SIE or high in AIE
  • New regurgitant heart murmur — indicates valve damage
  • Chills, malaise, fatigue, night sweats
  • Anorexia & weight loss — from chronic infection and inflammation
  • Splenomegaly — from immune activation and septic emboli
  • Positive blood cultures

Embolic Signs (Vegetations Break Off)

  • Petechiae — tiny red/purple skin spots from micro-emboli
  • Janeway Lesions — painless flat red spots on palms/soles
  • Osler Nodes — painful nodules on fingertips/toes
  • Roth Spots — retinal hemorrhages with pale center
  • Splinter Hemorrhages — nail bed linear hemorrhages
  • Stroke, renal infarct, PE (right-sided)
Memory Tip: Janeway = Painless Spots on Palms (J = Just lying there painless). Osler = Ouch (painful nodes on fingers).
Duke Criteria — Easy NCLEX Version

The Duke criteria help determine whether a patient has infective endocarditis. Major criteria = strong evidence. Minor criteria = supporting clues.

Major Criteria

  • Positive blood cultures — a typical endocarditis organism found in 2 or more separate blood-culture sets
  • Evidence of infection on the heart valve — echocardiogram shows a vegetation, abscess, or other valve damage; or new valvular regurgitation (new leaking heart valve)

Minor Criteria

  • Predisposition — previous valve disease, prosthetic valve, congenital heart disease, previous endocarditis, cardiac device, or injection drug use
  • Fever — temperature >38°C / 100.4°F
  • Vascular findings — emboli, Janeway lesions, septic pulmonary infarcts, or bleeding
  • Immunologic findings — Osler nodes, Roth spots, positive rheumatoid factor, or glomerulonephritis
  • Microbiologic evidence — positive culture or lab finding that suggests endocarditis but is not strong enough to count as a major criterion

Definite Infective Endocarditis

  • 2 major criteria
  • OR 1 major + 3 minor criteria
  • OR 5 minor criteria

Possible Infective Endocarditis

  • 1 major + 1 minor
  • OR 3 minor criteria
Memory Tip: Major = cultures and cardiac evidence. Minor = risk, fever, vascular, immune, and weaker microbiology evidence.
Diagnostic Tests

Workup

  • Blood cultures × 3 (from different sites, before starting antibiotics)
  • Echocardiogram — TEE (transesophageal) more sensitive than TTE for vegetations
  • CBC: leukocytosis, elevated ESR/CRP
  • Urinalysis: hematuria (renal emboli)
Treatment

Antibiotic Therapy

  • IV antibiotics for 4–6 weeks
  • Strep: Penicillin or Ampicillin
  • Staph: Nafcillin (or Vancomycin if MRSA)
  • Enterococcus: Ampicillin + Gentamicin
  • Blood cultures to confirm clearance

Surgical Indications

  • Valve destruction causing severe regurgitation
  • Uncontrolled infection despite antibiotics
  • Large vegetation (>10 mm) with high embolic risk
  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis
  • Fungal endocarditis
Nursing Priorities

NCLEX Focus

  • Obtain blood cultures BEFORE starting antibiotics
  • Monitor temperature — fever is first and most common sign
  • Assess for new murmur (indicates valve damage progression)
  • Assess for embolic complications: neuro changes, flank pain, sudden dyspnea
  • IV access maintenance — long-term IV antibiotics (PICC line often used)
  • Prophylaxis: Amoxicillin before dental procedures in high-risk patients
