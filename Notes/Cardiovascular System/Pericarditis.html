Cardiovascular System

Pericarditis

Inflammation of the pericardium — causes, friction rub, EKG findings, treatment, and vs MI comparison

Definition

What Is Pericarditis?

  • Inflammation of the pericardium — the fibrous sac surrounding the heart
  • Can be acute, recurrent, or chronic constrictive
  • Fluid can accumulate in pericardial space → pericardial effusion
  • Large effusion compresses the heart → Cardiac Tamponade (life-threatening complication)
Causes

Common Causes

  • Viral — most common (Coxsackievirus, Echovirus, Influenza)
  • Idiopathic — no identified cause (~85%)
  • Bacterial (TB, S. aureus)
  • Autoimmune (SLE, RA, scleroderma)
  • Post-MI (Dressler's Syndrome) — 1–6 weeks after MI

Other Causes

  • Uremia (renal failure) → uremic pericarditis
  • Malignancy (lung, breast, lymphoma)
  • Radiation therapy to chest
  • Post-cardiac surgery
  • Trauma
  • Medications (Hydralazine, Procainamide)
Signs & Symptoms

Classic Symptoms

  • Sharp, pleuritic chest pain — worsens with inspiration and lying flat
  • Relieved by leaning forward / sitting up (reduces pericardial stretch)
  • Radiates to left shoulder/trapezius ridge
  • Fever, malaise, myalgia (if viral)
  • Dyspnea

Pericardial Friction Rub

  • Pathognomonic sign of pericarditis
  • High-pitched, scratchy, grating sound
  • Best heard at left sternal border, with patient leaning forward
  • Triphasic (systole + 2 diastolic phases)
  • May disappear if effusion develops (fluid separates pericardial layers)
EKG Findings

Pericarditis EKG Pattern

  • Diffuse ST elevation — in most leads (saddle-shaped) — key differentiator from MI
  • PR depression — classic early finding (opposite of ST elevation)
  • No reciprocal ST depression (unlike MI)
  • T-wave inversion may appear in later stages
  • Electrical alternans: if large pericardial effusion (heart swings in fluid)
EKG Tip: In Pericarditis → ST elevation in ALL leads (diffuse). In MI → ST elevation in specific leads (territory-specific) WITH reciprocal changes.

Treatment

First-Line Treatment

  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen or Aspirin) — anti-inflammatory; reduce pain and effusion
  • Colchicine — added to NSAIDs; reduces recurrence risk
  • Rest and activity restriction during acute phase
  • Avoid strenuous exercise until symptom-free

Additional Management

  • Corticosteroids (Prednisone) — only if NSAID/Colchicine fails or specific cause (SLE)
  • Avoid NSAIDs in uremic pericarditis
  • Treat underlying cause
  • Bacterial: IV antibiotics
  • TB: anti-TB therapy
🚨 Complication — Cardiac Tamponade: Fluid accumulates in pericardial sac → compresses heart → Beck's Triad: Muffled heart sounds + JVD + Hypotension. Treatment: emergency pericardiocentesis.
Dressler's Syndrome: Autoimmune pericarditis occurring 1–6 weeks after MI or cardiac surgery. Treat with NSAIDs + Colchicine. Avoid anticoagulants (bleeding risk into pericardium).
