Cardiovascular System

Pulses

Pulse sites, assessment techniques, amplitude scale, and NCLEX nursing priorities

What Is a Pulse?

Definition

A pulse is the palpable rhythmic expansion of an artery caused by the contraction of the heart (left ventricular systole). It reflects:

  • Heart Rate (beats per minute)
  • Rhythm (regular vs. irregular)
  • Strength / Amplitude (weak, normal, bounding)
Common Pulse Sites

Pulse Assessment Locations

1. Apical
5th intercostal space, midclavicular line — most accurate pulse; used for cardiac meds (digoxin, beta-blockers), newborns, irregular rhythms
2. Radial
Inner wrist (thumb side) — most common site for routine pulse checks in adults
3. Brachial
Antecubital fossa (inner elbow) — used for BP measurement and infant CPR
4. Carotid
Neck (lateral to trachea) — used in emergencies (pulselessness, CPR assessment). Never palpate both sides simultaneously
5. Femoral
Groin (inguinal region) — assess circulation to lower extremities; used in trauma or shock
6. Popliteal
Behind the knee — difficult to find; essential for evaluating lower leg circulation
7. Posterior Tibial
Inner ankle (behind medial malleolus) — peripheral circulation check in lower limbs
8. Dorsalis Pedis
Top of the foot (dorsum) — assesses peripheral perfusion in diabetics, PAD, DVT
Pulse Amplitude Scale
ScaleDescriptionClinical Meaning
0AbsentNo pulse — assess immediately (arterial occlusion?)
1+Weak/ThreadyBarely palpable — reduced perfusion, shock, dehydration
2+NormalEasily palpable — expected finding
3+StrongEasily palpable, slightly increased
4+BoundingForceful — seen in fever, anxiety, anemia, hypertension
Pulse Equality

Always Compare Bilaterally

  • Compare both radial or both pedal pulses simultaneously
  • Asymmetry may suggest vascular obstruction or impaired perfusion
  • Absent pedal pulse + leg pain + pallor = arterial occlusion → emergency
Apical vs. Radial Pulse — Pulse Deficit

Apical-Radial Pulse Assessment

  • Two nurses simultaneously count apical (stethoscope over PMI) and radial pulse for 1 full minute
  • Pulse Deficit = Apical rate − Radial rate (normal = 0)
  • Pulse deficit indicates some heartbeats are not strong enough to produce a peripheral pulse
  • Seen in: A-fib, heart failure
NCLEX Priority Concepts

Always Assess Apical Pulse For:

  • Cardiac medications — digoxin, beta-blockers (hold if <60 bpm)
  • Newborns and infants
  • Irregular or weak peripheral pulses

Use Pedal Pulses To:

  • Evaluate circulatory status in diabetics
  • Assess compartment syndrome, DVT, PAD
  • Post-cardiac catheterization (femoral/radial site)
📌 NCLEX Rule: Digoxin — check apical pulse for 1 full minute before giving. Hold and notify provider if HR <60 bpm (adults) or <70 bpm (child) or <90 bpm (infant).
📌 6 P's of Acute Arterial Occlusion: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Poikilothermia (cold) → EMERGENCY — call provider immediately.
