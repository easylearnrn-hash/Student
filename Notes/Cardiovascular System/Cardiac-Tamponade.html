Cardiac Tamponade

Beck's Triad, pericardiocentesis, EKG findings, and emergency nursing management.

Cardiac Tamponade
Condition / Cause

Cardiac tamponade is a life-threatening condition in which fluid or blood accumulates in the pericardial sac — the protective sac around the heart.

  • Increased pressure compresses the heart
  • Prevents the ventricles from filling properly
  • Decreases stroke volumeAmount of blood pumped with each heartbeat
  • Quickly leads to decreased cardiac output and obstructive shock

Common Causes

  • Chest trauma
  • Pericarditis Inflammation of the pericardium
  • Cancer
  • Kidney failure
  • Cardiac procedures
  • Bleeding into the pericardial space
Symptoms
  • Sharp Chest PainPain during expiration or coughing.
  • OrthopneaDifficulty breathing when lying flat; a supine position is not recommended.
  • SOBDifficulty breathing even at rest or with minimal exertion.

Beck’s Triad (Hallmark Symptoms)

1
Hypotension
2
Jugular Venous Distension (JVD)
3
Muffled or Diminished Heart Tones
  • Pulsus Paradoxus An abnormal drop in systolic blood pressure when the patient breathes in. Normally it may drop a little during inspiration — in pulsus paradoxus it drops more than 10 mmHg. In cardiac tamponade, fluid squeezes the heart. When the patient breathes in, the heart has even less room to fill, so less blood is pumped out — the systolic number drops while the diastolic stays about the same, making the two numbers get closer together.
Treatment
  • 1. Pericardiocentesis
    • A procedure to remove excess fluid from the pericardial sac using a needle.
    • Has both therapeutic and diagnostic effects. The fluid is cultured to identify bacterial or microbial presence and antibiotic sensitivity, guiding appropriate treatment.
  • 2. Fluid Resuscitation
    • Intravenous fluids to maintain blood pressure and cardiac output.
    • Hydrate the patient before any fluid aspiration to prevent hypotension.
  • 3. Treatment of Underlying Cause
    • Address the root cause, such as infection, cancer, or autoimmune disease.
  • 4. Emergency Action
    • Cardiac tamponade is life-threatening.
    • Notify the provider or rapid response team immediately, give oxygen as ordered, place the patient on a cardiac monitor, establish IV access, and prepare the patient for emergency pericardiocentesis.
Monitor
  • Blood pressure and pulse pressure Monitor for hypotension and narrow pulse pressure, which show decreased cardiac output.
  • Heart rate and cardiac rhythm Monitor for tachycardia and dysrhythmias. ECG may show low-voltage QRS or electrical alternans.
  • Jugular venous distention (JVD) Assess for neck vein distention, which suggests blood is backing up because the heart cannot fill properly.
  • Heart sounds Auscultate for muffled or diminished heart sounds, caused by fluid around the heart.
  • Pulsus paradoxus Monitor for a systolic BP drop greater than 10 mmHg during inspiration.
  • Respiratory status and oxygenation Monitor shortness of breath, oxygen saturation, and work of breathing.
  • Signs of poor perfusion Monitor for cool clammy skin, weak pulses, confusion, restlessness, decreased urine output, and worsening shock.
Patient Assessment
  • Does the pain radiate? Radiating pain to the left arm, jaw, neck, back, or shoulder is more concerning for myocardial infarction (MI).
  • Is the pain above 8/10? Shows severity but does not clearly differentiate cardiac tamponade, MI, or pulmonary embolism (PE). Severe pain can occur with many conditions.
  • Does your cough produce blood-tinged sputum? Points more toward pulmonary embolism (PE) or a lung cause — not classic cardiac tamponade or MI.

Nurse Should Assess For

  • Beck’s Triad — hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds Strongly suggests cardiac tamponade because fluid is compressing the heart.
  • Pulsus Paradoxus — systolic BP drops >10 mmHg during inspiration Supports cardiac tamponade because the compressed heart cannot fill properly when the patient breathes in.
Pulsus Paradoxus
  • Normal: 120 / 80
  • During inspiration: 100 / 80
  • Systolic dropped from 120 → 100, diastolic stayed at 80 — numbers became closer together
