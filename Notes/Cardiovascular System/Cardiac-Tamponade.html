Cardiac Tamponade
Condition / Cause

Cardiac Tamponade — accumulation of fluid in the pericardial sac that compresses all four chambers, reducing cardiac output. A true life-threatening emergency.

  • Fluid ↑ intrapericardial pressure → ↓ ventricular filling → ↓ CO → obstructive shock
  • As little as 150–200 mL of rapidly accumulating fluid is enough — the faster it accumulates, the less time the heart has to compensate
  • Pericarditis — most common cause (viral, bacterial, uremic)
  • Trauma — penetrating (stabbing) or blunt chest injury
  • Post-cardiac surgery (hemopericardium), malignancy, aortic dissection
  • MI with free wall rupture, post-cath (iatrogenic), SLE, uremia, radiation
Symptoms
1
🔇 Muffled Heart Sounds
Fluid around the heart dampens auscultation — S1 & S2 are distant and hard to hear
2
📈 JVD — Jugular Vein Distension
Impaired right heart filling → backed-up venous pressure → visible neck veins
3
📉 Hypotension
↓ cardiac output → ↓ BP; narrow pulse pressure (<25 mmHg)
  • Pulsus ParadoxusSystolic BP drops >10 mmHg during inspiration — e.g. 120/80 on expiration → 109/80 on inspiration = 11 mmHg drop
  • Tachycardia — compensatory; heart speeds up to maintain output
  • Cool, clammy skin; ↓ urine output (shock state)
  • Dyspnea & orthopnea; anxiety; dysphagia (large effusion)
Treatment

Pericardiocentesis — definitive emergency treatment; needle aspiration of pericardial fluid.

  • Position patient semi-recumbent at 45°
  • Needle inserted subxiphoid, directed toward the left shoulder
  • Even 20–50 mL removed can dramatically ↑ cardiac output
  • Send fluid for culture, cytology, and chemistry
  • Surgical Pericardial WindowFor recurrent tamponade — permanent drainage opening
  • IV Fluid BolusMaintain preload — sustain CO until pericardiocentesis
  • High-Flow Oxygen

⚠️ AVOID — worsen the condition

  • Diuretics — DO NOT GIVEReduce preload → worsen cardiac output
  • Vasodilators — AVOID↓ venous return → worsens hemodynamic compromise
  • Positive Pressure Ventilation — AVOID if possible↓ venous return → further ↓ cardiac output
Note
  • First action: notify provider immediately + prepare for pericardiocentesis
  • DO NOT delay — patient can arrest within minutes
  • Post-procedure: monitor for re-accumulation — symptoms can return
  • Goal before procedure: keep preload up — fluids in, diuretics out
EKG & Diagnosis

Echocardiogram (ECHO) — gold standard; confirms pericardial effusion and chamber compression.

  • Electrical Alternans — pathognomonicBeat-to-beat variation in QRS amplitude = heart swinging in fluid
  • Low Voltage QRS in all leads
  • Sinus Tachycardia
  • Diffuse ST ElevationIf pericarditis is the underlying cause
  • “Water Bottle” Heart on CXREnlarged, flask-shaped cardiac silhouette
  • Pulsus Paradoxus >10 mmHgMeasure BP on expiration vs. inspiration
NCLEX Must Know
  • Beck’s Triad = Muffled sounds + JVD + Hypotension → tamponade until proven otherwise
  • ECHO = gold standard for diagnosis
  • Pericardiocentesis = definitive treatmentEven 20–50 mL removed = significant improvement in CO
  • Tamponade vs. Tension PTXBoth: JVD + hypotension. Tamponade: muffled sounds, NO tracheal deviation. Tension PTX: hyperresonance, tracheal deviation AWAY
  • Tamponade vs. MICough + chest pain worse on expiration = tamponade. MI does NOT cause cough.
Memory Tip
  • Beck’s = “Muffled Jug of Water” — the water bottle (CXR) makes muffled sounds, causes JVD, causes hypotension
  • Electrical Alternans on EKG = cardiac tamponade until proven otherwise
