Beck's Triad, pericardiocentesis, EKG findings, and emergency nursing management.

Cardiac Tamponade
Condition / Cause

Cardiac Tamponade — accumulation of fluid in the pericardial sac that compresses all four chambers, reducing ventricular filling and cardiac output. A true life-threatening emergency.

  • As little as 150–200 mL of rapidly accumulating fluid can cause tamponade — faster accumulation = less time to compensate
  • Fluid ↑ intrapericardial pressure → ↓ ventricular filling → ↓ cardiac output → obstructive shock
Causes
  • Pericarditis — most common (viral, bacterial, uremic)
  • Trauma — penetrating (stabbing) or blunt chest injury
  • Post-cardiac surgery (hemopericardium)
  • Malignancy — lung, breast, lymphoma
  • Aortic dissection
  • MI with free wall rupture
  • Post-catheterization (iatrogenic)
  • Autoimmune — SLE
  • Uremia / Radiation therapy
Signs & Symptoms

Beck's Triad — the three classic signs:

  • Muffled Heart Sounds Fluid around the heart dampens sounds — difficult to auscultate
  • JVD — Jugular Vein Distension Impaired right heart filling → backed-up venous pressure → visible neck veins
  • Hypotension ↓ cardiac output → ↓ BP; narrow pulse pressure
Additional Signs
  • Pulsus Paradoxus Systolic BP drops >10 mmHg during inspiration
    Example: 120/80 on expiration → 109/80 on inspiration = 11 mmHg drop
  • Tachycardia — compensatory; heart speeds up to maintain output
  • Narrow Pulse Pressure — <25 mmHg
  • Cool, clammy skin (shock)
  • Decreased urine output
  • Dyspnea & orthopnea
  • Anxiety, restlessness
  • Dysphagia — large effusion compresses esophagus
EKG & Diagnosis

Echocardiogram (ECHO) — gold standard; confirms pericardial effusion and chamber compression.

  • Electrical Alternans — pathognomonic Beat-to-beat variation in QRS amplitude = heart swinging in fluid
  • Low Voltage QRS in all leads
  • Sinus Tachycardia
  • Diffuse ST Elevation — if pericarditis is the underlying cause
Chest X-Ray & Other
  • "Water Bottle" Heart Enlarged, flask-shaped cardiac silhouette on CXR — classic tamponade finding
  • Pulsus Paradoxus >10 mmHg drop in systolic BP with inspiration = positive
Note
  • Electrical Alternans on EKG = cardiac tamponade until proven otherwise
  • Chest pain worse with expiration & coughing → think tamponade or pericarditis, NOT MI
  • MI does NOT typically present with cough
Treatment & Nursing Management
Emergency Treatment

Pericardiocentesis — definitive emergency treatment; needle aspiration of pericardial fluid.

  • Position patient semi-recumbent at 45°
  • Needle inserted subxiphoid, directed toward the left shoulder
  • Even removal of 20–50 mL can dramatically ↑ cardiac output
  • Send fluid for culture, cytology, and chemistry
  • Surgical Pericardial Window For recurrent tamponade — creates a permanent drainage opening
Note
  • First action: notify provider immediately + prepare for pericardiocentesis
  • DO NOT delay — patient can arrest within minutes
  • Post-procedure: monitor for re-accumulation — symptoms can return
Supportive Measures
  • IV Fluid Bolus Maintain preload — sustain cardiac output until pericardiocentesis is performed
  • High-Flow Oxygen — support oxygenation

⚠ MEDICATIONS / ACTIONS TO AVOID

  • Diuretics — DO NOT GIVE Reduce preload → worsen cardiac output
  • Vasodilators — AVOID ↓ venous return → worsens hemodynamic compromise
  • Positive Pressure Ventilation — AVOID if possible ↓ venous return → further ↓ cardiac output in tamponade
Note
  • Goal before pericardiocentesis: keep preload up — fluids in, diuretics out
  • Avoid anything that reduces venous return or preload
NCLEX Must Know
  • Beck's Triad Muffled heart sounds + JVD + Hypotension = tamponade until proven otherwise
  • Pulsus Paradoxus >10 mmHg Hallmark finding — measure BP on expiration vs. inspiration
  • Electrical Alternans on EKG Alternating QRS height = pathognomonic for cardiac tamponade
  • "Water Bottle" Heart on CXR Enlarged, flask-shaped cardiac silhouette
  • ECHO = gold standard for diagnosis
  • Pericardiocentesis = definitive treatment Even 20–50 mL removed = significant improvement in CO
Memory Tip
  • Beck's = "Muffled Jug of Water" — the water bottle (CXR) makes muffled sounds → JVD → hypotension
  • Tamponade vs. MI: cough + chest pain worse on expiration → tamponade; MI does NOT cause cough
  • Tamponade vs. Tension PTX: both cause JVD + hypotension — tamponade has muffled sounds, NO tracheal deviation
