Cardiovascular System

Ischemic Heart Disease (IHD)

Coronary anatomy, atherosclerosis, angina types, ACS, myocardial infarction, and the MONA protocol.

#1 IHD is one of the top causes of death worldwide
Higher Risk Risk goes up with age and heart disease risk factors
Less Blood Flow The heart muscle gets less blood than it needs
Ischemia Not just a drop in oxygen supply (hypoxia) — also a decrease in nutrient delivery and waste removal due to reduced blood flow to the myocardium.
Coronary Arteries & Blood Supply
  • Left Coronary Artery (LCA)
    • Branches into the Left Circumflex (LCX) — supplies the lateral wall of the left ventricle
    • And the Left Anterior Descending (LAD) — supplies 2/3 of the interventricular septum and the anterior wall of the LV
  • Right Coronary Artery (RCA)
    • Branches into the Posterior Descending Artery (PDA)
    • RCA & PDA supply the right ventricular wall, posterior wall of the septum, and the posterior inferior wall of the LV
Pathophysiology & Risk Factors
  • Atherosclerosis — primary cause of IHD
    • Lipid-based plaques form under the endothelium, narrowing coronary arteries
    • Stable Plaques — have a fibrous cap; cause stable angina that resolves with rest or vasodilators
    • Unstable Plaques — rupture, causing platelet aggregation and coronary thrombus; lead to unstable angina & MI
  • Coronary vasospasm — temporary coronary narrowing; severe or prolonged spasm can cause ischemia or MI
  • Microvascular dysfunction — small-vessel ischemia; can contribute to angina-like symptoms
  • Major risk factors — hyperlipidemia, hypertension, diabetes mellitus, metabolic syndrome, obesity, smoking, older age, and family history
Types of Angina
  • Stable Angina — caused by stable plaques; chest pain resolves with rest or nitroglycerin
  • Unstable Angina — caused by unstable plaques; chest pain may not resolve with rest or nitroglycerin; can progress to MI
  • Prinzmetal / Variant Angina — caused by transient vasospasm; chest pain resolves with vasodilators
Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) & Troponin
  • ACS includes unstable angina and myocardial infarction (MI)
  • NSTEMI — Non-ST Elevated MI; involves subendocardial infarction
  • STEMI — ST Elevated MI; involves transmural infarction
Troponin — Cardiac Cell Death Marker

Elevated troponin suggests myocardial cell injury/death. Reference ranges vary by facility; serial rising or falling troponins are most important for ACS evaluation.

Troponin INormal: < 0.04 ng/mL
Troponin TNormal: < 0.01 ng/mL
Myocardial Infarction (MI)

Acute condition causing irreversible necrosis of the heart muscle. Monitor closely post-MI to prevent recurrence.

Symptoms
  • Chest pain — squeezing, choking pressure
  • Shortness of breath (SOB), dyspnea
  • Hypoxia
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Epigastric pain — do not mistake for GI issues
Nitroglycerin Precautions
  • Store in a black bottle in a dark place — never in the car
  • Common side effect: headache (expected — vasodilation)
  • Can cause hypotension → orthostatic hypotension; patient should lie down when taking sublingually
  • Forms: sublingual tablets, patch (with gloves), cream (on arm), IV
  • Monitor for headache, hypotension, and dizziness
MONA Protocol for MI

Early priority: chewable Aspirin 162–325 mg for suspected MI unless contraindicated or allergic.

M
Morphine For persistent severe chest pain after nitroglycerin, per provider order. Assess pain location, radiation, severity, and response to treatment.

Stable Angina
Condition / Cause

Coronary arteries are narrowed by plaque buildup (fixed atherosclerotic cap).

  • During physical activity or stress, the heart needs more oxygen-rich blood than the narrowed arteries can supply
  • Results in chest pain — not just a drop in oxygen supply, but also a decrease in nutrient delivery and waste removal due to reduced blood flow
Symptoms

Symptoms are predictable and occur in a pattern — triggered by similar activities and relieved by rest or medication.

  • Chest Pain or Discomfort
    • Feels like pressure, squeezing, or fullness in the chest
    • Often triggered by exertion, stress, or cold weather
    • Usually lasts a few minutes and goes away with rest or nitroglycerin
  • Pain Radiating to Other AreasSpreads to arms, neck, jaw, shoulder, or back. Usually lasts < 10 minutes.
  • Shortness of BreathDifficulty breathing during physical activity or stress
  • FatigueUnusually tired during or after physical activity
  • Nausea — sometimes accompanied by lightheadedness
  • Sweating — cold, clammy skin
Treatment

Chest Pain Protocol — Nitroglycerin:

  • Dose 1: Take the first dose while lying down. If pain does not subside, call 911. The 2nd and 3rd doses provide 10 additional minutes for EMS to arrive.
  • Dose 2: If pain persists after 5 minutes, take a second dose.
  • Dose 3: If pain still persists, take a third dose. Maximum 3 doses, 5 minutes apart.

⚠ Signs of Stable → Unstable Angina

  • Nausea, vomiting, epigastric pain, and dyspepsia (indigestion)
  • These are early symptoms of MI — do not confuse with GI issues
Note
  • Stable angina is not a medical emergency by itself, but worsening pain patterns require urgent evaluation
  • Always check BP before giving nitroglycerin — hold if systolic BP < 90 mmHg
Monitor
  • Heart Rate & BPBefore and after nitroglycerin administration
  • Pain level and patternDuration, intensity, triggers, and response to nitroglycerin
  • EKGDuring episodes if possible; look for ST changes
Complications
  • Progression to Unstable Angina
  • Progression to Myocardial Infarction (MI)
Differentiate
  • Predictable — triggered by exertion or stress
  • Relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
  • Caused by fixed atherosclerotic plaque
  • Pain usually lasts < 10 minutes
Unstable Angina — NSTEMI & STEMI
Condition / Cause

A plaque in a coronary artery ruptures or forms a blood clot, partially or fully blocking the artery.

  • This significantly reduces blood flow to the heart
  • Unstable angina indicates that MI could occur soon
Symptoms
  • Sudden onset — may occur at rest or with minimal exertion
  • Chest PainFeels like pressure, squeezing, or severe tightness
  • May be more intense and longer-lasting than stable angina
  • Pain radiating to arms, neck, jaw, shoulder, or back
  • Difficulty breathing even without physical activity
  • Nausea — feeling sick to the stomach, possibly with vomiting
  • Profuse sweating — often cold and clammy
  • Unusual tiredness without a clear cause
  • Feeling faint or unsteady
  • Symptoms may not fully resolve with rest or nitroglycerin
Treatment

Initial Actions:

  • Place patient on continuous cardiac monitoring and obtain a 12-lead EKG quickly to evaluate for STEMI vs NSTEMI
  • Draw ordered blood tests:
    • Serial troponins — rising or falling values help confirm myocardial injury
    • CBC, CMP/electrolytes, and renal function
    • Coagulation studies if anticoagulation or cardiac catheterization is planned

Troponin Normal Ranges:

  • Troponin I — common reference: < 0.04 ng/mL
  • Troponin T — common reference: < 0.01 ng/mL
  • Ranges vary by facility and assay; serial trends are most important

Early Medication Priority:

  • Administer chewable Aspirin 162–325 mg for suspected MI unless contraindicated or allergic
MONA Memory Aid for MI
M — Morphine for persistent severe pain after nitroglycerin, per provider order
O — Oxygen for hypoxemia, respiratory distress, or SpO₂ < 90%
N — Nitroglycerin; monitor for headache, hypotension, and dizziness
A — Aspirin 162–325 mg chewable unless contraindicated
Monitor
  • EKGContinuous cardiac monitoring for ST changes
  • HR & BP
  • Troponin levelsSerial draws every 3–6 hours
Complications
  • Myocardial Infarction — heart muscle death due to complete artery blockage
Differentiate
  • Sudden onset — can occur at rest
  • More severe and longer-lasting than stable angina
  • May not be relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
  • Caused by rupture of an atherosclerotic plaque
  • Pain can last 20 minutes or more
Variant Angina — Prinzmetal's Angina
Condition / Cause

Coronary arteries experience temporary vasospasm, narrowing the arteries.

  • Spasms restrict blood flow to the heart, causing chest pain
  • Typically occurs at rest, often during the early morning
  • Not caused by plaque rupture — triggered by coronary artery spasm
Symptoms
  • Chest Pain Occurs at rest, often at night or early morning. Can be severe — pressure, squeezing, or tightness.
  • Pain Radiating to Other AreasArms, neck, jaw, shoulder, or back. Typically lasts a few minutes.
  • Shortness of BreathDifficulty breathing during an angina attack
  • Nausea — feeling sick, may be accompanied by vomiting
  • Sweating — cold, clammy skin
  • Palpitations — feeling of a rapid or irregular heartbeat
Treatment
  • Calcium Channel BlockersFirst-line treatment — relieve coronary vasospasm
  • Nitrates (Nitroglycerin)Relax and dilate coronary arteries
  • Avoid triggers: cold exposure, smoking, cocaine use
Note
  • Pain typically resolves spontaneously or with nitroglycerin
  • Unlike stable/unstable angina, Beta Blockers may worsen Prinzmetal's by promoting coronary spasm
  • EKG during an episode shows transient ST elevation that normalizes afterward
Monitor
  • EKGLook for transient ST elevation during episode; returns to baseline after
  • HR & BPEspecially when initiating calcium channel blockers
  • Symptom patternTime of day, frequency, triggers
Complications
  • Severe spasm can cause MI or arrhythmias even without fixed plaque
  • Recurrent spasms increase risk of sudden cardiac death
Differentiate
  • Occurs at rest — often at night or early morning
  • Caused by coronary artery spasm (not plaque)
  • Not triggered by exertion or stress
  • Responds to calcium channel blockers & nitrates
  • Pain typically lasts a few minutes
