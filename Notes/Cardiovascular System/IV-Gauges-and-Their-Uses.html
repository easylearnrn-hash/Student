IV Gauges & Blood Transfusions

IV catheter sizes, blood products, transfusion nursing, and transfusion reactions

IV Gauge Overview
🧠 Rule: Smaller number = Larger needle. Example: 14G is larger bore than 24G.
Mnemonic: "Big trauma? Go small gauge." — 14G = large bore → fast fluid delivery
GaugeColorUse
14GOrangeTrauma, massive hemorrhage, rapid fluid resuscitation, emergency surgery
16GGrayBlood transfusions (preferred), major surgery
18GGreenBlood transfusions (minimum), surgery, CT contrast
20GPinkGeneral IV meds, antibiotics, standard adults
22GBlueElderly, pediatric patients, small veins
24GYellowNeonates, infants, fragile veins
NCLEX Rule: Blood transfusions require at least 18–20G to prevent RBC hemolysis. Preferred is 16–18G.
Indications for Blood Transfusion

When to Transfuse

  • Anemia (low RBCs or hemoglobin)
  • Hemorrhage (active bleeding)
  • Clotting disorders
  • Surgery or trauma with blood loss
  • Thrombocytopenia (low platelets)

Pre-Transfusion Nursing Responsibilities

Before Starting a Transfusion

  • Verify the order — match correct patient, blood type, and product
  • Informed consent — patient or legal guardian must sign
  • Two-nurse verification — check patient ID, blood bank label, expiration, blood type at bedside
  • Obtain baseline vital signs before starting
  • Use blood administration tubing with 170-micron filter
  • Blood must be started within 30 minutes of release from blood bank
  • Run Normal Saline only — do NOT mix with D5W or other IV fluids

During Transfusion — Vital Sign Monitoring

  • Check vitals at: start, 15 min, end of transfusion
  • Administer slowly for first 15 minutes — most reactions occur here
  • Stay at bedside for first 15 minutes
  • Blood tubing changed every 4 hours or after 2 units
Transfusion Reactions — FAT CHIN Mnemonic

Signs of Acute Hemolytic Reaction (Most Dangerous)

Mnemonic: FAT CHIN

  • F — Fever
  • A — Anxiety
  • T — Tachycardia
  • C — Chills
  • H — Hematuria (red/dark urine — RBC hemolysis)
  • I — Impending doom (patient reports feeling something is wrong)
  • N — Nausea

Nursing Response to Transfusion Reaction

  • STOP the transfusion immediately
  • Keep IV line open with 0.9% Normal Saline
  • Notify provider and blood bank
  • Send blood bag and tubing back to blood bank
  • Collect urine sample (check for hemoglobin in urine)
  • Document all findings and interventions

• Never add medications to blood products
• Document: start/end time, vitals, patient tolerance, any reaction
• Pediatric/elderly patients may need slower rate and smaller volumes
