Shock Management

5 types of shock — early vs late signs, general treatment, vasopressors, and type-specific management

What Is Shock?

Definition

  • Shock = inadequate tissue perfusion → cells do not receive enough O₂
  • Results in anaerobic metabolism → lactic acidosis
  • Final pathway: multi-organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS)
  • Goal: restore oxygen delivery to tissues before irreversible organ damage

Early vs Late Signs of Shock

Early Shock (Compensated)

  • ↑ HR (tachycardia) — first compensatory sign
  • BP may be NORMAL (compensated)
  • Skin: pale, cool, clammy (vasoconstriction)
  • ↑ RR (tachypnea)
  • Anxiety, restlessness
  • Urine output slightly decreased

Late Shock (Decompensated)

  • ↓ BP (hypotension) — body can no longer compensate
  • Altered LOC, confusion → unresponsive
  • Anuric (urine output <30 mL/hr)
  • Mottled, cyanotic skin
  • Metabolic acidosis (lactic acid ↑)
  • MODS → cardiac arrest
NCLEX Priority: Tachycardia is the FIRST sign of shock. BP drops LAST. A normal BP does NOT rule out early shock.
General Shock Treatment

Initial Management (All Types)

  • Position: supine with legs elevated (Trendelenburg or modified) — EXCEPT cardiogenic → elevate HOB
  • High-flow oxygen (100% O₂ via NRB mask)
  • Establish IV access × 2 large-bore (16G or larger)
  • Monitor vital signs, SpO₂, urine output continuously
  • Foley catheter: monitor UO q1h (goal >0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Arterial line and central line in severe shock
  • Labs: ABG, lactate, CBC, BMP, coags, blood cultures (sepsis)
Type-Specific Treatment

Hypovolemic

  • IV fluid resuscitation (NS or LR)
  • Blood products if hemorrhagic (PRBCs, FFP)
  • Control source of bleeding
  • No vasopressors until volume replaced

Cardiogenic

  • Treat underlying cause (MI → PCI)
  • Inotropes: Dobutamine, Milrinone
  • Vasopressors: Norepinephrine (if hypotension persists)
  • Diuretics if fluid-overloaded
  • IABP or LVAD if refractory

Septic

  • Blood cultures × 2 BEFORE antibiotics
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour
  • 30 mL/kg IV fluid bolus within 3 hours
  • Norepinephrine: first-line vasopressor
  • Corticosteroids if refractory

Neurogenic

  • IV fluids (cautious)
  • Vasopressors: Norepinephrine or Phenylephrine
  • Atropine for bradycardia
  • Spinal immobilization

Obstructive

  • Cardiac tamponade → Pericardiocentesis
  • Tension PTX → Needle decompression (2nd ICS MCL)
  • PE → Thrombolytics or embolectomy
  • Remove the obstruction — no fluids/vasopressors alone will fix it
