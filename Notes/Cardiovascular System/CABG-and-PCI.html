CABG & PCI

Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting and Percutaneous Coronary Intervention — indications, pre/post care, complications

CAD Overview

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)

  • Atherosclerosis narrows coronary arteries → ↓ myocardial perfusion
  • Stable angina → Unstable angina → NSTEMI → STEMI
  • Two revascularization options: PCI (less invasive) or CABG (surgery)
  • Decision based on: number of vessels, anatomy, EF, comorbidities
PCI — Percutaneous Coronary Intervention

What Is PCI?

  • Catheter-based procedure via femoral or radial artery
  • Balloon Angioplasty: inflates balloon to compress plaque
  • Stent: metal mesh scaffold left in artery to keep it open
  • Drug-eluting stent (DES): coated with antiproliferative drug to prevent restenosis
  • Primary PCI: treatment of choice for STEMI within 90 min (door-to-balloon)

PCI Indications

  • STEMI / NSTEMI (acute MI)
  • Unstable angina refractory to medical therapy
  • 1–2 vessel CAD with suitable anatomy
  • When CABG risk too high (poor surgical candidate)
PCI — Pre & Post-Procedure Care

Pre-PCI

  • NPO status (usually 4–6 hrs)
  • IV access, type and screen
  • Antiplatelet therapy: Aspirin + Clopidogrel (P2Y12 inhibitor)
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin or Bivalirudin
  • Check creatinine (contrast nephropathy risk)
  • Assess and mark pedal pulses

Post-PCI Care

  • Vital signs q15 min × 1 hr, then q30 min × 2 hrs
  • Monitor puncture site for bleeding, hematoma
  • Keep affected limb straight (femoral: 4–6 hrs bed rest)
  • Encourage fluids to flush contrast dye (prevent nephropathy)
  • Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) for at least 1 year post-stent
  • Monitor for restenosis signs (return of chest pain)
🚨 PCI Complications: Bleeding at access site (most common), contrast-induced nephropathy, arterial dissection, re-occlusion/re-stenosis, stent thrombosis (emergency).
CABG — Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting

What Is CABG?

  • Open-heart surgery — grafts bypass blocked coronary arteries
  • Saphenous vein graft: from leg, reversed, attached around blockage
  • Internal mammary artery (IMA): left IMA to LAD — most durable graft (10+ yr patency)
  • Radial artery also used
  • Cardiopulmonary bypass (heart-lung machine) used during surgery

CABG Indications

  • Left main coronary artery disease (>50% stenosis)
  • 3-vessel CAD (especially with ↓ EF)
  • 2-vessel CAD involving proximal LAD
  • Failed PCI
  • Diabetic patients with multivessel CAD
CABG — Post-Op Care

Airway / Vent

  • Patient intubated post-op → extubated when awake and breathing adequately
  • Monitor ABGs
  • Incentive spirometry after extubation
  • Prevent atelectasis — deep breathing q1h

Chest Tubes

  • Mediastinal and pleural chest tubes present
  • Monitor output: >100 mL/hr for 2+ hrs = hemorrhage → notify
  • Keep tubing dependent, no kinks
  • Removed when output <25–50 mL/shift

Hemodynamics

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring
  • Atrial fibrillation: most common post-CABG arrhythmia (days 2–4)
  • Monitor for tamponade (Beck's Triad)
  • Maintain adequate MAP ≥65
Sternal Precautions

Post-CABG Activity Restrictions

  • No lifting >5–10 lbs for 6–8 weeks (sternal healing)
  • No pushing/pulling heavy objects
  • No driving for 4–6 weeks (airbag risk to sternum)
  • Splint sternum with pillow when coughing/sneezing ("heart pillow")
  • Report: sternal clicking, wound drainage, fever, increasing redness
