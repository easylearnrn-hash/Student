Right & Left-Sided Heart Failure

Side-by-side reference: causes, symptoms, treatment, monitoring, and complications.

Right-Sided Heart Failure
Condition/Cause

When the tricuspid valve doesn't close properly:

  • Blood leaks back into the right atrium instead of going to the lungs
  • Blood backs up into the systemic veins, causing body-wide congestion
Symptoms
  • Weight GainPeripheral edema — swelling in ankles & legs from fluid retention
  • JVD — Jugular Vein DistentionNeck veins visible when lying flat
  • AscitesAbdominal fluid buildup — tightness and discomfort
  • Shortness of BreathEven at rest
  • Hepatomegaly — Liver enlargement
  • Splenomegaly — Swollen spleen (in some cases)
  • Pitting EdemaIndentation remains when pressing swollen areas
  • Hypotension — Low blood pressure
  • TachycardiaRapid HR as the heart compensates for decreased efficiency
Treatment
  • Lasix (Furosemide) — Loop diuretic
    • Removes excess fluid from the body
    • Watch for potassium loss — monitor levels closely
  • Beta Blockers & Calcium Channel Blockers — Reduce HR and workload
    • STOP if HR < 60 bpm (adults)
    • STOP if HR < 70 bpm (children)
    • STOP if HR < 90 bpm (infants)
    • Same rule applies to Digoxin
  • Digoxin — Slows and strengthens heart rate
    • Can be used alongside beta blockers and CCBs
    • Requires careful dosage monitoring
Note
  • Position all HF patients in semi-Fowler's position (30–45°)
  • First thing RN does on entry: check patient position
  • Ideal target angle: 45 degrees
Monitor
  • Heart Rate Adjust medication dosage based on regular HR checks
  • Potassium Levels Lasix depletes potassium — supplement if necessary
Complications
  • Cor Pulmonale = COPD + Right-Sided HF Caused by pulmonary hypertension, commonly from COPD. Long-term pressure overload on the right side of the heart.
Note
  • Loop Diuretics lower potassium in the blood
  • Digoxin + Dehydration → risk of Digoxin toxicity
  • Digoxin with Furosemide: CAUTION
  • Early toxicity signs: nausea & vomiting
  • Late signs: visual disturbances — halos around objects
Left-Sided Heart Failure
Condition/Cause

When the mitral valve doesn't close properly:

  • Blood leaks back into the left atrium from the left ventricle instead of going to the aorta
  • Causes congestion and pressure buildup in the lungs
  • Blood returns to pulmonary circulation → respiratory symptoms dominate
Symptoms
  • Pulmonary EdemaFluid buildup in the lungs
  • Shortness of BreathDue to fluid in lungs
  • Dyspnea — Difficulty breathing
  • Crackles — Abnormal lung sounds
  • Rhonchi — Rattling lung sounds
  • HypoxiaLow oxygen levels in body tissues
  • Chest Pain — Discomfort or pressure
  • FatigueExtreme tiredness; reduced physical capacity
  • Hypertension — High blood pressure
Treatment
  • DiureticsReduce fluid buildup in the body
  • ACE Inhibitors or ARBsLower blood pressure and reduce cardiac strain
  • Aldosterone AntagonistsRemove excess sodium and water
  • DigoxinStrengthen contractions; slow heart rate — use with caution

⚠ MEDICATIONS TO AVOID

  • Beta Blockers — DO NOT GIVE Risk of bronchospasm, especially in patients with lung problems
  • Calcium Channel Blockers — AVOID Specifically Diltiazem & Verapamil — can worsen heart failure and cause bronchospasm
Note
  • Position all HF patients in semi-Fowler's position (30–45°)
  • First thing RN does on entry: check patient position
  • Ideal target angle: 45 degrees
Monitor
  • Oxygen Levels (SpO₂) Regularly check blood oxygen saturation
  • Blood Pressure Monitor to manage hypertension
