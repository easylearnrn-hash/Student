Cardiovascular System

Hypertension, Hypotension, CO & SV

Blood pressure physiology, cardiac output, stroke volume, and management of hypertension and hypotension

Key Formulas
BP = Cardiac Output (CO) × Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR)
CO = Stroke Volume (SV) × Heart Rate (HR)
MAP = (2 × DBP + SBP) ÷ 3 → Normal: 70–100 mmHg

Key Definitions

  • Blood Pressure (BP) — force of blood against artery walls; normal = <120/<80 mmHg
  • Cardiac Output (CO) — blood pumped by LV per minute; normal = 4–8 L/min
  • Stroke Volume (SV) — blood pumped per beat; normal = ~60–100 mL/beat
  • SVR (Systemic Vascular Resistance) — resistance the left ventricle must overcome
  • Preload — volume of blood returning to the heart (stretch)
  • Afterload — resistance the heart must overcome to eject blood

Risk Factors

  • Non-modifiable: age, family history, race (higher in Black Americans)
  • Modifiable: obesity, smoking, high Na⁺ diet, sedentary lifestyle, alcohol, stress

Complications of HTN

  • Stroke (CVA)
  • MI (myocardial infarction)
  • Heart failure
  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
  • Retinal damage

Hypertension Management

  • Lifestyle: DASH diet (↓ Na⁺, ↑ K⁺), exercise, weight loss, no smoking
  • First-line drugs:
  • → ACE Inhibitors (Lisinopril) — dry cough side effect; contraindicated in pregnancy
  • → ARBs (Losartan) — no cough; use if ACE inhibitor not tolerated
  • → Thiazide Diuretics (HCTZ) — first-line for most patients
  • → Calcium Channel Blockers (Amlodipine) — vasodilation
  • → Beta-Blockers (Metoprolol) — ↓ HR and contractility
🚨 Hypertensive Emergency: BP >180/120 + organ damage → IV antihypertensives (Nicardipine, Labetalol, Nitroprusside). Goal: reduce MAP by no more than 25% in first hour.
Hypotension

Definition & Causes

  • Systolic BP <90 mmHg OR diastolic BP <60 mmHg
  • Clinically significant when causing symptoms or organ hypoperfusion
  • Types: Orthostatic (positional), hypovolemic, neurogenic, cardiogenic
  • Causes: hypovolemia (bleeding/dehydration), HF, sepsis, meds (antihypertensives, diuretics), Addison's disease

Symptoms

  • Dizziness, fainting (syncope)
  • Blurred vision
  • Confusion
  • Tachycardia (compensatory)
  • Pale/cool skin
  • Low urine output

Management

  • Address underlying cause
  • IV fluids or blood products (if hypovolemic)
  • Vasopressors (Norepinephrine, Dopamine) if severe
  • Position: supine, legs elevated
  • Slow position changes (orthostatic)
