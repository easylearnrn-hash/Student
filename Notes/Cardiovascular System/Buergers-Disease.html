Cardiovascular System

Buerger's Disease

Thromboangiitis Obliterans (TAO) — tobacco-only arterial disease, BURGER mnemonic, and management

Definition

What Is Buerger's Disease?

  • Also called Thromboangiitis Obliterans (TAO)
  • Inflammatory occlusive disease of small and medium arteries and veins
  • Results in segmental thrombosis and fibrosis of vessels in the extremities
  • Almost exclusively caused by tobacco use (cigarettes or smokeless tobacco)
  • NOT atherosclerosis — it's an inflammatory, immune-mediated process
Who Gets It?

Typical Profile

  • Young men, typically under 45 years old
  • Heavy tobacco users — any form (cigars, pipes, chewing tobacco, e-cigarettes included)
  • Most common in Middle East, Asia, Southeast Europe
  • Women rarely affected but incidence rising with female tobacco use
  • Affects both upper and lower extremities (vs PAD = usually lower extremities only)
BURGER Mnemonic — Signs & Symptoms
B
Burning pain — in fingers, toes, feet (ischemic pain at rest)
U
Ulcers — painful ulceration of fingertips, toes
R
Redness / Rubor — when limb is dependent; Raynaud's-like color changes
G
Gangrene — dry gangrene of digits in advanced disease
E
Extremity cool — cool to touch, especially hands and feet
R
Reduced / Absent pulses — diminished pedal or radial pulses
Additional Signs: Migratory superficial thrombophlebitis (painful cord-like veins), intermittent claudication, nail changes, ischemic neuropathy.
Diagnosis

Allen's Test

  • Compress both radial and ulnar arteries at wrist
  • Have patient pump fist until palm blanches
  • Release one artery at a time
  • Abnormal: hand stays pale when artery released → occlusion confirmed
  • Helps assess upper extremity circulation

Diagnostic Studies

  • Doppler Ultrasound — assess flow in affected vessels
  • Angiography — "corkscrew collaterals" — pathognomonic sign
  • ABI (Ankle-Brachial Index)
  • Blood tests to rule out other causes (autoimmune, hypercoagulable state)
  • NO reliable biomarker — clinical and angiographic diagnosis
Treatment

#1 Treatment: STOP SMOKING

  • Absolute smoking cessation is the only intervention that halts disease progression
  • All other treatments fail if patient continues to smoke
  • Even a few cigarettes per day is enough to maintain disease activity
  • Nicotine patches/gums contain nicotine and may worsen the disease

Supportive Management

  • Wound care for ulcers
  • Calcium channel blockers for vasospasm (Nifedipine)
  • Prostaglandin analogues (Iloprost) for severe ischemia
  • Pain management (opioids for rest pain)
  • Amputation if gangrene develops and non-viable
  • Avoid cold environments (vasospasm trigger)

NCLEX Priority

Must Know

  • Young male + tobacco use + bilateral extremity ischemia = suspect Buerger's Disease
  • First and most important intervention: STOP ALL TOBACCO
  • Smoking cessation is NOT optional — disease WILL progress if patient smokes
  • Teach patient: even nicotine products can worsen condition
  • Monitor for gangrene progression → amputation may be necessary
