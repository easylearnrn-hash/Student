Cardiovascular System

Cardiomyopathy

Disease of the heart muscle — types, pathophysiology, signs/symptoms, and NCLEX nursing priorities

What Is Cardiomyopathy?

Definition

Cardiomyopathy = a disease of the heart muscle that makes it weaker, stretched, or stiff, affecting the heart's ability to pump blood effectively.

  • → Heart failure (heart can't pump enough blood)
  • → Dysrhythmias (abnormal heart rhythms)
  • → Sudden cardiac death
Types of Cardiomyopathy (All NCLEX-Tested)

A. Dilated Cardiomyopathy (Most Common)

  • Heart chambers stretch out (dilate), especially the left ventricle
  • Muscle becomes thin and weak → ↓ Ejection Fraction
  • Causes: genetics, alcohol/drug use, viral myocarditis, pregnancy (peripartum), hypertension
  • Signs: SOB, fatigue, edema, S3 gallop, displaced PMI

B. Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)

  • Heart muscle thickens (hypertrophies), especially the interventricular septum
  • Outflow tract becomes obstructed → ↓ cardiac output
  • Causes: genetic (autosomal dominant) — most common cause of sudden death in young athletes
  • Signs: SOB on exertion, syncope, chest pain, S4 gallop
  • Key: symptoms worsen with dehydration or Valsalva — do NOT give nitrates

C. Restrictive Cardiomyopathy

  • Heart muscle becomes stiff — can't stretch to fill with blood
  • Blood backs up into lungs or body
  • Causes: amyloidosis (protein buildup), sarcoidosis, radiation therapy

D. ARVC — Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy

  • Right ventricle muscle replaced with fat/scar tissue
  • Leads to dangerous arrhythmias — risk of sudden cardiac death
  • Often seen in young athletes
Pathophysiology (All Types)

Common Pathway Regardless of Type

  • ↓ Pumping ability → ↓ Cardiac output
  • → Fatigue, shortness of breath, fluid buildup
  • → Risk of heart failure or sudden cardiac death

Treatment

Medications

  • ACE inhibitors / ARBs — reduce cardiac workload
  • Beta-blockers — reduce HR and contractility
  • Diuretics — reduce fluid overload
  • Anticoagulants — prevent clots (especially in A-fib)
  • Antiarrhythmics (Amiodarone) — for rhythm control

Interventions

  • ICD (Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator) — for life-threatening arrhythmias
  • Cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT)
  • Heart transplant — end-stage dilated cardiomyopathy
  • AVOID alcohol, stimulants, high-sodium diet
NCLEX Nursing Priorities

Key Nursing Actions

  • Monitor daily weight — gain >2 lb/day = fluid retention → notify provider
  • Monitor I&O (intake/output), vital signs, SpO₂
  • Educate: no strenuous activity (especially HCM)
  • For HCM: avoid dehydration, Valsalva, nitrates — worsen obstruction
  • Position: HOB elevated 30–45° for dyspnea relief
  • Teach signs of worsening HF: SOB at rest, ↑ edema, sudden weight gain
🚨 NCLEX Alert: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is the most common cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes during exercise. Athletes with HCM should avoid competitive sports.
📌 Peripartum cardiomyopathy occurs in the last month of pregnancy or within 5 months postpartum — assess new moms for unexplained dyspnea and fatigue.
