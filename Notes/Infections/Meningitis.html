Meningitis

Inflammation of the meninges — bacterial is a medical emergency

Types of Meningitis

TypeCauseUrgencyNotes
BacterialN. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, Hib, ListeriaEMERGENCYAntibiotics STAT; high mortality without treatment
Viral (aseptic)Enteroviruses (most common), Herpes, MumpsModerateSelf-limiting; supportive care
FungalCryptococcus neoformansModerateCommon in HIV/AIDS patients
ParasiticNaegleria fowleri (brain-eating amoeba)FatalFrom warm freshwater; nearly 100% fatal

Classic Signs & Symptoms

Classic Triad:

  • Severe headache (sudden onset, "worst of life")
  • Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck — cannot flex chin to chest)
  • High fever

Also:

  • Photophobia (light sensitivity), phonophobia
  • Nausea, vomiting, altered mental status
  • Seizures
  • Petechiae / purpuric rash (meningococcal — hemorrhagic)

Infants:

  • Bulging fontanelle
  • High-pitched cry
  • Poor feeding, irritability, paradoxical irritability

Clinical Signs (Meningeal Irritation)

Kernig's Sign (+)

Flex hip to 90° → patient resists/experiences pain when knee is straightened

K = Knee straightening causes pain

Brudzinski's Sign (+)

Flex patient's neck forward → involuntary hip/knee flexion

B = Bend neck → Brings knees up

Diagnosis

  1. CT scan first — rule out ↑ ICP / mass before LP
  2. Lumbar puncture (LP) — gold standard for CSF analysis
⚠️ NCLEX PRIORITY: Do NOT delay antibiotics while waiting for LP results! Start IV antibiotics immediately, then LP if stable.

NCLEX KEY:

Bacterial meningitis CSF = cloudy + high neutrophils + LOW glucose + HIGH protein. Viral = clear + lymphocytes + normal glucose.

Treatment

Bacterial Meningitis — IMMEDIATE action:

  • IV Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin — broad-spectrum empiric therapy
  • Dexamethasone (steroid) — given with or before antibiotics to reduce inflammation
  • Add Ampicillin if Listeria suspected (elderly, immunocompromised, neonates)
  • Seizure precautions, darkened quiet room, HOB 30°
  • Monitor ICP: avoid neck flexion, no Trendelenburg

Viral: Supportive — rest, fluids, analgesics

Fungal: Amphotericin B + Flucytosine

Isolation & Infection Control

  • Droplet precautions for bacterial/meningococcal meningitis
  • Duration: first 24 hours after starting antibiotics
  • Surgical mask within 3–6 feet
  • Close contacts → prophylactic rifampin or ciprofloxacin

Prevention (Vaccines)

  • MenACWY — meningococcal conjugate; recommended at 11–12 yrs + booster at 16; required for college dorms
  • PCV13 / PPSV23 — pneumococcal vaccines
  • Hib vaccine — prevents H. influenzae type b meningitis in children

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Classic triad: severe headache + stiff neck + fever
  • Kernig's (knee pain) + Brudzinski's (knees up) = meningeal irritation signs
  • CT scan FIRST if ICP suspected, then LP
  • Start IV antibiotics IMMEDIATELY — do NOT wait for LP results
  • Bacterial CSF: cloudy, high neutrophils, low glucose, high protein
  • Droplet precautions for first 24 hours of antibiotics
  • Meningococcal rash = petechiae/purpura = medical emergency
