Mumps

Paramyxovirus affecting parotid glands — droplet transmission, MMR vaccine-preventable

Pathophysiology

  • Paramyxovirus (same family as measles)
  • Affects parotid (salivary) glands — causes parotitis
  • Incubation: 16–18 days
  • Contagious: 2 days before → 5 days after parotid swelling

Transmission

  • Droplet transmission (respiratory secretions)
  • Direct contact with saliva
  • Most common in unvaccinated children/young adults

Signs & Symptoms

  • Parotitis — painful swelling of one or both parotid glands (hallmark)
  • Jaw/ear pain, especially with chewing
  • Fever, headache, malaise
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)

Complications

  • Orchitis (most serious in post-pubertal males) → can cause infertility
  • Oophoritis in females
  • Viral/aseptic meningitis
  • Pancreatitis
  • Sensorineural hearing loss (permanent)
  • Encephalitis (rare)
🧠
NCLEX KEY:

Orchitis in post-pubertal males with mumps → potential cause of infertility. Ask about this in history.

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis:

  • Clinical diagnosis (parotid swelling + exposure history)
  • Serum IgM antibody (acute)
  • Viral culture from saliva
  • PCR for confirmation

Treatment (Supportive):

  • Rest and fluids
  • Acetaminophen/ibuprofen for pain & fever
  • Ice packs or warm compresses on parotid area
  • Soft diet (avoid chewing)
  • Scrotal support for orchitis

Isolation & Infection Control

  • Droplet precautions
  • Surgical mask within 3–6 feet
  • Isolate for 5 days after onset of parotid swelling
  • Private room preferred

Prevention

  • MMR vaccine (Measles-Mumps-Rubella) — live attenuated virus
  • Dose 1: 12–15 months; Dose 2: 4–6 years
  • Contraindicated: pregnancy, immunocompromised
  • No passive immunization available

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Parotid swelling + fever → think mumps
  • Droplet precautions for 5 days after swelling starts
  • Orchitis = main complication in post-pubertal males
  • MMR vaccine: live → avoid in pregnancy and immunosuppressed
  • Treatment = supportive only
