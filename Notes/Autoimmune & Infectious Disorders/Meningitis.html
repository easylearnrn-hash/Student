What Is Meningitis?

Meningitis is inflammation of the meninges (membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord). It can be caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites. Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency — untreated it is rapidly fatal, and even treated carries a significant risk of permanent neurological damage.

Types of Meningitis

TypeCommon CauseSeverityOutcome
Bacterial Neisseria meningitidis (meningococcal), Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Listeria monocytogenes (neonates/elderly) Medical emergency High mortality/morbidity if untreated; risk of deafness, brain damage, septicemia
Viral (aseptic) Enteroviruses (most common), HSV, mumps, HIV Usually self-limiting Most recover fully in 1–2 weeks without specific treatment
Fungal Cryptococcus neoformans (in HIV/AIDS) Severe in immunocompromised Treated with antifungals (amphotericin B)

Classic Triad of Meningitis

🧠
The Three Cardinal Signs — ALL THREE = Meningitis Until Proven Otherwise
  • Severe headache — sudden onset, "worst headache of my life"
  • Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck) — cannot flex neck forward; meningeal irritation
  • High fever — chills; may have sepsis

Additional signs: photophobia (light sensitivity), phonophobia (sound sensitivity), altered mental status, petechial/purpuric rash (bacterial meningococcemia)

Meningeal Signs

Physical Examination Signs
SignHow to TestPositive Result
Kernig's Sign Flex hip and knee to 90° → attempt to extend the knee Pain or resistance to knee extension = positive (meningeal irritation)
Brudzinski's Sign Passively flex the neck forward (patient supine) Involuntary flexion of hips and knees = positive (meningeal irritation)
NCLEX Tip: Both signs indicate meningeal irritation. Kernig's = knee can't extend. Brudzinski's = neck flex causes legs to flex. In infants: look for bulging fontanelle and high-pitched cry.

NCLEX Tip: Bacterial meningitis CSF = cloudy + ↑ WBC (neutrophils) + ↓ glucose + ↑ protein. Viral = clear + lymphocytes + normal glucose.
NCLEX Priority: If increased intracranial pressure (ICP) is suspected — papilledema, focal neuro deficits — perform CT scan BEFORE lumbar puncture to prevent brain herniation.

Treatment

Bacterial Meningitis — TREAT IMMEDIATELY, DO NOT DELAY
  • IV Ceftriaxone (3rd-generation cephalosporin) — covers N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae
  • IV Vancomycin — added to cover penicillin-resistant S. pneumoniae
  • Ampicillin — added for Listeria in neonates, elderly, immunocompromised
  • Dexamethasone (corticosteroid) — given BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose to reduce inflammation and edema; reduces risk of hearing loss in H. influenzae meningitis
  • IV fluids, fever management, seizure precautions
  • Dim lighting for photophobia; minimize noise
Viral Meningitis — Supportive
  • Rest, fluids, analgesics
  • Acyclovir if HSV meningitis suspected
  • Most resolve without specific therapy in 1–2 weeks

Prevention

Vaccination & Prophylaxis
  • Meningococcal vaccine (MenACWY) — for adolescents 11–12 yr + booster at 16; college freshmen in dorms; asplenic patients
  • Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV15/PCV20, PPSV23) — for infants, elderly, immunocompromised
  • Hib vaccine — for infants; prevents H. influenzae type b meningitis
  • Rifampin or Ciprofloxacin — chemoprophylaxis for close contacts of N. meningitidis cases (within 7 days)

Key Takeaways

  • Kernig's sign: pain on knee extension; Brudzinski's sign: neck flex causes leg flex
  • Infants: bulging fontanelle + high-pitched cry
  • Bacterial CSF: cloudy, ↑ WBC (PMN), ↓ glucose, ↑ protein
  • Do CT scan before LP if increased ICP suspected
  • Treatment: IV ceftriaxone + vancomycin IMMEDIATELY; add dexamethasone
  • Bacterial meningitis: droplet precautions for 24 hours after antibiotics started
  • Petechial/purpuric rash = meningococcemia → life-threatening emergency
