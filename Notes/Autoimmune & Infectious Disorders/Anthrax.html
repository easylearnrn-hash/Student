What Is Anthrax?

Anthrax is a serious infectious disease caused by Bacillus anthracis, a gram-positive, spore-forming rod. It primarily affects animals but can infect humans through spores in the environment. Because spores can be aerosolized and weaponized, inhalation anthrax is considered a Category A bioterrorism agent.

⚠️
Critical Fact — NOT Person-to-Person

Anthrax is NOT spread from person to person. Standard precautions are sufficient — no isolation or quarantine of contacts is needed.

Three Types of Anthrax

TypeRoute of EntryClassic SignsSeverity
Cutaneous Skin contact with spores (animal hides, wool, soil) Black painless eschar (ulcer) — starts as papule, becomes necrotic black lesion with surrounding edema; no pain Most common (95%); rarely fatal if treated
Inhalation (pulmonary) Inhaling aerosolized spores Initial flu-like symptoms → rapid progression → severe dyspnea, widened mediastinum on CXR, sepsis, meningitis Deadliest form; bioterrorism concern; high mortality
Gastrointestinal Eating undercooked meat from infected animals Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, fever; can progress to sepsis Moderate severity; rare in U.S.
NCLEX Tip: Cutaneous anthrax = black painless eschar. Inhalation anthrax = deadliest, bioterrorism risk. GI anthrax = undercooked infected meat.

Signs & Symptoms by Type

Inhalation Anthrax — Two-Phase Progression
  • Phase 1 (1–5 days): Flu-like — fever, chills, mild chest discomfort, fatigue, non-productive cough
  • Brief improvement (may mislead clinicians)
  • Phase 2 (rapid deterioration): Sudden severe dyspnea, stridor, cyanosis, hypoxia; sepsis; hemorrhagic meningitis; shock; death within hours
  • CXR: widened mediastinum (pathognomonic for inhalation anthrax)

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
  • Blood culture — gram-positive rods; rapid confirmation
  • PCR testing — detects B. anthracis DNA; most sensitive
  • Skin swab (cutaneous) or nasal swab (inhalation exposure)
  • Chest X-ray / CT: widened mediastinum (inhalation anthrax hallmark)
  • ELISA serology for antibody detection

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

💊
60-Day Course + 3 Vaccine Doses
  • After suspected exposure (e.g., bioterrorism event): 60 days of oral ciprofloxacin or doxycycline
  • Plus 3 doses of anthrax vaccine (at 0, 2, and 4 weeks)
  • Goal: eliminate any germinating spores before they cause disease

Infection Control

Precautions — Standard Only
  • Standard precautions — no isolation needed; NOT contagious person-to-person
  • Cutaneous lesions: wear gloves when handling wound/dressings
  • Inhalation: spore decontamination of environment required (EPA-approved disinfectants)
  • Contaminated surfaces: bleach solution (0.5%)
  • Report suspected cases to public health authorities immediately (notifiable disease)
