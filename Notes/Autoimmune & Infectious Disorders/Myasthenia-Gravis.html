Myasthenia Gravis
(MG)

NCLEX-focused study note — neuromuscular junction · ACh receptors · myasthenic vs cholinergic crisis

What It Is

Myasthenia Gravis is a chronic autoimmune neuromuscular disease where antibodies attack acetylcholine receptors (AChR) at the neuromuscular junction (the synapse where nerves signal muscles), causing skeletal muscle weakness that:

Worsens With
Activity, repetition, heat, stress, infections, missing medication
Improves With
Rest, anticholinesterase medications (pyridostigmine)

Pathophysiology — NMJ Attack

Motor Nerve Terminal
releases acetylcholine (ACh)
↓
Neuromuscular Junction (Synapse)
↓
Muscle AChR Attacked by Antibodies
→ Fewer working receptors → impaired muscle contraction
↓
Muscle Weakness & Fatigue

Key Symptoms — "PTOSIS First"

Ocular (Often First Signs)
  • Ptosis — drooping eyelids (often asymmetric)
  • Diplopia — double vision
Bulbar
  • Dysphagia — difficulty swallowing
  • Dysarthria — slurred speech
  • Chewing fatigue
Limbs / Trunk
  • Proximal limb weakness
  • Weak neck muscles
  • Fatigue worsens throughout day
Respiratory (Critical)
  • Diaphragm and intercostal weakness
  • Respiratory failure → Myasthenic Crisis

💡
Tensilon Test — NCLEX Must-Know

Edrophonium is a short-acting cholinesterase inhibitor → temporarily ↑ ACh → if MG, muscle strength improves dramatically within seconds.
Always have atropine ready (antidote for bradycardia/cholinergic effects).

Treatment

TreatmentMechanismKey Nursing Points
Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) Cholinesterase inhibitor → ↑ ACh at NMJ → improves muscle strength Give EXACTLY on time — missing dose → myasthenic crisis. Give 30–45 min before meals to aid swallowing.
Corticosteroids
(prednisone)		 Reduce autoimmune response May temporarily worsen symptoms at start; infection risk
Immunosuppressants
(azathioprine)		 Steroid-sparing; long-term immune modulation Monitor CBC, LFTs; infection risk
IVIG / Plasmapheresis IVIG blocks antibodies; plasmapheresis removes them from blood Used for crisis or severe exacerbation; short-term effect
Thymectomy Surgical thymus removal — may reduce antibody production Recommended especially if thymoma present; improves symptoms in many patients

Crisis Comparison — MUST KNOW for NCLEX

Myasthenic Crisis

Cause: Undermedication or illness/infection trigger

  • Severe muscle weakness
  • Respiratory failure
  • Difficulty swallowing, speaking

Treat: ↑ pyridostigmine + IVIG/plasmapheresis + ventilatory support

Cholinergic Crisis

Cause: Overmedication (too much pyridostigmine)

  • Weakness PLUS SLUDGE symptoms:
  • Salivation, lacrimation, urination
  • Diarrhea, GI cramps, emesis
  • Bradycardia, sweating, miosis

Treat: STOP pyridostigmine + give ATROPINE

💡
Tensilon Test Differentiates the Two Crises

Symptoms improve after edrophonium → Myasthenic Crisis (needs more ACh)
Symptoms worsen after edrophonium → Cholinergic Crisis (already too much ACh)

Nursing Care Priorities

  • 🫁 Airway first — monitor for respiratory fatigue; keep suction and emergency equipment at bedside
  • ⏰ Pyridostigmine on time — never delay or miss doses; schedule before meals
  • 🍽️ Aspiration precautions — upright positioning for all meals; soft foods if needed
  • 😴 Schedule rest — prioritize important activities when patient is strongest (morning)
  • 🌡️ Avoid triggers — heat, infection, stress, physical overexertion can trigger crisis
  • 💊 Medication safety — beta-blockers, aminoglycosides, sedatives WORSEN MG — avoid!

Patient Education

  • Wear a medical alert bracelet at all times
  • Never skip or delay pyridostigmine doses
  • Avoid medications that worsen MG: beta-blockers, aminoglycosides, magnesium, sedatives, quinidine
  • Stay vaccinated (avoid infections that can trigger crisis) — inactivated vaccines only
  • Know early signs of crisis — seek emergency care immediately
  • Report new symptoms, infections, or medication changes to provider
