Infectious Disorders

Lyme Disease

Borrelia burgdorferi · tick-borne · erythema migrans · doxycycline · 3 stages

What Is Lyme Disease?

Lyme disease is a bacterial infection caused by Borrelia burgdorferi, transmitted through the bite of an infected black-legged tick (Ixodes scapularis — deer tick). It is the most common tick-borne disease in the U.S. If untreated, it can spread to the joints, heart, and nervous system.

NCLEX Tip: Ticks must be attached for 36–48 hours to transmit Borrelia. Early tick removal prevents infection!

Transmission & Epidemiology

Key Facts
  • Vector: black-legged tick (Ixodes scapularis) — also called deer tick
  • Tick must be attached for at least 36–48 hours to transmit bacteria
  • Most common in wooded, grassy areas — high risk for hikers, campers, hunters, gardeners
  • Endemic in northeastern and upper midwestern U.S.
  • NOT transmitted person-to-person

Three Stages of Lyme Disease

StageTimeframeKey Symptoms
Stage 1
Early Localized		 3–30 days after bite Erythema migrans (bull's-eye rash) — expanding ring with central clearing; flu-like symptoms (fever, chills, myalgia, headache)
Stage 2
Early Disseminated		 Weeks to months Multiple rashes; Bell's palsy (facial paralysis); Lyme carditis (heart block/arrhythmias); joint pain; meningitis
Stage 3
Late Disseminated		 Months to years Lyme arthritis (chronic swollen painful joints, especially knees); memory loss; peripheral neuropathy; fatigue
NCLEX Tip: The bull's-eye rash (erythema migrans) is the classic early sign — a flat or slightly raised expanding red ring with central clearing at the site of the tick bite.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
TestPurpose
Clinical diagnosisErythema migrans + history of tick exposure in endemic area — sufficient for Stage 1
ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay)First screening test — detects Lyme antibodies
Western BlotConfirmatory test if ELISA is positive — more specific
Lumbar punctureCSF analysis for neurological symptoms (Stage 2–3)
NCLEX Tip: Early Lyme disease may test negative on antibody tests — it takes 2–6 weeks to develop detectable antibodies. Clinical diagnosis based on rash is sufficient in early disease.

Treatment

Antibiotic Therapy — First-Line
StageAntibioticDuration
Stage 1 (early localized)Doxycycline (first-line); Amoxicillin or Cefuroxime (alternatives)10–21 days
Stage 2–3 (disseminated, severe neuro/cardiac)IV Ceftriaxone2–4 weeks
NCLEX Tip: Doxycycline is NOT given to pregnant women or children under 8 (tooth discoloration, bone effects). Use Amoxicillin instead for these patients.

Prevention

Patient Teaching — Tick Prevention
  • Wear long sleeves and pants in tick-infested areas; tuck pants into socks
  • Use DEET-based tick repellent on skin or permethrin on clothing
  • Check skin and clothing thoroughly after outdoor activity
  • Remove ticks immediately with fine-tipped tweezers — grasp close to skin, pull straight out without twisting
  • Shower within 2 hours of being outdoors to remove unattached ticks
  • Do NOT use petroleum jelly, nail polish, or heat to remove ticks

Complications of Untreated Lyme Disease

Late Complications
  • Lyme arthritis — chronic joint swelling and pain, especially knees
  • Neurological problems — memory loss, concentration difficulties, peripheral neuropathy
  • Lyme carditis — heart block, arrhythmias
  • Bell's palsy — unilateral facial drooping (cranial nerve VII)
  • Post-treatment Lyme disease syndrome — persistent fatigue and pain after treatment (not active infection)
