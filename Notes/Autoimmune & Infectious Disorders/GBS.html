Guillain-Barré Syndrome
(GBS)

NCLEX-focused study note — ascending paralysis · autoimmune demyelination · respiratory emergency

What It Is

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) is an acute autoimmune disorder where the immune system attacks the myelin sheath of peripheral nerves (nerves outside the brain and spinal cord), causing progressive ascending muscle weakness that can lead to respiratory failure.

💡
Key Concept

GBS = peripheral nervous system (PNS) attack. Multiple Sclerosis = CNS attack. GBS usually follows an infection 2–4 weeks earlier.

Triggering Events

Most Common Trigger
Campylobacter jejuni — GI infection (#1 preceding infection)
Also: respiratory infections, CMV, EBV
Other Triggers
  • Recent vaccination (rare — flu vaccine)
  • Surgery or trauma
  • Zika virus infection

Pathophysiology

  1. Infection triggers immune response → antibodies formed
  2. Antibodies cross-react with myelin sheath of peripheral nerves (molecular mimicry)
  3. Demyelination → impaired nerve conduction → weakness, paralysis
  4. Ascending pattern — starts in legs and moves upward
  5. Risk: paralysis reaches respiratory muscles → respiratory failure

Classic Ascending Pattern

1
Bilateral leg weakness — starts distally (feet/toes), tingling, numbness
2
Moves upward — loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia), weakness spreads to thighs, trunk
3
Upper body — arms, hands, dysphagia (swallowing difficulty)
4
Respiratory muscles — diaphragm involved → respiratory failure EMERGENCY
+
Autonomic dysfunction — HR/BP instability, urinary retention, diaphoresis

Clinical Manifestations

Motor
  • Ascending weakness / paralysis
  • Loss of deep tendon reflexes (areflexia)
  • Weakness progresses over days–weeks
Sensory
  • Paresthesia — tingling/numbness
  • Pain (often back pain early)
  • Sensory changes usually less severe than motor
Respiratory (Top Priority)
  • Shallow breathing, decreased Vt
  • Inability to cough effectively
  • Decreased SpO₂ → intubation may be needed
Autonomic
  • Tachycardia or bradycardia
  • Fluctuating BP
  • Urinary retention
  • Diaphoresis (excessive sweating)

Diagnostics

TestFinding in GBS
Lumbar puncture (LP)↑ CSF protein with NORMAL WBC — "albuminocytologic dissociation"
Electromyography (EMG)Slowed nerve conduction (demyelination)
Nerve conduction studies (NCS)Prolonged latency; reduced amplitude
Pulmonary function testsVital capacity monitoring — watch for drop
💡
NCLEX Clue — LP Result

High CSF protein but normal white cells = classic GBS finding. This is called "albuminocytologic dissociation."

Treatment

Acute Treatment
  • IVIG (IV Immunoglobulin) — blocks damaging antibodies; first-line
  • Plasmapheresis — removes harmful antibodies from blood
  • Both equally effective; often IVIG preferred due to easier administration
Supportive
  • Mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure
  • Anticoagulants (heparin) — DVT prevention in immobile patients
  • Pain management
  • Physical therapy — rehab/recovery
🚨
Do NOT Use Corticosteroids for GBS

Steroids are NOT effective in GBS and may worsen outcomes. IVIG and plasmapheresis are the treatments.

NCLEX Nursing Priorities

🫁
Priority #1 — Airway & Breathing

Monitor respiratory rate, depth, SpO₂, ABGs, vital capacity.
Prepare for intubation if vital capacity falls or breathing becomes shallow.
Assess ability to swallow before all oral intake — aspiration risk is high.

Neuromuscular & Mobility Care

  • 🔄 Turn every 2 hours — prevent pressure ulcers and hypostatic pneumonia
  • 🦵 Passive ROM exercises — prevent contractures and muscle atrophy
  • 🦶 Foot drop prevention — splints/orthotics as ordered
  • 💊 DVT prophylaxis — anticoagulants + compression stockings
  • 👁️ Eye care — if facial nerve involved, use lubricating drops, eye patches

Autonomic Dysfunction Care

  • ❤️ Continuous cardiac monitoring — watch for sudden bradycardia or tachycardia
  • 📊 Frequent BP checks — BP swings can be severe and sudden
  • 🚽 Monitor urinary output — may need catheterization for retention

Communication & Psychosocial

  • Establish non-verbal communication system if intubated or facial muscles weak
  • Patient is cognitively intact but unable to move — severe psychological distress
  • Honest, reassuring communication about expected recovery
  • Most patients recover significantly — prognosis is generally good with treatment

Complications to Watch

🚨
Respiratory Failure — #1 Life-Threatening Complication

Signs: shallow breathing, ↓ SpO₂, inability to cough, accessory muscle use.
Action: Prepare for mechanical ventilation; monitor vital capacity serially.

🤧
Aspiration Pneumonia

Weak swallowing muscles → coughing when eating, drooling, silent aspiration.
Action: Swallowing assessment; semi-upright position; swallow study; NGT if needed.

🩸
DVT & Pressure Ulcers

Immobility → clot formation and skin breakdown. Anticoagulants, compression, repositioning every 2 hours.
