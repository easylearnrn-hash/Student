Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
(SLE / Lupus)

NCLEX-focused study note — autoimmunity · immune complexes · multi-organ involvement

What It Is

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic autoimmune disease where the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own cells, causing severe inflammation and potential permanent organ damage.

Chronic
Long-lasting, relapsing-remitting course with flares and remissions
Systemic
Affects multiple organ systems simultaneously — skin, kidneys, heart, lungs, brain, blood

Pathophysiology

  1. Defective apoptosis — cells die but aren't cleared; apoptotic bodies accumulate
  2. Ineffective phagocytosis — macrophages fail to clear debris, releasing nuclear antigens
  3. ANA formation — antinuclear antibodies (attack cell nuclei) are produced
  4. Immune complex deposition — antigen-antibody complexes lodge in tissues
  5. Complement activation — C3, C4 consumed → widespread inflammation and organ damage
💡
NCLEX Translation

Think "self-attack everywhere" — immune complexes deposit in kidneys, skin, joints, blood vessels → inflammation → damage.

Risk Factors & Triggers

Who Gets It
  • Women of childbearing age (9:1 female:male)
  • Black, Latina, Asian women — higher severity
  • Family history
Triggers / Flare Causes
  • Sun exposure (UV light)
  • Infections, stress
  • Pregnancy / postpartum / menstruation
  • Certain medications (drug-induced lupus)

Clinical Manifestations by System

Skin Hallmark
  • Butterfly (malar) rash — rash across cheeks and nose bridge
  • Discoid rash — coin-shaped lesions
  • Photosensitivity — rash/flare with sun exposure
  • Alopecia (hair loss)
Kidneys High Priority
  • Lupus nephritis — kidney inflammation
  • Proteinuria, hematuria
  • Hypertension, edema
  • Risk of renal failure
Heart
  • Pericarditis (most common cardiac finding)
  • Myocarditis, murmurs
  • Libman-Sacks endocarditis (sterile)
Lungs
  • Pleuritis, pleural effusion
  • Chest pain, dyspnea
  • Increased risk of pneumonia
Brain / Neuro
  • Fatigue, memory loss
  • Seizures, psychosis
  • Mood disorders (depression)
Blood
  • Anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
  • Risk of clots
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome — hypercoagulability
Joints / Muscles
  • Arthritis-like joint pain, swollen joints
  • Raynaud phenomenon
General
  • Fever, severe fatigue
  • Oral ulcers, dry red eyes

Medications

DrugPurposeKey Side Effects / Nursing
Corticosteroids
(prednisone)		 Rapid control of severe inflammation; flare management Infection risk, hyperglycemia, osteoporosis — taper slowly
NSAIDs
(ibuprofen, naproxen)		 Mild inflammation, joint pain, fever GI bleeding, kidney impairment — take with food
Hydroxychloroquine
(antimalarial)		 Long-term flare prevention; mainstay of therapy Annual eye exams required — retinal toxicity; takes months to work; smoking reduces efficacy
Azathioprine / Mycophenolate
(immunosuppressants)		 Severe disease; steroid-sparing; lupus nephritis Infection risk — avoid live vaccines
Belimumab
(biologic)		 Targets B-cell activating protein; reduces antibodies/inflammation Monitor for depression / suicidal ideation; injection site reactions; takes up to 6 months for effect
💡
Hydroxychloroquine — NCLEX Must-Know

Requires annual ophthalmologic exams. Takes months to show effects. Smoking decreases effectiveness. Used in ALL lupus patients long-term.

Pregnancy & Lupus

🚨
High Risk — Plan Ahead

Pregnancy increases flare risk. Risk of miscarriage, preeclampsia, clots (antiphospholipid syndrome).
Lupus should be controlled for at least 6 months before conception.
Close monitoring needed throughout pregnancy and postpartum period.

Nursing Management — Mnemonic "LUPUS"

L
Labs — ANA, anti-dsDNA, CBC, ESR, CRP, C3/C4, BUN/Cr, urinalysis
U
Use of medications — educate on steroids, NSAIDs, hydroxychloroquine, immunosuppressants; monitor side effects
P
Pregnancy precautions — disease control before conception; flare risks during/after pregnancy
U
Understanding flares — teach FLARE signs; teach LESS prevention strategies
S
Signs of complications — nephritis, pericarditis, CNS changes, thrombosis — report promptly

Key Nursing Interventions

  • 🌞 Sun protection always — SPF ≥30, protective clothing, avoid peak sun hours
  • 💊 Medication education — don't stop steroids abruptly; eye exams with hydroxychloroquine
  • 🦠 Infection prevention — hand hygiene, NO live vaccines (MMR, varicella, nasal flu, shingles) when immunosuppressed
  • 🩺 Kidney monitoring — daily weights, urine output, edema, BP; report changes in urination
  • ❤️ Cardiac monitoring — auscultate for friction rub (pericarditis)
  • 🧠 Psych support — assess depression, anxiety, coping with chronic illness
  • 🏃 Joint care — pain management, PT referral, ROM during remissions
  • 📋 Tailor to affected systems — match interventions to what's flaring

Critical Complications to Watch

🚨
Lupus Nephritis → Renal Failure

Monitor BUN/Cr, proteinuria, hematuria, edema, BP. Strictest monitoring priority.

🩸
Antiphospholipid Syndrome (APS)

Blood clots in arteries/veins → DVT, PE, stroke. Recurrent miscarriages. Monitor and anticoagulate as ordered.

⚠️
Opportunistic Infections

Immunosuppressive therapy → increased infection risk. Monitor for fever, cough, wound changes. No live vaccines.

🧠
CNS Lupus

Seizures, psychosis, cognitive changes. Report sudden neuro changes immediately.
