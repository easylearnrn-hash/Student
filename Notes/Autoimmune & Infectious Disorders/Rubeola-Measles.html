Rubeola (Measles)

Paramyxovirus · Koplik spots · 3 Cs · airborne · N95 · Vitamin A · head-to-toe rash · MMR

What Is Rubeola (Measles)?

Rubeola (measles) is a highly contagious viral illness caused by the rubeola virus (Paramyxovirus). It is one of the most contagious human diseases — a single infected person can infect up to 18 others. Before the vaccine, measles caused millions of deaths worldwide annually. It spreads through airborne transmission and remains a major cause of childhood mortality in unvaccinated populations.

Transmission & Incubation

Epidemiology
  • Pathogen: Rubeola virus (Paramyxovirus)
  • Transmission: airborne — virus lingers in air up to 2 hours after infected person leaves the room
  • Incubation: 7–14 days
  • Contagious: 4 days before to 4 days after rash onset — highly infectious during prodrome
  • Secondary attack rate: ~90% in unvaccinated household contacts
  • Humans are the only reservoir

Signs & Symptoms — Disease Progression

Phase 1: Prodromal (3–4 days)
  • The 3 Cs: Cough (severe, barking) + Coryza (runny nose) + Conjunctivitis (red, watery eyes)
  • High fever (up to 40–41°C / 104–105°F)
  • Koplik spots — tiny white spots with a red halo on the buccal mucosa (inner cheeks, near molars); appear 1–2 days before rash; pathognomonic for measles
  • Photophobia, malaise, anorexia
Phase 2: Rash Phase (3–5 days)
  • Maculopapular rash begins at hairline/face → spreads downward (head to toe)
  • Rash becomes confluent (lesions merge together) on face and trunk
  • Fever peaks as rash appears, then gradually decreases
  • Rash fades in same order it appeared (head to feet)
NCLEX Tip: Koplik spots = white spots on buccal mucosa = pathognomonic for measles. They appear BEFORE the rash and disappear as rash develops. This is the most tested measles fact on NCLEX!

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
  • Clinical diagnosis — 3 Cs + Koplik spots + rash pattern
  • Serology: measles IgM antibody — positive during acute infection
  • Nasopharyngeal swab or urine PCR — confirms rubeola virus
  • Labs: leukopenia (low WBC)

Treatment

Supportive Care + Vitamin A
  • No specific antiviral treatment — supportive care
  • Rest, fluids, fever management (acetaminophen — NOT aspirin)
  • Vitamin A supplementation — recommended for all children with measles by WHO; reduces severity and mortality, especially in malnourished children; prevents blindness
  • Treat secondary bacterial infections (otitis media, pneumonia) with antibiotics
  • Photophobia: dim lighting
NCLEX Tip: Vitamin A is a key treatment for measles — it reduces severity, complications (especially blindness), and mortality. Always give it to children with measles.

Infection Control

😷
Airborne Precautions Required
  • Airborne precautions — measles virus lingers in air for up to 2 hours
  • N95 respirator (fit-tested) for all healthcare workers entering room
  • AIIR (negative pressure room) — door closed at all times
  • Isolate for 4 days after rash onset (immunocompromised: entire course of illness)
  • Limit patient transport; patient wears surgical mask when outside AIIR
  • Only immune HCW should care for patient (previous MMR vaccination or confirmed titer)

Prevention — MMR Vaccine

Vaccination & Post-Exposure Prophylaxis
  • MMR vaccine — live attenuated; measles component ~97% effective after 2 doses
  • 2 doses: First at 12–15 months; second at 4–6 years
  • Post-exposure (unvaccinated susceptible): MMR vaccine within 72 hours of exposure
  • Post-exposure (immunocompromised/pregnant/infants): IVIG (immune globulin) within 6 days of exposure
  • Contraindicated: pregnancy, severely immunocompromised patients
NCLEX Tip: Unvaccinated person exposed to measles → MMR within 72 hours. Cannot give MMR? → IVIG within 6 days (for pregnant women, immunocompromised, or infants <12 months).
