Throat & Upper Airway — NCLEX Review

Pharyngitis · Tonsillitis · Laryngitis · Epiglottitis · Sleep Apnea · Suctioning · Tracheostomy

Basic Anatomy

  • Pharynx (throat) — connects mouth/nose to larynx and esophagus; shared pathway for air and food
  • Larynx (voice box) — contains vocal cords; protected by the epiglottis during swallowing
  • Upper airway: nasal cavity → pharynx → larynx → trachea (windpipe)
  • Function: warm, humidify, and filter air before it enters the lungs

Pharyngitis (Sore Throat)

  • Cause: viral (most common) or bacterial (strep — Group A Streptococcus)
  • Symptoms: red throat, pain with swallowing, swollen tonsils/lymph nodes, fever, malaise
  • Nursing care: warm saline gargles, hydration, analgesics (acetaminophen/ibuprofen), antibiotics if bacterial (penicillin or amoxicillin)
Always complete the full course of antibiotics for strep throat → prevents rheumatic fever and subsequent permanent heart valve damage.

Tonsillitis

  • Cause: viral or bacterial (often Group A Strep)
  • Symptoms: swollen/red tonsils, white/yellow spots, sore throat, bad breath, difficulty swallowing
  • Treatment: fluids, rest, antipyretics; antibiotics if bacterial; tonsillectomy if chronic/recurrent

Post-Tonsillectomy NCLEX Priorities

  • 🚨 Frequent swallowing = first sign of bleeding — assess and notify provider immediately
  • Offer cold liquids only (soothe and ↓ bleeding risk)
  • No straws — suction pressure can dislodge clot
  • No clearing throat or coughing forcefully
  • Avoid red/orange liquids — they mimic blood and mask bleeding
  • Monitor vital signs for tachycardia (may indicate hemorrhage)

Laryngitis

  • Cause: overuse, infection, or irritation of vocal cords
  • Symptoms: hoarseness or voice loss, dry throat, tickling, cough
  • Care: voice rest; humidifier; hydration; avoid smoking; avoid whispering (strains cords as much as talking)

Epiglottitis — AIRWAY EMERGENCY

🚨 Classic Triad: Drooling + Stridor + Tripod Position
Also: muffled voice, high fever, acute distress
Cause: bacterial (Haemophilus influenzae type B); children most at risk if unvaccinated

Nursing Actions — In Order of Priority

  1. DO NOT inspect the throat — tongue depressor can trigger complete airway closure
  2. Stay with the patient at all times
  3. Keep patient calm and in a position of comfort (tripod/upright)
  4. Prepare for emergency intubation or surgical airway (tracheostomy)
  5. Administer IV antibiotics, O₂
  6. Keep crash cart and emergency airway equipment at bedside
Airway obstruction is the #1 priority. Everything else is secondary.

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

  • Cause: viral infection (parainfluenza most common) causing subglottic swelling; typically children <5 years
  • Classic signs: barking cough (seal-like), inspiratory stridor, low-grade fever, hoarseness; worse at night
  • Distinguishing feature from epiglottitis: gradual onset, barking cough, able to swallow; NO drooling or tripod position

Treatment

  • Dexamethasone (corticosteroid) — reduces subglottic swelling; first-line
  • Racemic epinephrine (nebulized) — for moderate-to-severe croup; rapidly ↓ edema
  • Cool mist or humidified air — soothes inflamed airway
  • Keep child calm — crying worsens stridor
🚨 NCLEX: After racemic epinephrine, monitor for rebound edema 2–4 hours later — child may look better then suddenly worsen. Observe for at least 2–4 hours post-treatment before discharge.

Sleep Apnea

  • Cause: repeated collapse of airway during sleep (obstructive)
  • Symptoms: loud snoring, apneic episodes (>10 sec), daytime sleepiness, morning headaches
  • Treatment: CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure); weight loss; avoid alcohol and sedatives; possible surgery if severe
CPAP is the primary treatment for obstructive sleep apnea. Educate on proper fit, cleaning, and adherence for best results.

Suctioning — NCLEX Key Points

TypeRouteSterile?
Oral (Yankauer)MouthNo — clean technique
NasopharyngealNose to throatYes — sterile
Endotracheal / TracheostomyVia ET tube or trachYes — sterile
  • Pre-oxygenate before suctioning
  • Suction only on withdrawal — not while inserting
  • Limit suction to 10–15 seconds per pass
  • Monitor for bradycardia and SpO₂ drop during procedure

Tracheostomy Care — Key Points

  • Use sterile technique for dressing changes and suctioning
  • Provide humidified air to prevent secretion crusting
  • Secure trach ties — 1 finger should fit between tie and neck
  • Keep obturator, extra trach tube, suction, and scissors at bedside at all times
  • Monitor for: bleeding, obstruction, subcutaneous emphysema (air under skin), infection

NCLEX Summary Table

  • 🎯 Strep throat = complete antibiotics → prevent rheumatic fever
  • 🎯 Tonsillectomy = frequent swallowing = bleeding; no straws; cold liquids; avoid red liquids
  • 🎯 Epiglottitis = DO NOT inspect throat; prepare for intubation; stay with patient
  • 🎯 Croup = barking cough + stridor in child → dexamethasone; racemic epi for severe; monitor rebound edema 2–4 hrs
  • 🎯 Sleep apnea = CPAP; avoid sedatives/alcohol
  • 🎯 Suctioning = pre-oxygenate; 10–15 sec; suction on withdrawal only
  • 🎯 Trach = sterile technique; obturator at bedside; humidified air
  • 🎯 Stridor = narrowed upper airway → call for help immediately
  • 🎯 Tripod position = patient leaning forward on arms to breathe = airway emergency
