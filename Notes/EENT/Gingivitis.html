Gingivitis — NCLEX Review

Oral Health · Gum Disease · Periodontal Conditions

Definition

Gingivitis is inflammation of the gingiva (gums) — the mildest and most reversible form of periodontal disease. It does not involve bone or attachment loss (unlike periodontitis).

Causes (Etiology)

  • Plaque accumulation — sticky bacterial film on teeth (most common cause)
  • Poor oral hygiene — irregular brushing and flossing
  • Hormonal changes — pregnancy, puberty, menstruation
  • Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy)
  • Systemic diseases — diabetes mellitus
  • Smoking
  • Medications that cause gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth):
    • Phenytoin (Dilantin) — seizure medication
    • Cyclosporine — immunosuppressant
    • Calcium channel blockers (e.g., nifedipine)
If a patient on phenytoin or cyclosporine has gum overgrowth, suspect drug-induced gingival hyperplasia, not simple gingivitis.

Clinical Manifestations

  • Red, swollen gums
  • Bleeding while brushing or flossing
  • Tenderness to touch
  • Bad breath (halitosis)
  • No pain in early stages — important NCLEX point
  • No attachment loss or bone damage (differentiates from periodontitis)
Gingivitis is painless in early stages but bleeds easily — this is the key teaching point for patient education.

Diagnosis

  • Visual oral examination
  • Periodontal probing: normal pocket depth = 1–3 mm
  • No radiographic bone loss (distinguishes from periodontitis)

Treatment

  • Professional dental cleaning (scaling and polishing)
  • Improved oral hygiene — brushing twice daily + daily flossing
  • Treat underlying cause (change offending medication, manage diabetes)
  • Antimicrobial mouth rinse (chlorhexidine) if prescribed
  • Vitamin C supplementation if deficient

Complications if Untreated

  • Periodontitis — bone destruction and gum recession (irreversible)
  • Tooth loss
  • Systemic inflammation — linked to cardiovascular disease and preterm birth
  • In diabetics: worsened glycemic control (bidirectional relationship)

Nursing Care / Patient Teaching

  • Brush teeth twice daily with fluoride toothpaste
  • Floss daily — removes plaque between teeth
  • Get dental cleanings every 6 months
  • Stop smoking
  • Use antibacterial mouthwash (chlorhexidine) if prescribed
  • Control blood sugar in diabetic patients
  • Encourage adequate vitamin C intake
  • Nursing diagnoses: impaired oral mucous membrane; deficient knowledge (oral hygiene)

NCLEX Must-Knows

Mnemonic: GUMS BLEED
  • Gum redness
  • Unpleasant breath (halitosis)
  • Mild swelling
  • Sores rare
  • Bleeding with brushing
  • Loss of pain sensation (painless early)
  • Early stage reversible
  • Encourage flossing
  • Dental cleaning is key
  • 🎯 Early gingivitis is painless but bleeds easily — key teaching point
  • 🎯 Vitamin C deficiency = gum bleeding without significant plaque
  • 🎯 Phenytoin/cyclosporine/CCBs → drug-induced gingival hyperplasia
  • 🎯 Diabetes and immunosuppression (HIV, chemo) = ↑ risk for all oral infections
  • 🎯 Gingivitis = reversible; periodontitis = irreversible (bone loss)
  • 🎯 Priority nursing interventions = oral hygiene education + routine dental care
