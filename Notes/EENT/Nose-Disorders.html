Nose Disorders — NCLEX Review

Epistaxis · Sinusitis · Allergic Rhinitis · Deviated Septum · Nasal Fracture

Nose Anatomy — Quick Reference

  • Nares — nostrils; air entry points
  • Septum — cartilage wall dividing two nostrils
  • Turbinates — structures that warm, humidify, and filter air
  • Sinuses — air-filled facial spaces (maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid)
  • Olfactory Nerve (CN I) — sense of smell

Epistaxis (Nosebleed)

  • Causes: dry air, nose picking, trauma, hypertension, anticoagulants (aspirin, warfarin)
  • Types: anterior (front — common, manageable) vs. posterior (back — more serious, may need ENT)

Nursing Care

  1. Sit patient upright
  2. Lean forward (prevents blood from going to the airway or stomach)
  3. Pinch soft part of nose for 5–10 minutes continuously
  4. Apply cold compress to the bridge of the nose
  5. Tell patient to avoid blowing nose, bending over, or straining after
  6. Monitor for hypovolemia if severe/persistent
NEVER tilt the head back — blood flows into the airway and can be aspirated (inhaled into lungs).
If bleeding persists: nasal packing, cauterization of blood vessel, or ENT consult.

Sinusitis — Sinus Infection

  • Cause: infection or inflammation of sinuses; often follows a cold or allergies
  • Symptoms: facial pain/pressure (worse when bending forward), nasal congestion, thick yellow/green discharge, headache, tooth pain, possible fever

Sinus Locations

  • Maxillary — cheek area (most common site)
  • Frontal — forehead
  • Ethmoid/Sphenoid — deeper, behind the eyes

Treatment

  • Saline spray or nasal irrigation (flush sinuses)
  • Decongestants — short-term use only
  • Antibiotics only if symptoms persist >10 days (bacterial)
  • Steam inhalation, humidifier, increased fluid intake

Allergic Rhinitis (Hay Fever)

  • Cause: allergens — pollen, dust, pet dander, mold
  • Symptoms: sneezing, rhinorrhea (runny nose), itchy eyes/nose/throat, watery eyes, congestion

Treatment

  • Antihistamines: loratadine, cetirizine (2nd gen preferred — less sedating); diphenhydramine (1st gen — sedating)
  • Nasal corticosteroids: fluticasone — use consistently for best effect
  • Allergen avoidance; air filters; saline rinses; stay indoors on high-pollen days

Deviated Septum

  • Cause: injury to nose or congenital (present at birth)
  • Symptoms: one-sided nasal blockage, frequent nosebleeds, snoring, recurrent sinus infections
  • Treatment: septoplasty (surgical correction) if symptoms are severe

Nasal Fracture (Broken Nose)

  • Cause: trauma (falls, sports, car accidents)
  • Symptoms: pain, swelling, bruising, nasal deformity, difficulty breathing through nose
  • Clear nasal drainage after head trauma → may indicate CSF leak

Nursing Care

  • Elevate the head of bed
  • Apply ice to reduce swelling
  • Monitor for CSF leak: test fluid for glucose (positive = CSF) or halo sign (blood ring with clear outer ring on gauze)
  • No nose blowing
  • Surgery if fracture is displaced
CSF leak = EMERGENCY — risk of meningitis (brain infection). Report immediately.

NCLEX Nose Summary

  • 🎯 Epistaxis: sit up, lean forward, pinch nose 5–10 min — NEVER tilt back
  • 🎯 Sinusitis: fluids + steam + saline; antibiotics only if >10 days
  • 🎯 Allergic rhinitis: antihistamines; fluticasone (nasal steroid)
  • 🎯 Oxymetazoline max 3 days → rebound congestion
  • 🎯 Clear nasal drainage after trauma = test for CSF → halo sign or glucose test
  • 🎯 CSF leak = emergency → meningitis risk
  • 🎯 CN I = olfactory nerve = sense of smell
