Mental Health

Defense Mechanisms

Psychoanalytic theory — unconscious strategies to reduce anxiety · mature vs. immature · clinical examples · NCLEX

Definition

Defense mechanisms are unconscious psychological strategies the ego uses to protect the self from anxiety, distress, and unacceptable impulses or thoughts. They operate below conscious awareness and help individuals cope with internal conflict and external threats.

Concept originated with Sigmund Freud; further developed by Anna Freud. On NCLEX, you must identify which defense mechanism a patient is using and understand the nursing response.

Mature vs. Immature Defense Mechanisms

Mature (Healthy) Mechanisms

These mechanisms are adaptive, functional, and generally healthy. They help manage stress without significantly distorting reality.

  • Sublimation
  • Humor
  • Altruism
  • Suppression
Immature / Primitive Mechanisms

These are maladaptive, associated with mental illness, personality disorders, and significant distress. They distort or deny reality.

  • Splitting, Projection, Denial, Regression
  • Reaction Formation, Dissociation
  • Passive Aggression

Comprehensive Table — Defense Mechanisms

MechanismLevelDefinitionClinical Example
Repression Intermediate Involuntarily pushing threatening thoughts, feelings, or memories out of conscious awareness into the unconscious A sexual assault survivor has no memory of the traumatic event; PTSD with amnesia about the incident
Suppression Mature Consciously choosing to delay thinking about a distressing thought or feeling — "I'll deal with it later" A nurse consciously decides not to think about a difficult patient case until off shift
Denial Immature Refusing to acknowledge an unpleasant reality or its implications — the most primitive defense A patient with newly diagnosed cancer says, "I don't believe the test results — the lab must have made a mistake." An alcoholic says, "I can stop whenever I want."
Projection Immature Attributing one's own unacceptable thoughts, feelings, or impulses to another person A patient who is angry at their nurse says, "I can tell you don't like me and are trying to hurt me." A person who is attracted to someone else accuses their spouse of cheating.
Displacement Intermediate Redirecting emotional reactions from the real source to a safer, substitute target A patient who is angry at their physician yells at the nurse instead. A person who is frustrated at work comes home and argues with their family.
Rationalization Intermediate Creating logical, socially acceptable explanations for unacceptable behavior or feelings — making excuses "I didn't get the promotion because my boss is biased — it had nothing to do with my performance." A patient says "I drink because it helps me sleep — it's practically medicinal."
Reaction Formation Immature Expressing the opposite of one's true feelings — converting an unacceptable feeling into its opposite A person who feels deep hostility toward a sibling acts excessively loving and caring. A patient who is terrified of surgery tells everyone they're completely calm.
Sublimation Mature Channeling unacceptable impulses into socially acceptable or productive activities A person with violent urges becomes a surgeon. A person with aggressive tendencies takes up boxing. A person dealing with grief channels it into writing or art.
Regression Immature Reverting to behaviors characteristic of an earlier developmental stage when under stress A 5-year-old who was toilet-trained starts bedwetting after the birth of a sibling. An adult patient becomes demanding and dependent (child-like) when hospitalized.
Splitting Immature/Primitive Viewing people or situations as entirely good or entirely bad — inability to tolerate ambiguity; black-and-white thinking Characteristic of Borderline Personality Disorder. Patient says "You're the best nurse ever!" one day and "You're terrible and hate me!" the next. May pit staff members against each other.
Intellectualization Intermediate Using abstract thinking, data, or reasoning to avoid dealing with the emotional aspects of an issue A patient newly diagnosed with a terminal illness discusses survival statistics, treatment protocols, and research papers but never expresses any emotion about their diagnosis.
Undoing Intermediate Performing an action or ritual to "cancel out" or compensate for an unacceptable thought or action After having an angry thought about someone, a person buys them an expensive gift. Common in OCD — repetitive rituals to undo anxiety-provoking thoughts.
Compensation Adaptive Overachieving in one area to make up for real or perceived deficits in another — making up for a weakness A student who is poor at sports becomes an exceptional academic achiever. A person with a physical disability becomes a Paralympic champion.
Dissociation Immature/Pathological Disconnecting from thoughts, feelings, identity, or surroundings to escape distress; extreme form = dissociative disorders A trauma survivor feels detached from their body during a panic attack ("this doesn't feel real"). Dissociative identity disorder is the extreme form.
Identification Neutral Modeling oneself after another person, especially someone admired or someone who caused harm (trauma bonding) A young nurse models their behavior after a respected mentor. "Stockholm syndrome" — hostage identifying with captor.
Introjection Neutral Internalizing the values, beliefs, or attitudes of another person — making them part of the self A child internalizes a parent's critical voice and becomes self-critical as an adult. Healthy: adopting a mentor's professional values.

Nursing Implications

Nursing Response to Defense Mechanisms
  • Do NOT directly confront denial — especially in grief. Acknowledge what the patient is expressing and gently provide reality over time.
  • Do NOT reinforce unhealthy mechanisms (e.g., avoid validating splitting behavior — be consistent)
  • Recognize when a mechanism is protecting the patient temporarily (adaptive) vs. preventing necessary treatment
  • For splitting (BPD patients) — have consistent, unified team approach; do not take sides; hold firm, non-punitive limits
  • Explore the underlying anxiety driving the defense mechanism — address the root cause
  • Be non-judgmental — all people use defense mechanisms; they are normal responses to anxiety

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • All defense mechanisms are unconscious (except suppression — conscious)
  • Denial = most primitive; refusing to accept reality. Common in grief and substance abuse.
  • Projection = blaming others for your own feelings. "You hate me" when they feel hatred.
  • Displacement = redirect feelings to a safer target (yelling at the nurse instead of the doctor)
  • Rationalization = making excuses (logical-sounding reasons for unacceptable behavior)
  • Splitting = all-good or all-bad thinking → classic sign of Borderline Personality Disorder
  • Sublimation = most adaptive/mature; channeling negative impulses into something positive
  • NCLEX often asks to identify the mechanism AND describe the correct nursing response
