Delirium

Definition

An acute, reversible disturbance in consciousness with fluctuating symptoms. Caused by an underlying medical condition, medication, or substance.

Common Causes (Use: DELIRIUM mnemonic)

LetterCause
DDrugs (anticholinergics, narcotics, benzodiazepines, steroids)
EElectrolyte imbalance (↓Na, ↓Ca)
LLack of drugs (withdrawal — alcohol, benzos)
IInfection (UTI, pneumonia — especially in elderly)
RReduced oxygenation (hypoxia, hypercapnia)
IIntracranial process (stroke, seizure, head trauma)
UUrinary retention / pain / constipation
MMyocardial / metabolic (MI, hypoglycemia, liver failure)

Nursing Interventions for Delirium

  • Identify and treat the underlying cause — top priority
  • Reorient the patient frequently (state name, date, place, situation)
  • Maintain familiar environment — keep same staff if possible
  • Ensure adequate lighting, minimize sensory overload
  • Encourage sleep-wake cycle (lights off at night, on during day)
  • Keep family present when possible to calm patient
  • Avoid restraints unless absolutely necessary (increase agitation)
  • Correct glasses/hearing aids to reduce sensory deprivation
  • Avoid anticholinergic medications
⚠️
NCLEX Alert — Delirium in Elderly

In older adults, UTI and dehydration are the most common causes of delirium. Confusion is often the ONLY sign of infection in elderly patients.

Depression (Major Depressive Disorder)

DSM-5 Criteria — "SIG E CAPS" (5+ symptoms ≥ 2 weeks)
LetterSymptom
SSleep — insomnia or hypersomnia
IInterest — anhedonia (loss of pleasure in usual activities)
GGuilt — worthlessness, excessive guilt
EEnergy — fatigue, loss of energy
CConcentration — difficulty thinking or deciding
AAppetite — decreased or increased; weight changes
PPsychomotor — agitation or retardation
SSuicidal ideation — thoughts of death or suicide

Must include either depressed mood or anhedonia as one of the 5.

Nursing Interventions for Depression

  • Priority: Assess for suicidal ideation every shift
  • Encourage expression of feelings; therapeutic communication
  • Set simple, achievable goals to rebuild self-esteem
  • Encourage activity, exercise, social interaction — gradually
  • Administer antidepressants as ordered; educate about delayed onset (2–4 weeks)
  • Monitor for increased energy before mood improves — suicide risk highest at this point
  • Ensure adequate nutrition and sleep
🚨
NCLEX Safety Alert

Suicide risk is HIGHEST when the patient begins to feel better — they now have energy to act on a plan. Increase monitoring during early recovery.

Dementia

Definition

A chronic, progressive, irreversible decline in cognitive function. Multiple domains affected: memory, language, executive function, personality. Most common type: Alzheimer's disease (60–80% of cases).

Stages of Dementia

Early Stage
  • Forgets recent events
  • Gets lost in familiar places
  • Difficulty with complex tasks
  • Personality changes
  • Still independent in most ADLs
Middle Stage
  • Increasing memory loss
  • Needs help with ADLs
  • Wandering behavior
  • Sundowning (increased confusion in evening)
  • Agitation, repetitive behavior
Late Stage
  • Total dependence for all ADLs
  • Loss of speech / swallowing
  • Bedridden, susceptible to infections
  • Incontinence
  • Loss of recognition of family

Nursing Interventions for Dementia

ConcernIntervention
Safety / WanderingDoor alarms, identification bracelet, safe enclosed areas; do NOT use "wander guard" alone
SundowningIncrease activity during day; avoid stimulants in afternoon; ensure lighting at dusk; maintain routine
CommunicationShort, simple sentences; face-to-face eye contact; do NOT argue with confused statements; use gentle redirection
NutritionFinger foods (easy to self-feed); small frequent meals; monitor for dysphagia; thickened liquids if needed
Caregiver BurnoutAssess caregiver; refer to support groups; discuss respite care
MedicationsDonepezil (Aricept), Memantine — slow progression, do NOT cure
💡
Validation Therapy

For dementia patients in a confused state, do NOT correct delusions or argue. Instead, validate the emotion: "You seem worried. I'll stay right here with you."

NCLEX Must-Know Tips

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • Delirium = acute, reversible; Dementia = chronic, irreversible
  • Delirium in elderly → think UTI, dehydration, medications first
  • Depression = SIG E CAPS — 5+ symptoms ≥ 2 weeks
  • Antidepressants take 2–4 weeks to work
  • Most dangerous depression period = when patient starts feeling better (more energy to act)
  • Dementia → use validation, simple sentences, maintain routine
  • Sundowning → keep active during day, increase lighting at dusk
  • TCAs lethal in overdose → limit supply dispensed
