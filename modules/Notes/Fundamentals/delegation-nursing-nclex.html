DELEGATION – NCLEX COMPREHENSIVE REVIEW

WHAT IS DELEGATION?

Delegation is the process of assigning tasks to other members of the healthcare team while still being accountable for the outcome.

✅ KEY PRINCIPLE:

You can delegate tasks, but NOT responsibility or nursing judgment.

WHO CAN YOU DELEGATE TO?

Role What They Can Do What They Can't Do
UAP (Unlicensed Assistive Personnel, e.g., CNA) Basic care: bathing, feeding, vitals (stable), toileting, ambulating, linen changes No assessments, teaching, medications, or anything requiring judgment
LPN/LVN (Licensed Practical Nurse / Licensed Vocational Nurse) Stable patients: oral meds, wound care, enteral feeds, Foley insertion, sterile dressing changes, collecting specimens No initial assessments, IV meds, care plans, teaching, or unstable patients
RN (Registered Nurse) Everything including assessments, planning, teaching, IV meds, blood transfusions, triage, admission/discharge Can delegate but must supervise and follow up

THE 5 RIGHTS OF DELEGATION – MUST KNOW FOR NCLEX

1⃣ Right Task

Is this safe to delegate?

✅ Example: Ambulating a stable post-op patient

❌ Example: Administering IV meds

2⃣ Right Circumstance

Is the patient stable?

✅ Example: Vital signs on a stable patient

❌ Example: New onset chest pain

3⃣ Right Person

Is the person qualified to do this task?

✅ Example: Only RNs can assess

✅ Example: Only LPNs can give oral meds

4⃣ Right Direction/Communication

Was the task explained clearly and specifically?

✅ Example: "Take blood pressure every hour and report if over 140"

5⃣ Right Supervision/Evaluation

Did the RN follow up to check if the task was done correctly?

NEVER DELEGATE THESE (RN ONLY TASKS)

🛑 NCLEX loves to trick you with a task that looks simple — but if it needs nursing judgment, it stays with the RN.
  • Initial assessments (first time you see the patient)
  • Teaching (especially new or changing information)
  • Care plans
  • Nursing judgment or critical thinking
  • Unstable patients
  • IV meds or blood products
  • Triage and discharge
  • ADPIE steps (Assess, Diagnose, Plan, Implement, Evaluate) — the full nursing process belongs to the RN

WHAT CAN BE DELEGATED?

LPNS CAN DO… (UNDER RN SUPERVISION)

  • Oral and some IM/SQ medications (not IV or blood)
  • Wound care, sterile dressing changes
  • Collecting specimens (e.g., urine, sputum)
  • Reinforce teaching (not the initial teaching)
  • Suctioning and trach care
  • Enteral feeds (e.g., PEG tube)

🔑 LPN = "Licensed but Limited"

UAPS CAN DO… (BASIC CARE TASKS)

  • Bathing, dressing, toileting
  • Feeding (non-dysphagia patients)
  • Ambulating
  • Stable vital signs
  • Positioning and turning
  • Collecting I&O data (not interpreting it!)

🔑 UAP = "Routine, Repetitive, Low-risk"

COMMON NCLEX TRAPS (AND HOW TO AVOID THEM)

Trap Watch For
Stable vs Unstable Unstable = RN only
Initial vs Ongoing Initial assessment = RN
Teaching vs Reinforcing Only RNs teach new info
Evaluation of Tasks RN must follow up after delegating
Critical Thinking RNs use judgment — don't delegate decisions

SAMPLE NCLEX QUESTION PATTERN

Question: "The nurse is caring for a stable post-op patient. Which task is appropriate to delegate to the UAP?"

✔️ Answer: "Assist with ambulation to the bathroom"

❌ Wrong: "Assess surgical site" (RN only)

❌ Wrong: "Administer morphine" (RN only)

QUICK REFERENCE: DELEGATE vs NOT DELEGATE

NOT DELEGATE

  • A.D.P.I.E (Assess, Diagnose, Plan, Implement, Evaluate)
  • Unstable patients
  • Complex patients
  • Patients with complications
  • Patients in acute conditions

MAY DELEGATE

  • Stable patients
  • Chronic patients
  • Routine activities
  • Key words:
    • Remind
    • Review
    • Reinforce
    • Assist
    • Co-conduct
    • Co-assist
    • Co-facilitate
💡 FINAL NCLEX TIPS:
  • When in doubt, ask: "Does this require nursing judgment?" If yes → RN only
  • Look for keywords: stable, routine, reinforce = can delegate
  • Always remember the 5 Rights of Delegation
  • RN is always accountable even after delegating
  • UAPs can do ADLs and basic care only
  • LPNs are "licensed but limited" – stable patients only
