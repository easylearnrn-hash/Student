Mobility & Assistive Devices

NCLEX-RN High-Yield Notes

1. General Principles

  • Assess patient's strength, balance, and cognition before choosing and teaching a device
  • Proper fitting — handgrip at wrist level when arm hangs relaxed; elbow flexed 15–30° when holding device
  • Non-skid footwear is essential at all times
  • Teach slow, deliberate movement — avoid rushing
  • Always include fall prevention education

2. Crutches

Fitting

  • Top of crutch: 2–3 finger widths (2 inches) below the axilla
  • Handgrip: at wrist level
  • Never bear weight on axilla — risk of brachial nerve (brachial plexus) injury → "crutch palsy"
  • Weight is on hands (handgrips), NOT armpits

Crutch Gaits

GaitPatternUse
2-pointOne crutch + opposite foot simultaneouslyMild weakness — most like normal walking
3-pointBoth crutches + affected leg → then unaffected legNon-weight-bearing (one leg only)
4-pointRight crutch → left foot → left crutch → right footPartial weight-bearing; most stable; partial paralysis
Swing-throughBoth crutches forward → swing both legs throughParaplegia; non-weight-bearing on both legs

Stairs with Crutches

Going UP ↑
  1. Step up with unaffected (good) leg
  2. Bring crutches + affected leg up
Going DOWN ↓
  1. Move crutches + affected leg down first
  2. Step down with unaffected (good) leg
Mnemonic: "Up with the GOOD, down with the BAD." (Good = unaffected, Bad = affected/injured)

3. Cane

Correct Use

  • Hold cane on the strong (unaffected) side
  • Move cane with the affected (weak) leg simultaneously
  • Then move the strong leg forward

Stairs with Cane

Going UP ↑
  1. Strong leg up first
  2. Then cane + weak leg up
Going DOWN ↓
  1. Cane + weak leg down first
  2. Then strong leg down
Cane is held on STRONG side — it shifts body weight to the unaffected side, reducing load on the weak leg.

4. Walker

Using a Walker

  1. Pick up (or roll if wheeled walker) and advance walker 6–8 inches forward
  2. Step forward with affected (weak) leg first
  3. Then bring strong leg forward to meet or pass the weak leg
Do NOT:
  • Use walker to pull yourself up from sitting → risk of tipping and falling
  • Advance walker too far ahead → instability and falls
  • Lean excessively forward → risk of falling forward

6. Safe Transfers

  • Lock wheels on beds and wheelchairs before any transfer
  • Use gait belt around waist — guide, do NOT lift by belt
  • Encourage patient to push off surface (bed/chair) — do not have nurse do all the work
  • Count down together ("1-2-3 stand")
  • Move wheelchair to patient's strong side for easiest pivot
  • Maintain good body mechanics (nurse) — wide base of support, bend knees, straight back

7. Fall Prevention

Hospital

  • Bed in lowest position with brakes locked
  • Call light within reach at all times
  • Non-slip footwear
  • Remove equipment/cords from path
  • Night light in room
  • Teach slow position changes (prevent orthostatic hypotension)
  • Bed alarms or 1:1 sitter for high-risk patients

Home Safety

  • Install grab bars in bathroom (toilet, shower)
  • Raised toilet seat
  • Remove throw rugs and loose cords
  • Non-slip mats in shower/tub
  • Adequate lighting in all areas
  • Place frequently used items within easy reach (no climbing)

8. NCLEX Must-Know

  • ✅ Crutches: 2–3 fingers below axilla; weight on HANDS not axilla; no brachial nerve pressure
  • ✅ "Up with the GOOD, down with the BAD" — all devices, stairs
  • ✅ Cane: hold on STRONG side; moves WITH weak leg
  • ✅ Walker: advance 6–8 inches; affected leg moves FIRST; never use to pull up
  • ✅ Transfers: lock wheels; gait belt; patient pushes up; strong side first
  • ✅ Fall prevention: bed lowest, non-slip shoes, call light within reach
  • ✅ 3-point crutch gait = non-weight-bearing on affected leg
  • ✅ 4-point gait = safest, most stable crutch gait
4-point gait: most stable. 3-point gait: non-weight-bearing on one leg. 2-point gait: closest to normal walking. Swing-through: paraplegia.
