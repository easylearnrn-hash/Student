Eye Disorders

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

Progressive central vision loss — most common cause of blindness in adults over 65

Overview
⚠️ NCLEX Priority: AMD destroys the macula — the area responsible for fine detail and central vision. Peripheral vision is preserved. The Amsler grid is the key screening tool. Wet AMD is the emergency form requiring urgent anti-VEGF treatment.
Dry vs. Wet AMD

Dry (Atrophic) AMD — 85-90%

  • Gradual accumulation of drusen under retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)
  • RPE atrophy → geographic atrophy → photoreceptor death
  • Slow, progressive central vision loss (years)
  • No treatment reverses; AREDS2 supplements slow progression
  • Can convert to wet AMD at any time

Wet (Neovascular) AMD — 10-15%

  • Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) under macula
  • New vessels leak → subretinal fluid/blood → rapid vision loss
  • Accounts for 80% of severe vision loss from AMD
  • Rapid onset — can cause legal blindness in weeks
  • Treatment: intravitreal anti-VEGF (first-line)
Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors

  • Age >60 (strongest risk factor)
  • Smoking (doubles risk — most modifiable RF)
  • Family history (ARMS2/CFH gene variants)
  • Caucasian race
  • Cardiovascular disease

Protective Factors

  • AREDS2 supplements (vitamins C, E, zinc, copper, lutein, zeaxanthin)
  • Smoking cessation
  • Diet rich in leafy greens and fish (omega-3)
  • UV protection (sunglasses)
  • Cardiovascular health
Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms

  • Central scotoma (blind spot in center of vision)
  • Metamorphopsia (straight lines appear wavy/distorted) — classic wet AMD symptom
  • Loss of fine detail (difficulty reading, recognizing faces)
  • Colors appear faded or washed out
  • Peripheral vision preserved (contrast to glaucoma)
  • Scotoma may cause difficulty finding specific words on page
Diagnostics
TestFinding
Amsler GridWavy/missing lines = metamorphopsia (wet AMD); home monitoring tool
FunduscopyDrusen (yellow-white deposits), pigmentary changes, subretinal fluid/blood
OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography)Gold standard — shows subretinal fluid, CNV membrane thickness
Fluorescein AngiographyLeaking CNV vessels (wet AMD); staining patterns
ICGA (Indocyanine)Polypoidal CNV detection
Treatment
1
Wet AMD — Intravitreal Anti-VEGF: Ranibizumab (Lucentis), Aflibercept (Eylea), Bevacizumab (Avastin) — monthly initially, then PRN based on OCT. Can stabilize or improve vision.
2
Dry AMD — AREDS2 Supplements: Vitamin C 500mg, Vitamin E 400IU, Zinc 80mg, Copper 2mg, Lutein 10mg, Zeaxanthin 2mg — for intermediate/advanced dry AMD or one eye wet AMD.
3
Photodynamic Therapy (PDT): Verteporfin + laser — for select CNV types; less used since anti-VEGF.
4
Low Vision Rehabilitation: Magnifiers, large-print, screen readers, home safety modifications.
Amsler Grid Teaching

How to Use Amsler Grid

  • Hold grid 14 inches (35 cm) from eye; wear reading glasses if used
  • Cover one eye at a time
  • Focus on central dot
  • Report: wavy lines, missing areas, distorted squares
  • Use daily — new changes in wet AMD can occur rapidly
  • Any new change → call provider same day
Nursing Care

Priorities

  • Amsler grid education — daily self-monitoring for wet AMD conversion
  • Smoking cessation counseling — #1 modifiable risk factor
  • AREDS2 supplement education — does NOT restore vision, slows progression
  • Safety: fall prevention (central scotoma impairs depth perception)
  • Psychosocial: AMD can cause severe depression — address quality of life
  • Post anti-VEGF injection: watch for endophthalmitis, IOP spike, pain
  • Refer to low vision services as needed
⚠️ NCLEX: New sudden distortion or blank spot on Amsler grid in a patient with dry AMD = possible conversion to wet AMD. Call provider immediately — wet AMD can cause legal blindness in weeks.
