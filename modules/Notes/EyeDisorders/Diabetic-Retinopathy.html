Eye Disorders

Diabetic Retinopathy

Microvascular damage to the retina — leading cause of blindness in working-age adults

Overview
⚠️ NCLEX Priority: Diabetic retinopathy is the #1 cause of new blindness in working-age adults in the US. All diabetic patients need annual dilated eye exams. Control of blood glucose, blood pressure, and lipids is the cornerstone of prevention.
Pathophysiology

Mechanism

Chronic hyperglycemia → advanced glycation end-products → capillary basement membrane thickening → pericyte loss → microaneurysm formation → blood-retinal barrier breakdown.

  • Hyperglycemia → sorbitol accumulation (aldose reductase pathway)
  • Endothelial dysfunction → ↑ permeability → retinal edema
  • Capillary occlusion → ischemia → VEGF release → neovascularization
Staging

Non-Proliferative (NPDR)

  • Mild: microaneurysms only
  • Moderate: + dot-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates
  • Severe: cotton-wool spots, venous beading, IRMA (4-2-1 rule)
  • Vision often normal early
  • Macular edema can occur at any stage

Proliferative (PDR)

  • Neovascularization of disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
  • Vitreous hemorrhage — sudden painless vision loss
  • Tractional retinal detachment
  • Neovascular glaucoma
  • Advanced: blindness
Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)

DME — Most Common Cause of Vision Loss in DM

  • Fluid accumulation in central macula → blurred central vision
  • Can occur at any stage of retinopathy
  • Clinically significant: involves or threatens fovea
  • Treatment: intravitreal anti-VEGF (ranibizumab, bevacizumab, aflibercept)
  • Focal laser photocoagulation — for non-center-involving DME

Screening Guidelines

When to Screen

  • Type 1 DM: Initial exam 5 years after diagnosis; then annually
  • Type 2 DM: At diagnosis; then annually
  • Pregnancy + DM: First trimester; q3 months; postpartum
  • Method: dilated fundus exam by ophthalmologist; wide-field retinal photography
Treatment
1
Glycemic Control — HbA1c <7% slows progression significantly (DCCT/UKPDS trials)
2
BP Control — Target <130/80 mmHg; ACE inhibitors/ARBs protect retinal vasculature
3
Intravitreal Anti-VEGF — First-line for DME and PDR (ranibizumab, aflibercept)
4
Panretinal Photocoagulation (PRP) — Laser burns to peripheral retina; reduces VEGF; for severe NPDR/PDR
5
Vitrectomy — For non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment
Nursing Priorities

Key Interventions

  • Emphasize glycemic control — HbA1c monitoring, medication compliance
  • Annual dilated eye exam — non-negotiable for all diabetics
  • Sudden painless vision loss in diabetic → call provider STAT (vitreous hemorrhage)
  • Post-intravitreal injection: watch for endophthalmitis (pain, redness, vision loss)
  • Post-PRP: may have transient blurred vision and reduced peripheral/night vision
  • Smoking cessation — worsens retinal ischemia
⚠️ NCLEX: A diabetic patient with sudden, painless vision loss → vitreous hemorrhage from PDR until proven otherwise. Immediate ophthalmology referral.
