Eye Disorders

Conjunctivitis vs. Keratitis

Critical clinical distinction — one is benign, the other threatens sight

Why This Matters
🚨 NCLEX Critical: Keratitis (corneal inflammation) can lead to corneal ulceration, scarring, and permanent vision loss within hours. It is frequently misidentified as conjunctivitis. Photophobia + decreased vision = keratitis until proven otherwise.
Side-by-Side Comparison

Conjunctivitis

  • Conjunctival inflammation only
  • Vision: normal or mildly blurred
  • Pain: mild burning/irritation
  • Photophobia: absent or mild
  • Discharge: varies by type
  • Cornea: clear
  • Prognosis: excellent; self-limiting (viral)
  • Management: topical abx, supportive care

Keratitis

  • Corneal inflammation/ulceration
  • Vision: significantly decreased
  • Pain: SEVERE, foreign body sensation
  • Photophobia: MARKED
  • Discharge: purulent or watery
  • Cornea: hazy, infiltrate, ulcer
  • Prognosis: vision loss risk; scarring
  • Management: URGENT ophthalmology, intensive antibiotics/antivirals

Nursing Priorities

Triage Decision Points

  • ANY decrease in visual acuity → urgent/emergent ophthalmology
  • Marked photophobia → suspect keratitis or iritis
  • Contact lens wearer with red eye + pain → bacterial keratitis until proven otherwise
  • Instruct patients: REMOVE contact lenses immediately with any red eye symptoms
  • Never use topical steroids in a red eye without confirmed diagnosis (can worsen HSV keratitis)
⚠️ Steroid Warning: Topical corticosteroids in HSV keratitis can cause corneal perforation. NEVER prescribe or administer without ophthalmology confirmation.
Contact Lens Safety

Education Points

  • Never sleep in contact lenses (↑ Pseudomonas keratitis risk 10–15×)
  • Never rinse lenses or case with tap water (Acanthamoeba)
  • Replace lens case every 3 months
  • Remove lenses with ANY redness, pain, or vision change
  • No swimming/showering in lenses
