Eye Disorders

Cataracts

Lens opacification → painless progressive vision loss → surgical treatment

Overview

Definition

A cataract is a clouding of the normally clear crystalline lens. Proteins aggregate and denature over time, scattering light and reducing visual clarity. The most common cause of preventable blindness worldwide.

Key Fact: Cataracts are painless, progressive, and bilateral (though asymmetric). Surgery is the only treatment — no medications reverse lens clouding.
Risk Factors

Modifiable

  • UV light exposure (wear sunglasses)
  • Smoking
  • Alcohol
  • Corticosteroid use (long-term)
  • Poorly controlled DM
  • Radiation exposure

Non-Modifiable

  • Age (most common — senile cataract)
  • Congenital (TORCH infections, galactosemia)
  • Trauma (traumatic cataract)
  • Family history
  • Female sex

Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms

  • Painless, progressive blurred vision
  • Glare/halos around lights — especially at night
  • Decreased color saturation (yellowing)
  • "Second sight" — temporary near vision improvement (nuclear cataract)
  • Monocular diplopia
  • White/gray pupillary reflex (leukocoria) in advanced cases
Diagnosis

Examination

  • Slit-lamp biomicroscopy — gold standard; characterizes location/density
  • Visual acuity testing — degree of functional impairment
  • Ophthalmoscopy — dark spot against red reflex
  • Contrast sensitivity testing
Surgical Management
Surgery Indication: When cataracts interfere with ADLs and quality of life — not based solely on visual acuity numbers.
1
Phacoemulsification — ultrasonic probe emulsifies lens; aspirated through small incision (2–3 mm). Standard of care.
2
IOL Implantation — intraocular lens (monofocal, multifocal, toric) implanted in capsular bag.
3
ECCE (Extracapsular) — larger incision; used for very dense cataracts or limited phaco.
Pre-operative Nursing Care

Pre-op Interventions

  • Dilate pupil: mydriatic drops (tropicamide, phenylephrine) as ordered
  • Measure axial length (A-scan biometry) for IOL power calculation
  • Instruct: stop anticoagulants per surgeon order
  • NPO per anesthesia protocol (usually local/topical anesthesia — brief)
  • Informed consent; address visual expectations
Post-operative Nursing Care

Post-op Priorities

  • Apply eye shield at night × 2 weeks — prevent accidental rubbing
  • Antibiotic + steroid eye drops per schedule (usually 4–6 weeks)
  • Avoid: bending, lifting >5 lbs, straining (increases IOP)
  • No swimming, eye rubbing, eye makeup × 2–4 weeks
  • Dark glasses for photophobia and UV protection
  • Inform: vision may be hazy/blurry 24–48h; improves within days
Complications
🚨 Report Immediately: Sudden pain, vision loss, increasing redness, purulent discharge — signs of endophthalmitis (surgical emergency).
