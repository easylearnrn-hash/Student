Eye Disorders

Glaucoma

Increased intraocular pressure → optic nerve damage → irreversible vision loss

Overview
⚠️ NCLEX Priority: Glaucoma causes irreversible blindness — early detection and IOP control are the nursing priorities. Peripheral vision is lost first (tunnel vision).

Open-Angle (Chronic)

  • Most common (90%)
  • Gradual, painless peripheral vision loss
  • Normal anterior angle; aqueous outflow obstructed
  • Bilateral; insidious onset
  • IOP >21 mmHg

Angle-Closure (Acute)

  • Medical emergency
  • Sudden severe eye pain, halos around lights
  • N/V, headache, blurred vision
  • Iris bulges → blocks trabecular meshwork
  • IOP can spike to 50–70 mmHg
Pathophysiology

IOP Mechanism

Aqueous humor produced by ciliary body → flows through pupil → drains via trabecular meshwork → canal of Schlemm. Obstruction = ↑IOP = optic nerve compression = axon death.

  • Normal IOP: 10–21 mmHg
  • Cup-to-disc ratio >0.5 = suspicious
  • Visual fields: arcuate scotomas → nasal step → tunnel → blindness

Diagnostics

Key Tests

  • Tonometry — measures IOP (normal 10–21 mmHg)
  • Gonioscopy — visualizes angle (open vs. closed)
  • Ophthalmoscopy — optic disc cupping (C:D ratio)
  • Visual field testing — peripheral loss pattern
  • OCT — retinal nerve fiber layer thickness
Medical Management
Beta-Blockers — Timolol (Timoptic)
  • ↓ aqueous production
  • First-line for open-angle
⚠️ CI: asthma, COPD, heart block — absorbed systemically via nasolacrimal duct
Prostaglandin Analogs — Latanoprost (Xalatan)
  • ↑ aqueous outflow via uveoscleral route
  • Once daily at bedtime
⚠️ Side effect: iris color change (irreversible), eyelash growth
Alpha Agonists — Brimonidine (Alphagan)
  • ↓ production + ↑ outflow
⚠️ Avoid in children, MAOIs
CAIs — Acetazolamide (Diamox)
  • ↓ aqueous production; systemic use for acute angle-closure
⚠️ Sulfa allergy CI; watch K+, metabolic acidosis
Miotics — Pilocarpine
  • Constricts pupil → opens angle (angle-closure rescue)
⚠️ Brow ache, myopia; not for open-angle
Acute Angle-Closure: Emergency Protocol
🚨 Emergency: Acute angle-closure is a sight-threatening emergency — call ophthalmology STAT.
1
IV Acetazolamide — rapid IOP reduction
2
IV Mannitol (20%) — osmotic diuresis, ↓ vitreous volume
3
Topical Pilocarpine — constrict pupil, open angle
4
Laser Peripheral Iridotomy — definitive treatment; creates alternate aqueous drainage
Surgical Options

Procedures

  • Laser Trabeculoplasty (ALT/SLT) — enhances trabecular drainage (open-angle)
  • Trabeculectomy — surgical fistula (bleb); risk: infection, hypotony
  • Tube Shunt (Ahmed/Baerveldt) — implanted drainage device
  • Laser Iridotomy — angle-closure prevention/treatment
Nursing Care

Priority Interventions

  • Administer eye drops as scheduled — never miss a dose
  • Teach nasolacrimal occlusion (press inner canthus 1 min after drops)
  • Avoid mydriatics/anticholinergics — dilate pupil, worsen angle-closure
  • Position: HOB 30–45°, affected eye down post-op if applicable
  • Avoid Valsalva (lifting, straining, bending), tight collars
  • Dark glasses for photophobia
  • Teach: glaucoma is chronic — compliance = vision preservation
⚠️ NCLEX Trap: Atropine and other anticholinergics are CONTRAINDICATED in angle-closure glaucoma — they dilate the pupil and worsen the blockage.
