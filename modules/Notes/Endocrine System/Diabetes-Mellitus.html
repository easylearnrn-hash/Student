Diabetes Mellitus

Pancreatic function, Type 1 vs Type 2, hyperglycemia vs hypoglycemia, 3 Ps, treatment ladder

Pancreatic Function

Alpha Cells

  • Produce glucagon
  • Released during hypOglycemia
  • Raises blood sugar by releasing glycogen from the liver

Beta Cells

  • Produce insulin
  • Released during hypERglycemia
  • Drives glucose from blood into cells for energy

Pancreatic Enzymes (Exocrine)

  • Amylase — breaks down carbohydrates → glucose
  • Lipase — breaks down fats → lipids (fatty acids)
  • Protease — breaks down proteins → amino acids
  • Released through the ductus pancreaticus into the duodenum

HypERglycemia — The 3 Ps + More

Classic Symptoms (3 Ps)

  • Polydipsia — extreme thirst
  • Polyuria — increased urination (large volumes)
  • Polyphagia — excessive hunger

Additional Signs

  • Dry mucous membranes
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Headache
  • Confused LOC (altered mental status)
  • Nausea & vomiting (severe)
HypOglycemia — Cold & Clammy Needs Candy

Symptoms

  • Diaphoresis (sweating), cold/clammy skin
  • Trembling / shaking hands
  • Tachycardia / palpitations
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Hunger (sudden, intense)
  • Irritability, mood changes
  • Confusion, difficulty concentrating
  • Blurred vision
  • Pale skin
  • Numbness/tingling (lips, tongue, fingers)
  • Seizures (severe)

Treatment Protocol

IF ALERT:

  1. Give 15 g simple carbs immediately: ½ cup OJ, ½ can regular soda, ½ cup milk, 3–4 glucose tablets
  2. Wait 15 minutes
  3. Check blood glucose (BG)
  4. If still low → give complex carbs + protein (protein slows carb absorption, sustains glucose longer)

IF NOT ALERT / UNCONSCIOUS:

  1. Glucagon injection — IM, IV, or SubQ
  2. Dextrose 50% (D50W) — IV push
"Rule of 15": 15 g carbs → wait 15 min → recheck BG. Repeat if still <70 mg/dL. Do NOT over-treat hypoglycemia.
Insulin Injection Sites & Technique

Subcutaneous (SQ) Injection

  • Abdomen: 1.5–2 inches (5 cm) away from the umbilicus (navel) — fastest absorption
  • Thighs — outer, upper thigh
  • Buttocks
  • Upper arms — under the biceps
  • Rotate sites daily — prevents lipodystrophy (abnormal fat deposits from repeated use)
Treatment Ladder

Type 1 DM

  1. Insulin — always required immediately

Type 2 DM

  1. Diet and exercise
  2. Metformin — first-line oral medication
  3. Insulin — if Metformin fails (HbA1c >7%)
Complications of Uncontrolled DM

Microvascular

  • Diabetic retinopathy — blindness
  • Nephropathy — kidney disease
  • Neuropathy — peripheral nerve damage

Macrovascular

  • Atherosclerosis — excess glucose stored as plaque
  • MI, stroke, PVD
  • Diabetic foot ulcers → amputation

Acute

  • DKA — Type 1: ketones + acidosis
  • HHS — Type 2: extreme hyperglycemia, no ketones
  • Hypoglycemia from over-medication
Dawn Phenomenon vs. Somogyi Effect

Both conditions cause high blood glucose in the morning — but for entirely different reasons. Identifying the cause requires checking blood glucose at 2:00–3:00 AM.

🌅 Dawn Phenomenon

  • Cause: Normal early-morning hormone surge (cortisol, growth hormone, epinephrine) signals the liver to release glucose
  • Mechanism: Hormones prepare the body for waking — blood glucose rises naturally
  • Night: No hypoglycemia during the night
  • 2–3 AM glucose: Normal or elevated
  • Think: Natural hormonal rise → morning high

🔄 Somogyi Effect

  • Cause: Overnight hypoglycemia triggers a counter-regulatory hormone release (rebound hyperglycemia)
  • Mechanism: Blood sugar drops too low → body over-corrects → rebounds too high by morning
  • Night: Hypoglycemia occurs during the night
  • 2–3 AM glucose: Low (<70 mg/dL)
  • Think: Nocturnal low → rebound morning high

How to Tell the Difference — The 2–3 AM Check

  • Check blood glucose at 2:00–3:00 AM
  • Low BG at 2–3 AM → Somogyi Effect — reduce nighttime insulin
  • Normal or high BG at 2–3 AM → Dawn Phenomenon — consider adjusting evening dose or timing

Signs & Clues

  • Both present with high fasting glucose in the morning
  • Both cause difficulty controlling glucose levels
  • Somogyi clue: reports of night sweats, nightmares, or restlessness (signs of nocturnal hypoglycemia)
  • Dawn clue: gradual rise in BG in early morning without any nighttime low

Nursing Implications

  • Monitor blood glucose trends — not just morning readings
  • Schedule a 2–3 AM glucose check to differentiate
  • Teach patients to track bedtime, nighttime, and morning glucose levels
  • Recognize that morning hyperglycemia does not always mean more insulin is needed
  • For Somogyi: decreasing nighttime insulin and adding a complex carb bedtime snack may resolve the pattern
🧠 Memory Trick:   Dawn = morning rise from hormones (no nighttime low)  |  Somogyi = rebound after nocturnal low — "Somogyi slipped low first."
⚡ NCLEX Tip: High morning BG? Ask what happened overnight. No nighttime low → Dawn Phenomenon. Nighttime low → Somogyi Effect. Treatment is opposite — never increase insulin blindly for morning hyperglycemia until the cause is identified.
NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Alpha cells → glucagon (↑ BG in hypoglycemia); Beta cells → insulin (↓ BG in hyperglycemia)
  • Normal fasting BG: 70–110 mg/dL
  • HbA1c reflects past 3 months average; diabetic target <7%
  • 3 Ps = Polydipsia, Polyuria, Polyphagia → hyperglycemia
  • Hypoglycemia: cold, clammy, shaky → Rule of 15
  • Unconscious hypoglycemic → glucagon IM or D50W IV (NOT oral glucose)
  • Abdomen = fastest insulin absorption; rotate sites daily to prevent lipodystrophy
  • Type 1 treatment = insulin only; Type 2 = diet → Metformin → insulin
  • DKA = Type 1; HHS = Type 2 (no ketones)
  • Dawn Phenomenon = morning hyperglycemia without nighttime hypoglycemia — caused by early-morning hormone surge
  • Somogyi Effect = morning hyperglycemia after nocturnal hypoglycemia — rebound response; check BG at 2–3 AM to differentiate
