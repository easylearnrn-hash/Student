Reye Syndrome

Rare but life-threatening — aspirin + viral illness + brain & liver damage in children

📖 Definition

Reye syndrome is a rare but life-threatening condition characterized by acute non-inflammatory encephalopathy (brain swelling without infection) and hepatic dysfunction (liver damage). It occurs predominantly in children and adolescents, most often following a viral illness.

🚨
Medical Emergency

Reye syndrome can progress rapidly to coma and death. It must be recognized and treated immediately.

🔬 What Causes It

The exact cause is not fully understood, but Reye syndrome has a strong established link to:

Primary Trigger
  • Aspirin (salicylate) use in children during or after a viral illness
  • Interferes with mitochondrial function in liver and brain cells
  • Leads to cellular energy failure and organ dysfunction
Associated Viral Illnesses
  • Influenza (flu) — most common
  • Varicella (chickenpox) — most common
  • Other upper respiratory viral infections
Viral Illness  +  Aspirin Use  →  Reye Syndrome

👶 Who Is at Risk

Risk Profile
  • Primarily children and teenagers under 18 years old
  • Peak incidence in children ages 4–12 years
  • Recent history of influenza or chickenpox
  • Exposure to aspirin or salicylate-containing products during the viral illness
  • Rare since public health warnings about aspirin in children were widely adopted

⚙️ What Happens in the Body

Aspirin metabolites disrupt mitochondrial function in liver and brain cells during a period of viral stress. This leads to:

Liver
  • Hepatocyte mitochondria fail
  • Fat accumulates in liver cells (microvesicular steatosis)
  • ↑ AST, ALT, ammonia
  • Hypoglycemia (liver can't maintain glucose)
Brain
  • Ammonia accumulates (liver can't detoxify)
  • Cerebral edema develops
  • ↑ Intracranial pressure (ICP)
  • Neurologic deterioration progresses rapidly
Net Effect
  • No jaundice (non-inflammatory liver damage)
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Hyperammonemia
  • ↑ ICP → coma if untreated

🩺 Signs & Symptoms

Symptoms typically develop 3–5 days after onset of the viral illness and progress in a recognizable pattern:

Stage Clinical Features Severity
Early Persistent vomiting, lethargy, irritability, behavior changes Moderate
Progressing Confusion, disorientation, combativeness, stupor Serious
Severe Seizures, decreased level of consciousness, coma, respiratory failure Critical
Neurologic Signs
  • Confusion / disorientation
  • Irritability and agitation
  • Personality and behavior changes
  • Decreased level of consciousness
  • Seizures
  • Coma (severe)
Systemic Signs
  • Persistent, severe vomiting
  • Lethargy and fatigue
  • Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)
  • No jaundice — despite liver involvement
  • Tachycardia
  • Hyperreflexia in early stages
⚠️
Key Clinical Clue

A child who recently had the flu or chickenpox and now presents with vomiting + sudden mental status change — think Reye syndrome, especially with any aspirin history.

💀 Why It Is Dangerous

Life-Threatening Complications
  • Increased intracranial pressure (ICP) — can cause brain herniation
  • Severe hypoglycemia — can cause irreversible neurologic damage
  • Hyperammonemia — toxic ammonia levels worsen encephalopathy
  • Respiratory failure — requires mechanical ventilation
  • Coagulopathy — liver failure impairs clotting factors
  • Death if not recognized and treated immediately

🩹 Nursing Implications

Priority Assessments
  • Treat as a medical emergency — notify provider immediately
  • Monitor neurologic status — LOC, pupils, ICP signs
  • Monitor blood glucose — correct hypoglycemia promptly
  • Assess liver function and ammonia levels
  • Monitor for signs of increased ICP: Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations)
  • Monitor respiratory status — be prepared to support airway
Nursing Actions
  • Ask about recent viral illness (flu, chickenpox)
  • Ask whether the child received aspirin or salicylate products
  • Establish IV access — administer IV dextrose for hypoglycemia
  • Position: head of bed elevated 30° to reduce ICP
  • Minimize stimulation to avoid ICP spikes
  • Supportive care is the primary treatment — no cure exists
  • Document and report any sudden neurologic changes immediately
Key History Question

Always ask: "Was the child given aspirin or any salicylate-containing medicine during the viral illness?" — This is the most critical piece of history.

🛡️ Prevention

🚫
Golden Rule — Do Not Give Aspirin to Children

Never administer aspirin or salicylate-containing medications to children or teenagers with viral illnesses unless specifically prescribed by a physician. This is the single most important prevention strategy.

Prevention Points
  • Use acetaminophen (Tylenol) or ibuprofen for fever and pain in children — never aspirin
  • Educate parents about reading labels — many OTC cold medications contain salicylates
  • Annual influenza vaccination reduces the risk of flu-related Reye syndrome
  • Varicella vaccination eliminates chickenpox as a trigger
  • Public health education has dramatically reduced incidence since the 1980s
💊
Important Exception — Kawasaki Disease

Aspirin is prescribed at high doses for children with Kawasaki disease — this is a deliberate, physician-supervised exception. Always clarify the indication before administering aspirin to any child.

⚡ NCLEX Must-Know

High-Yield NCLEX Points
  • Reye syndrome = viral illness + aspirin + brain swelling + liver damage in children
  • Classic presentation: persistent vomiting + altered mental status days after flu or chickenpox
  • Labs: ↑ AST/ALT, ↑ ammonia, ↓ glucose, prolonged PT — no jaundice
  • Treatment is supportive — correct hypoglycemia (IV dextrose), manage ICP, monitor closely
  • Prevention = never give aspirin to children with viral illness — use acetaminophen or ibuprofen instead
  • Exception: aspirin is appropriate in Kawasaki disease (physician-prescribed)
  • Nursing priority: assess neuro status, blood glucose, and ask about aspirin history
🧠
NCLEX Clinical Scenario Tip

Child had the flu 4 days ago, was given aspirin for fever, and now presents with vomiting and confusion — think Reye syndrome. First action: assess neuro status, check glucose, notify provider.
