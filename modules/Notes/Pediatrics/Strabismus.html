Pediatrics — Eye Disorders

Strabismus ("Crossed Eyes")

Misalignment of the eyes — can lead to amblyopia if untreated

Definition

Strabismus is a condition where the eyes do not align properly when looking at an object. One eye may turn inward (esotropia), outward (exotropia), upward (hypertropia), or downward (hypotropia), while the other eye focuses normally. Commonly referred to as "crossed eyes" or "wandering eye."

Pathophysiology

  • One or more extraocular muscles are weakened or uncoordinated
  • The brain receives two different images, causing:
    • Double vision (diplopia) in older children/adults
    • Suppression — brain ignores one image in young children to prevent confusion
  • Over time leads to amblyopia ("lazy eye") if untreated

Types of Strabismus

TypeDirection of Eye Deviation
EsotropiaEye turns inward (crossed eyes)

HypotropiaEye turns downward

Causes

  • Congenital (present at birth) — most common
  • Family history
  • Neurological conditions (e.g., cerebral palsy, brain tumor)
  • Uncorrected refractive error (e.g., severe farsightedness)
  • Trauma, muscle or nerve problems
  • Tumors or lesions affecting the brain or eye nerves

Signs & Symptoms

  • Eyes that do not move together
  • Eye that wanders or drifts
  • Squinting or head tilting to see better
  • Double vision (diplopia)
  • Poor depth perception
  • Frequent eye rubbing or blinking
  • Amblyopia (decreased vision in misaligned eye if untreated)

Diagnosis

  • Corneal light reflex test (Hirschberg test) — light shined in eyes to check symmetry of reflection
  • Cover-uncover test — detects hidden (latent) strabismus
  • Visual acuity testing — checks for differences in vision between eyes
  • Referral to ophthalmologist for detailed eye movement evaluation

Treatment Options

Goals: Restore proper eye alignment and binocular vision; Prevent amblyopia

TreatmentPurpose
Corrective lensesFixes refractive errors (e.g., farsightedness) that may cause strabismus
Patching (occlusion therapy)Covers the stronger eye to stimulate the weaker one
Atropine eye dropsTemporarily blurs the good eye so the weaker eye is forced to work
Eye exercises (vision therapy)Strengthen weak eye muscles (more effective in intermittent strabismus)
Botox injectionTemporarily paralyzes overactive muscles (select cases)
SurgeryRealigns extraocular muscles; typically around ages 2–5 if needed

NCLEX Teaching Points

  • Start treatment as early as possible — ideally before age 7
  • Eye alignment surgery is common and safe
  • Teach parents the importance of compliance with patching
  • Reassure that strabismus is not caused by poor parenting
  • Monitor for signs of amblyopia and vision loss
  • Encourage early screening in all children by age 3

Complications If Untreated

  • Amblyopia (lazy eye)
  • Permanent vision loss in one eye
  • Psychosocial issues (self-esteem, teasing)
  • Loss of depth perception

NCLEX Practice Q&A

Q: A parent is teaching about an eye patch for amblyopia. Parent says "I'll apply the patch to my child's weaker eye." Is this correct?
A: No — the patch is applied to the stronger eye to force the weaker eye to work.

Q: A 3-year-old is diagnosed with esotropia. What does this mean?
A: One of the child's eyes turns inward.
