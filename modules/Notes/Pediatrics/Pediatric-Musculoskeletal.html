Pediatrics — Musculoskeletal

Pediatric Musculoskeletal Disorders

DDH, clubfoot, osteogenesis imperfecta, Legg-Calvé-Perthes, SCFE, scoliosis

DDH — Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip

Hip joint dislocated or unstable at birth. Risk: girls, firstborn, breech, family history, tight swaddling. Signs: asymmetrical gluteal folds, limited hip abduction, positive Ortolani (click on abduction) and Barlow (hip dislocates on adduction) signs.

Treatment: Pavlik harness (<6 months, worn 24/7); Bryant traction or spica cast if older or harness fails. Do NOT double-diaper. Neurovascular checks every shift.

Clubfoot (Talipes Equinovarus)

Foot twisted inward and downward. Bilateral in 50% of cases. Detected at birth or prenatally on ultrasound.

Treatment: Start within first 1–2 weeks; Ponseti method = serial casting changed weekly for ~6 weeks; may need Achilles tenotomy; followed by Denis Browne bar bracing (23 hrs/day for months).

💡
NCLEX Tip

Bones most flexible and responsive to correction in early weeks. Don't delay treatment. Check toes for color, warmth, cap refill after each cast change. Never put anything inside cast.

Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI) — "Brittle Bone Disease"

Genetic disorder → defective Type I collagen → extremely fragile bones that fracture with minimal trauma or even spontaneously.

Signs
  • Multiple fractures with minimal or no trauma
  • Blue/grey sclerae (classic finding)
  • Short stature
  • Hearing loss (bone conduction)
  • Dental abnormalities (dentinogenesis imperfecta)
Nursing Care
  • Extremely gentle handling — support trunk and head; do not lift by arms or pull limbs
  • Padded bed/crib environment
  • Bisphosphonates (pamidronate) to strengthen bones
  • PT/OT, lightweight splints/braces
  • Educate family and school personnel
⚠️
May Look Like Child Abuse

Multiple fractures at different healing stages can mimic abuse. Full social and medical history is essential. Alert the interdisciplinary team. Document objectively.

Legg-Calvé-Perthes (LCP) Disease

Temporary interruption of blood supply to the femoral head → avascular necrosis → bone dies and attempts to regrow/remodel. Affects boys aged 4–8 years.

Signs: Painless limp, hip or referred knee pain, stiffness, limited hip ROM, Trendelenburg gait.

Treatment: Protect femoral head during regrowth — rest, non-weight-bearing (crutches), bracing (abduction brace), traction, or surgery. Disease self-limited but takes 2–4 years.

SCFE — Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis

The femoral head (capital epiphysis) slips off the femoral neck at the growth plate. Risk: obese adolescent males (most common). Can be gradual or sudden.

Signs: Hip or groin pain, referred knee pain, limp, affected leg externally rotated (turned outward). Acute slip = sudden severe pain, unable to bear weight.

Treatment: Surgical pinning (in-situ fixation). No weight-bearing until surgery. Risk of avascular necrosis of femoral head if blood supply compromised.

🚨
NCLEX Priority

Obese teenager with sudden hip/knee pain and externally rotated leg = SCFE. NPO, no weight-bearing, urgent surgical consult. Do not give pain meds and send home.

Scoliosis

Lateral curvature of the spine >10° with rotation. Types: idiopathic (most common — adolescent girls), congenital, neuromuscular.

Screening: Adam's Forward Bend Test — look for rib hump, asymmetrical waist/hips/shoulders.

Curve DegreeManagement
<20°Observation; follow-up every 4–6 months; exercises
20–40°Bracing (Boston brace or TLSO) 23 hrs/day; removes only for bathing; stops progression, does NOT correct
>40°Surgery — spinal fusion with instrumentation (rods/screws)

Post-op care: Log-roll technique when repositioning, neurovascular monitoring, deep breathing, early ambulation, pain management, monitor for spinal cord compromise.

⚠️
Brace Compliance

Body image and peer concerns are significant. Brace should be worn 23 hours/day. Check skin under brace daily. Psychosocial support is a nursing priority.

Rickets — Vitamin D Deficiency

Rickets is a bone disease caused by vitamin D deficiency (most common), calcium deficiency, or phosphate deficiency, leading to failure of bone mineralization and softening of bones in growing children.

  • Causes: inadequate dietary vitamin D, insufficient sunlight exposure, malabsorption (celiac disease, Crohn's), exclusively breastfed infants without vitamin D supplementation
  • At-risk groups: exclusively breastfed infants (breast milk low in vitamin D), dark-skinned children (less UV absorption), children in northern latitudes
RICKETS Mnemonic
LetterFinding
RRachitic rosary — beaded enlargement of costochondral junctions (rib-cartilage junctions); feel like beads
IIll-defined epiphyses on X-ray — cupped, frayed metaphyses (classic radiographic sign)
CCraniotabes — soft skull bones (ping-pong ball feel) in infants
KKnock-knees (genu valgum) or bowlegs (genu varum) — bowing deformities when weight-bearing begins
EEnlarged fontanels — delayed fontanel closure; frontal bossing
TTeeth delayed — delayed dentition, enamel defects
SShort stature + soft bones → fractures with minimal trauma; muscle weakness/hypotonia

Diagnosis: ↓ serum 25-OH vitamin D, ↓ calcium, ↓ phosphate, ↑ alkaline phosphatase; X-ray shows cupped/frayed metaphyses

Treatment: oral vitamin D supplementation (cholecalciferol/ergocalciferol) + adequate calcium intake; sunlight exposure; address underlying cause

Prevention: AAP recommends 400 IU vitamin D daily for all exclusively or partially breastfed infants beginning at birth

💡
NCLEX Tip — Rickets vs. OI

Both rickets and OI cause fractures. Key difference: Rickets = nutritional deficiency (vitamin D/Ca²⁺) → bowing deformities, rachitic rosary. OI = genetic (collagen mutation) → blue sclerae, multiple fractures, brittle bones. Also see dedicated Rickets.html note for full detail.

📝
NCLEX Summary

DDH = asymmetrical folds + limited abduction → Pavlik harness full-time. Clubfoot = treat early with serial casting. OI = gentle handling, fractures may look like abuse (blue sclerae). SCFE = obese teen + sudden pain + externally rotated leg = emergency surgery. Scoliosis brace = 23 hours/day + daily skin checks; surgery if >40°. Rickets = vitamin D deficiency → bowlegs, rachitic rosary, soft skull → supplement vitamin D 400 IU daily for breastfed infants.
