Pediatrics — Eye Disorders

Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP)

Abnormal retinal vessel development in premature infants — risk of blindness

Definition

ROP is a disorder of abnormal blood vessel development in the retina that primarily affects premature infants. Immature retinal blood vessels grow abnormally and can leak, bleed, or cause scarring, leading to retinal detachment and permanent blindness.

Pathophysiology

  • The retina normally finishes developing in the last weeks of gestation (32–40 weeks)
  • In premature infants, retinal vessels are underdeveloped at birth
  • When exposed to high oxygen levels, rapid changes in blood flow, or stress, fragile vessels:
    • Grow abnormally
    • Leak or bleed
    • Form scar tissue that can pull the retina away (retinal detachment)
  • The younger the gestational age and lower the birth weight → greater the risk

Who Is At Risk?

Risk FactorExplanation
Prematurity <31 weeks gestationRetina not yet fully developed

Stages of ROP

StageDescription
Stage 1Mild: Thin demarcation line between normal and abnormal retina
Stage 2Moderate: Ridge forms at the junction
Stage 3Severe: Abnormal blood vessels grow (neovascularization)
Stage 4Partial retinal detachment
Stage 5Total retinal detachment → blindness
Plus DiseaseMore aggressive form with vessel dilation and tortuosity → urgent treatment

Signs & Symptoms

📌
ROP is usually asymptomatic in early stages — routine screening is essential!

Advanced cases: white pupil reflex (leukocoria), strabismus, absent/poor visual tracking, nystagmus, retinal detachment signs in late stages

Diagnosis

  • Dilated eye exam by a pediatric ophthalmologist
  • First exam: 4–6 weeks after birth or 31–33 weeks postmenstrual age (whichever is later)
  • Ophthalmoscopy — visualizes the retina directly
  • Ocular imaging (RetCam) — used to document findings in some NICUs

Treatment

TreatmentPurpose
ObservationMany mild cases (Stage 1–2) resolve on their own
Laser therapy (Photocoagulation)Destroys abnormal peripheral retina to stop new vessel growth
Anti-VEGF injections (e.g., Bevacizumab)Inhibits abnormal blood vessel formation
Scleral buckle or vitrectomySurgical treatments for retinal detachment (Stage 4–5)

Nursing Responsibilities — NCLEX Focus

  • Ensure screening is scheduled for all at-risk infants
  • Maintain oxygen saturation within prescribed target range (typically 90–95%)
  • Use blended oxygen to avoid hyperoxia (too much oxygen)
  • Monitor pulse oximetry continuously in NICU
  • Educate parents about the importance of follow-up eye exams
  • Be aware of long-term visual complications

Complications of ROP

  • Retinal detachment
  • Permanent blindness
  • Amblyopia (lazy eye)
  • Strabismus
  • Myopia (nearsightedness)
  • Glaucoma or cataracts later in life

NCLEX Practice Q&A

Q: A premature infant (29 weeks) is on oxygen in NICU. Which action helps prevent ROP?
A: Keep oxygen within prescribed saturation range (avoid hyperoxia)

Q: Which infant is at highest risk for ROP?
A: Infant born at 28 weeks, weight 980g, receiving oxygen
