Varicella (Chickenpox) Airborne

  • Varicella-zoster virus; highly contagious
  • Signs: low fever → itchy vesicular rash ("dew drops on rose petal" pattern), appears in crops
  • Contagious: 1–2 days before rash until all lesions crusted
  • Care: calamine lotion, cool baths, trim nails/mittens, diphenhydramine for itch, acetaminophen for fever
  • High-risk: immunocompromised → acyclovir antiviral
  • NEVER aspirin (Reye's syndrome)
  • Prevention: 2-dose vaccine (12–15 mo, 4–6 yr)

Measles (Rubeola) Airborne

  • Paramyxovirus; extremely contagious
  • Signs: high fever, cough, coryza (runny nose), conjunctivitis, then Koplik spots (tiny white dots inside cheeks — early diagnostic sign), followed by maculopapular rash spreading head-to-toe
  • Airborne isolation; Vitamin A if severe (reduces pneumonia and blindness risk)
  • Prevention: MMR vaccine (12–15 mo, 4–6 yr)

Mumps Droplet

  • Signs: swollen/painful parotid glands, fever, earache, difficulty chewing
  • Contagious: 2 days before → 5 days after parotid swelling onset
  • Complications: orchitis (males), oophoritis, meningitis, encephalitis, hearing loss
  • Prevention: MMR vaccine

Rubella (German Measles) Droplet

  • Signs: mild pink maculopapular rash (spreads rapidly head-to-toe in 3 days), low fever, swollen posterior cervical lymph nodes
  • Contagious: 7 days before → 5–7 days after rash
  • SERIOUS IN PREGNANCY → Congenital Rubella Syndrome: deafness, cataracts, heart defects, intellectual disability
  • Isolate infected person for 7 days after rash appears
  • Prevention: MMR vaccine (live — contraindicated in pregnancy and immunocompromised)

Pertussis (Whooping Cough) Droplet

  • Bacteria: Bordetella pertussis
  • Signs: paroxysmal coughing spells → high-pitched inspiratory "whoop" → vomiting after coughing; infants may have apnea instead of whoop
  • Treatment: azithromycin (or clarithromycin, erythromycin); start within 3 weeks
  • Isolate until 5 days after antibiotics started
  • Prevention: DTaP series; Tdap booster; give Tdap in every pregnancy (protects newborn)

Fifth Disease (Parvovirus B19) Droplet

  • Signs: bright red "slapped cheek" rash on face → lacy rash on trunk and extremities
  • Contagious before rash appears; once rash appears = no longer contagious
  • Supportive care only (viral, self-limiting)
  • Dangerous in: pregnancy (hydrops fetalis), immunocompromised, hemolytic anemia (aplastic crisis)

Hand-Foot-and-Mouth Disease (HFMD) Contact

  • Coxsackievirus; Contact + fecal-oral transmission
  • Signs: fever, painful mouth sores (stomatitis), vesicular rash on palms and soles
  • Care: cool liquids, popsicles, soft foods; avoid acidic drinks; acetaminophen/ibuprofen for pain/fever
  • Exclude from school until: no fever + blisters healing + eating normally

Diphtheria Droplet

  • Bacteria: Corynebacterium diphtheriae; toxin-producing
  • Signs: sore throat, low fever, malaise → gray/white pseudomembrane over tonsils and pharynx ("bull neck" appearance from cervical lymphadenopathy)
  • Pseudomembrane bleeds when removed — do NOT attempt to remove it
  • Complications: airway obstruction, myocarditis, neuropathy (toxin-mediated)
  • Treatment: diphtheria antitoxin (neutralizes toxin) + antibiotics (erythromycin or penicillin); droplet precautions
  • Prevention: DTaP series (diphtheria, tetanus, acellular pertussis)

Scarlet Fever Droplet

  • Caused by: Group A Streptococcus (Strep pharyngitis + erythrogenic toxin)
  • Signs:
    • Sandpaper rash — rough, red, blanching rash; starts on trunk/neck, spreads to extremities; feels like coarse sandpaper
    • Strawberry tongue — white coating days 1–2, then bright red bumpy tongue
    • Circumoral pallor — pale area around the mouth
    • Pastia's lines — red streaks in skin folds (axillae, groin)
  • Treatment: penicillin (or amoxicillin) × 10 days; prevents rheumatic fever
  • Exclude from school until 24 hours after antibiotics started and fever-free

NCLEX Key

Sandpaper rash + strawberry tongue + fever = Scarlet Fever → treat with penicillin to prevent rheumatic fever (cardiac damage).

Roseola (HHV-6) Droplet

  • Caused by: Human Herpesvirus-6 (HHV-6); common in infants 6 months–2 years
  • Classic pattern:
    • Phase 1: Abrupt high fever (103–105°F / 39–40°C) lasting 3–5 days — child appears surprisingly well despite fever
    • Phase 2: Fever drops suddenly → rose-colored maculopapular rash appears (trunk first, then spreads to face/extremities)
  • Rash is non-itchy and fades within 1–3 days; child is contagious during fever phase
  • Treatment: supportive care (antipyretics, fluids); self-limiting
  • Complication: febrile seizures (due to rapid fever spike)
💡
NCLEX Tip — Roseola

High fever → fever breaks → rash appears. This sequence is the classic test clue. Reassure caregivers: rash means the illness is ending, not worsening.

Lyme Disease Contact (Vector)

  • Caused by: Borrelia burgdorferi; transmitted by deer tick (Ixodes scapularis)
  • Signs:
    • Bull's-eye rash (erythema migrans) — expanding circular rash with central clearing at tick bite site; appears 3–30 days after bite
    • Flu-like symptoms: fever, headache, fatigue, myalgia, joint aches
    • Late disease: Bell's palsy (facial nerve), migratory joint pain, carditis (AV block), neurological symptoms
  • Treatment:
    • Children <8 years: amoxicillin × 14–21 days
    • Children ≥8 years: doxycycline × 14–21 days (drug of choice)
  • Prevention: tick checks after outdoor activities, long clothing, DEET, promptly remove ticks with tweezers
