Pediatrics

Kawasaki Disease

Acute systemic vasculitis in children — NCLEX overview

Definition

Kawasaki Disease is an acute systemic vasculitis (inflammation of blood vessels throughout the body) of unknown cause that mainly affects children under 5 years old. It can lead to coronary artery aneurysms (ballooning of heart blood vessels) and is a pediatric emergency.

Pathophysiology

  • Inflammation targets medium-sized arteries, especially coronary arteries
  • Immune system activation → widespread inflammation → blood vessel wall damage
  • If untreated, can cause aneurysms or myocarditis (heart muscle inflammation)
Who Gets It?
  • Most common in children <5 years
  • Peak age: 1–2 years
  • More common in boys, Asian descent
  • Usually in winter or spring

Diagnostic Criteria — CRASH & BURN Mnemonic

Child must have Fever ≥ 5 days PLUS 4 of the 5 following:

CRASH & BURN
C
Conjunctivitis — non-purulent (no pus)
R
Rash — polymorphous (many shapes)
A
Adenopathy — cervical lymph node swelling >1.5 cm
S
Strawberry tongue — red, bumpy tongue; red cracked lips
H
Hands/feet — swelling, redness of palms/soles → peeling skin
&
Burn — Fever ≥ 5 days (high, unresponsive to meds)

Three Phases

Acute Phase (1–2 Weeks)
  • High fever unresponsive to meds
  • Red eyes, mouth, lips
  • Swollen hands/feet
  • Rash, irritability
  • Lymphadenopathy
Subacute Phase (2–4 Weeks)
  • Skin peeling (desquamation), esp. hands/feet
  • Joint pain (arthralgia)
  • Risk for coronary aneurysms
  • Thrombocytosis (↑ platelet count)
Convalescent Phase (Weeks to Months)

Symptoms resolve gradually; inflammation markers return to normal

Complications

⚠️
NCLEX Focus: Coronary Artery Aneurysms

Also watch for: Myocarditis, pericardial effusion, thrombosis, shock

Treatment — NCLEX Priority

IVIG

Intravenous immune globulin — decreases inflammation. Must be given within 10 days of fever onset. Watch for allergic reaction during infusion.

Aspirin (High-dose)

Anti-inflammatory + antiplatelet. Exception to "no aspirin in kids" rule. Therapy continues even after fever subsides. Watch for Reye's syndrome if viral illness occurs.

Monitor heart with echocardiogram (ultrasound of the heart).

NCLEX Nursing Priorities

  • Monitor for coronary aneurysms (check echo)
  • IVIG infusion — watch for allergy or reaction
  • Strict I&O (for cardiac status)
  • Comfort care for fever, irritability
  • Soft foods, cool fluids for oral mucosa
  • Monitor for aspirin side effects (bleeding, GI upset)

High-Yield NCLEX Tips

💉
Vaccine Delay

IVIG may interfere with live vaccines → delay MMR/Varicella for 11 months

  • First action for Kawasaki = start IVIG and monitor for allergic reaction
  • Labs monitored: ESR, CRP, platelets, echocardiogram
  • Discharge teaching: delay live vaccines, continue aspirin, monitor for cardiac issues
  • Monitor for MI signs (unusual in kids): chest pain, vomiting, restlessness
