Med-Surg Pharmacology

High-alert medications, pain management, antibiotics, and antidotes for NCLEX

High-Alert Medications

High-alert meds carry a high risk of serious harm if misused.

1. Opioids

Used for moderate to severe pain. Examples: Morphine, Hydromorphone (Dilaudid), Fentanyl

  • Side effects: respiratory depression, sedation, constipation, nausea, urinary retention
  • Antidote: Naloxone (Narcan) — reverses opioid overdose
🧠 NCLEX Tip: Always assess respiratory rate (<12 = hold dose), pain level, and LOC before giving.

2. Anticoagulants

Prevent or treat blood clots (thrombosis).

  • Heparin (IV/SQ): Monitor aPTT (goal 1.5–2.5× normal) | Antidote: Protamine sulfate
  • Warfarin (Coumadin, oral): Monitor INR (goal 2–3) | Antidote: Vitamin K
  • Enoxaparin (Lovenox, SQ): Low-molecular-weight heparin; less monitoring needed
  • Major Risk: Bleeding — monitor gums, urine, stool, bruising
🧠 NCLEX Tip: No aspirin or NSAIDs with anticoagulants unless ordered.

4. Chemotherapy Agents

Target cancer cells; highly toxic.

  • Use PPE during handling
  • Monitor WBCs, platelets, RBCs
  • Watch for: stomatitis, alopecia, nausea, immunosuppression
🧠 NCLEX Tip: Neutropenic precautions = no fresh flowers/fruits, avoid crowds.

Pain Medications

Non-Opioids

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol): mild pain/fever | Risk: liver toxicity | Max dose: 4 g/day
  • NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen): pain and inflammation | Risk: GI bleeding, renal damage
🧠 NCLEX Tip: Avoid NSAIDs in patients with peptic ulcers, kidney disease, or bleeding risk.

Perioperative Medications

Common drugs used in the pre-op, intra-op, and post-op settings that NCLEX tests frequently.

Pain & Anti-inflammatory

DrugUseKey Points
Ketorolac (Toradol)NSAID for moderate–severe post-op pain (IV/IM)Max 5 days; risk of GI bleeding and renal injury; avoid if creatinine elevated or platelet disorder; do NOT use with other NSAIDs

GI / Antiemetics

DrugUseKey Points
Ondansetron (Zofran)Antiemetic — prevents post-op/post-anesthesia nausea and vomitingGiven pre-op and post-op; can prolong QT interval — monitor ECG in high-risk patients; common side effects: headache, constipation
Metoclopramide (Reglan)Antiemetic + GI motility agent (prokinetic)Promotes gastric emptying; helps post-op ileus; risk of tardive dyskinesia with long-term use; avoid in bowel obstruction
Famotidine (Pepcid)H₂-blocker — reduces gastric acid secretionGiven pre-op to reduce aspiration risk (reduces stomach acid volume and acidity); also used post-op for stress ulcer prophylaxis in critically ill patients

Diuretics

DrugUseKey Points
Furosemide (Lasix)Loop diuretic — treats fluid overload, edema, cardiogenic shock with pulmonary edemaMonitor: I&O, potassium (risk of hypokalemia), BP; give slowly IV to prevent ototoxicity; contraindicated in anuria (no urine output)
🧠 NCLEX Tip: Furosemide = "Lasix loses potassium" → monitor K⁺. Ondansetron = first-line antiemetic pre/post-op. Famotidine pre-op = reduces aspiration risk. Ketorolac = NSAID post-op pain but max 5 days + GI bleed risk.

NCLEX Priority Safety Concepts

  • Always assess before and after administering pain meds
  • Monitor labs: INR, aPTT, platelets, glucose
  • Know side effects and antidotes cold
  • Educate patients on medication adherence and warning signs
