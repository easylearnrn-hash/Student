What Is HIV?

Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) is a retrovirus that attacks and destroys CD4+ T-helper lymphocytes — the immune cells responsible for coordinating the immune response. As CD4 cells decrease, the immune system weakens, leaving the person susceptible to opportunistic infections and cancers. Without treatment, HIV progresses to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

Transmission

How HIV Is Transmitted
  • Blood-to-blood contact — sharing needles/syringes, needlestick injuries, blood transfusions (rare with current screening)
  • Sexual contact — unprotected sex; anal sex highest risk
  • Vertical transmission — mother to child during pregnancy, labor/delivery, or breastfeeding

NOT transmitted by: casual contact, hugging, touching, sharing food/water, mosquitoes, saliva, tears, or sweat

NCLEX Tip: HIV is NOT spread by casual contact. Standard precautions are sufficient — no isolation is needed for HIV alone.

AIDS-Defining Conditions

Opportunistic Infections & AIDS-Defining Cancers
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) — most common AIDS-defining infection; dry cough, dyspnea, fever
  • Candidiasis (thrush) — oral white plaques; esophageal candidiasis = AIDS-defining
  • Kaposi's sarcoma — purple skin lesions; AIDS-defining cancer
  • CMV retinitis — cytomegalovirus; progressive vision loss; CD4 <50
  • Toxoplasmosis — brain lesions (ring-enhancing on CT); seizures
  • Cryptococcal meningitis — fungal meningitis; CD4 <100
  • Wasting syndrome — involuntary >10% body weight loss
  • MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex) — CD4 <50; disseminated bacterial infection

💊
Zidovudine (AZT) — Key Facts
  • First antiretroviral drug approved; still used in combination ART
  • Given to HIV-positive pregnant women during pregnancy, labor, and delivery to prevent vertical transmission
  • Given to newborns of HIV+ mothers for 6 weeks after birth
  • Also used in PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) for healthcare workers
  • Side effects: bone marrow suppression → anemia, neutropenia; monitor CBC
NCLEX Tip: ART is lifelong — adherence is critical. Missing doses leads to viral resistance and treatment failure. Goal = undetectable viral load (U=U: Undetectable = Untransmittable).

Infection Control & Precautions

Precautions
  • Standard precautions — no isolation needed for HIV alone
  • Gloves when handling blood, body fluids, secretions
  • Safe sharps disposal; never recap needles
  • Report needlestick injuries immediately → PEP within 72 hours (ideally <2 hours)
  • Patient education: avoid sharing needles, razors, toothbrushes; use condoms
  • Instruct immunocompromised patients to avoid raw meat, sick contacts, gardening (fungal spores)

Prevention

Prevention Strategies
  • PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) — daily tenofovir/emtricitabine for high-risk HIV-negative individuals; reduces transmission risk >99%
  • PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) — 28-day ART regimen started within 72 hours of potential exposure (needlestick, unprotected sex)
  • Condom use; abstinence or mutual monogamy
  • No sharing of needles; harm reduction programs
  • Universal HIV screening (CDC recommends for all adults 13–64)
  • Pregnant women: universal HIV testing + ART if positive
