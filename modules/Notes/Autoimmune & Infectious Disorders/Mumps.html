Infectious Disorders

Mumps

Paramyxovirus · parotitis · droplet precautions · orchitis · MMR vaccine · supportive care

What Is Mumps?

Mumps is an acute viral illness caused by the paramyxovirus. It primarily affects the salivary glands, especially the parotid glands (parotitis). Spread by respiratory droplets and saliva, mumps can lead to serious complications including infertility in males. It is preventable by the MMR vaccine.

Transmission & Incubation

Epidemiology
  • Pathogen: Paramyxovirus
  • Transmission: respiratory droplets, direct contact with infected saliva
  • Incubation: 16–18 days (range 12–25 days)
  • Contagious: 2 days before symptoms appear until 5 days after parotid swelling begins
  • Humans are the only reservoir

Signs & Symptoms

Clinical Presentation
  • Prodrome (1–2 days): Low-grade fever, headache, malaise, anorexia, muscle aches
  • Parotitis — hallmark sign: swelling and tenderness of parotid glands; typically unilateral → becomes bilateral; jaw pain, especially with chewing or swallowing
  • Earache (related to parotid swelling)
  • Low-grade to moderate fever (up to 38.9°C / 102°F)
  • ~20% of mumps infections are asymptomatic
NCLEX Tip: Parotitis (swollen, painful parotid glands causing jaw swelling) is the hallmark of mumps. Pain worsens with sour or acidic foods.

Complications

Serious Complications
ComplicationDescription
OrchitisTesticular inflammation — occurs in 20–50% of post-pubertal males with mumps; unilateral; can lead to testicular atrophy and infertility
OophoritisOvarian inflammation in post-pubertal females; rarely causes infertility
Aseptic (viral) meningitisMost common CNS complication; usually self-limiting
PancreatitisUpper abdominal pain, nausea; usually mild
Sensorineural hearing lossRare but permanent; virus damages cochlea
EncephalitisRare; more serious CNS involvement
NCLEX Tip: Orchitis is the most important complication — assess post-pubertal male patients for scrotal swelling and pain; can cause infertility.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
  • Clinical diagnosis — parotitis + known mumps exposure
  • Buccal swab or urine PCR — confirms mumps virus (most sensitive)
  • Serology: IgM antibody (positive in acute infection)
  • Labs: may show leukopenia, elevated serum amylase (parotitis)

Treatment

Supportive Care — No Antiviral Exists
  • No antiviral treatment available — management is supportive only
  • Rest and adequate fluid intake
  • Analgesics: acetaminophen or ibuprofen for fever and pain
  • Ice packs applied to parotid glands for comfort
  • Soft, bland diet — avoid sour/acidic foods (stimulate parotid gland, worsen pain)
  • Orchitis care: scrotal support (snug underwear), ice packs, rest in bed
  • Adequate nutrition — monitor for dehydration

Infection Control

Droplet Precautions
  • Droplet precautions for 5 days after onset of parotid swelling
  • Private room; keep door closed
  • Surgical mask for HCW within 3 feet
  • Isolate patient from susceptible individuals (unvaccinated, immunocompromised, pregnant)
  • Exclude from school/work for 5 days after parotid swelling begins
  • Report to public health (notifiable disease)

Prevention — MMR Vaccine

Vaccination
  • MMR vaccine — live attenuated (measles, mumps, rubella combined)
  • 2 doses: First at 12–15 months; second at 4–6 years
  • Highly effective (~88% for 2 doses)
  • Contraindicated in: pregnancy, severely immunocompromised patients, allergy to gelatin or neomycin
  • Healthcare workers should confirm immunity (2 MMR doses or positive titer)
NCLEX Tip: MMR is a live vaccine — NEVER give to pregnant women or immunocompromised patients. Advise patients to avoid pregnancy for 4 weeks after receiving MMR.
