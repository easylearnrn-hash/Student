Infectious Disorders

Chickenpox (Varicella)

Varicella-zoster virus · airborne precautions · Reye's syndrome · acyclovir · vaccination

What Is Chickenpox?

Chickenpox (varicella) is a highly contagious viral infection caused by the varicella-zoster virus (VZV). It typically occurs in children but can affect unvaccinated adults. After recovery, VZV remains dormant in nerve ganglia and can reactivate later in life as herpes zoster (shingles).

Transmission & Incubation

How It Spreads
  • Airborne transmission — virus spreads via tiny respiratory droplets suspended in air
  • Direct contact with vesicle fluid (blister fluid)
  • Contagious from 1–2 days before rash appears until all lesions have crusted over (~5–7 days after rash onset)
  • Incubation period: 10–21 days after exposure (typically ~14–16 days)
NCLEX Tip: Varicella requires airborne + contact precautions (not just droplet) — negative-pressure private room, N95 mask. Isolation continues until ALL lesions are crusted.

Risk Factors

High-Risk Groups
  • Unvaccinated children and adults
  • Immunocompromised patients (leukemia, HIV, organ transplant)
  • Pregnant women — fetal complications (congenital varicella syndrome, neonatal varicella)
  • Neonates born to non-immune mothers
  • Healthcare workers without documented immunity

Signs & Symptoms

Prodromal Phase (Before Rash)
  • Low-grade fever, fatigue, headache, loss of appetite — begins 1–2 days before rash
Rash Progression — Classic 3 Stages (All Present Simultaneously)
  1. Macules — flat red spots
  2. Papules — raised bumps
  3. Vesicles — fluid-filled blisters (intensely itchy)
  4. Vesicles → pustules → crusting/scabbing

Rash begins on the trunk → face → extremities. Lesions in ALL stages of healing are present at the same time — this is the hallmark of varicella.

NCLEX Tip: "Lesions in all stages simultaneously" = classic chickenpox. This distinguishes varicella from smallpox (all lesions in same stage).

Diagnosis

  • Usually clinical — based on characteristic rash appearance and exposure history
  • Lab confirmation: PCR or direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) test on vesicle fluid — used in atypical or severe cases

Treatment

Supportive Care (Healthy Children)
  • Antihistamines (diphenhydramine) — relieve itching
  • Antipyretics — acetaminophen for fever (NEVER aspirin — Reye's syndrome risk)
  • Skin care — oatmeal baths, calamine lotion, keep nails trimmed to prevent scratching and secondary infection
Antiviral Therapy — Acyclovir or Valacyclovir

Reserved for high-risk patients:

  • Immunocompromised patients
  • Adolescents/adults with severe disease
  • Pregnant women
  • Neonates
NCLEX Tip: No aspirin in children with viral illness — causes Reye's syndrome (severe brain and liver swelling). Use acetaminophen instead.

Infection Control

Isolation Requirements
  • Airborne + contact precautions
  • Negative-pressure private room
  • N95 respirator for healthcare workers (surgical mask is insufficient)
  • Isolation continues until all lesions are crusted and dry
  • Non-immune staff should NOT care for varicella patients

Prevention — Varicella Vaccine

Varicella (Chickenpox) Vaccine
  • Live attenuated vaccine
  • Schedule: 1st dose at 12–15 months; 2nd dose at 4–6 years
  • May be given to adults with no documented immunity
  • Contraindicated in: pregnancy, severe immunosuppression
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis: varicella-zoster immune globulin (VZIG) for high-risk non-immune exposures
NCLEX Tip: Varicella vaccine is a live attenuated vaccine — same as MMR. Both are contraindicated in pregnant women and immunocompromised patients.

Complications

ComplicationNCLEX Focus
Secondary bacterial infectionMost common — keep skin clean; monitor for cellulitis, impetigo
PneumoniaMonitor respiratory status — especially in adults and immunocompromised
EncephalitisAltered mental status, seizures — serious CNS complication
Reye's syndromeAvoid aspirin in children with ANY viral illness — liver and brain swelling
Herpes zoster (shingles)Reactivation of dormant VZV later in life — unilateral dermatomal rash
Congenital varicella syndromeIf mother infected in 1st/2nd trimester — fetal limb hypoplasia, scarring, eye/brain defects
