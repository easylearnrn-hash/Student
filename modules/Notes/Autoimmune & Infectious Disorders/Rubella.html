Infectious Disorders

Rubella (German Measles)

Togavirus · 3-day measles · CRS · postauricular lymphadenopathy · MMR vaccine · teratogenic in pregnancy

What Is Rubella?

Rubella (also called German measles or 3-day measles) is a contagious viral illness caused by rubella virus (Togavirus). In children and adults it is typically mild, but infection during pregnancy — especially the first trimester — is devastating, causing Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS).

Transmission & Incubation

Epidemiology
  • Pathogen: Rubella virus (Togavirus)
  • Transmission: airborne and droplet — respiratory secretions; direct contact
  • Incubation: 14–21 days
  • Contagious: 7 days before rash appears to 7 days after rash onset
  • Largely eradicated in U.S. due to MMR vaccination; still endemic in developing countries

Signs & Symptoms

Classic Triad
  • Low-grade fever (<38.3°C / 101°F)
  • Maculopapular rash — begins on face, spreads downward (head to toe); clears in 3 days (hence "3-day measles")
  • Postauricular lymphadenopathy — swollen lymph nodes behind the ears and at the base of the skull (distinctive and diagnostic)
  • Mild upper respiratory symptoms (cough, runny nose)
  • Arthralgia/arthritis (joint pain) — common in adult women
  • Forchheimer spots: petechiae on soft palate (non-specific)
NCLEX Tip: Key distinguishing feature of rubella = postauricular lymphadenopathy (swollen lymph nodes BEHIND the ears). This is rare in other rash illnesses.

Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS)

🤰
Rubella in Pregnancy — Highest Risk in 1st Trimester
  • Rubella during first 12 weeks of pregnancy causes CRS in up to 85% of cases
  • Classic CRS triad:
    • Cataracts (congenital blindness)
    • Sensorineural deafness (hearing loss)
    • Congenital heart defects — most commonly patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)
  • Also: microcephaly, intellectual disability, developmental delays, glaucoma, thrombocytopenic purpura
  • Spontaneous abortion or stillbirth may occur
NCLEX Tip: CRS classic triad = cataracts + deafness + heart defects (PDA). Most teratogenic in first trimester. Riskiest before 12 weeks gestation.

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
  • Rubella IgM antibody — confirms acute infection (positive within days of rash onset)
  • Rubella IgG titer — checks immunity status (prenatal screening)
  • Nasopharyngeal swab + viral culture or PCR for virus confirmation
  • Prenatal screening: non-immune women identified → vaccinate postpartum

Treatment

Supportive Care — No Antiviral
  • No specific antiviral therapy — supportive care only
  • Rest and adequate fluid intake
  • Acetaminophen for fever and joint pain
  • Isolation of infected individuals from susceptible persons
  • Pregnant women exposed to rubella: consult OB immediately; monitor for CRS

Infection Control

Droplet Precautions
  • Droplet precautions — surgical mask, private room
  • Isolate for 7 days after rash onset
  • Infants with CRS may shed virus for months — isolate and use contact precautions
  • Exclude from school or work for 7 days after rash appears
  • Protect pregnant women — keep non-immune pregnant HCW away from patient

Prevention — MMR Vaccine

Vaccination
  • MMR vaccine — live attenuated; rubella component provides lifelong immunity
  • 2 doses: 12–15 months and 4–6 years
  • Prenatal care: check rubella titer; non-immune women vaccinated postpartum (after delivery)
  • DO NOT give MMR during pregnancy — live vaccine, teratogenic
  • Avoid pregnancy for 28 days (4 weeks) after receiving MMR vaccine
  • Contraindicated: pregnancy, immunocompromised, severe allergy to gelatin or neomycin
NCLEX Tip: If a woman is found to be non-immune to rubella during pregnancy, vaccinate her postpartum before discharge — NEVER during pregnancy.
