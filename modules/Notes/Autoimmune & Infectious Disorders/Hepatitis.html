Infectious Disorders

Hepatitis

Types A–E · fecal-oral vs. blood-borne · jaundice · ALT/AST · HBsAg · 3 phases

Hepatitis Types At a Glance

TypeTransmissionChronic?Vaccine?Key Feature
Hep A (HAV) Fecal-oral (contaminated food/water, shellfish) ❌ No ✅ Yes Self-limiting; most common in travelers
Hep B (HBV) Blood, sexual contact, vertical (mother → baby at birth) ✅ Yes (10% adults; 90% neonates) ✅ Yes (HBV vaccine series) Most common cause of chronic hepatitis worldwide
Hep C (HCV) Blood-to-blood (IV drug use, needlestick, tattoos) ✅ Yes (~85%) ❌ No vaccine Most common chronic bloodborne infection in U.S.
Hep D (HDV) Blood/sexual; ONLY occurs with HBV (co-infection or superinfection) ✅ Yes ✅ Prevented by HBV vaccine Requires HBV to replicate; worsens HBV prognosis
Hep E (HEV) Fecal-oral; contaminated water ❌ No ❌ No (not available in U.S.) Dangerous in pregnancy — high maternal mortality
NCLEX Tip: A & E = fecal-oral; B, C, D = blood/body fluids. Only C has no vaccine. Hep D only in Hep B patients. Hep E is deadly in pregnancy.

Three Phases of Hepatitis

Disease Course
PhaseDurationKey Symptoms
1. Prodromal (Pre-icteric) 1–2 weeks Fatigue, malaise, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, RUQ pain, low-grade fever, myalgia; may have dark urine (bilirubin starting to rise)
2. Icteric (Jaundice Phase) 2–6 weeks Jaundice (yellowing of skin/sclera); dark (cola-colored) urine; clay-colored (pale) stools; pruritus (itching); hepatomegaly; splenomegaly; symptoms improve
3. Recovery (Post-icteric) Weeks to months Jaundice fades; appetite returns; fatigue may persist; liver enzymes normalize
NCLEX Tip: The classic triad of the icteric phase: jaundice + dark urine + clay-colored stools. This occurs due to impaired bilirubin conjugation and excretion.

Key Laboratory Findings

Important Labs
LabSignificance
ALT (alanine aminotransferase)Most specific liver enzyme; elevated in hepatitis; best indicator of liver cell damage
AST (aspartate aminotransferase)Elevated in liver damage; also elevated in muscle damage
Total bilirubinElevated → jaundice; direct (conjugated) bilirubin excreted in urine
HBsAg (hepatitis B surface antigen)Positive = active HBV infection (acute or chronic)
Anti-HBs (antibody to HBsAg)Positive = immunity (after vaccination or recovery from HBV)
HCV RNA (PCR)Detects active HCV infection; viral load monitoring
PT/INRProlonged = severe liver dysfunction (clotting factor synthesis impaired)

Treatment & Nursing Care

Management
  • Rest — activity restriction during acute phase
  • Nutrition — high-calorie, high-carbohydrate, low-fat diet; small frequent meals; largest meal in the morning (when appetite is best)
  • Hydration — adequate fluid intake
  • No alcohol — alcohol is hepatotoxic; absolute prohibition
  • Hepatitis B: antivirals — tenofovir, entecavir; alpha-interferon
  • Hepatitis C: direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — sofosbuvir/ledipasvir (Harvoni); high cure rates (>95%)
  • Monitor prothrombin time, bilirubin, liver enzymes

Hepatitis CStandard precautionsNo sharing needles, razors, toothbrushes; no vaccine — prevent exposure

HBV Prophylaxis

💉
Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) for Hepatitis B
  • Unvaccinated persons exposed to HBV: HBIG (hepatitis B immune globulin) + begin HBV vaccine series within 24 hours
  • Newborns of HBsAg-positive mothers: HBIG + HBV vaccine within 12 hours of birth
  • HBV vaccine series: 3 doses (0, 1, 6 months)

NCLEX Must-Know Summary

⭐
Key Takeaways
  • A & E = fecal-oral; B, C, D = blood/body fluids
  • No vaccine for Hep C; vaccine for A and B
  • Hep D only with Hep B; Hep E deadly in pregnancy
  • 3 phases: prodromal → icteric (jaundice) → recovery
  • Icteric phase: jaundice + dark urine + clay-colored stools
  • Best liver enzymes: ALT and AST
  • HBsAg positive = active HBV infection; Anti-HBs positive = immune
  • Diet: high-calorie, low-fat; no alcohol; largest meal in morning
  • HBV PEP: HBIG + vaccine within 24 hours
