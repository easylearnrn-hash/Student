Infectious Disorders

Clostridium difficile (C. diff)

Gram-positive · spore-forming · contact precautions · soap & water · oral vancomycin · NO antidiarrheals

What Is C. diff?

Clostridioides difficile (C. diff) is a gram-positive, spore-forming, anaerobic bacterium that produces toxins (A and B) causing inflammation of the colon. It is the #1 cause of healthcare-associated infectious diarrhea in the U.S. It thrives when normal gut flora is disrupted by antibiotics.

Risk Factors

Who Is at Risk?
  • Recent antibiotic use — especially clindamycin, fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin), cephalosporins, ampicillin
  • Age >65 years (older adults most vulnerable)
  • Recent hospitalization or long-term care facility stay
  • Use of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) — reduce stomach acid, allowing spore survival
  • Immunocompromised patients (HIV, cancer, organ transplant)
  • Previous C. diff infection
NCLEX Tip: Any patient with diarrhea after antibiotic use should be suspected for C. diff until proven otherwise.

Signs & Symptoms

Clinical Presentation
  • Profuse, watery diarrhea — ≥3 loose stools in 24 hours (hallmark)
  • Foul-smelling stools (distinctive odor)
  • Low-grade fever
  • Diffuse abdominal cramping and pain
  • Leukocytosis (elevated WBC — sometimes very high, >30,000)
  • Nausea, anorexia
  • Severe: rebound tenderness, abdominal distension (toxic megacolon)

Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests
  • Stool toxin assay — detects toxin A and/or B (most common)
  • PCR of stool — most sensitive and specific test
  • EIA (enzyme immunoassay) for GDH antigen + toxin
  • Colonoscopy/sigmoidoscopy: pseudomembranes (yellow-white plaques on colon wall) — confirms pseudomembranous colitis
  • Labs: elevated WBC, hypoalbuminemia, elevated creatinine (severe disease)

🚫
NEVER Give Antidiarrheals

Antidiarrheals (loperamide/Imodium) are contraindicated in C. diff — they slow bowel motility and trap toxins in the colon, causing toxic megacolon (life-threatening dilation of the colon).

NCLEX Tip: Vancomycin for C. diff must be given orally (PO) — IV vancomycin does NOT reach the colon and is ineffective for C. diff.

Infection Control

Contact Precautions — Critical Rules
  • Contact precautions — private room; gown and gloves on entry
  • Soap and water ONLY — alcohol-based hand sanitizers (Purell) do NOT kill C. diff spores; MUST use soap and water
  • Bleach (sodium hypochlorite) solution for environmental cleaning — kills spores
  • Dedicated patient equipment (thermometer, blood pressure cuff, stethoscope)
  • Continue contact precautions until diarrhea resolves (>48 hours after last loose stool)
  • Do NOT use shared commodes or bathrooms without thorough cleaning between uses
NCLEX Tip: Soap and water (NOT alcohol gel) is mandatory for hand hygiene with C. diff — the most tested infection control point for this disease!

Key Takeaways
  • Cause: C. diff — gram-positive, spore-forming bacterium; disrupts gut flora after antibiotics
  • #1 hospital-acquired infectious diarrhea
  • S&S: profuse watery diarrhea (≥3/day), foul smell, abdominal cramps, fever, leukocytosis
  • Treatment: oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin — NOT IV vancomycin
  • NO antidiarrheals — risk of toxic megacolon
  • Hand hygiene: soap and water ONLY — alcohol gel does not kill spores
  • Cleaning: bleach for environmental decontamination
  • Contact precautions — gown + gloves; private room
