Heparin (Short-Term Anticoagulant)

Heparin Key Facts

  • Route: IV or SubQ
  • Onset: Immediate (IV)
  • Monitor: aPTT every 6 hrs during titration
  • Antidote: Protamine Sulfate
  • Uses: DVT, PE, ACS, hemodialysis, surgery prevention

If aPTT >80 seconds:

  • Stop infusion immediately
  • Assess vitals for bleeding signs (↓ BP, ↑ HR, pallor)
  • Prepare to administer Protamine Sulfate
  • Notify provider before administering antidote
HIT — Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia: If platelet count drops >50% on Heparin → suspect HIT → STOP Heparin immediately → switch to direct thrombin inhibitors (Argatroban). Paradoxically increases clotting risk.
Warfarin (Long-Term Anticoagulant)

Warfarin Key Facts

  • Route: Oral (tablet)
  • Onset: 2–5 days (delayed)
  • Monitor: INR and PT only
  • Therapeutic INR: 2–3 (standard); 2.5–3.5 (mechanical heart valves)
  • Antidote: Vitamin K
  • Uses: A-fib, DVT, PE, mechanical heart valves

Warfarin Teaching

  • Consistent Vitamin K intake (leafy greens) — do NOT avoid entirely, just be consistent
  • Avoid NSAIDs (↑ bleeding risk)
  • No grapefruit (↑ Warfarin levels)
  • Report any unusual bleeding, blood in urine/stool
  • Regular INR monitoring (can be done at home)
  • Wear medical alert bracelet
Antidotes — Critical NCLEX Knowledge
Heparin → Protamine Sulfate
Warfarin → Vitamin K (Phytonadione)
Dabigatran (Pradaxa) → Idarucizumab (Praxbind)
Rivaroxaban / Apixaban → Andexanet alfa (Andexxa)
Transitioning Heparin → Warfarin

Overlap Both Medications for 5–7 Days

  • Warfarin takes 2–5 days to reach therapeutic INR
  • Heparin provides immediate anticoagulation during this bridging period
  • Overlap duration: 5–7 days minimum
  • NEVER stop Heparin until INR is therapeutic (2–3) for 2 consecutive days
  • Once INR is stable at 2–3, discontinue Heparin and continue Warfarin alone
  • Warfarin INR monitoring: check every 3 weeks once stable
🚨 Critical Rule: INR >4 or aPTT >80 sec = high hemorrhage risk → STOP anticoagulant → assess vitals → prepare antidote → call provider (in that order).

Internal Bleeding Priority — NCLEX

"STOP! ASSESS! PREPARE! CALL!"

  • STOP the anticoagulant infusion or medication
  • ASSESS vitals: ↓ BP, ↑ HR, pale skin, active bleeding
  • PREPARE the antidote
  • CALL the provider immediately
📌 Signs of Internal Bleeding: ↓ BP + ↑ HR = possible internal bleeding → assess immediately. Dark tarry stools (melena), blood in urine (hematuria), severe headache (intracranial bleed).
