Cardiovascular System

Pacemakers & ICDs

Implantable devices for bradycardia and life-threatening arrhythmias — indications, nursing care, and chest pain priorities

Pacemakers

What Is a Pacemaker?

A device implanted under the skin that delivers electrical impulses to stimulate the heart to contract and maintain an adequate heart rate.

Temporary
External wires — used post-MI or during acute bradycardia
Permanent
Surgically implanted under the skin
Indications

When Is a Pacemaker Needed?

  • Bradycardia — slow heart rate (<60 bpm with symptoms)
  • Heart Block — 2° Type II or 3° (electrical signals blocked between atria and ventricles)
  • Asystole — flatline with reversible cause
  • Sick Sinus Syndrome
  • Post-cardiac surgery prophylaxis
Nursing Priorities — Pacemaker

Post-Implant Nursing Care

  • Monitor ECG — verify pacing spikes appear correctly
  • Check pacing threshold — lowest current needed to pace the heart
  • Assess for failure to pace (no spike) or failure to capture (spike with no QRS)
  • Restrict affected arm movement for 24–48 hrs (prevent lead displacement)
  • Avoid strong electromagnetic fields (MRI, arc welding, some electrical equipment)
  • Teach patient: carry pacemaker ID card at all times
  • Instruct to report: hiccups (diaphragm stimulation = lead displacement), dizziness, syncope
ICDs — Implantable Cardioverter Defibrillators

What Is an ICD?

  • Device that monitors rhythm continuously and delivers a shock if it detects V-Fib or V-Tach
  • Indicated for patients at risk of sudden cardiac death

ICD Nursing Education

  • If ICD fires: patient should lie down, note time, and notify provider if symptoms continue
  • Others touching patient when ICD fires may feel a mild shock — not dangerous
  • Avoid strong magnetic fields (same as pacemaker)
  • If ICD fires repeatedly → report immediately (storm = emergency)
Chest Pain Priority Protocol
⚠️ When a patient complains of chest pain — follow ABC approach + MONA

MONA (Morphine, Oxygen, Nitrates, Aspirin)

Note: Not listed in administration order — prioritize as below

1
Assess ABCs — airway, breathing, circulation
2
Apply Oxygen — if O₂ sat <90%
3
Vital signs — BP, HR, RR, SpO₂
4
12-lead ECG within 10 minutes — detect STEMI
5
IV access — large-bore IV
6
Labs: Troponin, CK-MB, BMP
7
Medications: Aspirin (antiplatelet) → Nitroglycerin (vasodilator) → Morphine (if needed) → Heparin
🚨 Check BP before giving nitroglycerin — do NOT give if systolic BP <90 mmHg or if patient took sildenafil (Viagra) in last 24–48 hrs.

Patient Teaching — Lifestyle

Diet

  • Low sodium (<2 g/day)
  • Low saturated fat & cholesterol
  • High fiber, fruits & vegetables
  • Avoid processed foods, deli meats, canned soups

Lifestyle

  • No smoking
  • Weight loss if BMI >25
  • Fluid restriction 1.5–2 L/day (HF patients)
  • Monitor daily weight — report >2 lb gain
📌 NCLEX Tip: Supine + hypotension? Avoid Trendelenburg. Always support oxygenation and circulation first.
