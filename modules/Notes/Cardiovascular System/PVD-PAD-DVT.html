PVD / PAD / DVT

Peripheral Vascular Disease, Peripheral Arterial Disease, and Deep Vein Thrombosis — comparison, assessment, and management

Quick Overview
ConditionVesselsProblemKey Sign
PVD (Peripheral Vascular Disease) Arteries and/or Veins Umbrella term — includes PAD + venous disease Skin changes, ulcers, varicosities
PAD (Peripheral Arterial Disease) Arteries only Atherosclerosis → narrowed arteries → ↓ blood flow Intermittent claudication, 6 P's
DVT (Deep Vein Thrombosis) Deep veins (usually leg) Thrombus formation → risk of PE Unilateral calf pain, warmth, swelling
PVD — Peripheral Vascular Disease

Causes

  • Valve incompetence (venous insufficiency)
  • Thrombus formation
  • Atherosclerosis
  • Vasospasm
  • Inflammation

Venous Insufficiency Signs

  • Varicose veins, edema
  • Brownish skin pigmentation (hemosiderin)
  • Venous stasis ulcers — medial malleolus, irregular edges, painless
  • Warm, moist skin
  • Pulses present
PAD — Peripheral Arterial Disease

Pathophysiology & Symptoms

  • Atherosclerosis narrows peripheral arteries
  • Most common: femoral, popliteal arteries
  • Intermittent claudication — leg pain with walking, relieved by rest
  • Rest pain in advanced disease
  • ABI (Ankle-Brachial Index) < 0.9 = PAD

6 P's of Acute Arterial Occlusion

  • Pain — sudden, severe
  • Pallor — pale, white limb
  • Pulselessness — absent distal pulses
  • Paresthesia — numbness/tingling
  • Poikilothermia (Polar) — cold limb
  • Paralysis — inability to move (late sign)

PAD Assessment Tests

  • Buerger's-Allen's Test: elevate legs → pallor → dependent position → rubor (redness) confirms PAD
  • ABI: ankle pressure ÷ brachial pressure; <0.9 abnormal
  • Doppler ultrasound
  • CT angiography

PAD Management

  • Smoking cessation (absolute priority)
  • Antiplatelet therapy (Aspirin, Clopidogrel)
  • Exercise therapy — supervised walking program
  • Revascularization (PTA, stent, bypass)
  • Position: legs dependent (hang down) to promote arterial flow
DVT — Deep Vein Thrombosis

Virchow's Triad (DVT Causes)

  • Stasis — immobility, long flights, post-op
  • Hypercoagulability — oral contraceptives, cancer, clotting disorders
  • Endothelial damage — trauma, surgery, catheter

Signs & Symptoms

  • Unilateral calf pain, tenderness
  • Unilateral edema, warmth, redness
  • Homan's Sign: calf pain on dorsiflexion — NOT reliable, rarely tested now
  • Low-grade fever possible
  • D-dimer elevated
🚨 DVT → PE Risk: Thrombus can embolize to pulmonary vasculature → sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hypoxia, tachycardia. Suspect PE if DVT patient suddenly deteriorates.

DVT Treatment

  • Anticoagulation: Heparin (acute) → Warfarin or NOAC (long-term, 3–6 months)
  • Leg elevation (above heart level)
  • Compression stockings (graduated)
  • Early ambulation once anticoagulated
  • IVC filter if anticoagulation contraindicated

Nursing Priorities

NCLEX Key Points

  • Never massage a suspected DVT leg — risk of embolization
  • Elevate venous insufficiency legs; hang PAD legs (dependent)
  • Assess pulses before and after any intervention
  • DVT prevention: ambulation, compression devices, anticoagulation in post-op patients
  • 6 P's = arterial emergency → notify provider immediately
Remember: Venous = Veins = Varicosities, medial malleolus, elevation helps. Arterial = Arteries = Absent pulses, pallor, pain, dependent positioning helps.
