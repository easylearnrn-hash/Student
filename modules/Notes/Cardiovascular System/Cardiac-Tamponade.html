Cardiovascular System

Cardiac Tamponade

Beck's Triad, pericardiocentesis, EKG findings, and emergency nursing management

Definition

What Is Cardiac Tamponade?

  • Accumulation of fluid in the pericardial sac that compresses the heart
  • Fluid increases intrapericardial pressure → compresses all four chambers
  • Results in ↓ ventricular filling → ↓ cardiac output → obstructive shock
  • Can be rapidly fatal if not treated — true emergency
  • As little as 150–200 mL of acute fluid can cause tamponade (rapid accumulation = less time to compensate)
Causes

Common Causes

  • Pericarditis — most common (viral, bacterial, uremic)
  • Trauma — penetrating (stabbing) or blunt chest injury
  • Post-cardiac surgery (hemopericardium)
  • Malignancy (lung, breast, lymphoma)
  • Aortic dissection

Additional Causes

  • MI with free wall rupture
  • Post-catheterization (iatrogenic)
  • Autoimmune diseases (SLE)
  • Uremia
  • Radiation therapy
Beck's Triad — Classic Signs
🔇
Muffled Heart Sounds
Fluid around heart dampens sounds — difficult to auscultate
📈
Jugular Venous Distension (JVD)
Impaired right heart filling → backed-up venous pressure
📉
Hypotension
↓ cardiac output → ↓ BP; narrow pulse pressure
Additional Signs & Symptoms

Hemodynamic Signs

  • Tachycardia (compensatory)
  • Narrow pulse pressure (<25 mmHg)
  • Pulsus Paradoxus — ↓ systolic BP >10 mmHg during inspiration
    Example: BP 120/80 on expiration → 109/80 on inspiration = 11 mmHg drop = pulsus paradoxus
  • Cool, clammy skin (shock)
  • Decreased urine output

Respiratory & General

  • Dyspnea, orthopnea
  • Anxiety, restlessness
  • Distant/muffled heart tones
  • Dysphagia (large effusion compressing esophagus)
EKG Findings

Cardiac Tamponade on EKG

  • Electrical Alternans — alternating QRS amplitude (heart swings in fluid) — pathognomonic
  • Low voltage QRS complexes in all leads
  • Sinus tachycardia
  • Diffuse ST elevation if pericarditis is the cause
Electrical Alternans: Beat-to-beat variation in QRS height on EKG = cardiac tamponade until proven otherwise.
Diagnosis

Diagnostic Tests

  • Echocardiogram (ECHO) — gold standard; shows pericardial effusion and chamber compression
  • Chest X-ray: enlarged "water bottle" cardiac silhouette
  • EKG: electrical alternans, low voltage, tachycardia
  • Pulsus paradoxus: >10 mmHg drop in systolic BP with inspiration
Treatment

Emergency Treatment

  • Pericardiocentesis — needle aspiration of pericardial fluid (immediate)
  • Position: semi-recumbent, 45° angle
  • Needle inserted subxiphoid, directed toward left shoulder
  • Even small volume removal (20–50 mL) can dramatically ↑ CO
  • Send fluid for culture, cytology, chemistry

Supportive Measures

  • IV fluid bolus to maintain preload
  • High-flow oxygen
  • Avoid diuretics and vasodilators (worsen preload)
  • Avoid positive pressure ventilation if possible (↓ venous return)
  • Surgical pericardial window for recurrent tamponade
Nursing Priority

NCLEX Must Know

  • Beck's Triad = Muffled heart sounds + JVD + Hypotension → tamponade until proven otherwise
  • First action: notify provider immediately + prepare for pericardiocentesis
  • Do NOT delay — patient can arrest within minutes
  • Electrical alternans on EKG = tamponade
  • Post-pericardiocentesis: monitor for re-accumulation (symptoms can return)
Memory: Beck's = "Muffled Jug(JVD) of water(hypotension)" — imagine a bottle of water (water bottle heart on X-ray) making muffled sounds, causing JVD, causing hypotension.
📌 Differentiation: Cardiac tamponade causes a sharp chest pain that worsens during expiration and coughing. MI does NOT typically present with a cough — cough with chest pain = think tamponade or pericarditis.
