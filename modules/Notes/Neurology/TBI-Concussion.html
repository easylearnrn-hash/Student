📌 What Is TBI & Concussion?

Core Definitions

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) — injury to the brain caused by an external physical force such as a blow, jolt, or penetrating injury. Includes mild, moderate, and severe injuries.

Concussion — a mild traumatic brain injury that temporarily disrupts normal brain function. It is the most common mild form of TBI.

Word Breakdown — TBI
  • Traumatic — caused by physical injury
  • Brain — the organ controlling consciousness, thinking, movement, sensation, breathing, and vital body functions
  • Injury — damage to body tissue
Common Causes
Falls Motor Vehicle Accidents Motorcycle Accidents Sports Injuries Assault Firearms Blast Injury Penetrating Trauma

Older adults → higher risk from falls. Young adults → car accidents and sports.

⚠️
Most Important NCLEX Concept

The skull is rigid and cannot expand. When bleeding, swelling, or fluid builds up after trauma, pressure rises, reducing blood flow to the brain — causing serious damage or death.

📝
Concussion — Key Points

A concussion is called "mild" based on severity classification, but it can still cause important symptoms and requires careful monitoring.
Loss of consciousness is NOT required for a concussion diagnosis. A patient may be fully awake and still have a concussion.

📊 Classification of TBI

Mild TBI

Usually includes concussion

  • Brief confusion
  • Possible brief loss of consciousness
  • Headache, dizziness, nausea
  • Memory problems
Moderate TBI

More serious — longer altered consciousness, more risk for bleeding and swelling

Severe TBI

Major neurologic impairment

  • Coma
  • Significant brain swelling
  • May need airway support, surgery, or ICU care
💡
NCLEX Key Relationship

All concussions are TBIs — but not all TBIs are concussions. Concussion is a mild subset of the broader TBI category.

🔬 Primary vs Secondary Injury

Primary Injury

Immediate damage at the moment of trauma — cannot be undone.

  • Skull fracture
  • Contusion (brain bruise)
  • Laceration (tissue tear)
  • Hemorrhage (bleeding)
  • Diffuse axonal injury (widespread nerve fiber damage from shearing forces)
Secondary Injury

Damage that develops after initial trauma — this is where nursing care matters most.

  • Cerebral edema (brain swelling)
  • Increased ICP
  • Hypoxia (low oxygen)
  • Ischemia (inadequate blood flow)
  • Hypotension (low blood pressure)
  • Infection
  • Seizures
🎯
Major Nursing Goal

Primary injury cannot be undone. The major nursing goal is to prevent or limit secondary brain injury — by protecting oxygenation, perfusion, and ICP.

🔄 Pathophysiology — How It Develops

Step-by-Step Flow After Trauma
  • 1
    Brain strikes inside of skullBrain tissue may bruise, tear, or bleed
  • 2
    Blood vessels rupture → hemorrhageBleeding into or around brain tissue
  • 3
    Brain tissue becomes inflamed → edemaSwelling develops within the rigid skull
  • 4
    Skull cannot expand → ICP risesPressure builds inside the fixed bony structure
  • 5
    Cerebral perfusion decreasesBrain receives less oxygenated blood
  • 6
    Ischemia → more brain damageNeurons deprived of oxygen begin to fail

Concussion Pathophysiology

In concussion, the injury causes temporary changes in brain function rather than a large structural lesion in most cases. The patient may have:

Confusion Headache Dizziness Amnesia Slowed Thinking Poor Concentration
⚠️
Don't Be Fooled

A concussion may look mild at first, but symptoms can worsen later if bleeding or swelling develops. Always monitor closely.

🧠 Important Types of Brain Injury in TBI

TypeWhat It IsKey Notes
Concussion Mild TBI with temporary brain dysfunction Headache, dizziness, confusion, light sensitivity, memory problems
Contusion Bruise on brain tissue More serious than concussion; may cause localized deficits
Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI) Widespread nerve fiber damage from rapid acceleration/deceleration High-speed trauma; often causes prolonged unconsciousness or coma

Hematomas — Bleeding in the Brain

TypeLocationBleeding SourceKey NCLEX Feature
Epidural Hematoma Between skull and dura mater (outer brain covering) Arterial (rapid) Often associated with skull fracture. Classic: brief LOC → lucid interval → rapid decline. Neurosurgical emergency.
Subdural Hematoma Between dura and arachnoid mater Venous (slower) Can be acute, subacute, or chronic. Common in older adults (brain shrinkage stretches veins). Symptoms may be delayed.
Intracerebral Hemorrhage Within brain tissue itself Arterial or venous Directly increases ICP and damages brain tissue
Subarachnoid Hemorrhage Between arachnoid and pia mater (CSF space) Arterial May occur after trauma or spontaneously from aneurysm rupture
🚨
Lucid Interval — Classic NCLEX Trap (Epidural Hematoma)

Patient has a brief loss of consciousness → then appears to wake up and feel fine (lucid interval) → then deteriorates rapidly. This is a neurosurgical emergency.

Open vs Closed Head Injury

Closed Head Injury

Brain injury without skull penetration

  • Concussion
  • Contusion
  • Diffuse axonal injury
Open Head Injury

Skull is penetrated

  • High risk for infection
  • Direct tissue destruction

💀 Skull Fractures

TypeDescription
Linear FractureSimple break in bone
Depressed FracturePortion of skull pushed inward — may need surgical elevation
Basilar Skull FractureFracture at the base of the skull — has characteristic clinical signs

Signs of Basilar Skull Fracture — High-Yield NCLEX

Classic Signs
  • Battle sign — bruising behind the ears over the mastoid area
  • Raccoon eyes — bilateral periorbital bruising
  • Hemotympanum — blood behind the eardrum
  • CSF leak from nose (rhinorrhea) or ears (otorrhea)
CSF Leak Clues
  • Clear drainage from nose or ear
  • Positive glucose in drainage (in some settings)
  • Halo sign — ring pattern when bloody drainage spreads on gauze (CSF mixed with blood)
🚨
NCLEX Safety Rule — Basilar Skull Fracture

Do NOT insert anything into the nose if basilar skull fracture is suspected — including a nasogastric tube or nasal suction — unless specifically ordered and cleared. Risk of entering the brain.

🩺 Signs & Symptoms

Mild TBI / Concussion
  • Headache
  • Dizziness, nausea, vomiting
  • Confusion, amnesia
  • Feeling "foggy"
  • Blurred vision
  • Fatigue, irritability
  • Sensitivity to light or noise
  • Balance problems
  • Difficulty concentrating
Moderate to Severe TBI
  • Decreasing LOC
  • Repeated vomiting
  • Severe headache
  • Unequal pupils
  • Seizures
  • Weakness
  • Slurred speech
  • Abnormal posturing
  • Agitation, coma
  • Signs of increased ICP

Loss of Consciousness & Amnesia

Loss of Consciousness (LOC)

Temporary unresponsiveness — may or may not happen with concussion. Not required for the diagnosis.

Amnesia
  • Inability to remember events before the injury (retrograde)
  • Inability to remember events after the injury (anterograde)

Supports concussion diagnosis

⬆️ Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

Increased ICP can happen due to bleeding, swelling, increased CSF, or expanding hematoma. As ICP rises, cerebral perfusion decreases — worsening brain injury.

Signs of Increased ICP
  • Decreasing LOC
  • Restlessness, confusion
  • Headache
  • Vomiting (especially without nausea)
  • Pupillary changes
  • Weakness
  • Abnormal posturing
  • Seizures
☠️ Cushing's Triad — Late & Dangerous

Indicates impending herniation — medical emergency.

  • Bradycardia — slow heart rate
  • Widened pulse pressure — large gap between systolic and diastolic BP
  • Irregular respirations — abnormal breathing pattern

🔴 Brain Herniation & Posturing

🚨
Brain Herniation

Dangerous shifting of brain tissue due to severe pressure — compresses vital areas controlling breathing and circulation. Life-threatening emergency.

Signs: sudden drop in LOC · fixed or dilated pupils · posturing · abnormal respirations · Cushing's triad

🦵
Decorticate Posturing

Abnormal flexion of arms toward the body with extended legs.
Indicates severe injury above the brainstem.

⚠️
Decerebrate Posturing

Abnormal extension and pronation of arms and legs.
Indicates brainstem involvement — worse than decorticate.

🔑 Memory Trick
Decorticate = toward Core (flexion)  |  Decerebrate = away (extension) = worse

🔍 Assessment of TBI

📌
Always Start With ABCs

The brain-injured patient may lose airway protection. Airway, breathing, circulation come before any neurologic assessment.

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Standardized Consciousness Tool
ComponentWhat It Measures
Eye OpeningSpontaneous, to voice, to pain, none
Verbal ResponseOriented, confused, words, sounds, none
Motor ResponseObeys, localizes, withdraws, flexion, extension, none

Lower score = worse neurologic status. A falling GCS is alarming — report immediately.

Full Neuro Assessment Includes

Neurologic
  • LOC and orientation
  • Pupil size and reaction
  • Motor response and strength
  • Speech and sensation
  • GCS score and trend
  • Seizure activity
Vital Signs
  • Blood pressure
  • Heart rate
  • Respiratory rate and pattern
  • Temperature
  • Oxygen saturation

🧪 Diagnostic Tests

TestPurposeNotes
CT Scan Quickly shows bleeding, swelling, fractures, and mass effect First choice in acute head trauma
MRI Detailed brain imaging with magnetic fields and radio waves More detailed than CT; used for further evaluation after initial stabilization
X-Rays May identify fractures Less informative than CT for head injury
ICP Monitoring Directly measures pressure inside skull via implanted device Used in severe TBI
Neurologic Checks Repeated ongoing assessments of LOC, pupils, motor, GCS Essential and continuous
ABGs Arterial blood gases — measure oxygen, CO₂, and acid-base balance Ordered if respiratory compromise is present

🩺 Priority Nursing Management

1. Maintain Airway

Always First Priority
  • Keep airway open
  • Suction if needed
  • Administer oxygen as ordered
  • Prepare for intubation if necessary

A decreased LOC can cause loss of airway protection.

2. Support Breathing

  • Monitor respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and breathing pattern
  • ABGs if ordered
  • Hypoxia worsens brain injury — maintain adequate oxygenation at all times

3. Maintain Circulation

  • Avoid hypotension — low blood pressure reduces cerebral perfusion
  • Monitor blood pressure, heart rate, IV access, and fluid status

4. Frequent Neuro Checks

  • Assess LOC, GCS, pupils, motor function, posturing, and orientation
  • Report any deterioration immediately

5. Prevent Increased ICP

ICP Nursing Interventions
  • Elevate HOB ~30 degrees — unless contraindicated
  • Keep head midline — avoid turning to either side
  • Avoid neck flexion
  • Avoid hip flexion if possible
  • Reduce environmental stimulation
  • Prevent coughing and straining when possible
  • Administer medications as ordered (mannitol, hypertonic saline)

6. Seizure Precautions

Seizure Safety Setup
  • Padded side rails
  • Suction and oxygen available at bedside
  • Bed in lowest position
  • Protect from injury during seizure — do not restrain

7. Monitor Temperature

Fever increases metabolic demand and may worsen brain injury. Monitor and treat promptly.

8. Fluid & Electrolyte Monitoring

SIADH

Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH — too much antidiuretic hormone → water retention → dilutional hyponatremia (low sodium)

Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

Lack of ADH effect → excessive urination and dehydration — opposite of SIADH

Monitor: serum sodium · intake and output · hydration status

💊 Medications Used in TBI

MedicationPurposeKey Nursing Point
Mannitol (Osmotic Diuretic) Draws fluid out of brain tissue — reduces ICP and swelling Monitor fluid balance, kidney function, blood pressure, serum osmolality
Hypertonic Saline High-concentration salt solution — pulls fluid from swollen brain tissue Monitor sodium levels and neurologic response
Anticonvulsants Prevent or treat seizures May be given prophylactically in severe TBI
Analgesics Pain relief Use carefully — can mask neuro changes
Sedatives Calm patient or decrease agitation Used in critical care settings; monitor neurologic response

📚 Concussion-Specific Teaching

Recovery Instructions
  • Cognitive rest — reduce intense studying, screen use, and concentration tasks
  • Physical rest — avoid strenuous activity
  • Avoid alcohol and sedatives unless prescribed
  • Avoid driving until cleared if symptoms are significant
  • Have a responsible adult observe the patient
Return to Activity

Should be gradual and only after medical clearance.

Return to sports or high-risk activity before full recovery can be extremely dangerous.

🚨
Return for Care Immediately If

Repeated vomiting · worsening headache · increasing confusion · slurred speech · weakness · seizures · unequal pupils · unusual drowsiness · inability to wake up · clear fluid from nose or ears

Second Impact Syndrome

⚠️
Second Impact Syndrome

Rapid and often fatal brain swelling that can occur if a second concussion happens before recovery from the first. Rare but very serious. A patient with concussion must NOT return to sports or high-risk activity until fully cleared.

🛏️ General Care in Severe TBI

Ongoing Care
  • Maintain adequate nutrition
  • Protect skin — turn regularly
  • Perform range-of-motion exercises
  • Prevent aspiration
  • Monitor bowel and bladder function
If Swallow Is Impaired
  • Assess swallow before any oral intake
  • Keep NPO (nothing by mouth) until safe
  • Tube (enteral) feeding may be needed

📋 Nursing Diagnoses

Common Nursing Diagnoses for TBI
  • Ineffective cerebral tissue perfusion — decreased ability to maintain adequate blood flow to the brain
  • Risk for aspiration — risk of inhaling food, fluid, or secretions
  • Risk for injury — from seizures, falls, or confusion
  • Acute confusion — sudden change in awareness and cognition
  • Impaired physical mobility — difficulty moving independently
  • Impaired verbal communication — difficulty expressing or understanding language
  • Risk for ineffective airway clearance — risk of inability to clear secretions and maintain an open airway

⚖️ High-Yield Comparison: Epidural vs Subdural Hematoma

Epidural Hematoma
  • Usually arterial bleeding — rapid
  • Often associated with skull fracture
  • Classic: brief LOC → lucid interval → rapid decline
  • Emergency surgery may be needed quickly
Subdural Hematoma
  • Usually venous bleeding — may be slower
  • Common in older adults
  • Symptoms may be delayed (especially chronic type)
  • Can be acute, subacute, or chronic

📈 Prognosis

Prognosis depends on:

Severity of Injury Speed of Treatment Bleeding or Swelling Age Presence of Hypoxia or Hypotension Complications
  • Concussions often improve with time, but some patients develop prolonged symptoms
  • Post-concussion syndrome — persistent symptoms such as headache, dizziness, concentration problems, and irritability may occur after recovery
