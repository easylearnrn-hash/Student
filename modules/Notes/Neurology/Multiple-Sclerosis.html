Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
Demyelination · Exacerbations · CNS-Only Disease

NCLEX-focused study note — types · symptoms · medications · nursing priorities · Uhthoff's phenomenon

What Is MS?

Multiple Sclerosis is a chronic, progressive, autoimmune disorder affecting the CNS only (brain and spinal cord). The immune system attacks the myelin sheath → slows or blocks nerve impulses → plaques/scars form on nerves → permanent damage over time.

Pathophysiology Key Points
  • CNS only — peripheral nervous system spared
  • Immune cells attack myelin → demyelination
  • Leads to plaques (scars) on nerves
  • Results in variable, relapsing symptoms
Relapse & Remission Pattern
  • Exacerbation = flare-up of symptoms
  • Remission = symptoms improve or disappear
  • Symptoms can vary widely between patients
  • Triggered by heat, stress, illness, infection

Types of MS

Type 1 — Most Common
Relapsing-Remitting (RRMS)
Symptoms come and go; clear relapses followed by remissions. Most patients start here.
Type 2
Primary Progressive
Steady worsening from onset, no clear relapses. No remission periods.
Type 3
Secondary Progressive
Begins as RRMS, then transitions to steady decline. Most common progression.
Type 4 — Rare
Progressive-Relapsing
Steady worsening from onset with acute flare-ups superimposed.

Signs & Symptoms

Motor & Sensory
  • Fatigue — #1 most common & debilitating symptom
  • Muscle weakness or spasticity
  • Numbness/tingling (paresthesia)
  • Ataxia — unsteady, uncoordinated gait
Cranial Nerve / Other
  • Vision changes — blurred, diplopia (double vision)
  • Optic neuritis — inflammation of optic nerve
  • Dysarthria (slurred speech)
  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) → aspiration risk
  • Bladder/bowel dysfunction
  • Cognitive changes (memory, concentration)
🌡️
Uhthoff's Phenomenon — Heat Sensitivity

Symptoms worsen with increased body temperature (heat, hot showers, fever, exercise). Teach patients to use cool environments, air conditioning, and cooling vests. This is a major NCLEX teaching point.

Diagnostics

TestFindings in MS
MRIMost Useful Shows brain and spinal cord lesions (plaques) — white matter changes
Lumbar Puncture (CSF)↑ Oligoclonal bands (IgG) — indicates CNS inflammation; not diagnostic alone
Evoked PotentialsTests slowed nerve conduction; helps detect subclinical lesions

Medications

Disease-Modifying Drugs (Slow Progression)

DrugMechanism / Notes
Interferon beta-1a / 1bReduces frequency of relapses; most commonly used first-line
Glatiramer acetate (Copaxone)Mimics myelin — diverts immune attack away from myelin
NatalizumabPrevents immune cells from crossing the blood-brain barrier
FingolimodTraps immune cells in lymph nodes; oral medication

Symptom Management

DrugTarget Symptom
Methylprednisolone / PrednisoneCorticosteroids — reduce inflammation during acute flares; shorten relapse duration
Baclofen, TizanidineMuscle spasticity (stiffness)
OxybutyninBladder urgency / overactive bladder
BethanecholUrinary retention — stimulates bladder contraction
Amantadine, ModafinilFatigue management
Gabapentin, PregabalinNeuropathic pain, paresthesias

Nursing Priorities

Safety & Mobility
  • Fall prevention — assistive devices, remove hazards
  • Encourage PT and ROM exercises
  • Allow extra time for activities
Energy & Environment
  • Energy conservation — rest periods, avoid overexertion
  • Cool environment — AC, cool showers
  • Avoid stress, illness, overheating
Swallowing & Bowel/Bladder
  • Monitor swallowing — dysphagia → aspiration risk
  • Timed voiding, bladder training
  • Bowel program; high fiber, fluid intake
  • Eye patch for diplopia
