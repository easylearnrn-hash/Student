What Are Seizures?

Seizures are caused by abnormal, excessive electrical firing of neurons in the brain — a disruption between excitatory (glutamate) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmitters.

Excitatory Imbalance

Glutamate — stimulates brain activity

When glutamate overactivates → uncontrolled firing → seizure

Inhibitory Deficit

GABA — calms brain activity

When GABA is insufficient → can't stop neuronal firing

Classification of Seizures

Generalized Seizures — Both Brain Hemispheres

Tonic-Clonic (Grand Mal) — Most Severe
  • Aura may precede it
  • Tonic phase: body stiffens, apnea, cyanosis, tongue biting
  • Clonic phase: jerking movements, possible incontinence
  • Postictal phase: confusion, fatigue, soreness
  • High risk for Status Epilepticus
Absence (Petit Mal) — Common in Children
  • Sudden staring episodes
  • Very brief (seconds) — immediate postictal recovery
  • May go unnoticed; child appears to "blank out"
  • No aura, no confusion after
Atonic (Drop Attacks)
  • Sudden loss of muscle tone → falls
  • High injury risk — often require helmet
  • Immediate recovery after

Focal (Partial) Seizures — One Brain Area

Focal Onset Aware (Simple Partial)
  • Patient remains aware during seizure
  • Symptoms vary by location (occipital = visual changes)
  • Often serves as the aura before a bigger seizure
Focal Impaired Awareness (Complex Partial)
  • Patient is unaware during seizure
  • Automatisms — lip-smacking, repetitive hand movements
  • Most common in temporal lobe

⏳ Stages of a Seizure

Phase 1
Prodromal
Days before — depression, anxiety, GI changes
Phase 2
Aura
Seconds–minutes before — smells, déjà vu, dizziness
Phase 3
Ictus
The seizure itself — TIME IT! >5 min = emergency
Phase 4
Postictal
Recovery — fatigue, confusion, headache, amnesia
🚨
Status Epilepticus — Emergency

Seizure lasting > 5 minutes, or repeated seizures without regaining consciousness. Activate emergency response immediately. First-line treatment: benzodiazepines (lorazepam IV, diazepam). Monitor airway — risk of hypoxia and brain damage.

Nursing Interventions

Before (Seizure Precautions)
  • Pad side rails
  • Suction + O₂ at bedside
  • IV access secured
  • Bed in lowest position
  • Remove restrictive clothing
During — Safety First
  • DO NOT restrain the patient
  • DO NOT insert anything in mouth
  • Turn to lateral/side position (protect airway)
  • Pillow under head
  • Time the seizure!
  • Remove glasses, hazardous items
After (Postictal Care)
  • Assess airway, vitals, neuro status
  • Allow rest and reorientation
  • Document: duration, movements, LOC
  • Notify provider
  • Prepare for EEG, labs, meds
