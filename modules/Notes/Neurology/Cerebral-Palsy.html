Cerebral Palsy (CP)
Non-Progressive Motor Disorder · Brain Development

NCLEX-focused study note — types · causes · early signs · management · nursing priorities

Definition

Cerebral Palsy (CP) is a group of permanent, non-progressive motor disorders caused by abnormal brain development or damage to the developing brain — typically occurring before, during, or shortly after birth.

Permanent
The brain lesion itself does not heal or progress — it is static (fixed). However, the presentation of symptoms may change as the child grows and the nervous system matures.
Non-Progressive
CP does not get worse over time. If a patient's motor function is rapidly declining, consider a different diagnosis — CP alone should not cause neurological deterioration.
⭐
NCLEX Critical Fact

CP is non-progressive. NCLEX answers that suggest progression or worsening of the underlying brain injury are incorrect. Choose answers focused on supportive management and early intervention.

Pathophysiology

Injury or abnormal development in the cerebrum (largest part of the brain) disrupts voluntary motor control pathways. The result is a spectrum of motor impairment — affecting movement, posture, muscle tone, and coordination — often with associated sensory, cognitive, communication, or behavioral deficits.

Core Triad of CP
1. Motor Impairment — problems with voluntary movement, strength, coordination

2. Postural Abnormalities — abnormal tone (hypertonia or hypotonia), abnormal resting postures

3. Associated Deficits (vary by severity) — cognitive, sensory, speech, epilepsy, behavioral

Causes & Risk Factors

  • P
    Prenatal — Before Birth
    • Maternal infections: rubella, cytomegalovirus (CMV), toxoplasmosis
    • Exposure to toxins, alcohol, radiation
    • Placental insufficiency — ↓ oxygen/nutrients to fetus
    • Genetic or structural brain abnormalities
  • D
    Perinatal — During Birth
    • Birth asphyxia — oxygen deprivation during delivery (most common cause)
    • Prematurity — brain still developing; fragile vasculature (risk of intraventricular hemorrhage)
    • Low birth weight
    • Traumatic delivery — forceps, vacuum, prolonged labor
  • Po
    Postnatal — After Birth
    • Head trauma — shaken baby syndrome, falls
    • Infections: meningitis, encephalitis
    • Kernicterus — brain damage from severe neonatal jaundice (bilirubin toxicity)
    • Stroke or hypoxic event in neonatal period

Types of Cerebral Palsy

Spastic CP — Most Common (70–80%)
  • Hypertonia — increased, stiff muscle tone
  • Jerky, scissor-like movements
  • Hemiplegia — one side of body
  • Diplegia — both legs (most common in preterm infants)
  • Quadriplegia — all four limbs + often severe cognitive involvement
  • Hyperreflexia, positive Babinski, clonus may be present
Dyskinetic / Athetoid CP
  • Involuntary, writhing or twisting movements
  • Affects face, arms, and trunk most
  • Movement worsens with stress; improves during sleep
  • Often associated with kernicterus
  • Speech and swallowing frequently impaired
Ataxic CP
  • Affects balance and coordination
  • Wide-based gait (feet spread far apart)
  • Poor depth perception, tremor
  • Least common type
  • Safety is priority — high fall risk
Mixed CP
  • Combination of two or more types
  • Most commonly spastic + dyskinetic
  • Presentation is complex — address dominant features first

Diagnosis

There is no single diagnostic test for CP. Diagnosis is clinical + imaging-based:

Clinical
  • Developmental screening tools (ASQ, BSID)
  • Neurological examination — tone, reflexes, posture, movement
  • Observation of milestones over time
Imaging & Labs
  • MRI — best for identifying brain lesions, white matter injury
  • CT scan — if MRI unavailable or acute setting
  • EEG — if seizures are present or suspected
  • Blood tests — rule out metabolic or genetic causes

Multidisciplinary Management

Therapies
  • Physical therapy (PT) — strengthen muscles, prevent contractures, improve gait
  • Occupational therapy (OT) — ADL independence, adaptive equipment
  • Speech-language pathology (SLP) — communication and swallowing
  • Special education services
Medications
  • Baclofen — oral or intrathecal; muscle relaxant; ↓ spasticity
  • Diazepam — short-term spasticity/spasm relief
  • Tizanidine — alpha-2 agonist; reduces spasticity
  • Botulinum toxin (Botox) — local injections for focal spasticity
  • Antiepileptics — phenytoin, levetiracetam for seizures
Surgical
  • Selective dorsal rhizotomy — cuts sensory nerve roots; permanently reduces spasticity
  • Orthopedic surgery — tendon lengthening/release, joint correction
  • Intrathecal baclofen pump — continuous spinal delivery for severe spasticity

Nursing Priorities

  • 🍽️ Nutrition & feeding safety — swallowing dysfunction is common; position upright, small bites, involve SLP, monitor for aspiration pneumonia
  • 🩺 Seizure precautions — seizures occur in ~35% of CP patients; padded rails, suction at bedside, rescue meds available
  • 🏃 Mobility & contracture prevention — passive ROM exercises, splints, braces; reposition every 2 hours
  • 🛡️ Skin integrity — immobility increases pressure ulcer risk; frequent repositioning, pressure-relieving surfaces
  • 🗣️ Communication support — augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices if speech impaired; never assume cognitive deficit from motor impairment
  • 🧸 Family support & education — guide parents on therapy adherence, realistic expectations, community resources, caregiver respite
  • ⚠️ Fall safety — especially ataxic CP; bed/chair alarms, assistive devices, safe environment
🚨
Aspiration Risk — Top Priority

Dysphagia and poor oral motor control in CP = leading cause of aspiration pneumonia and malnutrition. Elevate HOB ≥ 30°–45° during/after meals, provide thickened liquids if ordered, and perform swallowing assessment before initiating PO feeding.
