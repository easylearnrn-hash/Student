Clonus
Involuntary Rhythmic Contractions · UMN Sign

NCLEX-focused study note — upper motor neuron lesions · hyperreflexia · pre-eclampsia · spasticity

What Is Clonus?

Clonus is a series of involuntary, rhythmic, repetitive muscle contractions and relaxations triggered by sudden, sustained stretching of a muscle — most commonly elicited at the ankle by rapid dorsiflexion of the foot.

Clonus is a sign of upper motor neuron (UMN) dysfunction. Healthy nervous systems suppress reflex arcs — when the UMN pathway is damaged, this inhibition is lost, producing exaggerated, sustained reflexes.

Normal
0–1 clonic beats — reflexes intact and inhibited.
No pathology.
Abnormal
≥ 3 beats or sustained clonus — UMN inhibition is lost.
CNS pathology confirmed.

Key Terminology

TermDefinition
ClonusInvoluntary rhythmic muscle oscillations from sudden, maintained stretch
HyperreflexiaExaggerated deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) — loss of UMN inhibition
Upper Motor Neurons (UMN)Neurons in the brain and spinal cord that send inhibitory signals down to lower motor neurons; damage → hyperreflexia, spasticity, clonus
Lower Motor Neurons (LMN)Neurons exiting the spinal cord to muscles; damage → flaccidity, atrophy, hyporeflexia
SpasticityIncreased muscle tone (hypertonia) causing stiffness; also a UMN sign
DorsiflexionMoving the top of the foot toward the shin (upward)
Plantar flexionPointing the foot downward (like pressing a gas pedal)
Sustained clonusContractions that continue as long as stretch is maintained — always pathologic
Unsustained clonusA few beats that self-terminate — may be normal in some contexts

UMN vs LMN Lesion — Critical Differentiator

Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) Lesion
  • Hyperreflexia — exaggerated DTRs
  • Spasticity — increased tone
  • Clonus — present
  • Babinski sign — positive (great toe extends upward)
  • No muscle atrophy early
  • Examples: stroke, MS, spinal cord injury, CP
Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) Lesion
  • Hyporeflexia or absent DTRs
  • Flaccidity — decreased/no tone
  • No clonus
  • Fasciculations — visible muscle twitches
  • Muscle atrophy
  • Examples: polio, Guillain-Barré, peripheral neuropathy
💡
NCLEX Rule

Clonus = UMN lesion — always. If you see clonus or hyperreflexia on NCLEX, think brain or spinal cord involvement, NOT peripheral nerve damage.

How to Elicit Ankle Clonus

  1. Position the patient supine or sitting. Support the knee in slight flexion and keep the leg relaxed.
  2. Grasp the foot firmly around the sole and distal leg.
  3. Sharply dorsiflex the foot — push the top of the foot toward the shin — quickly, then maintain the stretched position.
  4. Observe for rhythmic oscillations — the foot bouncing back and forth in repetitive flexion/extension cycles.
  5. Count the beats — document the number; sustained clonus has no endpoint until stretch is released.

Normal or borderline — no pathology
2
Equivocal — may warrant further evaluation

Sustained

Causes of Clonus High-Yield

ConditionMechanismKey NCLEX Point
Stroke Brain damage → loss of descending inhibitory UMN control Contralateral hemiplegia + hyperreflexia on affected side
Multiple Sclerosis (MS) Demyelination of UMN pathways disrupts motor inhibition Relapsing-remitting pattern; spasticity + clonus during flares
Cerebral Palsy (CP) Early brain injury → abnormal UMN development Spastic CP has hyperreflexia; clonus present in severe cases
Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) Disruption of UMN descending tracts below injury level Spastic paralysis below injury level after spinal shock resolves
Pre-eclampsia / Eclampsia CNS hyperexcitability from cerebral vasospasm and edema NCLEX Priority Clonus = impending seizure → Mag sulfate NOW
Hepatic Encephalopathy Ammonia toxicity to CNS → hyperreflexia Also look for asterixis (flapping tremor), confusion, jaundice

Pre-Eclampsia & Clonus — NCLEX Priority Scenario

Clonus in a pregnant patient with elevated blood pressure is one of the most tested NCLEX neurological emergencies.

🚨
Clonus in Pre-Eclampsia = Impending Eclampsia (Seizure)

Findings: BP ≥ 140/90, proteinuria, headache, visual changes, hyperreflexia, clonus
Priority action: Administer magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) — reduces CNS excitability and prevents eclamptic seizures
Monitor: Respiratory rate ≥ 12, urinary output ≥ 30 mL/hr, DTRs present (MgSO₄ toxicity causes loss of DTRs first)
Antidote if MgSO₄ toxicity: Calcium gluconate — keep at bedside always

💡
MgSO₄ Toxicity Signs — In Order of Appearance

Loss of DTRs (patellar reflex) → respiratory depression (RR < 12) → respiratory arrest
Always check patellar reflex before each MgSO₄ dose. If absent → hold dose → notify provider.

Nursing Assessment of Clonus

  • 🦵 Elicit and count beats — document number of clonic beats and duration
  • 🧠 Assess other UMN signs — Babinski sign, hyperreflexia, spasticity, hemi/paraplegia
  • 👁️ Assess LOC and orientation — clonus with altered LOC = high-priority emergency
  • 📊 Vital signs — blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate; any sudden changes?
  • 🤰 If pregnant — check BP, protein dipstick, deep tendon reflexes, headache, vision changes
  • 📋 Compare to baseline — is clonus new or previously documented?

Management Priorities

Treat Underlying Cause
  • Stroke → thrombolytics / intervention
  • MS flare → corticosteroids
  • SCI → stabilize, prevent secondary injury
  • Hepatic encephalopathy → lactulose, protein restriction
Pre-Eclampsia Protocol
  • Magnesium sulfate — seizure prophylaxis
  • Antihypertensives (labetalol, hydralazine)
  • Continuous maternal/fetal monitoring
  • Calcium gluconate at bedside
Chronic Spasticity / Clonus
  • Baclofen — GABA-B agonist; reduces spasticity
  • Diazepam — muscle relaxant (short-term)
  • Tizanidine — alpha-2 agonist, antispastic
  • Botulinum toxin (Botox) injections
  • Physical therapy — stretching, splinting
