What Are Cranial Nerves?

The brain controls the body primarily through the spinal cord. However, 12 special nerve pairs bypass the spinal cord entirely — these are the cranial nerves (CNs). They emerge directly from the brainstem and brain to serve the head, neck, and many internal organs.

Three Types
Sensory Carry information to the brain (smell, vision, hearing, taste, balance)

Motor Carry commands from the brain (eye movement, facial expression, tongue, swallowing)

Mixed Both sensory and motor functions (face sensation & chewing, swallowing & taste, etc.)

Complete Cranial Nerve Reference

# Name Type Function NCLEX Key Point
I Olfactory Sensory Smell Loss = anosmia; seen in head trauma, COVID-19
II Optic Sensory Vision — retina to occipital lobe Papilledema (↑ ICP) — seen on fundoscopy; visual field defects
III Oculomotor Motor Most eye movement · pupil constriction · eyelid elevation (ptosis) Damage → ptosis, fixed dilated pupil, eye deviates down/out
IV Trochlear Motor Superior oblique muscle — downward/inward eye rotation Damage → diplopia on downward gaze (stairs); head tilt to compensate
V Trigeminal Mixed Sensory: face, teeth, sinuses (3 branches: V1 ophthalmic, V2 maxillary, V3 mandibular) · Motor: chewing muscles (masseter, temporalis) Trigeminal neuralgia — severe stabbing facial pain · assess corneal reflex (V1)
VI Abducens Motor Lateral rectus muscle — eye abduction (outward gaze) Damage → eye cannot abduct; lateral gaze palsy; diplopia
VII Facial Mixed Motor: facial expression · Sensory: taste anterior 2/3 tongue · Parasympathetic: lacrimal and salivary glands Bell's palsy — unilateral facial droop; stroke — also facial droop (central vs peripheral distinction)
VIII Vestibulocochlear Sensory Cochlear branch: hearing · Vestibular branch: balance and equilibrium Damage → sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo, nystagmus
IX Glossopharyngeal Mixed Sensory: taste posterior 1/3 tongue · Motor: swallowing, saliva (parotid gland) · Parasympathetic Damage → loss of gag reflex (IX + X together); dysphagia
X Vagus Mixed Parasympathetic: heart, lungs, GI tract · Motor: pharynx/larynx (voice, swallowing) · Sensory: pharynx Most widespread CN — damage → hoarseness, dysphagia, bradycardia, vasovagal syncope
XI Accessory (Spinal) Motor Sternocleidomastoid (SCM) — head rotation · Trapezius — shoulder elevation Damage → cannot shrug shoulder or turn head; often injured by neck surgery/lymph node biopsy
XII Hypoglossal Motor Tongue movement — protrusion, side-to-side, speech articulation Damage → tongue deviates toward the side of the lesion; dysarthria

💡
Clinical Significance

Knowing the origin helps localize brainstem lesions. Multiple CN deficits from the same brainstem region (e.g., pons) = brainstem stroke until proven otherwise.

Cranial Nerve Nursing Assessment

  • CN I: Ask patient to identify a familiar scent (coffee, soap) with each nostril separately
  • CN II: Assess visual acuity (Snellen chart), visual fields by confrontation, pupillary light reflex
  • CN III, IV, VI: Assess extra-ocular movements (EOM) — "H" pattern tracking; check for ptosis and nystagmus
  • CN V: Light touch on face (3 branches); corneal reflex; assess jaw strength (bite down)
  • CN VII: Ask to smile, raise eyebrows, puff cheeks, close eyes tightly; observe symmetry
  • CN VIII: Whisper test, Rinne/Weber tests, assess balance (Romberg)
  • CN IX & X: Assess gag reflex, voice quality (hoarseness), swallowing — aspiration precautions if absent gag
  • CN XI: Resist head turn and shoulder shrug against examiner's hands
  • CN XII: Ask patient to protrude tongue and move side to side; assess speech clarity
🚨
Priority: Absent Gag Reflex (CN IX & X)

No gag reflex = high aspiration risk. Institute NPO status, elevate HOB, request speech-language pathology (SLP) swallowing evaluation, and notify provider immediately. Aspiration pneumonia is a leading cause of death in neurological patients.
