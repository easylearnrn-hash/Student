Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)
Syndrome Types · Motor & Sensory Deficits

NCLEX-focused study note — 6 syndrome types · deficit patterns · autonomic dysreflexia · nursing priorities

Overview

Spinal cord injuries (SCI) cause varying degrees of motor, sensory, and autonomic dysfunction depending on the level and pattern of cord damage. Rather than all-or-nothing injury, partial cord injuries produce distinct syndrome patterns — highly tested on NCLEX.

Complete vs Incomplete Injury

Complete: Total loss of motor and sensory function below the lesion

Incomplete: Partial preservation of motor or sensory function — produces the classic syndromes below

Diagnosis
  • MRI — best for soft tissue and cord damage
  • CT — bony injury, vertebral fractures
  • Neurological assessment: motor, sensory, reflexes

The 6 SCI Syndrome Types

1 · Central Cord Syndrome — Most Common Incomplete
  • Injury to the center of the cervical cord
  • Cause: hyperextension in older adults with degenerative changes
  • Arms weaker than legs (upper > lower extremity deficit)
  • Sensory loss is variable
  • Memory tip: "walking but not feeding"
2 · Anterior Cord Syndrome
  • Damage to front (anterior) portion of the cord
  • Cause: flexion injury or anterior spinal artery occlusion
  • Motor lost (paralysis below injury)
  • Pain and temperature lost
  • Touch, vibration, proprioception: PRESERVED
  • Mnemonic: "No motor, no pain/temp — touch OK"
3 · Brown-Séquard Syndrome
  • Hemisection — one side of the cord damaged
  • Cause: penetrating trauma (gunshot, stab wound)
  • Same side (ipsilateral): motor loss, proprioception loss, vibration loss
  • Opposite side (contralateral): pain & temperature loss
  • Mnemonic: "MVP same side; pain/temp opposite"
4 · Posterior Cord Syndrome — Rarest
  • Damage to the back (posterior) cord
  • Motor function PRESERVED
  • Proprioception, vibration, fine touch: LOST
  • Rare syndrome
5 · Conus Medullaris Syndrome
  • Injury at the tapered end of spinal cord (L1–L2)
  • Sudden onset, symmetric lower limb weakness
  • Areflexia of bladder and bowel → incontinence
  • Saddle anesthesia (loss of feeling in buttocks, inner thighs, perineum)
6 · Cauda Equina Syndrome EMERGENCY
  • Compression of nerve roots below spinal cord (L2 and lower)
  • Cause: herniated disc, tumor, trauma
  • Asymmetric leg weakness
  • Loss of bowel/bladder control
  • Saddle anesthesia
  • Requires immediate surgery — permanent damage if delayed
🚨
Cauda Equina Syndrome = Surgical Emergency

If a patient presents with sudden saddle anesthesia + bowel/bladder dysfunction + asymmetric leg weakness, recognize this as Cauda Equina and anticipate immediate surgical intervention. This is the highest-priority SCI syndrome on NCLEX.

Autonomic Dysreflexia — T6 and Above

🚨
Autonomic Dysreflexia — Hypertensive Emergency

Triggers: Full bladder (most common), tight clothing, constipation, pressure ulcer, painful stimulus below injury level.
Symptoms: Sudden severe headache, hypertension, bradycardia, flushing/sweating above injury, pale/cold below.
Action: Sit patient upright immediately (lowers BP), find and remove the trigger, check Foley catheter first, administer antihypertensives if ordered.

Nursing Priorities

  1. Spine stabilization — cervical collar, backboard; log-roll technique for repositioning
  2. Airway and respiratory assessment — cervical injuries (C3–C5) can paralyze the diaphragm (phrenic nerve); prepare for ventilator support
  3. Neurogenic shock — hypotension + bradycardia + warm skin (sympathetic pathway disrupted); differentiate from hypovolemic shock
  4. Bladder management — intermittent catheterization; prevent overdistension (autonomic dysreflexia trigger)
  5. DVT and pressure ulcer prevention — SCDs, repositioning q2h, special mattresses
  6. Bowel program — high fiber, adequate fluids, scheduled toileting
  7. Emotional support — grief reaction; body image changes; involve rehabilitation team early
