What Is a Cerebral Aneurysm?

A cerebral aneurysm is a localized ballooning or dilation of a weakened arterial wall in the brain. If it ruptures, it causes a subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) — a life-threatening neurological emergency.

Unruptured (May Be Silent)
  • Headaches
  • Vision problems, eye pain
  • Cranial nerve deficits
  • Often found incidentally on imaging
Ruptured Aneurysm — SAH
  • "Worst headache of my life" — sudden onset
  • Sudden loss of consciousness
  • Nausea, vomiting
  • Photophobia (light sensitivity)
  • Nuchal rigidity (stiff neck)
  • Hemiparesis, confusion, altered LOC
🚨
NCLEX Classic Presentation

Sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache + stiff neck (nuchal rigidity) + photophobia = suspect ruptured aneurysm → SAH. This is a neurological emergency — act immediately.

Causes & Risk Factors

Structural / Congenital
  • Congenital arterial wall defects (present at birth)
  • Atherosclerosis (fatty plaques weaken walls)
  • Genetic disorders (e.g., polycystic kidney disease)
  • Head trauma
Modifiable Risk Factors
  • Hypertension — #1 modifiable risk
  • Smoking
  • Age > 40
  • Female sex (slightly higher risk)

Diagnostics

TestPurpose / NCLEX Points
CT Scan (no contrast)1st test — detects blood in subarachnoid space after rupture; fast and widely available
Lumbar PunctureIf CT is negative but suspicion is high → checks for xanthochromia (yellow-tinged CSF = old blood). Order AFTER CT.
MRI / MRADetects aneurysm size, shape, and location; better for unruptured aneurysms
Cerebral AngiographyGold standard — detailed vessel imaging; confirms location before surgery

Treatment

Emergency — If Ruptured
  • Stabilize ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation)
  • Control ICP (HOB 30°, quiet environment)
  • Prevent rebleeding
  • Nimodipine — prevents vasospasm
  • Anticonvulsants — prevent post-rupture seizures
Surgical Interventions
  • Clipping — surgical clip placed on neck of aneurysm to stop blood flow into the sac
  • Endovascular coiling — coils inserted into aneurysm to promote clotting; less invasive
If Unruptured
  • Monitor size and growth with periodic imaging
  • BP control (antihypertensives)
  • Smoking cessation
  • Surgical clipping or coiling in high-risk cases

Nursing Priorities

Prevent ↑ ICP
  • HOB 30°, head midline
  • Quiet, low-stimulation environment
  • Avoid coughing, straining, neck flexion
  • Monitor neuro status & LOC closely
Prevent Rebleeding
  • Administer nimodipine on schedule
  • Control BP — not too high, not too low
  • Stool softeners (prevent Valsalva/straining)
  • Limit activity; strict bed rest early
Monitor Complications
  • Vasospasm — can cause ischemic stroke
  • Hydrocephalus — blood blocks CSF flow
  • Seizures — seizure precautions
  • SIADH — monitor Na⁺, I&O, urine output

NCLEX Priorities Summary

① Sudden severe headache + nuchal rigidity = suspect SAH → emergency
② Nimodipine = vasospasm prevention — must know drug and timing
③ Monitor for SIADH: ↓ Na⁺, concentrated urine, weight gain, confusion
④ Never LP first — always CT scan before lumbar puncture in suspected SAH
