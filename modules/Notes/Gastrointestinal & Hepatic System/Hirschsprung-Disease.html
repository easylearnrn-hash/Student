Hirschsprung Disease

Congenital Aganglionic Megacolon

1. Definition

  • Congenital absence of ganglion cells (nerve cells) in part of the colon
  • Most commonly affects the sigmoid colon
  • Can extend proximally — severity depends on length of aganglionic segment

2. Pathophysiology

No ganglion cells → No nerve stimulation → No peristalsis → Stool cannot pass → Obstruction + bowel dilation
💡 The aganglionic segment stays contracted — it cannot relax to move stool through

3. Symptoms

Newborn Signs

  • 🚩 Failure to pass meconium within 48 hours after birth — KEY sign!
  • 🟢 Bilious (green) vomiting
  • 🫃 Distended abdomen
  • Poor feeding / feeding difficulty

Later Signs

  • 💩 Ribbon-like stools (partial obstruction)
  • 🫃 Megacolon — severe bowel enlargement
  • Chronic constipation
  • Failure to thrive
🚨 Failure to pass meconium in 48 hrs = red flag → think Hirschsprung's or cystic fibrosis!

4. Most Deadly Complication

Hirschsprung-Associated Enterocolitis (HAEC)

  • Bowel infection leading to sepsis
  • Can cause sudden septic shock
  • Signs: fever, explosive foul-smelling diarrhea, worsening abdominal distension, high WBC (leukocytosis)
🚨 Enterocolitis = most deadly complication — monitor closely and intervene immediately!

5. Diagnosis

TestFindings
Rectal Suction BiopsyGold standard — absence of ganglion cells confirmed
Barium Enema X-rayShows transition zone (narrow aganglionic → dilated normal colon)
Anorectal ManometryAbsence of internal anal sphincter relaxation reflex
Abdominal X-rayDistended bowel loops, air-fluid levels

6. Treatment

✅ Surgical removal of the aganglionic bowel segment — the only definitive cure

Surgical Approach

  • Pull-through procedure — removes aganglionic segment and connects normal bowel to anus
  • May require temporary colostomy in severe cases

7. Nursing Priorities

  • Early diagnosis is critical — delays can lead to fatal enterocolitis
  • Monitor for sepsis signs: fever, leukocytosis, worsening abdominal distension
  • Monitor for enterocolitis: explosive diarrhea, fever, rapid deterioration
  • Post-op: monitor for infection, manage pain, watch for return of bowel function
  • Family education — signs of complications to watch for at home

8. NCLEX Must-Know Summary

✅ No meconium in 48 hrs = red flag (think Hirschsprung's)
✅ Bilious vomiting = serious obstruction (not normal spit-up)
✅ Gold standard diagnosis = rectal biopsy (no ganglion cells)
✅ Treatment = surgical removal of aganglionic segment
⚠️ Enterocolitis = most deadly complication
⚠️ Signs of enterocolitis: fever + explosive diarrhea + distension
⚠️ Delayed meconium: also think cystic fibrosis
