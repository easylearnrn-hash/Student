Portal Vein & Liver Circulation

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · note_gi_19

Overview

The liver has a dual blood supply — unique among all organs. Blood enters via two routes and exits via one.

Hepatic Artery

  • Oxygen-rich, nutrient-poor blood
  • Flow: 300–500 mL/min
  • Branch of the celiac artery
  • ~10–20% of liver blood supply (at rest)

Portal Vein

  • Nutrient-rich, oxygen-poor blood
  • Flow: 1000–1300 mL/min
  • Drains GI organs, pancreas, spleen
  • ~80–90% of liver blood supply (postprandial)
After a meal: ~90% of liver blood comes from the portal vein.

Blood Flow Pathway

  1. Heart pumps ~5 L/min via the aorta
  2. Abdominal aorta branches into:
    • Celiac artery (700 mL/min) → hepatic artery, spleen, stomach
    • Superior mesenteric artery (700 mL/min) → pancreas, small intestines, part of colon
    • Inferior mesenteric artery (400 mL/min) → part of colon
  3. GI organs drain deoxygenated, nutrient-rich venous blood → converges into portal vein
  4. Portal vein + hepatic artery blood enters liver sinusoids → filters bacteria, stores proteins & carbohydrates (non-fat nutrients)
  5. Blood exits via hepatic veins → inferior vena cava → back to heart

The portal system is normally a high-volume, low-pressure system.

Portal Hypertension

Cause: Liver damage (alcoholism, hepatitis, cirrhosis) → fibrotic sinusoids → increased resistance → portal pressure rises to 15–20 mmHg

⚡ NCLEX Priorities

  • Portal vein = nutrient-rich, oxygen-poor | Hepatic artery = oxygen-rich, nutrient-poor
  • Normal portal system = high volume, low pressure
  • Portal HTN → varices, ascites, splenomegaly, encephalopathy
  • Variceal bleed → airway first, then IV access + octreotide + emergent endoscopy
  • Hepatic encephalopathy → lactulose (lowers ammonia), low-protein diet
  • Sinusoids filter bacteria and store non-fat nutrients (proteins, carbs)
