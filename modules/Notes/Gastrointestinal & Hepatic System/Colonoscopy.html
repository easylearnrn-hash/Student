Colonoscopy

Gastrointestinal & Hepatic System · note_gi_17

What is a Colonoscopy?

A diagnostic and therapeutic procedure using a long, flexible fiber-optic scope (colonoscope) inserted via the rectum to examine the inner lining of the colon (large intestine).

Allows: direct visualization, biopsy, and polyp removal.

📅 Routine colorectal cancer screening: starting at age 45, every 10 years for average risk.

Indications

  • Colorectal cancer screening (age ≥ 45)
  • Unexplained GI bleeding
  • Persistent changes in bowel habits
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis, Crohn's)
  • Polyp removal
  • Biopsy for suspicious lesions

Pre-Procedure Nursing Care

  1. Bowel Preparation: Clear liquid diet for 24 hrs; NPO after midnight; bowel cleansing with laxatives (e.g., polyethylene glycol — GoLYTELY, MiraLAX)
  2. Education: Inform patient about possible cramping/gas post-procedure; stress importance of completing bowel prep for clear visualization; stay near a toilet after starting prep
  3. Consent: Ensure informed consent signed (nurse witnesses, does NOT explain procedure)
  4. Labs: Review renal function (BUN, creatinine) if using phosphate- or magnesium-based preps
Continue prep until stool is clear or yellow liquid — confirms adequate prep.

Bowel Prep Medications

Medication Mechanism Nursing Considerations
Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)
GoLYTELY, MiraLAX		 Osmotic laxative — draws water into colon 2–4 L over several hours; can cause nausea/bloating; ensure hydration; contraindicated in bowel obstruction
Bisacodyl (Dulcolax) Stimulant — increases bowel motility Given night before; can cause cramping; often combined with PEG
Sodium Phosphate
Fleet Phospho-Soda		 Osmotic — rapid bowel emptying Rarely used now (risk of phosphate nephropathy); avoid in elderly, renal disease, or HF
Magnesium Citrate Saline laxative — flushes the bowel Risk of hypermagnesemia in renal disease; potent
Simethicone Antifoaming agent — reduces bubbles Sometimes added to PEG solution; improves visibility; reduces gas/bloating

During the Procedure

  • Sedation: midazolam or propofol
  • Position: left side-lying (Sims' position)
  • Continuous monitoring: HR, BP, RR, SpO₂
If sedation is used → patient must have a ride home; no driving for 24 hours.

Post-Procedure Nursing Care

  • Resume diet as tolerated
  • Encourage passing gas to relieve bloating

Monitor for Complications

ComplicationSigns
Perforation ⚠️Sudden severe abdominal pain, rigidity, fever, tachycardia
BleedingExcessive rectal bleeding (small amounts normal if biopsy taken)
InfectionFever, chills

Red Flags to Report

  • Severe abdominal pain or cramping
  • Fever or chills
  • Bright red blood in stool
  • Dizziness or signs of hypotension

⚡ NCLEX Priorities

  • Screening starts at age 45, every 10 years for average risk
  • Bowel prep = essential for test accuracy; prep until stool is clear
  • Sedation → patient needs a driver home, no driving 24 hrs
  • Post-procedure: monitor for perforation (sudden severe pain + rigidity)
  • Avoid sodium phosphate in elderly/renal/HF patients → nephropathy + electrolyte imbalances
  • Sims' position = left side-lying during procedure
