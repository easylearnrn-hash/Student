Pancreatitis

Inflammation of the Pancreas — Acute & Chronic

1. What is Pancreatitis?

  • Inflammation of the pancreas — vital organ for digestion & blood sugar control
  • When inflamed → pancreas digests itself (autodigestion)
  • Digestive enzymes leak into surrounding tissues → systemic complications
💡 Acute = sudden, often reversible
⚠️ Chronic = long-term, irreversible damage

2. Anatomy & Function of the Pancreas

Located behind the stomach, near the duodenum

Exocrine (Digestion)

  • Amylase → breaks down carbohydrates
  • Protease → breaks down proteins
  • Lipase → breaks down fats
  • Bicarbonate → neutralizes stomach acid

Endocrine (Hormones)

  • Insulin → lowers blood sugar
  • Glucagon → raises blood sugar
  • Somatostatin & Pancreatic Polypeptide → regulate enzymes

3. Pathophysiology

  • Normally: enzymes activate after leaving the pancreas
  • In pancreatitis: enzymes activate too early → pancreas digests itself → tissue damage, necrosis, swelling

System Effects

Malabsorption Steatorrhea (fatty stool) Blood sugar problems Multi-organ failure (severe)

4. Causes

Acute Pancreatitis

  • 🫒 Gallstones (block ducts) — #1 cause
  • 🍷 Alcohol abuse — #2 cause
  • Trauma, infections, tumors
  • Medications (steroids, diuretics)
  • ERCP complications

Chronic Pancreatitis

  • 🍷 Long-term alcohol abuse — #1 cause
  • Cystic fibrosis (thick mucus blocks ducts)
  • Hypercalcemia (high calcium)
  • Hyperlipidemia (high fat)

5. Symptoms

Acute

  • 🔥 Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back
  • Pain worse lying flat → better sitting up/leaning forward
  • Fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Nausea, vomiting
🔵 Cullen's Sign — bluish belly button = internal bleeding
🔵 Grey Turner's Sign — bluish flanks = retroperitoneal bleeding

Chronic

  • Chronic dull pain
  • Weight loss, malabsorption
  • Steatorrhea (fatty, smelly, floating stool)
  • Jaundice (yellow skin/eyes)
  • Diabetes mellitus (islet cell damage)
🚨 Cullen's + Grey Turner's signs = internal/retroperitoneal bleeding — EMERGENCY!

6. Diagnosis

Labs

  • ↑ Amylase
  • ↑ Lipase — more specific!
  • ↓ Calcium
  • ↑ Blood sugar
  • Electrolyte imbalances

Imaging

  • CT scan
  • Ultrasound
  • ERCP (scope for ducts)
✅ Lipase is more specific than amylase for diagnosing pancreatitis!

7. Nursing Interventions

Goals: Rest the Pancreas, Manage Pain, Support Nutrition & Glucose

Diet & Nutrition

  • 🚫 NPO to rest the pancreas
  • 💧 IV fluids for hydration
  • 🏥 TPN if NPO long-term
  • Resume: low-fat, high-protein diet
  • 🚫 No alcohol ever

Pain Management

  • IV pain meds
  • Avoid morphine — may cause sphincter of Oddi spasms
  • ✅ Use Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) instead
  • Position: sitting up / lean forward for comfort

Blood Sugar Monitoring

  • Monitor for hyperglycemia
  • Give insulin if needed

NG Tube

  • Decompression if vomiting/bloating
  • Prevents further pancreatic stimulation

Medications

  • Pancreatic enzymes (e.g., Creon) for chronic pancreatitis
  • PPIs / H2 blockers to reduce stomach acid
  • Antibiotics if infection suspected

8. Acute vs Chronic — NCLEX Focus

FeatureAcuteChronic
OnsetSuddenLong-term, progressive
ReversibilityOften reversibleIrreversible
CausesGallstones, alcoholAlcohol, cystic fibrosis
Key SymptomsSharp pain, fever, N/VDull pain, steatorrhea, diabetes
Major RisksShock, ARDS, atelectasisMalnutrition, insulin dependence

9. NCLEX Must-Know Summary

✅ Priority: NPO + IV fluids + pain meds
✅ Lipase = more specific than amylase
✅ Pain relief: sit up/lean forward
✅ Enzyme replacement for chronic pancreatitis
⚠️ Avoid morphine → sphincter of Oddi spasm; use Dilaudid
⚠️ Cullen's + Grey Turner's = internal bleeding
⚠️ Worsening signs: ↓ BP, ↑ HR, ↓ urine, ARDS
⚠️ No alcohol — ever!
