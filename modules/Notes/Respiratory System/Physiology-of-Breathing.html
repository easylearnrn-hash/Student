Respiratory System

Physiology of Breathing

Ventilation mechanics, gas exchange, and respiratory control — NCLEX essentials

Overview
Core Concept: Breathing (ventilation) moves air in and out so the body gains O₂ and expels CO₂. Controlled automatically by the brainstem — CO₂ is the primary driver, not O₂.
Two Phases of Breathing

↓ Inhalation (Inspiration)

  • Diaphragm contracts → moves DOWN
  • Rib cage lifts up and out
  • Chest cavity expands → negative pressure
  • Air rushes IN (high → low pressure)

↑ Exhalation (Expiration)

  • Diaphragm relaxes → moves UP
  • Rib cage lowers and in
  • Chest cavity decreases → pressure rises
  • Air pushed OUT
NCLEX Tip: Inhale = diaphragm DOWN, chest expands. Exhale = diaphragm UP, chest shrinks.
What Controls Breathing?

Brainstem Control Center

  • Medulla oblongata — sets the basic rhythm of breathing
  • Pons — fine-tunes the rate and depth
  • They sense CO₂ levels in the blood
  • High CO₂ → breathe faster
  • Normal CO₂ → breathing slows back down
NCLEX #1 Fact: CO₂ is the primary driver of breathing — not O₂! Exception: COPD patients develop hypoxic drive (O₂ becomes the driver — do not give high-flow O₂).
Gas Exchange — Alveoli

Diffusion in the Alveoli

  • Alveoli = tiny air sacs surrounded by capillaries
  • O₂ moves from alveoli → into bloodstream
  • CO₂ moves from bloodstream → into alveoli → exhaled
  • Movement by diffusion (high → low concentration)
  • Gas exchange happens only in alveoli — NOT in airways

Muscles of Breathing

Primary Muscle

  • Diaphragm — main muscle of breathing
  • Innervated by the phrenic nerve (C3–C5)
  • "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive"

Accessory Muscles

  • Intercostals (between ribs)
  • Sternocleidomastoid
  • Scalenes
  • Use = sign of respiratory distress
NCLEX High-Yield Facts

Must-Know for NCLEX

  • Diaphragm = primary muscle of breathing
  • Brainstem (medulla + pons) controls breathing automatically
  • CO₂ levels — not O₂ — drive respiratory rate in healthy patients
  • Phrenic nerve damage (C3–C5) → diaphragm paralysis → respiratory failure
  • Gas exchange only happens in the alveoli
  • Negative pressure pulls air in during inhalation
  • Dead space = air that does NOT participate in gas exchange
  • Accessory muscle use = always a sign of respiratory distress
Pressure Concepts

Boyle's Law Applied to Breathing

Pressure × Volume = Constant (when temperature is fixed)

Inspiration: Volume ↑ → Pressure ↓ → Air flows IN (external air pressure is higher)
Expiration: Volume ↓ → Pressure ↑ → Air flows OUT (lung pressure is now higher)
