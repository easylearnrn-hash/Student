Respiratory System

Emphysema — "Pink Puffer"

Alveolar destruction, air trapping, hyperinflation — COPD subtype NCLEX review

Definition
Emphysema: Chronic lung disease characterized by destruction of alveoli, leading to air trapping, decreased gas exchange, and hyperinflation of the lungs. Part of COPD. Nickname = "Pink Puffer" (pink complexion + pursed-lip breathing).
Pink Puffer vs. Blue Bloater

Emphysema — "Pink Puffer"

  • Pink complexion — compensatory hyperventilation
  • Pursed-lip breathing
  • Barrel chest
  • Puffer — dyspnea and air hunger
  • Thin, cachexic appearance
  • Diminished breath sounds
  • Prolonged expiration + wheezing

Chronic Bronchitis — "Blue Bloater"

  • Blue — cyanosis (poor O₂)
  • Productive cough with thick mucus
  • Bloater — peripheral edema (cor pulmonale)
  • Overweight appearance
  • Crackles and wheezing
Pathophysiology

What Happens in Emphysema

  • Smoke/irritants destroy alveolar walls → large air spaces (bullae)
  • Loss of lung elasticity → airways collapse during exhalation → air trapping
  • Hyperinflation → barrel chest, flattened diaphragm
  • Decreased surface area for gas exchange → hypoxia
  • CO₂ retention → respiratory acidosis risk
Causes & Risk Factors

Risk Factors

  • Smoking — #1 cause (destroys alveolar walls)
  • Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency (genetic — even in non-smokers)
  • Air pollution, occupational dust exposure
  • Chronic respiratory infections
Signs & Symptoms — Classic "Pink Puffer"
Sign/SymptomExplanation
Pink complexionCompensatory hyperventilation maintains near-normal O₂ (unlike Blue Bloater)
Pursed-lip breathingCreates back-pressure to keep airways open during exhalation — prevents air trapping
Barrel chestChronic hyperinflation pushes ribs outward → AP diameter = lateral diameter
Dyspnea on exertionProgressive — worsens over time with activity
Diminished breath soundsDestroyed alveoli = less lung tissue → quieter sounds
Prolonged expiration + wheezingAirway collapse during exhalation prolongs the expiratory phase
CachexiaWeight loss + muscle wasting from high work of breathing and decreased appetite
Complications

Respiratory

  • Respiratory acidosis (CO₂ retention)
  • Frequent infections (impaired mucus clearance)
  • Pneumothorax (ruptured bullae)
  • Acute respiratory failure

Cardiovascular

  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Cor pulmonale (right-sided heart failure)
  • Polycythemia → DVT risk
Diagnosis
TestFinding in Emphysema
PFTs (Pulmonary Function Tests)↓ FEV1/FVC ratio (obstructive pattern), ↑ TLC (air trapping)
Chest X-rayHyperinflated lungs, flattened diaphragm, increased AP diameter
ABG (late stage)↑ CO₂, ↓ O₂, ↓ pH (respiratory acidosis)
CT ScanGold standard — shows bullae (destroyed alveolar spaces)
NCLEX Priority Interventions

Priority Nursing Actions

  • Pursed-lip breathing — prolongs exhalation, prevents air trapping — NCLEX #1
  • High Fowler's position — optimizes breathing mechanics
  • Low-flow O₂ (1–2 L/min via nasal cannula) — High O₂ can eliminate hypoxic drive → apnea
  • Bronchodilators — Albuterol (SABA), Ipratropium
  • Corticosteroids — for inflammation during exacerbations
  • Smoking cessation — most important intervention!
  • Pulmonary rehab + breathing exercises
CRITICAL: High-flow O₂ in emphysema removes the hypoxic drive → patient stops breathing. Always use low-flow O₂ targeting SpO₂ 88–92%.
Patient Education

Home Management

  • Avoid high-carb diets — carbs produce more CO₂ during metabolism; eat high-protein, high-fat instead
  • Increase fluids — 2–3 L/day to loosen mucus
  • Frequent small meals — prevent breathlessness while eating (big meals push diaphragm up)
  • Vaccinations — flu and pneumonia yearly
  • Avoid respiratory irritants — smoke, perfumes, cold air, pollution
  • Diaphragmatic breathing — strengthen diaphragm, reduce accessory muscle use
NCLEX Diet Tip: Emphysema patients should AVOID high-carbohydrate foods — carbohydrate metabolism produces more CO₂ than fat or protein metabolism, which worsens hypercapnia.
