Respiratory System

ARDS — Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Refractory hypoxemia, 3 phases, ARDS CARE mnemonic, PEEP, prone positioning, hypoxemia vs hypoxia

Overview
ARDS = inflammatory response causing fluid to leak from capillaries into the alveoli → alveoli fill with fluid → refractory hypoxemia (low O₂ that does NOT improve even with high-flow O₂). Life-threatening. Requires mechanical ventilation.
Causes

Direct (Pulmonary) Causes

  • Pneumonia (most common direct)
  • Aspiration of gastric contents
  • Pulmonary contusion (chest trauma)
  • Near-drowning
  • Inhalation injury

Indirect (Extrapulmonary) Causes

  • Sepsis — #1 most common cause overall
  • Pancreatitis
  • Massive blood transfusion
  • Burns
  • Drug overdose
  • Shock
3 Phases of ARDS
Phase 1
Exudative
Days 1–7

Capillary injury → fluid + protein leaks into alveoli → alveolar collapse. Refractory hypoxemia begins. Bilateral infiltrates on X-ray.

Phase 2
Proliferative
Days 7–14

Lung begins repair. Type II pneumocytes proliferate. Fibroblasts form. Some patients improve; others progress to fibrosis.

Phase 3
Fibrotic
Weeks 2–3+

Permanent scar tissue (fibrosis). Severe lung stiffness. Very poor prognosis if reached. May require long-term ventilation.

Signs & Symptoms — Mnemonic: ARDS
A
Atelectasis — widespread alveolar collapse
R
Refractory hypoxemia — O₂ does NOT improve despite high FiO₂
D
Decreased lung compliance — lungs are stiff, hard to ventilate ("stiff lung")
S
Severe dyspnea — tachypnea, accessory muscle use, cyanosis, air hunger

Additional Symptoms

  • Tachycardia, tachypnea
  • Restlessness/confusion — early hypoxia sign
  • Cyanosis (late, severe)
  • Crackles/rales bilaterally on auscultation
  • Increased work of breathing (intercostal retractions)
Diagnostics

ABG Findings

  • ↓ PaO₂ (hypoxemia)
  • ↓ SaO₂
  • Early: Respiratory alkalosis (↓ CO₂, hyperventilating)
  • Late: Respiratory acidosis (CO₂ builds up as patient tires)

Chest X-Ray (CXR)

  • "White-out lungs" — bilateral diffuse infiltrates
  • No cardiomegaly (differentiates from CHF)
  • No pleural effusion (differentiates from pulmonary edema)
PaO₂/FiO₂ Ratio (P/F ratio): ARDS diagnosis requires P/F <300 (mild), <200 (moderate), <100 (severe). Key Berlin Definition criterion.
Treatment — Mnemonic: ARDS CARE
A
Airway — intubation + mechanical ventilation with low tidal volume (6 mL/kg) + PEEP
R
Respiratory assessment — continuous ABGs, breath sounds, RR, SpO₂
D
Drugs — sedatives (↓O₂ demand), diuretics (↓lung fluid), corticosteroids (↓inflammation), paralytics (severe cases)
S
Suctioning + Prone positioning — suction as needed; prone 16+ hrs/day → opens dorsal alveoli → ↑ oxygenation
C
Continuous monitoring — SpO₂, vent alarms, hemodynamics, fluid balance
A
Avoid fluid overload — conservative IV fluid strategy → prevents more fluid in alveoli
R
Restrict activity — minimize O₂ demand; prevent further injury
E
Enteral nutrition early — start within 24–48 hrs; prevents muscle wasting, maintains gut integrity
PEEP — Positive End-Expiratory Pressure

Why PEEP is Critical in ARDS

  • Keeps alveoli open at the end of exhalation → prevents alveolar collapse (atelectasis)
  • Increases functional residual capacity (FRC)
  • Allows use of lower FiO₂ while maintaining adequate oxygenation → reduces O₂ toxicity
  • Typical PEEP in ARDS: 5–20 cmH₂O
  • Risk: Too high PEEP → barotrauma (pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum)
Prone Positioning

Best Position for ARDS = PRONE (Face Down)

  • Redistributes lung perfusion to better-ventilated regions
  • Opens collapsed dorsal (back) alveoli
  • Improves V/Q (ventilation-perfusion) matching
  • Target: 16+ hours per day prone for moderate-severe ARDS
  • Requires specialized nursing team (minimum 5 staff to turn safely)
NCLEX: Best position for ARDS = prone position. This is the most evidence-based positioning strategy to improve oxygenation.
Hypoxemia vs. Hypoxia — Critical Distinction

Hypoxemia

  • Lab value — low oxygen in the blood
  • PaO₂ <80 mmHg or SpO₂ <90%
  • Measured on ABG or pulse oximeter
  • May exist without symptoms (early)

Hypoxia

  • Clinical state — inadequate O₂ at the tissue level
  • Signs: restlessness, confusion, cyanosis, tachypnea, tachycardia
  • Late signs: bradycardia, hypotension
  • Can occur even with normal blood O₂ (e.g., anemia)
Complications

VAP

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia — most common complication. Prevent with HOB 30–45°, oral care, cuff pressure monitoring.

Barotrauma

Pressure injury from high vent pressures → pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum. Prevent with low TV + optimal PEEP.

Multi-Organ Failure

Prolonged hypoxemia → kidney, liver, heart failure. Monitor creatinine, LFTs, urine output hourly.

NCLEX Must-Know

Top NCLEX Points

  • Most specific sign of ARDS = refractory hypoxemia (O₂ doesn't improve)
  • First priority = O₂ therapy → then notify provider
  • Best position = prone
  • Best diagnostic = ABG + CXR (bilateral white-out, no cardiomegaly)
  • Key vent setting = PEEP
  • Use lowest FiO₂ to maintain PaO₂ >60 or SpO₂ >90%
  • Early = respiratory alkalosis; Late = respiratory acidosis
  • #1 cause = sepsis
  • Hypoxemia = blood O₂; Hypoxia = tissue O₂
  • Enteral nutrition within 24–48 hours (prevents wasting)
  • Prevent VAP: HOB 30–45°, oral care, vent bundle
