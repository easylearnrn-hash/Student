Pyelonephritis

Upper UTI — ascending infection reaching the kidney(s); E. coli most common cause

Flank Pain + Fever + CVA Tenderness Culture BEFORE Antibiotics Ascending E. coli Sepsis Risk

1. Pathophysiology

  • Bacteria ascend from the urethra → bladder (cystitis) → ureters → kidney(s)
  • Most common pathogen: E. coli (~80% of cases)
  • Risk factors: female anatomy (short urethra), pregnancy, sexual intercourse, urinary tract abnormalities, catheterization, diabetes, immunosuppression
  • Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) — common risk in children; allows urine to flow backward from bladder into ureters/kidneys → ascending infection

2. Classic Signs & Symptoms

  • 🔴 Flank pain (unilateral or bilateral) — dull to severe ache
  • 🔴 High fever (>38.5°C / 101°F) + rigors (shaking chills)
  • 🔴 CVA tenderness — pain at costovertebral angle when tapped
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Dysuria, frequency, urgency (lower UTI symptoms may also be present)
  • Malaise, fatigue
CVA Tenderness Assessment: Percuss (tap) the area at the lower back where the ribs meet the spine (costovertebral angle). Positive = patient winces or reports pain.

3. Mnemonic — "PAINFUL KIDNEY"

PAINFUL KIDNEY

  • P — Pyuria (pus in urine — WBCs)
  • A — Ascending E. coli
  • I — Infection of the upper tract
  • N — Nausea / vomiting
  • F — Flank pain
  • U — Urgency + frequency
  • L — Lab: UA + culture before antibiotics
  • K — Kidney involvement (upper UTI)
  • I — IV antibiotics if severe
  • D — Dysuria + high fever + chills
  • N — No delay — treat promptly to prevent sepsis
  • E — E. coli most common
  • Y — Yield culture results before changing antibiotics

4. Diagnostics

CRITICAL: Obtain urine culture & sensitivity BEFORE starting antibiotics — this ensures correct organism identification
TestExpected Finding
Urinalysis (UA)Pyuria (WBCs), bacteriuria, possible casts, hematuria, positive nitrites & leukocyte esterase
Urine culture & sensitivityE. coli or other pathogen; guides antibiotic choice
CBCElevated WBC (leukocytosis) — confirms infection/inflammation
BMPElevated creatinine / BUN if AKI develops
CT scan (if needed)Rules out obstruction, abscess, or structural abnormality

5. Treatment

SeverityTreatment
Mild / OutpatientCiprofloxacin PO × 7–14 days; or TMP-SMX × 14 days (if sensitive)
Moderate–Severe / InpatientIV ceftriaxone or IV ciprofloxacin → transition to PO when afebrile × 24–48 hrs
PregnantIV cephalosporins; hospitalize if severe
  • Adequate hydration: 2–3 L/day to flush kidneys
  • Antipyretics (acetaminophen) for fever management
  • Analgesics for flank pain

6. Complications

ComplicationDescription
Urosepsis / SepticemiaBacteria enter bloodstream → septic shock; life-threatening
Renal abscessPus collection in kidney — may need drainage
Acute kidney injury (AKI)Sudden ↓ in GFR from inflammation
Chronic kidney disease (CKD)Repeated infections → scarring → permanent damage
Emphysematous pyelonephritisGas-forming infection — more common in diabetics; surgical emergency

7. Patient Teaching

  • ✅ Wipe front to back after toileting (women)
  • ✅ Void before and after intercourse
  • ✅ Drink 8–10 glasses of water per day
  • ✅ Complete the full antibiotic course even if feeling better
  • ✅ Return to provider if symptoms worsen or fever persists
  • ✅ Avoid prolonged holding of urine
  • ✅ Follow-up urine culture after treatment to confirm clearance
🔑 Key Takeaway: Upper UTI → kidney infection. E. coli most common. Classic triad: flank pain + high fever + CVA tenderness. Culture BEFORE antibiotics. Complications: sepsis, renal abscess, AKI, CKD. Teach: front-to-back, void after sex, complete full antibiotic course.
