What Is Cancer?

Cancer is a disease of uncontrolled, abnormal cell growth caused by DNA mutations. Cancer cells do not respond to normal cell-cycle regulation — they grow, invade nearby tissue, and can spread (metastasize) to distant organs.

What Causes Cancer?

Genetic
DNA mutations — inherited or acquired during life
Carcinogens
Tobacco, radiation, chemicals, UV light
Viruses
HPV → cervical; HepB/C → liver
Chronic Inflammation
Repeated tissue damage/repair cycle promotes mutations
Weakened Immune System
Immunosuppression allows mutated cells to escape surveillance

Staging & Grading

Staging — How Far Has It Spread?
  • TNM System: T = tumor size, N = node involvement, M = metastasis
  • Stage 0: In situ — no invasion
  • Stage I: Localized, small
  • Stage II: Local spread
  • Stage III: Extensive lymph node involvement
  • Stage IV: Distant metastasis — most advanced
Grading — How Aggressive Are the Cells?
  • Low-grade (well-differentiated): Slow-growing, look like normal cells
  • High-grade (poorly differentiated): Fast-growing, look abnormal
  • Grade I → IV (I=slowest, IV=most aggressive)
💡
NCLEX Tip

M0 = no distant metastasis (positive indicator). M1 = metastatic disease. Staging does NOT change after initial diagnosis even if disease progresses.

Treatment Modalities

1) Surgery

Curative
Remove tumor entirely — goal is cure
Palliative
Relieve symptoms (pain, obstruction) — not curable

2) Chemotherapy Side Effects High-Yield

  • Myelosuppression — ↓WBC (neutropenia), ↓RBC (anemia), ↓platelets (thrombocytopenia)
  • Nausea/vomiting — give antiemetics (ondansetron) before chemo
  • Alopecia — hair loss; reassure patient it is temporary
  • Mucositis — painful mouth sores; oral care with soft toothbrush, avoid alcohol-based mouthwash
  • Fatigue — conserve energy, priority nursing concern

3) Radiation Therapy

External Beam Radiation
Focused radiation from outside the body; protect skin (no harsh soaps, no sun exposure)
Brachytherapy
Radioactive seeds/implants placed inside or near tumor; radiation precautions — limit visitor time, distance, shielding

4) Immunotherapy

  • Uses the immune system to fight cancer
  • Examples: pembrolizumab (PD-1 inhibitor — lung, melanoma), trastuzumab/Herceptin (HER2+ breast/gastric)
  • Side effects: immune-related reactions (colitis, pneumonitis, endocrinopathies)

5) Hormone Therapy

  • Blocks or lowers hormones that feed hormone-sensitive tumors
  • Examples: tamoxifen (breast — ER+), leuprolide/flutamide (prostate — androgen deprivation)

6) Stem Cell Transplant

  • Autologous — patient's own cells; less rejection risk
  • Allogeneic — donor cells; risk of GVHD (Graft-vs-Host Disease: rash, liver damage, GI involvement)

Nursing Priorities (NCLEX-Style)

🚨
Priority #1 — Infection Risk (Neutropenia)

ANC <500 = severe neutropenia. Strict hand hygiene, no fresh flowers/raw produce, private room, report temp ≥38°C (100.4°F) immediately.

🩸
Priority #2 — Bleeding Risk (Thrombocytopenia)

Platelets <50,000 = bleeding risk; <20,000 = emergency. Soft toothbrush, electric razor, avoid IM injections, report bruising/bleeding.

💊
Chemotherapy Nursing

Give antiemetic (ondansetron) before chemo. Monitor CBC — report nadir (lowest blood count, usually 7–14 days post-chemo). Oral care for mucositis. Small frequent meals for nausea.

Core Nursing Interventions

  • 🦠 Infection prevention: strict hand hygiene, neutropenic precautions, monitor temp
  • 🩹 Skin/mucous membrane care: oral hygiene, protect radiation skin field
  • 🍽️ Nutrition: small frequent meals, antiemetics, high-protein diet; involve dietitian
  • 😴 Fatigue management: pace activities, prioritize rest, conserve energy
  • 💬 Psychosocial: body image (alopecia), anxiety, grief — therapeutic communication, support groups
  • 📋 Education: teach CAUTION warning signs, screening schedules, treatment side effects
