Female Reproductive Cancers

Cervical · Endometrial · Ovarian · Vaginal/Vulvar · Breast — NCLEX-focused

Cervical Cancer

Risk Factors
  • HPV (Human Papillomavirus) — #1 cause (especially HPV 16 & 18)
  • Multiple sexual partners / early sexual activity
  • Smoking
  • Immunosuppression (HIV)
  • Long-term oral contraceptive use
Classic Symptoms
  • Postcoital bleeding — bleeding after intercourse (classic NCLEX clue)
  • Watery or foul-smelling vaginal discharge
  • Pelvic pain (later stages)
  • Abnormal vaginal bleeding between periods
  • Early stages — often asymptomatic

Cervical Cancer Screening (Pap + HPV)

Age GroupRecommended Screening
<21No screening
21–29Pap smear alone every 3 years
30–65Pap smear + HPV co-test every 5 years (preferred) OR Pap alone every 3 years
>65Stop screening if adequate prior normal results and no high-risk factors
Post-hysterectomyStop screening if cervix removed and no history of high-grade dysplasia

Treatment

Early / Pre-invasive
  • LEEP (Loop Electrosurgical Excision Procedure) — removes abnormal cells from cervix
  • Conization (cold knife cone biopsy) — removes cone-shaped tissue with precancerous cells
  • Cryotherapy — for mild dysplasia
Advanced / Invasive
  • Radical hysterectomy — remove uterus, cervix, upper vagina, pelvic LN
  • Concurrent chemoradiation (cisplatin-based)
  • Radiation alone for non-surgical candidates
💉
Prevention — HPV Vaccine (Gardasil)

Recommended for girls and boys aged 9–26 years. Protects against HPV strains 6, 11, 16, 18 (and more with 9-valent). Can be given up to age 45. Vaccination + screening together = best prevention.

Endometrial (Uterine) Cancer

Risk Factors
  • Unopposed estrogen — #1 risk (no progesterone to counteract)
  • Obesity — adipose tissue converts androgens → estrogen
  • Nulliparity (never pregnant)
  • Late menopause / early menarche
  • Diabetes mellitus / metabolic syndrome
  • PCOS (polycystic ovarian syndrome)
  • Tamoxifen use (stimulates endometrium while blocking breast)
  • Lynch syndrome (hereditary)
Classic Symptoms
  • Postmenopausal vaginal bleeding — #1 symptom (KEY NCLEX CLUE!)
  • Any abnormal uterine bleeding in postmenopausal woman requires evaluation
  • Pelvic pain or pressure
  • Abnormal premenopausal bleeding between periods

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis
  • Endometrial biopsy — gold standard
  • Transvaginal ultrasound — assess endometrial thickness (>4mm in postmenopausal is suspicious)
  • D&C (dilation and curettage) if biopsy inconclusive
Treatment
  • Total abdominal hysterectomy + bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy (TAH-BSO) — surgical standard
  • + pelvic lymph node dissection
  • Adjuvant radiation and/or chemotherapy for advanced stages
  • Progestins for patients who cannot have surgery
🚨
NCLEX Alert — Postmenopausal Bleeding

Any postmenopausal bleeding must be evaluated for endometrial cancer. It is NOT normal. Report immediately. Gold standard diagnosis = endometrial biopsy.

Ovarian Cancer — "The Silent Killer"

Risk Factors
  • BRCA1 / BRCA2 gene mutations — highest risk
  • Family history of ovarian or breast cancer
  • Nulliparity
  • Older age (postmenopausal)
  • Endometriosis
  • Hormone replacement therapy (long-term)
  • Protective factors: oral contraceptives, pregnancy, breastfeeding
Why "Silent Killer"
  • Vague, late-presenting symptoms — diagnosed at advanced stage (Stage III/IV)
  • Persistent bloating / abdominal distension
  • Pelvic pressure or pain
  • Urinary urgency / frequency
  • Early satiety / decreased appetite
  • No effective early screening test! — CA-125 not specific enough

Diagnosis & Treatment

StepDetail
CA-125Tumor marker — elevated in ovarian cancer (not specific; also elevated in endometriosis, PID, pregnancy). Used to monitor treatment response, not for screening.
Pelvic ultrasound / CT scanInitial imaging; assess mass characteristics
Definitive diagnosisSurgical biopsy / tissue from debulking
SurgeryTAH-BSO + surgical debulking (cytoreductive surgery — remove as much tumor as possible)
ChemotherapyPaclitaxel (Taxol) + Cisplatin or Carboplatin — standard adjuvant
ScreeningNo routine population screening — pelvic exam + symptom awareness

Vaginal & Vulvar Cancers

Vaginal Cancer
  • Risk: HPV, prior cervical cancer, DES (diethylstilbestrol) exposure in utero
  • Symptoms: abnormal vaginal bleeding, foul discharge, dyspareunia, pelvic pain
  • Dx: Pap smear, colposcopy, biopsy
  • Tx: Radiation ± surgery
Vulvar Cancer
  • Risk: HPV, smoking, lichen sclerosis (chronic vulvar condition), older age
  • Symptoms: persistent vulvar itching, pain, burning, visible lump or ulcer, bleeding
  • Dx: Vulvar biopsy — any suspicious lesion must be biopsied
  • Tx: Surgical excision (vulvectomy), sentinel LN biopsy, radiation

Breast Cancer

Risk Factors
  • BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations
  • Family history (1st-degree relative)
  • Age >50 (most common)
  • Early menarche / late menopause
  • Nulliparity / late first pregnancy
  • Obesity (postmenopausal)
  • Alcohol use
  • Prior breast cancer or radiation to chest
Classic Symptoms
  • Painless lump — most common presentation (typically upper outer quadrant)
  • Peau d'orange — "orange peel" skin — indicates inflammatory breast cancer or skin edema from lymph blockage
  • Nipple retraction or discharge (non-lactating)
  • Breast asymmetry / skin dimpling
  • Axillary lymphadenopathy
Diagnostics
  • Mammogram — standard screening (start age 40–50 depending on guideline)
  • Ultrasound — differentiate cyst vs solid mass
  • MRI — high-risk patients (BRCA+), dense breasts
  • Core needle biopsy — definitive diagnosis
  • Hormone receptor testing (ER/PR/HER2)

Treatment

Treatment TypeDetail
LumpectomyBreast-conserving surgery; remove tumor + margin + axillary LN; followed by radiation
MastectomyFull breast removal; simple (breast only), modified radical (breast + axillary LN), radical (rare)
ChemotherapyPre-op (neoadjuvant) or post-op; regimens vary by stage and receptor status
TamoxifenFor ER/PR-positive cancers in premenopausal women; blocks estrogen receptors; take 5–10 years
Aromatase inhibitorsAnastrozole, letrozole — for postmenopausal ER/PR-positive breast cancer
Trastuzumab (Herceptin)For HER2-positive cancers; targeted antibody therapy
💪
Post-Mastectomy Lymphedema — Critical NCLEX Points

Affected arm precautions (lifetime):
• No blood pressure measurements on affected arm
• No IV access or venipuncture on affected arm
• No tight clothing, jewelry, or compression on affected arm
• Protect from cuts and infection
• Elevate arm when at rest
• Begin arm exercises (pendulum) as directed post-op to restore ROM and reduce lymphedema risk

Post-Hysterectomy Nursing Care

⚠️
Post-Hysterectomy: DVT Risk + Activity Restrictions

• DVT risk — immobility + pelvic surgery; apply SCDs (sequential compression devices), early ambulation, assess for calf pain/Homan's sign
• No heavy lifting (more than 10 lbs) for 4–6 weeks
• No sexual intercourse for 6 weeks (until pelvic floor healed)
• Report: excessive bleeding, fever, foul vaginal discharge, difficulty urinating
• Grief/emotional support — loss of fertility; menopause if ovaries removed (BSO)
