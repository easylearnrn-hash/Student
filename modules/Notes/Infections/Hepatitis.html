Hepatitis A–E

Liver inflammation from hepatitis viruses — know routes, chronicity, and NCLEX priorities

Hepatitis Type Comparison Table

Type Transmission Chronicity Vaccine Key Points
Hep A Fecal-oral Acute only ✅ Yes Contaminated food/water, shellfish; best prevented by hand hygiene + vaccine
Hep B Blood/body fluids Chronic 10% adults, 90% infants ✅ Yes HBsAg = infected; anti-HBs = immune; most common cause of cirrhosis + liver cancer
Hep C Blood-to-blood Chronic most cases ❌ No vaccine Leading cause of liver transplant; IV drug use, needle sticks; treat with DAAs (sofosbuvir)
Hep D (Delta) Blood (co-/super-infection) Worsens HBV HBV vaccine prevents Only occurs with HBV; Hep B vaccine prevents D as well
Hep E Fecal-oral Acute only ❌ Not in US High mortality in pregnant women (1st trimester); common in developing countries

Clinical Phases

Prodromal Phase

  • Fatigue, nausea, vomiting
  • RUQ pain / liver tenderness
  • Low-grade fever
  • Headache, arthralgias, malaise

Icteric Phase (Jaundice)

  • Jaundice (yellow sclera/skin)
  • Dark "cola" urine (bilirubinuria)
  • Clay-colored/pale stools
  • Pruritus (itching)
  • Hepatomegaly

Recovery Phase:

  • Jaundice resolves, energy returns, liver enzymes normalize
  • Can take several weeks to months

Key Lab Values

Lab MarkerMeaning
ALT / AST ↑↑Liver cell damage (AST > ALT = more severe)
Total Bilirubin ↑Jaundice; dark urine; clay stools
PT / INR ↑Impaired clotting factor synthesis (liver dysfunction)
Alkaline Phosphatase ↑Bile duct involvement
HBsAgActive HBV infection (surface antigen)
Anti-HBsImmunity (vaccine or recovery)
Anti-HCVHep C exposure; confirm with RNA viral load

Treatment

  • Hep A/E: Supportive — rest, nutrition, hydration, avoid alcohol/hepatotoxic drugs
  • Hep B (chronic): Tenofovir or Entecavir (antivirals)
  • Hep C: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — sofosbuvir/ledipasvir (Harvoni); >95% cure rate
  • Monitor PT/INR — avoid ASA, NSAIDs, alcohol

NCLEX Must-Know

  • A & E = fecal-oral; B, C, D = blood/body fluids
  • Jaundice + dark urine + clay stools = classic triad
  • Monitor PT/INR (liver makes clotting factors)
  • Hep C = no vaccine; leading cause of liver transplant
  • Hep D only occurs in presence of HBV → Hep B vaccine prevents HDV
  • Hep B vaccine series for all newborns (within 12 hours of birth)
🧠
NCLEX KEY:

A patient with clay-colored stools and dark urine — think hepatitis icteric phase; check bilirubin + liver enzymes. Monitor PT/INR for bleeding risk.
