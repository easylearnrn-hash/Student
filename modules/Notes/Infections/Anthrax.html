Anthrax

Serious bacterial infection caused by Bacillus anthracis spores

🧠 KEY: Anthrax is NOT spread person-to-person — no isolation needed beyond standard precautions.

3 Types of Anthrax

TypeRouteKey SignsSeverity
CutaneousSkin cuts/breaksBlack painless ulcer (eschar) with swellingMost common, least deadly
InhalationBreathing in sporesFlu-like → rapid progression to sepsis/meningitisDeadliest form
GastrointestinalEating infected meatNausea, vomiting, fever, severe abdominal painModerate-severe
🧠
NCLEX:

Black painless ulcer = cutaneous. Flu symptoms that worsen rapidly = inhalation anthrax (bioterrorism concern).

Diagnosis

  • Blood cultures
  • Chest X-ray / CT (inhalation form — widened mediastinum)
  • Wound swab (cutaneous)
  • Exposure history is critical

Treatment

  • Start antibiotics immediately — don't wait for confirmation
  • Ciprofloxacin (fluoroquinolone) or Doxycycline — oral or IV for serious forms
  • Antitoxin may be added for inhalation anthrax

Prevention & PEP

  • Vaccine available (high-risk: military, lab workers)
  • Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP):
    • 60 days of oral Ciprofloxacin or Doxycycline
    • 3 doses of anthrax vaccine

Infection Control

  • NOT contagious person-to-person → no isolation required
  • Standard precautions for wound care (gloves, hand hygiene)

NCLEX Must-Know

  • Black painless eschar → cutaneous anthrax
  • Flu + rapid deterioration → inhalation anthrax (bioterrorism)
  • No person-to-person spread → no isolation
  • Start Cipro/Doxycycline immediately
  • PEP = 60 days antibiotics + vaccine
